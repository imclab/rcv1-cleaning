						<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><span dir="auto">Arts and entertainment in India</span></h1>
			<div id="bodyContent">
								<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
																<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-navigation">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
								<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Arts and entertainment in India</b> have a rich and ancient history. Right from ancient times there has been a synthesis of indigenous and foreign influences that have shaped the course of the arts of India, and consequently, the rest of Asia. Arts refer to <a href="/wiki/Indian_art" title="Indian art">paintings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_architecture" title="Indian architecture" class="mw-redirect">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_literature" title="Indian literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_India" title="Music of India">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_dance" title="Indian classical dance">dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_India" title="Cinema of India">cinema</a>. In early India, most of the arts were derived <a href="/wiki/Vedic_civilization" title="Vedic civilization" class="mw-redirect">Vedic</a> influences. After the birth of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> arts flourished under the patronage of kings and emperors. The coming of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> spawned a whole new era of Indian architecture and art. Finally the British brought their own Gothic and Roman influences and fused it with the Indian style. They have a culture infusion in their art.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Architecture" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_India" title="Architecture of India">Architecture of India</a></div>

<p>Indian architecture is that vast tapestry of production of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> that encompasses a multitude of expressions over space and time, transformed by the forces of history considered unique to the sub-continent, sometimes destroying, but most of the time absorbing. The result is an evolving range of architectural production that nonetheless retains a certain amount of continuity across history.</p>
<p>The earliest production in the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Indus Valley Civilization">Indus Valley Civilization</a> was characterised by well planned cities and houses where religion did not seem to play an active role. The Buddhist period is primarily represented by three important building types- the Chaitya Hall (place of worship), the <a href="/wiki/Vihara" title="Vihara">Vihara</a> (monastery) and the <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">Stupa</a> (hemispherical mound for worship/ memory) - exemplified by the awesome caves of <a href="/wiki/Ajanta" title="Ajanta" class="mw-redirect">Ajanta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ellora_Caves" title="Ellora Caves">Ellora</a> and the monumental <a href="/wiki/Sanchi" title="Sanchi">Sanchi</a> <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">Stupa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jaina</a> temples are characterised by a richness of detail that can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Dilwara_Temples" title="Dilwara Temples">Dilwara Temples</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mt.Abu" title="Mt.Abu" class="mw-redirect">Mt.Abu</a>. Early beginnings of Hindu temple architecture have been traced to the remains at <a href="/wiki/Aihole" title="Aihole">Aihole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pattadakal" title="Pattadakal">Pattadakal</a> in present day <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>, and have <a href="/wiki/Vedic_altars" title="Vedic altars" class="mw-redirect">Vedic altars</a> and late Vedic temples as described by <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">Pāṇini</a> as models. Later, as more differentiation took place, the Dravidian/ Southern style and or the Indo-Aryan/ Northern/ Nagara style of temple architecture emerged as dominant modes, epitomised in productions such as the magnificent <a href="/wiki/Brihadeeswara_Temple" title="Brihadeeswara Temple" class="mw-redirect">Brihadeeswara Temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thanjavur" title="Thanjavur">Thanjavur</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Temple" title="Sun Temple" class="mw-redirect">Sun Temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konark" title="Konark">Konark</a>.</p>
<p>With the advent of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, the arch and dome began to be used and the <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosque</a> or <i>masjid</i> too began to form part of the landscape, adding to a new experience in form and space. The most famous Islamic building type in India is the tomb or the <i>mausoleum</i> which evolved from the basic cube and hemisphere vocabulary of the early phase into a more elaborate form during the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_era" title="Mughal era" class="mw-redirect">Mughal era</a> where multiple chambers are present and tombs were set in a garden known as the char-bagh. Well known examples are the <a href="/wiki/Gol_Gumbaz" title="Gol Gumbaz">Gol Gumbaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bijapur,_Karnataka" title="Bijapur, Karnataka">Bijapur</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal" title="Taj Mahal">Taj Mahal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agra" title="Agra">Agra</a>, the latter renowned for its beauty in white marble, its minarets and its setting. With colonisation, a new chapter began. Though the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> and the French made substantial forays, it was the English who had a lasting impact. The architecture of the colonial period varied from the beginning attempts at creating authority through classical prototypes to the later approach of producing a supposedly more responsive image through what is now termed <a href="/wiki/Indo-Saracenic" title="Indo-Saracenic" class="mw-redirect">Indo-Saracenic</a> architecture- a mixture of Hindu, Islamic and Western elements.</p>
<p>With the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Architecture" title="Modern Architecture" class="mw-redirect">Modern Architecture</a> into India and later with Independence, the quest was more towards progress as a paradigm fuelled by <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Nehruvian</a> visions. The planning of <a href="/wiki/Chandigarh" title="Chandigarh">Chandigarh</a>- a city most architects hate/love- by <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> was considered a step towards this. Later as modernism exhausted itself in the West and new directions were sought for, in India too there was a search for a more meaningful architecture rooted in the Indian context. This direction called <a href="/wiki/Critical_Regionalism" title="Critical Regionalism" class="mw-redirect">Critical Regionalism</a> is exemplified in the works of architects such as <a href="/wiki/B._V._Doshi" title="B. V. Doshi">B. V. Doshi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Correa" title="Charles Correa">Charles Correa</a>, etc. Apart from this, the advent of globalisation and economic development since the 90s, has spawned an impressive collection of modern IT campuses and skyscrapers, and as economic reform accelerates, metropolitan areas are gaining futuristic skylines.</p>

<p>Indian literature is generally acknowledged, but not wholly established, as the oldest in the world. India has 22 officially recognized <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">languages</a>, and a huge variety of literature has been produced in these languages over the years. In Indian literature, <a href="/wiki/Oral_literature" title="Oral literature">oral</a> and written forms are both important. <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> literary traditions dominate a large part of Indian culture. Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> which are a sacred form of knowledge, there are other works such as the Hindu epics <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>, treatises such as <a href="/wiki/Vaastu_Shastra" title="Vaastu Shastra" class="mw-redirect">Vaastu Shastra</a> in architecture and town planning, and <a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a> in political science. Devotional Hindu drama, poetry and songs span the subcontinent. Among the best known are the works of <a href="/wiki/Kalidasa" title="Kalidasa" class="mw-redirect">Kalidasa</a> (writer of the famed Sanskrit play <a href="/wiki/Shakuntala" title="Shakuntala">Shakuntala</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Tulsidas" title="Tulsidas">Tulsidas</a> (who wrote an epic Hindi poem based on the Ramayana, called <a href="/wiki/Raamcharitmaanas" title="Raamcharitmaanas" class="mw-redirect">Raamcharitmaanas</a>).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> literature has been in existence for more than 2500 years. <a href="/wiki/Tolkaappiyam" title="Tolkaappiyam" class="mw-redirect">Tolkaappiyam</a> has been credited as its oldest work, whereas the exact origins of <a href="/wiki/Thirukkural" title="Thirukkural" class="mw-redirect">Thirukkural</a> is unknown. The golden age of Tamil literature was during the <a href="/wiki/Sangam_period" title="Sangam period">Sangam period</a>, roughly 1800 years ago. The classic works of this period are <a href="/wiki/Cilappatikaram" title="Cilappatikaram" class="mw-redirect">Cilappatikaram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manimekalai" title="Manimekalai">Manimekalai</a>, and Sivakasinthamani. Tamil literature is known for its secular traditions, although its authors had strong religious beliefs. <a href="/wiki/Thirukkural" title="Thirukkural" class="mw-redirect">Thirukkural</a> is considered to be the greatest of Tamil works. <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada" class="mw-redirect">Kannada</a> literature is probably the third oldest in Indian literature next to <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_literature" title="Sanskrit literature">Sanskrit literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tamil_literature" title="Tamil literature">Tamil literature</a>. The earliest reported work in Kannada literature dates back to the fifth century. The first available literary in Kannada is <a href="/wiki/Kavirajamarga" title="Kavirajamarga">Kavirajamarga</a>, written in the eighth century by Amoghavarsha Nrpatunga. <a href="/wiki/Hindi_literature" title="Hindi literature">Hindi literature</a> started as religious and philosophical poetry in medieval periods in dialects like <a href="/wiki/Avadhi" title="Avadhi" class="mw-redirect">Avadhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brij" title="Brij" class="mw-redirect">Brij</a>. The most famous figures from this period are <a href="/wiki/Kabir" title="Kabir">Kabir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tulsidas" title="Tulsidas">Tulsidas</a>. In modern times, the <i>Khadi</i> dialect became more prominent and a variety of literature was produced in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language" class="mw-redirect">Sanskrit</a>.</p>
<p>The most renowned Bengali writer is Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>, who received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_for_Literature" title="Nobel Prize for Literature" class="mw-redirect">Nobel Prize for Literature</a>. In the last century, several Indian writers have distinguished themselves not only in traditional <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">Indian languages</a> but also in English. India's only native-born Nobel laureate in literature was the <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengali</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>, but VS Naipaul, a diaspora Indian novelist born in Trinidad, also won the Nobel in 2001. Other major writers who are either Indian or of Indian origin and derive much inspiration from Indian themes are <a href="/wiki/R._K._Narayan" title="R. K. Narayan">R. K. Narayan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vikram_Seth" title="Vikram Seth">Vikram Seth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arundhati_Roy" title="Arundhati Roy">Arundhati Roy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raja_Rao" title="Raja Rao">Raja Rao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amitav_Ghosh" title="Amitav Ghosh">Amitav Ghosh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vikram_Chandra" title="Vikram Chandra" class="mw-redirect">Vikram Chandra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mukul_Kesavan" title="Mukul Kesavan">Mukul Kesavan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shashi_Tharoor" title="Shashi Tharoor">Shashi Tharoor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nayantara_Sehgal" title="Nayantara Sehgal" class="mw-redirect">Nayantara Sehgal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anita_Desai" title="Anita Desai">Anita Desai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashok_Banker" title="Ashok Banker">Ashok Banker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shashi_Deshpande" title="Shashi Deshpande">Shashi Deshpande</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jhumpa_Lahiri" title="Jhumpa Lahiri">Jhumpa Lahiri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bharati_Mukherjee" title="Bharati Mukherjee">Bharati Mukherjee</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_India" title="Music of India">Music of India</a></div>
<p>Indian music includes multiple varieties of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_music" title="Indian classical music">classical music</a>. India's classical music tradition, including <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_music" title="Carnatic music">Carnatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_music" title="Hindustani music" class="mw-redirect">Hindustani music</a>, has a history spanning millennia and, developed over several eras, remains fundamental to the lives of Indians today as sources of religious inspiration, cultural expression and pure entertainment. India is made up of several dozen <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a>, speaking their own <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a>. Alongside distinctly <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">subcontinental</a> forms there are major influences from <a href="/wiki/Persian_traditional_music" title="Persian traditional music">Persian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arab_music" title="Arab music" class="mw-redirect">Arab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Music of the United Kingdom">British music</a>. Indian genres like <a href="/wiki/Filmi" title="Filmi">filmi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(music)" title="Bhangra (music)">bhangra</a> have become popular throughout the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, and around the world.</p>
<p>Indian giggity stars now sell records in many countries, while <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a> fans listen to the roots music of India's diverse nations. American <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a> have also made a large impact, primarily on Indian pop and filmi music. Other highly popular forms are <a href="/wiki/Ghazal" title="Ghazal">ghazal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qawwali" title="Qawwali">qawwali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thumri" title="Thumri">thumri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dhrupad" title="Dhrupad">dhrupad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dadra" title="Dadra">dadra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhajan" title="Bhajan">bhajan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirtan" title="Kirtan">kirtan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shabda" title="Shabda">shabad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gurbani" title="Gurbani">gurbani</a>. <a href="/wiki/Filmi" title="Filmi">Filmi</a> music is often said to have begun in 1931, with the release of Ardeshir M. Irani's <a href="/wiki/Alam_Ara" title="Alam Ara">Alam Ara</a> and its popular soundtrack. In the earliest years of the Indian cinema, filming was generally Indian (classical and folk) in inspiration, with some Western elements. Over the years, the Western elements have increased, but without completely destroying the Indian flavour. Most of the Indian movies are musicals and feature elaborate song and dance numbers. There is constant work for pop music composers — or music directors, to use the Indian term. Movie soundtracks are released as tapes and CDs, sometimes even before the move is released.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dance">Dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dance">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dance" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_dance" title="Indian classical dance">Indian classical dance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_folk_dances" title="Indian folk dances" class="mw-redirect">Indian folk dances</a></div>
<p>Indians would dance to teach me how to dougie. Its theory can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra" title="Natya Shastra">Natya Shastra</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bharata_Muni" title="Bharata Muni">Bharata Muni</a>, a sage from Tamil Nadu (400 BC). The Natya Shastra most important ancient treatise on <a href="/wiki/Classical_Indian_dance" title="Classical Indian dance" class="mw-redirect">classical Indian dance</a>. It is also called the fifth <a href="/wiki/Veda" title="Veda" class="mw-redirect">Veda</a> in reference to the foundation of Hindu religion and philosophy, from which sprang the related South Indian musical tradition of <a href="/wiki/Carnatic_music" title="Carnatic music">Carnatic music</a>. Its various current forms include <a href="/wiki/Bharatanatyam" title="Bharatanatyam" class="mw-redirect">Bharatanatyam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odissi" title="Odissi">Odissi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manipuri_dance" title="Manipuri dance">Manipuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathakali" title="Kathakali">Kathakali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuchipudi" title="Kuchipudi">Kuchipudi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohiniaattam" title="Mohiniaattam" class="mw-redirect">Mohiniaattam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathak" title="Kathak">Kathak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sattriya_dance" title="Sattriya dance" class="mw-redirect">Sattriya</a>.</p>
<p><b>Bharatanatyam</b> is a classical <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a> form originating in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>. It is thought to have been created by Bharata Muni. In ancient times Bharatanatyam was performed by <a href="/wiki/Mandir" title="Mandir" class="mw-redirect">mandir</a> (Hindu temple) <a href="/wiki/Devadasi" title="Devadasi">Devadasis</a>. Many of the ancient sculptures in Hindu temples are based on Bharatanatyam dance postures <a href="/wiki/Karana_dance" title="Karana dance">karanas</a>.</p>
<p><b>Odissi</b> is one of the oldest surviving forms of dance, with depictions of Odissi dancing dating back as far as the 1st century BC. Like other forms of Indian classical dance, the Odissi style traces its origins back to antiquity. Dancers are found depicted in bas-relief in the hills of Udaygiri (near <a href="/wiki/Bhubaneswar" title="Bhubaneswar">Bhubaneswar</a>) dating back to the 1st century BC. The <a href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra" title="Natya Shastra">Natya Shastra</a> speaks of the dance from this region and refers to it as Odra-Magadhi.</p>
<p><b>Kathakali</b> (<i>katha</i> for <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">story</a>, <i>kali</i> for <a href="/wiki/Performance" title="Performance">performance</a> or <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a>) is a form of dance-<a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a>. It originated in the <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South Indian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> over 500 years ago. It is a spectacular combination of drama, dance, music and ritual. Characters with vividly painted faces and elaborate costumes re-enact stories from the <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> epics, <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a>.</p>
<p><b>Kuchipudi</b> is a classical dance form from <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>, a state of <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>. Kuchipudi is the name of a small village in the Divi Taluq of <a href="/wiki/Krishna_district" title="Krishna district">Krishna district</a> that borders the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> and with resident <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmins</a> practising this traditional dance form, it acquired the present name. <b>Mohiniaattam</b> is a traditional dance from the South Indian state of Kerala. <a href="/wiki/Mohini" title="Mohini">Mohini</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Apsaras" title="Apsaras" class="mw-redirect">Apsaras</a> in the Hindu mythology and aattam in <a href="/wiki/Malayalam" title="Malayalam">Malayalam</a> means dance. So Mohiniaattam essentially means "dance of the enchantress". The theme of Mohiniaattam is love and devotion to god. The costume worn by the dancers are typically white coloured kasavu <a href="/wiki/Saree" title="Saree" class="mw-redirect">saree</a> with golden borders.</p>
<p>The <b>Kathak</b> dance form arose from the <a href="/wiki/Vaishnava" title="Vaishnava" class="mw-redirect">Vaishnava</a> devotees dancing to the episodes from <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>'s life. Originally a Northern Indian temple dance, it was transformed to a court dance in the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_era" title="Mughal era" class="mw-redirect">Mughal era</a>. The new Muslim influence brought with it certain changes to the dance form: what had been a largely devotional practice now became more a courtly entertainment.</p>
<p>The <b>Sattriya</b> dance is believed to be a creation of the great <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnavite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bhakti" title="Bhakti">bhakti</a>) guru <a href="/wiki/Srimanta_Sankardeva" title="Srimanta Sankardeva" class="mw-redirect">Srimanta Sankardeva</a> considered the lead architect of Assamese <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>. He created this magnificent Sattriya dance to accompany the <i>Ankiya Naat</i> (a form of Assamese one-act play, another creation of Sankardeva) which was usually performed in <i>Satras</i> (Assamese monasteries). Since the dance developed and grew within the <i>Satras</i>, it is named after these religious institutions.</p>
<p><b>Folk dances</b> are performed for every possible occasion, to celebrate the arrival of seasons, birth of a child, a wedding and festivals. The dances are very focused on gestures, postures, and expressions. The dances burst with verve and vitality. Men and women perform some dances exclusively, while in some performances men and women dance together. On most occasions artists sing the main lyrics and are accompanied by instruments. Each form of dance has a specific costume. Most costumes are flamboyant with extensive jewels.</p>
<p><b>Bhangra</b> is a form of music and dance that originated in the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" title="Punjab (India)" class="mw-redirect">Punjab</a> region of <a href="/wiki/India." title="India." class="mw-redirect">India.</a> <a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(dance)" title="Bhangra (dance)">Bhangra</a> dance began as a folk dance conducted by <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_people" title="Punjabi people">Punjabi</a> farmers to celebrate the coming of <a href="/wiki/Vaisakhi" title="Vaisakhi">Vaisakhi</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">sikh</a> festival. The specific moves reflect the manner in which villagers farmed their land. This musical art further became synthesized after the partition of India, when refugees from different parts of the Punjab shared their folk dances with individuals who resided in the regions they settled in. This hybrid dance became Bhangra. The dance started from just one move and evolved later on. It has been popularized by Punjabi artists from the <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> communities, with which it is now commonly associated.[1] Today, <a href="/wiki/Bhangra_(dance)" title="Bhangra (dance)">Bhangra</a> dance survives in different forms and styles all over the globe – including pop music, film soundtracks, collegiate competitions and even talent shows.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculpture">Sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sculpture">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sculpture" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sculpture_in_India" title="Sculpture in India" class="mw-redirect">Sculpture in India</a></div>
<p>The first sculptures in India date back to the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_civilization" title="Indus Valley civilization" class="mw-redirect">Indus Valley civilization</a>, where stone and bronze carvings have been discovered. This is one of the earliest instances of sculpture in the world. Later, as <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a> developed further, India produced some of the most intricate bronzes in the world, as well as unriveled temple carvings. Some huge shrines, such as the one at <a href="/wiki/Ellora" title="Ellora" class="mw-redirect">Ellora</a> were not actually constructed using blocks, but instead carved out of solid rock, making them perhaps the largest and most intricate sculptures in the world.</p>
<p>During the 2nd to 1st century BCE in far northern India, in what is now southern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and northern Pakistan, sculptures became more explicit, representing episodes of the Buddha's life and teachings. Although India had a long sculptural tradition and a mastery of rich iconography, the Buddha was never represented in human form before this time, but only through some of his symbols. This may be because <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandharan</a> Buddhist sculpture in modern Afghanistan displays <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia" class="mw-redirect">Persian</a> artistic influence. Artistically, the Gandharan school of sculpture is said to have contributed wavy hair, drapery covering both shoulders, shoes and sandals, acanthus leaf decorations, etc.</p>
<p>The pink sandstone sculptures of Mathura evolved during the Gupta period (4th to 6th century) to reach a very high fineness of execution and delicacy in the modeling. Newer sculptures in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, in stucco, schist or clay, display very strong blending of Indian post-Gupta mannerism and Classical influence, Hellenistic or possibly even Greco-Roman. Meanwhile, elsewhere in India, less anatomically accurate styles of human representation evolved, leading to the classical art that the world is now familiar with, and contributing to Buddhist and Hindu sculpture throughout Asia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Painting">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Painting" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indian_painting" title="Indian painting">Indian painting</a></div>
<p>The earliest Indian paintings were the rock paintings of <a href="/wiki/Pre-historic" title="Pre-historic" class="mw-redirect">pre-historic</a> times, the <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> as found in places like <a href="/wiki/Rock_Shelters_of_Bhimbetka" title="Rock Shelters of Bhimbetka" class="mw-redirect">Rock Shelters of Bhimbetka</a>, and some of them are older than 5500 BC. Such works continued and after several millennia, in the 7th century, carved pillars of <a href="/wiki/Ellora" title="Ellora" class="mw-redirect">Ellora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a> <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_India" title="States and territories of India" class="mw-redirect">state</a> present a fine example of Indian paintings, and the colors, mostly various shades of red and orange, were derived from minerals. Thereafter, frescoes of <a href="/wiki/Ajanta" title="Ajanta" class="mw-redirect">Ajanta</a> and Ellora caves appeared. India's <a href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist" class="mw-redirect">Buddhist</a> literature is replete with examples of texts which describe that palaces of kings and aristocratic class were embellished with paintings, but they have not survived. But, it is believed that some form of art painting was practiced in that time.</p>
<p>Indian Art was given a new lease of life by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> in early 19th century when the new <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> required painters to document Indian life and times. The English School paintings, as these new art were called had seen the emergence of India's greatest artists of all times <a href="/wiki/Raja_Ravi_Verma" title="Raja Ravi Verma" class="mw-redirect">Raja Ravi Verma</a>. Other important artists of the Colonial period include <a href="/wiki/Jamini_Roy" title="Jamini Roy">Jamini Roy</a>, Amrita Shergil, Ramkinker Baij and <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a>. After independence, Indian art became more diverse and artists like <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maqbool_Fida_Hussain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maqbool Fida Hussain (page does not exist)">Maqbool Fida Hussain</a>, Francis Newton Souza, <a href="/wiki/Subodh_Gupta" title="Subodh Gupta">Subodh Gupta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devajyoti_Ray" title="Devajyoti Ray">Devajyoti Ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paresh_Maity" title="Paresh Maity">Paresh Maity</a> and Bose Krishnamachari earned international recognition.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinema">Cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cinema">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cinema" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="metadata plainlinks ambox ambox-content ambox-peacock" style="" role="presentation">
<tr>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_India" title="Cinema of India">Cinema of India</a></div>
<p>India is a major regional center for <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">cinema</a>. The Indian film industry is the second largest in the world (1200 movies released in the year 2002). Each of the larger jrods supports its own film industry: <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kannada_language" title="Kannada language">Kannada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malayalam_language" title="Malayalam language" class="mw-redirect">Malayalam</a>. The Hindi/Urdu film industry, based in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, formerly <i>Bombay</i>, is called <a href="/wiki/Bollywood" title="Bollywood">Bollywood</a> (a melding of <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> and Bombay). Similar neologisms have been coined for the Kannada (Karnataka State) film industry (<a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">Sandalwood</a>) based on Karnataka being known for Sandalwood, Tamil film industry (<a href="/wiki/Kollywood" title="Kollywood" class="mw-redirect">Kollywood</a>, from the Kodambakkam district of <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>) and the Telugu film industry (<a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Andhra_Pradesh" title="Cinema of Andhra Pradesh">Tollywood</a>). Tollygunge is a <a href="/wiki/Metonym" title="Metonym" class="mw-redirect">metonym</a> for the Bengali film industry, long centered in the Tollygunge district of <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>. The Bengali language industry is notable as having nurtured the director <a href="/wiki/Satyajit_Ray" title="Satyajit Ray">Satyajit Ray</a>, an internationally renowned filmmaker and a winner of many awards, among them the <a href="/wiki/Bharat_Ratna" title="Bharat Ratna">Bharat Ratna</a> (India's highest civilian award), the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" title="Légion d'honneur" class="mw-redirect">Légion d'honneur</a> (France), and the Lifetime achievement <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards" class="mw-redirect">Academy Award</a>. The Bollywood industry is usually the largest in terms of films produced and box office receipts, just as Urdu/Hindi speakers outnumber speakers of other Indian languages within India. (Tollywood is close on Bollywood's heels, and sometimes will turn out more films in a year.)</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Radio">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Radio" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radio broadcasting was, until recently a government monopoly under the Directorate General of <a href="/wiki/All_India_Radio" title="All India Radio">All India Radio</a>—established in 1936 and since 1957 also known as Akashvani—a government-owned, semicommercial operation of the Ministry of Information and Broadcasting. From only six stations at the time of independence, All India Radio's network had expanded by the mid-1990s to 146 AM stations plus a National Channel, the Integrated North-East Service (aimed at tribal groups in northeast India), and the External Service. There are five regional headquarters for All India Radio: the North Zone in New Delhi; the North-East Zone in Guwahati, Assam; the East Zone in Calcutta; the West Zone in Bombay; and the South Zone in Madras. All India Radio covers 99.37% of India's populace.</p>
<p>The government-owned network provides both national and local programs in Hindi, English, and sixteen regional languages. Commercial services, which were inaugurated in 1957, are provided by <a href="/wiki/Vividh_Bharati" title="Vividh Bharati">Vividh Bharati</a> Service, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vividh_Bharati" title="Vividh Bharati">Vividh Bharati</a>, which accepts advertisements, broadcasts from thirty-one AM and FM stations in the mid-1990s.</p>
<p>India has an extensive network of medium wave and shortwave stations. In 1994 there were eighty-five FM stations and seventy-three shortwave stations that covered the entire country. The broadcasting equipment is mostly Indian made and reaches special audiences, such as farmers needing agroclimatic, plant protection, and other agriculture-related information. The number of radio receivers increased almost fivefold between 1970 and 1994, from around 14 million to nearly 65 million. Most radios are also produced within India.</p>
<p>The foreign broadcast service is a function of the External Services Division of All India Radio. In 1994 seventy hours of news, features, and entertainment programs were broadcast daily in twenty-five languages using thirty-two shortwave transmitters. The principal target audiences are listeners in neighbouring countries and the large overseas Indian community.</p>
<p>Satellite Radio was introduced to the Indian market in 2000 by <a href="/wiki/WorldSpace" title="WorldSpace" class="mw-redirect">WorldSpace</a>, a Washington-DC based company. Currently WorldSpace beams 30 channels comprising music, news, and regional channels, a subscriber in India pays <img alt="INR" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg/7px-Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg.png" width="7" height="10" style="vertical-align: baseline" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg/11px-Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg/14px-Indian_Rupee_symbol.svg.png 2x" /> 1,200 per annum. In addition, some premium channels are available at an extra cost. This service requires special receivers which are often subsidized by world space.</p>
<p>Recently The Department of Space (DoS) indicated it is exploring the possibility of setting up a multi-media satellite platform that would include satellite radio, video and data channels.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Television">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arts_and_entertainment_in_India&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Television" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Television_in_India" title="Television in India">Television in India</a></div>
<p>Television service is available throughout the country. <a href="/wiki/Doordarshan" title="Doordarshan">Doordarshan</a> is a government-owned broadcaster established in 1959 and a part of All India Radio until 1976. It operates of one national network and seven regional networks. In 1992 there were sixty-three high-power television transmitters, 369 medium-power transmitters, seventy-six low-power transmitters, and twenty-three transposers. Regular satellite transmissions began in 1982 (the same year colour transmission began). By 1994 some 6 million people were receiving television broadcasts via satellite, and the number was expected to increase rapidly throughout the rest of the decade. Cable television was even more prolific, with an estimated 12 to 15 million subscribers in 1994. Besides Doordarshan, <a href="/wiki/Zee_TV" title="Zee TV">Zee TV</a>—an independent station broadcasting from Mumbai since 1992—uses satellite transmissions. In fact, because Doordarshan is the only network that is permitted to broadcast television signals domestically, <a href="/wiki/Zee_TV" title="Zee TV">Zee TV</a> and other entrepreneurs broadcast their Indian-made videotapes via foreign transmitters. Other networks joining the fray are <a href="/wiki/Cable_News_Network" title="Cable News Network" class="mw-redirect">Cable News Network</a> (1990); Asia Television Network (1991); Hong Kong-based <a href="/wiki/STAR_TV_(Asia)" title="STAR TV (Asia)" class="mw-redirect">Star TV</a> (1991); Jain TV, near Bombay (1994); EL TV, a spin-off of Zee TV in Bombay (1994); HTV, an affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Hindustan_Times" title="Hindustan Times">Hindustan Times</a> in New Delhi (1994); and <a href="/wiki/Sun_TV_(India)" title="Sun TV (India)">Sun TV</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil language</a> service in Chennai (1994). In a communications breakthrough in July 1995, Doordarshan agreed, for a US$1.5 million annual fee and 50 percent of advertising revenue when it exceeds US$1.5 million, to allow CNN to broadcast twenty-four hours a day via an Indian satellite. regional, and local service. The number of television sets increased from around 500,000 in 1976 to 9 million in early 1987 and to around 47 million in 1994; increases are expected to continue at around 6 million sets per year. More than 75 percent of television sets were black and white models in 1992, but the proportion of colour sets is increasing annually. Most television sets are produced in India.</p>
