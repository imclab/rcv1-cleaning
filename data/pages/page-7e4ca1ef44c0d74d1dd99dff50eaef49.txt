<p><b>EXECUTIVE EDITOR BOB GRITZINGER:</b> Talk about your major hoots. I'm not sure anybody&#8212;anybody&#8212;needs this much power at hand for their day-to-day drives. It could only lead to trouble. But when that lane opens up and you can fill it with a quick downshift and a mash on the pedal, the 2013 <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/section/camaro&template=newcar&make=Chevrolet">Chevrolet Camaro</a> ZL1 convertible is a joy to behold.</p><p></p><p>Top down, the car is an open-air aural delight, exhaust pops ricocheting like small-arms fire off the pavement, bridges, other cars, everything. Punch the throttle and everything gets a little wiggy for a moment before settling in on the rear haunches and accelerating with purpose. One needs to keep a close eye on things, however, because if the power doesn't bite you, the responsive steering will. Look away long enough to reprogram a radio station and you might find yourself quickly into the next lane. Best to focus on the work at hand and leave dial fiddling for when you're stopped.</p><p></p><p>Top up, the car is also quite fun, though in a more muted manner. There's still all the good stuff, but you're at least under some protection from the raucousness going on all around you that is the <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/article/20121204/CARREVIEWS/121209969">Camaro ZL1</a>.</p><p></p><p>Big fun.</p><p></p><p><b>ASSOCIATE EDITOR JAKE LINGEMAN:</b> This <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/article/20120925/CARREVIEWS/120929885">Camaro ZL1 convertible</a> looks badass. The charcoal exterior, black wheels and black suede interior of our tester couldn't complement each other any better than they do. With the top down, this car looks dark, blocky and aggressive -- and it doesn't even look bad with the top up. The heat extractors on the hood really go a long way to making this car look better than the standard SS.</p><p></p><p>I'd like to think that '69 Camaros actually felt like this back in the day. But they didn't. I've driven them. This is new speed, new technology. I'd wager that you could take the most staid of classic Camaro fans, stick them in this and they'd never look back.</p><p></p><p>It doesn't feel as upscale as the <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/article/20120925/CARREVIEWS/120929881">Ford Mustang Shelby GT500</a> does on the interior, but nothing stood out to me as a deal breaker. The new MyLink system works well, though I was surprised there was no navigation function. For so long I've equated car screens with maps, so it's unsettling when I don't see it. The suede everywhere is great; the wheel stays snuggly in your hand, as does the shifter. That shifter, which is really good, doesn't compare to the GT500. The throws on the 'Stang are about half the distance of the Camaro.</p><p></p><p>On the road, the top Camaro behaves like you'd think it would. It spins the tires with the traction control on, off or in sport mode. It has power in every gear, at almost any rpm. And personally I love the sound it makes.</p><p></p><p>At low rpm, you can hear just the tiniest bit of supercharger whine. At full blast, it just roars down the highway. My advice? Get one, move near a long tunnel and enjoy the rest of your years.</p><p></p><p>It's also powerful enough that the convertible top barely makes a noticeable difference. You would probably feel it at 10/10ths on the track, but in everyday cruising, you can't feel the weight, and barely the wobble.</p><p></p><p><b>EDITOR WES RAYNAL:</b> Well, that was a bummer. Or half a bummer I guess I should say. Basically my first-ever ZL1 drive, to which I was greatly looking forward, can be summed up this way: Rain stopped half the play. </p><p></p><p>Last night was fine. Down went the top, up went some country rock, down went the foot. I was livin' the high life, baby. All's I needed was a Pabst Blue Ribbon to fill out the scene (once I got home, of course). This morning, not so much. Rain, top up, traction-control light almost constantly blinking. </p><p></p><p>It's a cool driving experience, nonetheless. </p><p></p><p>I'm hearing rumblings around the office that the car is too raw. Really? I thought in many ways it was quite tame, actually. Now, granted, I was (especially in this morning's rain) light footing it so as not to do anything stupid. Love the V8 rumble and its ready-for-anything nature. </p><p></p><p>I was also impressed with the ride, which I feared would beat on me, but didn't come close.</p><p></p><p>But oh man, that engine! There's instant power at any rpm and one never hears the supercharger. There's power and torque right now, and all the time. Not a bad gearbox, either.</p><p></p><p>Handling? Like I said, the ride was fine around the D, and the steering feels connected and weights up nicely as speed increases. I'd need more time, and drier roads, to get a real feel of how easy it is to fling around.</p><p></p><p>This is the best Camaro in the lineup. You could argue that it had better be, seeing that it's also the most expensive. Still, if I had a fleet of toys, this would be one of 'em.</p><p></p><p><b>ROAD TEST EDITOR JONATHAN WONG:</b> Lopping the roof off of a car is no laughing matter, and Chevrolet knew it when it began to develop the Camaro. Lucky for us, a drop-top model was in the plans from the beginning, and engineers made sure to leave room to reinforce the chassis in order to make up for the stiffness lost without a fixed roof. Additional reinforcements on the Camaro ZL1 convertible come courtesy of a shock-tower bar, beefier transmission support, engine cradle, underbody tunnel brace and front X-brace along with a rear V-brace. All that adds 260 pounds of weight to the ZL1 convertible compared to the coupe.</p><p></p><p>Most of the stuff the ZL1 coupe gets is here, such as the more aggressive body treatments, magnetic suspension, and the responsive and powerful supercharged V8 engine. However, what the ZL1 convertible doesn't get is the coupe's five-mode Performance traction-management system. Instead, it gets the competition mode on its StabiliTrack traction system like the Camaro SS gets. What's the story behind that? Chevy figures buyers of the ZL1 convertible won't be venturing to road courses and instead will be more inclined to head out on spirited weekend drives instead. It makes sense to me, but I don't see the problem with just throwing on the five-mode system anyway. At least give the option.</p><p></p><p>If it were my money and I were looking for an insanely powerful drop-top American muscle car, it would indeed be the Camaro ZL1 over the Ford Mustang Shelby GT500. While I do appreciate raw, no-frills vehicles such as the Shelby, that car's live rear axle just can't handle the power that car puts down. It's crude to a fault in my mind. That's not so with the Camaro ZL1: It has an independent rear suspension, a magnetic damping system that can play both parts as a comfortable cruiser and it's stiffened to offer lots of grip for hard driving with only a little lean.</p><p></p><p>As with my previous ZL1 experiences, I'd want the steering to offer some more feel to it. It responds to inputs quick enough, but just doesn't offer the connected feeling that I like. I did notice some shake through the steering column, too.</p><p></p><p>I'm not the biggest convertible fan and I will always go with a coupe over a convertible, but I will say there is something quite satisfying with convertibles. The open-air experience is nice every now and then. And when you have an incredible exhaust note from a supercharged V8 providing the soundtrack to your drive, it's really nice.  </p><p></p><p><b>2013 Chevrolet Camaro ZL1 convertible</b> </p><p><b>Base Price:</b> $61,745</p><p><b>As-Tested Price:</b> $62,245</p><p><b>Drivetrain:</b> 6.2-liter supercharged V8; RWD, six-speed manual</p><p><b>Output:</b> 580 hp @ 6,000 rpm, 556 lb-ft @ 4,200 rpm</p><p><b>Curb Weight:</b> 4,380 lb</p><p><b>Fuel Economy (EPA City/Highway/Combined):</b> 14/19/16 mpg</p><p><b><i>AW</i>Observed Fuel Economy:</b> 15.1 mpg</p><p><b>Options:</b> Sueded microfiber interior package ($500)
