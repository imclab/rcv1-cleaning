<p class="bodytextns"><span class="none"><a id="h10753" class="jumptemplate" title="Integrated Five-Year Undergraduate/Master of Arts in Film Studies Program" href="3807.htm#o10669" target="_self">Integrated Five-Year Undergraduate/Master of Arts Film Studies Program</a></span></p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The undergraduate programs are housed in the Sodaro&#8211;Pankey School of Media Arts within the Lawrence and Kristina Dodge College of Film and Media Arts. The Sodaro&#8211;Pankey School of Media Arts invites students to engage in the study and practice of self&#8211;expression through the media arts.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Acceptance into Dodge College programs is by application only. Students wishing to apply should contact Dodge College for supplemental application requirements and materials.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">All entering students enroll in a two-course program of study during the first year. <a id="h6190" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 130 Introduction to Visual Storytelling" href="3807.htm#o3819" target="_self">FTV 130</a> Introduction to Visual Storytelling, is an intense, hands&#8211;on production course in which students work with exploratory exercises, in assignments crafted to encourage risk&#8211;taking and creativity. In <a id="h6193" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 140 Introduction to Film Aesthetics" href="3807.htm#o3821" target="_self">FTV 140</a> Introduction to Film Aesthetics (<a id="h7468" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 247 History and Aesthetics of Digital Arts" href="3807.htm#o7338" target="_self">FTV 247</a>, History and Aesthetics of Digital Arts for Digital Arts majors), students begin the scholarly examination of films both as expressions of culture and models of rhetorical style that can then inform students&#8217; own work. Together these classes form the &#8220;gateway&#8221; experience for every Dodge College student.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">From there, the curriculum continues to encourage creative and intellectual exploration throughout the undergraduate course of study. In every semester students are expected to develop both fluency in expression through the media arts (through courses in the major) and an understanding of the broader cultural context in which they participate (through courses in general education.)</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">As students proceed into upper&#8211;division courses, the degree paths become more distinct. More emphasis is put on the &#8220;business of the business,&#8221; whether the business is the film industry, film scholarship, public relations, advertising, television, or broadcast journalism.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Each of the school&#8217;s eight bachelor degrees ends with a specific &#8220;capstone&#8221; project, to prepare the student with a calling card intended to lead them to meaningful, satisfying work after graduation.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Students pursuing a BA or BFA in the Sodaro&#8211;Pankey School of Media Arts must maintain at least a 2.000 GPA in the major. All courses in the major must be taken for a letter grade (except for independent internships) and passed with a &quot;C&#8211;&quot; or higher.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">Departmental Honors</span></p>
<p class="bodytextns">Students must earn a GPA of 3.700 or higher in major courses to be considered for departmental honors. In addition, students must be recommended by the faculty for outstanding work in the Sodaro&#8211;Pankey School of Media Arts.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">Class Fees: Technology Fee and Insurance</span></p>
<p class="bodytextns">Some courses in the Dodge College of Film and Media Arts carry a technology fee, which ranges from $75 to $1,000. Money collected through these fees supports the College&#8217;s special events and week-night screenings, the equipment necessary for creating film and video productions, the computer labs and software, and the production environment generally.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Students in very advanced production courses will also be billed to defray the cost of insurance for equipment, personnel and locations. Details are available in the Production Handbook.</p>
<a name="o3808"></a><h2 class="heading2">Bachelor of Arts in Film Studies</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The film studies degree offers students the opportunity to explore film history, aesthetics, theory, and criticism in depth, along with a hands&#8211;on introduction to production. This combination of theoretical study with production experience gives students a unique understanding of how film creates meaning.</p>

<a name="o3809"></a><h2 class="heading2">Bachelor of Arts in Public Relations and Advertising</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The degree in public relations and advertising prepares students to perform creative and strategic work in any type of business, in the entertainment industry, or in the non-profit arena as well as for PR, advertising, marketing or social media agencies. In a media environment dominated by storytelling and engagement, students learn to use the visual tools of film and television as well as print, social media and the Internet to create conversations and pursue organizational objectives. Following course work introducing students to both fields, students may choose their elective courses to pursue a focus in either public relations or advertising.</p>

<a name="o3811"></a><h2 class="heading2">Bachelor of Fine Arts in Film Production</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The film production degree provides professional education in a liberal arts environment, a combination that prepares students to be creative film artists who understand the social, cultural and historic context of film language while developing their skills in one or more areas of production. Prior to their junior year, students must choose cinematography, directing, editing, production design or sound design to pursue for their advanced area of study requirements. However, they are still free to take classes in other disciplines.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Majors in film production may not enroll in most other courses until they successfully pass <a id="h6190_7" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 130 Introduction to Visual Storytelling" href="3807.htm#o3819" target="_self">FTV 130</a> with a grade of &quot;B&#8211;&quot; or better. If a student receives a grade lower than &quot;B&#8211;&quot; then he or she must repeat the course, and additional courses that may be taken concurrently are restricted to 100 and 200&#8211;level courses until the successful completion of <a id="h6190_8" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 130 Introduction to Visual Storytelling" href="3807.htm#o3819" target="_self">FTV 130</a> with a grade of &quot;B&#8211;&quot; or better.</p>

<a name="o7960"></a><h2 class="heading2">Bachelor of Fine Arts in Screen Acting</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Acceptance to the BFA in screen acting is by audition only for those students who demonstrate potential for excellence in acting and a strong interest in acting for the screen. This is a single interdisciplinary degree offered cooperatively through the Chapman University theatre department and Dodge College of Film and Media Arts.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The BFA in screen acting degree is offered to prepare students to work as performers in all recorded media, including film, television, and new media in addition to traditional live theatre. Students will be trained in and prepared to meet the current demands of the motion picture and television industries as well as the evolving and expanding opportunities in internet, portable media, and computer-generated digital effects which require actors to be familiar with green screen and motion capture performance techniques.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">In this degree, students will study traditional acting techniques with a strong emphasis on techniques appropriate and specific to working in front of a camera. The curriculum is structured in a traditional sequence from introductory classes in film and acting basics to more advanced out-of-class film production work. Students also study editing, screenwriting, directing for film and film history.  Students are provided with professional career training in the senior year, including development of their actor reel. All screen acting students will be required to act in film production students' films or other projects approved by the faculty in addition to auditioning for the theatre department stage productions. </p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Students are admitted to the BFA in screen acting by audition for the department of theatre at entrance to Chapman. Following the audition, the applicant's file is also reviewed by the Dodge College of Film and Media Arts.  Currently enrolled theatre majors and Dodge film majors may audition in the spring semester of the freshman year. For audition and supplemental application information required, interested students should contact the department of theatre secretary. </p>

<a name="o3812"></a><h2 class="heading2">Bachelor of Fine Arts in Television and Broadcast Journalism</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">The BFA in television and broadcast journalism offers students a broad introduction to the storytelling forms of the medium of television, from episodic programs in comedy or drama to news, talk shows, sports broadcasting and documentaries. Following introductory course work in production techniques as well as the history and business operations of television as a medium, students declare narrative television, broadcast journalism or documentary filmmaking as an area of study.</p>

<a name="o10669"></a><h2 class="heading2">Integrated Five-Year Undergraduate/Master of Arts in Film Studies Program</h2>
<p class="bodytextextraspace">Dodge College offers a specialized master of arts program for Chapman University undergraduates, which enables students to begin taking MA course work in the fall of the senior year and receive a master's in film studies within one year of finishing their undergraduate studies. The integrated program is open to undergraduate film studies majors, undergraduate majors who fall under the film division of Dodge College and have taken two upper-division film studies classes or an undergraduate in good standing who will be completing a film studies minor by the end of the fall of their senior year. 12 credits of specified graduate classes taken in the senior year may count toward the 48 credits required for the bachelor degree, and may also count toward the MA.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">Admission to the program</span></p>
<p class="bodytextns">Admission to the program may be achieved by the completion of the following requirements:</p>
<ol class="listnumber"><li class="listnumber">Submit an application for admission in the spring of their junior year. (This includes submission of an upper-division paper from a film studies course).</li><li class="listnumber">Be enrolled at Chapman University and have completed 75 credits or more of undergraduate course work with a cumulative GPA of 3.250 or higher.</li><li class="listnumber">Submit two letters of recommendation from faculty (not in film studies).</li><li class="listnumber">Complete a personal interview. (This will happen in the second round of application review.)</li></ol><p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">Admission deadlines</span></p>
<p class="bodytextns">Prospective students are encouraged to submit their application by March 1st for full consideration and first priority.</p>
<p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">Requirements for the degree</span></p>
<ol class="listnumber"><li class="listnumber">Complete 12 credits at the 500 or 600 level during the undergraduate senior year. Complete remaining 24 credits the following year.</li><li class="listnumber">Successfully complete the thesis project.</li><li class="listnumber">Pass an oral defense of the thesis project.</li></ol><p class="bodytextextraspace"><span class="none">senior year of undergraduate studies</span></p>

<a name="o8803"></a><h2 class="heading2">Course Descriptions - Film and Media Arts </h2>
<a name="o3815"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 103 Broadcast News</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, television and broadcast journalism major, or broadcast journalism minor. Students are trained in gathering information and translating that information into news stories for broadcast. Students deal with style and format of writing. The course also covers the essentials of news videography and editing. Fee: $300. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o7483"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 105 Art of Story</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">In a format of weekly lectures before all new Dodge College students, this course aims to establish storytelling in its various forms as the lingua franca of all disciplines at dodge college. It will enhance the awareness of storytelling, as well as expand the student's knowledge of the humanities through the study of music, commercials, dance, poetry, theater, art, literature, etc. Open to non-majors. (Offered fall semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o9210"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 109 Fundamental Graphics I for Digital Arts</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, digital arts major. An introduction to the fundamentals of graphic imagery, including shapes, forms, structure, composition, value, tone, light and shadow specifically as they apply to the world of digital arts. Students are taught to observe, analyze, and understand and apply these essential components of imagery in preparation for work in animation, character development and production design. Fee: $100. (Offered fall semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o3816"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 114 Introduction to Television Production: Studio</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, television and broadcast journalism major, or broadcast journalism minor. Some sections may be open to non-majors. Corequisite, <a id="h10065_3" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 114L Introduction to Television Production: Studio Lab" href="3807.htm#o10048" target="_self">FTV 114L</a>. An introduction to the skills used in television production. Using a lecture/lab format, this course provides a comprehensive overview of the medium and provides the student with hands-on experience in television studio techniques. Fee: $300. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o10048"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 114L Introduction to Television Production: Studio Lab</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Corequisite, <a id="h6188_3" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 114 Introduction to Television Production: Studio" href="3807.htm#o3816" target="_self">FTV 114</a>. Lab component of Introduction to Television Production: Studio. Some sections may be restricted to majors, or minors only. (Offered every semester.) 0 credits.</p>
<a name="o3817"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 115 Editing I</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, <a id="h6190_14" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 130 Introduction to Visual Storytelling" href="3807.htm#o3819" target="_self">FTV 130</a>. Students study the basic principles and aesthetics of editing film, video, and digital media, with practical experience through the completion of short editing projects. Fee: $300. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o7842"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 116 Introduction to Television Production: Location</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, television and broadcast journalism major. Some sections may be open to non-majors. An exploration of the fundamental creative, technical and logistical challenges of shooting television productions on a practical location. Some sections may be restricted to majors and minors only. Fee: $300. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o3818"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 120 Introduction to Digital Arts</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">An introduction to the world of digital imagery, visual effects and animation, focusing on their history, current status, and effect on film, television, and other media and on the issues that relate to the digitalization of our culture such as intellectual property, personal privacy, and related ethical questions. Students will create projects which combine the elements of story with computer generated visual effects. Some sections of this course may be restricted to digital arts majors only. May be repeated for credit. Fee: $75. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o7330"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 127 Screenwriting Intensive</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, screenwriting majors only. Corequisite, <a id="h6190_15" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 130 Introduction to Visual Storytelling" href="3807.htm#o3819" target="_self">FTV 130</a>. An intensive immersion in the building blocks of cinematic writing: character, visualization, dialogue, scenes and basic dramatic structure. Students begin with character and scene writing exercises and proceed to the development of several short scripts. Fee: $75. (Offered fall semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o9675"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 128 Character Analysis for Writers</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisites, <a id="h7533_2" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 127 Screenwriting Intensive" href="3807.htm#o7330" target="_self">FTV 127</a>, screenwriting major. <p class="coursedescription">Prerequisite, DCFMA majors, or advertising, or television minors. An introduction to production. Some sections of this course may be restricted to continuing students and/or majors only in the course schedule. Fee: $300. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o3820"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 133 Audio Techniques</h3>

<a name="o3821"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 140 Introduction to Film Aesthetics</h3>

An exploration of the essential elements of film through lecture, discussion, and viewing of a wide variety of films and film excerpts. Class discussions focus on analyzing the ways in which cinematography, mise en scene, editing, sound, and other aspects of film combine to make it a unique and meaningful form of art, entertainment, and instruction. <a name="o3822"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 203 Broadcast News II</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisites, <a id="h6187_4" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 103 Broadcast News" href="3807.htm#o3815" target="_self">FTV 103</a>, and television and broadcast journalism major, or broadcast journalism minor. During the course of the semester, students will be assigned to cover a variety of news stories including spot news, political news, business news, and light features. Students will be assigned a beat to cover. Some of the stories will air on a weekly news show on local cable. Fee: $300. (Offered spring semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<a name="o9212"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 206 Mechanics of Motion</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">Prerequisites, <a id="h9314_2" class="jumptemplate" title="FTV 109 Fundamental Graphics I for Digital Arts" href="3807.htm#o9210" target="_self">FTV 109</a>, digital arts major. Fee: $100. (Offered spring semester.) 3 credits.</p>

Fee: $100. (Offered spring semester.) 3 credits.</p>

<a name="o7720"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 262 Prime Time: The Game of Television </h3>
<p class="coursedescription">PRIME TIME is a role-playing simulation of the business of television which explores the aesthetic, sociological, economic, demographic, and technological trends that impact prime time television programming decisions: what gets on the air and what stays on the air. As members of the prime time community (network executives, producers, and advertisers) students define what constitutes success, as they face trade-offs between commercial viability, artistic merit, advertiser demands, and public pressure. Open to non-majors. Fee: $75. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>

<a name="o3864"></a><h3 class="heading3">FTV 342 Film Genre Studies</h3>
<p class="coursedescription">An intensive study of one film genre, with a different genre covered in each course offering. May be repeated in a different genre. Open to non--majors. (Offered every semester.) 3 credits.</p>
<p class="coursedescription"></p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342a Film Noir</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">An exploration of the crime films of the 1940s and 1950s, called &#8220;black&#8221; by French critics because of their violent, nihilistic content, and distinctive style of extreme&#8211;angled, deep&#8211;focus cinematography and shadowy low-key lighting.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342b The Horror Film</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">Beginning with Thomas Edison&#8217;s<em class="emphasis"> Frankenstein</em> (1910), this course examines such influential movements as German Expressionism, the Val Lewton horror films of the 1940s, sci&#8211;fi hybrids of the 1950s, the &#8216;slasher&#8217; horror of the 1970s, and the recent wave of Asian horror films and their Hollywood remakes.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342c The Musical</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">Intensive study of the history and aesthetics of the movie musical form its stage roots and cinematic birth coinciding with the coming of sound film through the waning of the genre's popularity during the decline of the Hollywood studio system and the many attempts since then to revive the form.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342d The Science Fiction Film</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">A study of cinematic science fiction from George Melies&#8217; <em class="emphasis">A Trip to the Moon</em> (1902) through contemporary films such as <em class="emphasis">The Matrix</em>. Emphasis is placed on certain developments, such as the alien invasion pictures of the 1950s and the dystopian cycle exemplified by <em class="emphasis">Blade Runner</em>.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342e Screwball Comedy</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">This classic film genre of the 1930s and &#8216;40s is examined in terms of its reflection of certain cultural changes such as the emergence of the independent &#8216;New Woman,&#8217; the rising divorce rate, and the notion of equality of the sexes. Emphasis is placed on key directors within the form: Leo McCarey, Frank Capra, Preston Sturges.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342f The Animated Film</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">From Winsor McCay&#8217;s <em class="emphasis">Gertie the Dinosaur</em> (1906) through Disney, Pixar, and the rise of anime, this course examines the history and development of one of the most popular and groundbreaking of contemporary genres.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342g The Western</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">Provides an overview of the oldest and most enduring of Hollywood genres exploring the mythology of the genre as well as its historical origins, with an emphasis on the impact of such classic film directors as John Ford, and Anthony Mann, and on the many waves of &#8220;revisionist&#8221; westerns in the past forty years.</p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342h The Melodrama</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">An examination of a misunderstood form with radical roots in the theater of the French Revolution and the basis for much of Hollywood&#8217;s output from D.W. Griffith through <em class="emphasis">Million Dollar Baby</em> and <em class="emphasis">Brokeback Mountain.</em></p>
<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">342j The Gangster Film</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">A study of the history and impact of this most American of film genres, which was &#8220;ripped from the headlines&#8221; of newspaper accounts of the violent exploits of Al Capone, John Dillinger, and Bonnie and Clyde.</p>

<p class="bodytextindented"><span class="none">444i The History and Aesthetics of Stereoscopic Cinema</span></p>
<p class="bodytextnsindented">This course will explore the history and aesthetics of stereoscopic 3-D cinema through readings, screenings, lectures, classroom discussions and written assignments. Though stereoscopic imagery can be found in a variety of media, including photography, comic books, theme parks and video games, this course engages specifically with stereoscopic cinema within the tradition of the Hollywood narrative feature film. The course follows a largely chronological trajectory from the pre-cinema era before 1895 to the digital present, tracing the technological, industrial and aesthetic issues that have shaped the production, exhibition and reception of stereoscopic cinema at various points along the way.
