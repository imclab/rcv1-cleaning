                    <h3>Ron recommends</h3>
                    <p>What makes Ron White laugh?</p>
<p>"The Doyle and Debbie Show," a spoof of country music that has been running in Nashville for several years.</p>
<p>"I laughed so hard I thought I was going to throw up," he insists. "And I don't laugh very easily...I'm very critical. But 'Doyle and Debbie' is so funny it's worth getting on a plane to see."</p>
<p>White's goal as a comedian? "To get the people to laugh as hard as they can. 'Doyle and Debbie' did that for me."</p>

                </div>
            
                <div class="fact box">
                    <h3>Details</h3>
                    <p>Who: Ron White</p>
<p>When: 7 p.m. Sept. 9</p>
<p>Where: Orpheum Theatre</p>
<p>Tickets: At All Ticketmaster outlets</p>
<p>Details: The Grammy-nominated White has experienced comic success as a member of the Blue Collar Comedy team and as a standup on his own. His latest show, "Moral Compass," is intended for mature audiences.</p>

									<!-- AP Content -->
									<div class="entry-content">
										<p>There's no such thing as "going too far" in comedy, says Ron White -- a man who knows.</p>
<p>"There's always an angle," he says. "You just have to know how to approach it."</p>
<p>Taboo subjects and situations are fair game "if you figure out what it is you can laugh about ... maybe it's ourselves and how we react," White says. "I left 9/11 completely alone. I'd do the same with (the shootings in) Aurora. But, really, they're just words. Eventually, people will get over them."</p>
<p>In the course of a Grammy-nominated career, White has been able to mine humor out of some fairly raunchy situations. Family members may protest but, he says with a laugh, "they live pretty well off it. I'm not asking for writing advice. Everybody knows that. I've had them complain about a bit before and I always say, 'Write me something to replace it with. I'll change it.'"</p>
<p>The goal? To make people laugh. "To make you laugh as hard as I can. I don't care who gets offended. If they're offended by what I say, they need to watch something else. There are other comedians out there." Quickly, he names his old Blue Collar friends -- Jeff Foxworthy and Bill Engvall. "I've always been able to take the gloves off. My goal is not to offend."</p>
<p>Over the course of a decade, White says, his comedy has changed. "I'm a better comic now. I understand more. But I don't think it's necessarily easier to write at this level. I probably don't go down as many dead-end paths."</p>
<p>Instead, the 55-year-old works at the act, one joke at a time. By the time he's got enough great material, he records it for an album. "You've got to release it, right at its peak," he says. A follow-up to his last CD, "Behavioral Problems," should come within the next year. The curing is almost done.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, White appeared in a Billy Bob Thornton film, "Jayne Mansfield's Car," that has yet to be released.</p>
<p>"If they ever put it out, you'll see it's the best movie I've been in...and Billy Bob just handed me the role."</p>
<p>The two met in a bar in Houston before a concert. "We got drunk on his tour bus. It was going to be Ron White Day in the state of Texas. I was supposed to be in a coat and tie at the House of Representatives, which I kind of did...but Billy wrote me a note."</p>
<p>In the film -- "a metaphor for train wrecks within families -- White plays a former Georgia football star who owns six car dealerships in the greater Atlanta area.</p>
<p>"He's so ignorant, but people hang on his words." John Hurt, Robert Patrick and Robert Duvall also star in the film. White was thrilled to share scenes with Duvall and, when the cast came to his house, couldn't believe the Oscar winner was hanging out with him.</p>
<p>"I'd love to do more films, but they've got to fit my schedule," White says. "I won't quit standup to do them."</p>
<p>He worked around "Jayne Mansfield" by using "the plane my fans bought me."</p>
<p>The idea of leaving that power, that affirmation doesn't even enter his mind. "I walk out on stage every night and people respond like I'm one of the Beatles. That's all I asked the universe for -- 'Make me a famous comedian.' So the universe made me a famous comedian and I'm addicted to it.</p>
<p>"It'll fade," he says of the comedy career. "But I'll stick with it. I'm a Baby Boomer. The biggest population (group) is as old as me and they don't care what Dane Cook or Dave Osborne has to say. But they will come to see me."</p>
<p>To keep up with the pace, White makes a deal with himself. "I don't drink all day," he says. "My goal is to take my first drink of the day on stage. It's my little carrot. Left to my own devices I'd probably way up with a joint and a case of beer. But I learned, you have to pace yourself."</p>
<p> </p>
