              <p>Alejandro D&Igrave;az once described himself as &quot;a South Texas decorator and embellisher trapped in the body of a conceptual artist.&quot;</p>
<p>To substantiate, see the cheekily titled &quot;Just in Queso,&quot; an exhibition of the New York-by-way-of-San Antonio artist's work at <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22David+Shelton+Gallery%22">David Shelton Gallery</a>.</p>
<p>Among the many things going on in Alejandroworld, from social and political commentary to the gentle hijacking of everything from Modernism to Madison Avenue, the underlying proposition seems to be: Why can't art be funny? Why does it have to be so &quot;serious?&quot;</p>
<p>&quot;I think all good art should elicit an emotion from the viewer,&quot; D&Igrave;az wrote in a recent e-mail. &quot;In my case I use humor as way to address political and socioeconomic issues of disparity. I think if I were to make in-your-face political art no one would be interested &mdash; I know I wouldn't. With humor you give the viewer an opportunity to be a part of the work without putting them immediately on the defensive. In a way, humor disarms people and allows them to internalize (in a more contemplative way) the issues being addressed. I also like giving viewers a choice &mdash; to accept the work of art solely for its humor and face value or to consider (listen to) the work's political undertones.&quot;</p>
<p>Text is an essential element in much of D&Igrave;az's work, and slogans slapped on the windows greet visitors to the Stone Oak gallery and set the tone for what's inside. These include &quot;By Disappointment Only&quot; and &quot;Naked Artist Inside.&quot;</p>
<p>Once you enter, it's impossible to suppress smiles, and you may just find yourself laughing along with the artist. At, say, the &quot;Mexi-cans,&quot; fake plants in five facsimiles of Mexican brand-name cans &mdash; Jumex tomates and <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Herdez%22">Herdez</a> products.</p>
<p>(These are smaller versions of big trash-can-size planters commissioned by New York's <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Public+Art+Fund%22">Public Art Fund</a> and exhibited on a Brooklyn boulevard median in 2005, now part of the permanent public art collection of the city of San Antonio and displayed at the <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22San+Antonio+International+Airport%22">San Antonio International Airport</a>.)</p>
<p>&quot;I can't tell you how many people have come in and seen those cans and started telling a story about how their grandmother used them as planters on the kitchen-window sill,&quot; says gallery owner David Shelton.</p>
<p>D&Igrave;az mines his Mexican American &mdash; specifically, South Texas &mdash; heritage in his work, which he has called &quot;a collage of both high and low culture.&quot;</p>
<p>In a 2003 photograph titled &quot;Enchiladas at the Plaza,&quot; the artist stands outside the famous <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22New+York+City+hotel%22">New York City hotel</a> in full mariachi regalia, including sombrero and a curling moustache, holding a cardboard sign that reads: &quot;Available for speaking role in a major motion picture.&quot; Behind him is one of his now ubiquitous &quot;Make tacos not war&quot; signs.</p>
<p>The artist, who holds a master's degree from the <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Center+for+Curatorial+Studies%22">Center for Curatorial Studies</a>, <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=entertainment%2Fvisual_arts&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Bard+College%22">Bard College</a>, began making and selling those cardboard signs &mdash; &quot;Mexican Wallpaper&quot; &mdash; on New York streets in the '90s. Later, he translated them into neon. One of those, alternately flashing the words &quot;Siesta&quot; and &quot;Fiesta,&quot; is included in &quot;Just in Queso,&quot; as is the brilliant &quot;No Shirt No Shoes You're probably rich.&quot;</p>
<p>In the center of the Shelton gallery, D&Igrave;az has displayed a pile of hand-lettered cardboard signs on a serape in an untitled floor sculpture. Among these are &quot;Mexicans without borders,&quot; &quot;In the future everyone will be famous for $15,&quot; &quot;The filet mignon of affordable conceptual art,&quot; &quot;Happiness is expensive&quot; and, of course, &quot;I can't remember the Alamo.&quot;</p>
<p>The stereotypical moustache, which adorned the invitation to &quot;Just in Queso,&quot; plays a key role in a new work titled &quot;Mothers and Fathers.&quot; It is a bi-portrait &mdash; a great aunt perhaps &mdash; of a woman photographically printed on two medium-sized canvases, side by side, with a fake moustache attached as a Fridalike unibrow in one picture and pasted on the woman's upper lip, Mexican revolutionary-style, in the second image.</p>
<p>The work, a comment on gender and sexual roles as well as a trick to the eye, speaks to the way the artist grew up &mdash; in a matriarchy of liberated, activist women prominent in the Chicano rights movement.</p>
<p>&quot;At first glance, the work looks humorous, but the piece is about mothers being both mothers and fathers in Mexican American households where men are often absent,&quot; D&Igrave;az says. &quot;You also see this in African American families and families where there is economic hardship. The transgender nature of this work adds a personal note of the bond between mothers and sons. In most cultures, it's the mothers &mdash; not the fathers &mdash; that become the protectors of gay progeny.</p>
<p>&quot;What I try to do is merge a sense of pure absurdity with activism,&quot; he adds. &quot;Being raised in South Texas, in a matriarchy of educated, hard-working Mexican American women who lived through the civil rights movement and womens lib, I not only inherited a rich visual background but also a background full of social and political consciousness.&quot;</p>
<p>&quot;Just in Queso&quot; remains on view through Saturday at David Shelton Gallery, 20626 Stone Oak Parkway. Hours are 11 a.m. to 6 p.m. Tuesday-Friday and noon to 6 p.m. Saturday. Call (210) 481-5200 or visit <a href="http://www.davidsheltongallery.com.">www.davidsheltongallery.com.</a></p>
