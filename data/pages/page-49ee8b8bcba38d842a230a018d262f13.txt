                      <p class="introduction" id="story_continues_1">Alfred Hitchcock&#039;s Vertigo has replaced Orson Welles&#039;s Citizen Kane at the top of a poll that sets out to name one film &quot;the greatest of all time&quot;.</p>
        <p>The British Film Institute&#039;s Sight and Sound magazine polls a selected panel once a decade and Citizen Kane has been its top pick for the last 50 years.</p>
        <p>This time 846 distributors, critics and academics championed Vertigo, about a retired cop with a fear of heights.</p>
        <p>Starring James Stewart and Kim Novak, Vertigo beat Citizen Kane by 34 votes.</p>
        <p>In the last poll held 10 years ago, Hitchcock&#039;s 1958 thriller came five votes behind Welles&#039;s 1941 classic.</p>
        <p>Its triumph coincides with the launch of the BFI&#039;s Genius of Hitchcock season, a major retrospective celebrating the acclaimed &quot;master of suspense&quot;.</p>
  <span class="cross-head">Camera trick</span>
	      <p>Vertigo, the film Hitchcock regarded as his most personal, sees the director tackle obsessional love, one of his recurring themes.</p>
        <p>It opens with police officer Scotty Ferguson, played by Stewart, retiring after his vertigo inadvertently leads to the death of a colleague.</p>

	<!-- pullout-body-->
	      <p><strong>1.</strong> Vertigo (Hitchcock, 1958)</p>
        <p><strong>2.</strong> Citizen Kane (Welles, 1941)</p>
        <p><strong>3.</strong> Tokyo Story (Ozu, 1953)</p>
        <p><strong>4.</strong> La Regle du Jeu (Renoir, 1939)</p>
        <p><strong>5.</strong> Sunrise: a Song for Two Humans (Murnau, 1927)</p>
        <p><strong>6.</strong> 2001: A Space Odyssey (Kubrick, 1968)</p>
        <p><strong>7.</strong> The Searchers (Ford, 1956)</p>
        <p><strong>8.</strong> Man with a Movie Camera (Dziga Vertov, 1929) </p>
        <p><strong>9.</strong> The Passion of Joan of Arc (Dreyer, 1927)</p>
        <p><strong>10.</strong>  8Â½ (Fellini, 1963) </p>
  
	<!-- pullout-links-->
	</div>      <p id="story_continues_2">He is then hired by an old friend whose beautiful wife - played by Novak - is behaving strangely.</p>
        <p>As the story plays out against a San Francisco skyline, there are several revelations that challenge the audience&#039;s preconceptions about characters and events.</p>
        <p>The film is famous for a camera trick Hitchcock invented to represent Scotty&#039;s vertigo - a simultaneous zoom-in and pull-back of the camera that creates a disorientating depth of field.</p>
        <p>The visual, often imitated, has become known as a &quot;dolly zoom&quot; or &quot;trombone shot&quot;.</p>
        <p>Like Citizen Kane, Vertigo received mixed reviews on release but has grown in stature as the years have passed.</p>
        <p>The Sight and Sound list contains few surprises, with all of the films cited more than 40 years old.</p>
        <p>Yasujiro Ozu&#039;s Tokyo Story, from 1953, is ranked third - bettering its 2002 placement at five - while Jean Renoir&#039;s La Regle du Jeu (The Rules of the Game) drops one place, from three to four.</p>
  <span class="cross-head">Silent films</span>
	      <p>Both new entries in the Top 10 are silent - Dziga Vertov&#039;s Man with a Movie Camera at eight, and Carl Theodor Dreyer&#039;s The Passion of Joan of Arc at nine. </p>
        <p>The newest film in the Top 10 is Stanley Kubrick&#039;s 2001: A Space Odyssey, released in 1968, which charts at six.</p>
        <p>The top British film in the countdown is The Third Man, which came in at the relatively low placing of number 73.</p>

        <p><em>Source: Sight &amp; Sound</em></p>
  
	<!-- pullout-links-->
	</div>      <p id="story_continues_3">The panel, which voted for 2,045 films overall, was asked to interpret &quot;greatest&quot; how it saw fit.</p>
        <p>Its results, said Sight and Sound editor Nick James, &quot;reflects changes in the culture of film criticism&quot;.</p>
        <p>Vertigo, he continued, was &quot;the ultimate critics&#039; film&quot;.</p>
        <p>&quot;It is a dream-like film about people who are not sure who they are but who are busy reconstructing themselves and each other to fit a kind of cinema ideal of the ideal soul-mate.&quot;</p>
        <p>In a separate poll run by the monthly publication involving 358 film directors, Ozu&#039;s Tokyo Story was voted the greatest film ever made.</p>
        <p>Citizen Kane is ranked at number two jointly with 2001, while Vertigo occupies seventh place.</p>
        <p>Martin Scorsese, Quentin Tarantino, Francis Ford Coppola, Woody Allen and Mike Leigh were among the participants in the poll. </p>
