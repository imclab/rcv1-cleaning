<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A">A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia:Requested_articles/Arts_and_entertainment/Literature/Books&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: A">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wikipedia:Requested_articles/Arts_and_entertainment/Literature/Books&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: A" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul>
<li><i>Ashok Rajagopalan</i>, illustrator and writer of children's books. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.saffrontree.org/2010/04/ashok-rajagopalan-illustrator-graphic.html">[1]</a></li>

<p><br />
John Malcolm Andrews (born 21 August 1936) is an English antiques author and journalist, crime writer and engineering businessman, author – as John Malcolm – of the Tim Simpson series of art crime novels, author as John Andrews of the first Price Guide to Antique Furniture and Managing Editor of Antique Collecting magazine.</p>
<p>Biography</p>
<p>Born in Chorlton-cum-Hardy, Manchester, Andrews was educated at Sale High School, Manchester and The British School, Montevideo, (1946-1950) before returning to England as a boarder at Bedford Modern School (1950-1955), and at St. John’s College, Cambridge, where he read engineering and was Captain of the Lady Margaret Boat Club. He worked as an export sales manager, management consultant, and international marketing manager before setting up his own business as a machinery broker, travelling abroad extensively.</p>
<p>In 1966 he was a founder member of the Antique Collectors’ Club and became its first author as John Andrews with The Price Guide to Antique Furniture. He went on to produce more books on antique furniture and is currently managing editor of Antique Collecting magazine. He was chairman of the Trustees of Rye Art Gallery 1995-2004.</p>
<p>In 1984 his first crime novel, A Back Room in Somers Town, written as John Malcolm, was published by Collins Crime Club. He went on to produce a total of fifteen of the Tim Simpson series of crime novels plus two more with different central characters. He was Chairman of the Crime Writers’ Association 1994-5 and wrote a number of short stories. The art background to many of the Tim Simpson series involves the work of modern painters like Walter Sickert, Sir William Orpen, Augustus and Gwen John, Sir William and Ben Nicholson, Sir Alfred Munnings, James Tissot, Wyndham Lewis, C.R.W. Nevinson, the Pre-Raphaelites, Camille Pissaro, Whistler, Winslow Homer, Van Gogh and John Sargent.</p>
<p>He and his wife live in East Sussex and have one son, Sam, journalist and publisher.</p>

<p>The March Wind by Inez Rice and illustrated by Vladimir Bobri. First printed in USA in 1957 by Lothrop Lee ans Shephard Co., Inc. First printed in Great Britain in 1960. A charming children's story about a boy who picks up a hat blowing in the wind and takes on all sorts of personaes whilst wearing it. Finally, he must return the hat to the March wind. Wonderful illustrations by renowned designer and illustrator Vladimir Bobri.</p>

<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georgina_Hammick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Georgina Hammick (page does not exist)">Georgina Hammick</a>, British short-story writer, born in Hampshire, educated in Kenya and England; she later attended the Academie Julian, Paris, and the Salisbury School of Art. Her first collection of stories, People for Lunch (1987), was acclaimed for its wit, irony, and compassion. The stories in Spoilt (1992), her second collection, return to the affectionately depicted parochial and suburban landscapes of the earlier work, and are characterized by Hammick's customary elegance of style; here, however, her skill with satirical dialogue and her narrative dexterity often conceal tragic depths, as in the superb ‘The Dying Room’. Widely anthologized herself, Hammick has also edited an anthology, The Virago Book of Love and Loss (1992), which includes a number of influences and affinities—Elizabeth Bowen, Sylvia Townsend Warner, Elizabeth Taylor, Alice Munro.</li>
