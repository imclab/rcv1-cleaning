		<p>For nearly five years, Burch had been the friendly face of the music business program at UGA, rallying both community and financial support for the fledgling school.</p>




		<p>At the meeting, Burch was joined by George Fontaine, head of New West Records and a big supporter of the program. Barbe, who's now the music business program's interim director, had no idea what he was in for.</p>




		<p>"(Burch) asked if I knew what the meeting was about, and I said, 'It's your meeting. ...' " Barbe said.</p>




		<p>As it turned out, Burch announced he, along with program director Keith Perissi and development officer Heather Malcom, was leaving UGA to start a similar program at Kennesaw State University.</p>




		<p>Fontaine told Barbe he and Burch felt Barbe was the right fit to take the helm of the UGA program.</p>




		<p>"It was a double surprise," Barbe said. "First to Bruce, it was, 'You're leaving?' and then to George, it was, 'You want me to do what?' "</p>




		<p>UGA's business and music schools launched the music business program as a donor-funded certificate program in January 2006.</p>




		<p>It started with a student base of about 35. Some 130 students have signed up for courses in spring semester 2011, said office manager Ansley Stewart.</p>




		<p>One change since Burch's departure is that the Terry College of Business development office now handles fundraising for the program.</p>




		<p>Barbe's style is different, too.</p>




		<p>"As music industry people, we're each going to bring what we know to the program," he said, adding that Burch urged him to make it his own.</p>




		<p>Barbe has settled in quickly. As students and professors finished up fall semester, he sat at his desk in Caldwell Hall one afternoon this month and prepared to plug in his students' grades.</p>




		<p>"It's a great (challenge), because unlike five months ago, when it was just 100 names on a piece of paper, these are now 100 people I know and care about," he said.</p>




		<p>Barbe warmed to the idea of taking the job when he considered his career in the industry, he said.</p>




		<p>"I knew I could draw on the resources I have, and the specific people I do business with on a regular basis are sharp, successful people but also people I value as really quality human beings," he said.</p>




		<p>That, coupled with his 10 years as a Little League baseball coach, he said with a laugh, helped him find his footing at the front of a classroom.</p>




		<p>Athens, meanwhile, is the perfect place for the program to flourish, Barbe said.</p>




		<p>"There are other good programs in the country, but this is the best fit that could possibly exist," he said. "In a bigger city, there's not going to be as many hands-on opportunities for internships, and in a smaller town, you wouldn't have the enormous music community that Athens has."</p>




		<p>The title of interim director was a mutual decision between Barbe and the university, Barbe said, but he's officially applied for the job.</p>




		<p>"I would like to continue doing it," he said, even if that means continuing to balance time between the classroom and his recording studio, which he opened with partners Andy Baker and Andy Lemaster in 1997.</p>




		<p>As a music business man, he said that's how he'll continue to be valuable to the program.</p>




		<p>"It's important to be an active participant," he said, adding associate director Tom Lewis, also a top-notch engineer, brings his professional expertise as well. "In this industry, there's only so much you can share from a textbook."</p>




		<p>Barbe's also laying out plans for the program's future.</p>




		<p>"I want to expand the course offerings to include audio production, intellectual property and entertainment law," he said, noting that's just a small part of what he sees as a department that will continue to grow.</p>




		<p>He's also got professionals in mind who'll be good fits for other positions in the program. Among those is Cracker frontman David Lowery, who in January will teach a class - application of financial, economic and business principles as they apply to the music industry. In addition to a mathematics degree, Lowery has written a book about learning economics and finance through experiences in a band.</p>




		<p>"He's incredibly well-qualified to teach," Barbe said.</p>




		<p>Barbe said the students are bringing a lot to the table as well, as the music industry now is "an open playing field" with the widespread availability of digital music and production.</p>




		<p>"Times of great change and great uncertainty are also times of great opportunity for any sort of ingenuity, any sort of creative thinking," Barbe said. "For the music industry, it's changing from the top-down corporate structure to things being in the hands of young, energetic, free-thinking people with great ideas on how they want to do things. The possibilities are endless."</p>
