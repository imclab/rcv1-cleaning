<p>A <b>crossover</b> (or <b>CUV</b>: <b>crossover utility vehicle</b>) is a vehicle built on a <a href="/wiki/Automobile_platform" title="Automobile platform">car platform</a> and combining, in highly variable degrees, features of a <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicle</a> (SUV) with features from a passenger vehicle, especially those of a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a>.</p>
<p>Using the <a href="/wiki/Unibody" title="Unibody" class="mw-redirect">unibody</a> construction typical of passenger vehicles instead of the <a href="/wiki/Body-on-frame" title="Body-on-frame">body-on-frame</a> platform used in light trucks and the original SUVs, the crossover combines SUV design features such as tall interior packaging, high <a href="/wiki/H-point" title="H-point">H-point</a> seating, high center of gravity, high ground-clearance or all-wheel-drive capability — with design features from an automobile such as a passenger vehicle's platform, <a href="/wiki/Independent_rear_suspension" title="Independent rear suspension" class="mw-redirect">independent rear suspension</a>, car-like handling and superior <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel economy</a>.</p>
<p>A crossover may borrow features from a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling#One-box_and_Two-Box_design" title="Three-box styling">two-box</a> design of a shared passenger/cargo volume with rear access via a third or fifth door, a <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a> — and flexibility to allow configurations that favor either passenger or cargo volume, e.g., fold-down rear seats.</p>
<p>Most crossovers produced by mass market auto manufacturers are <a href="/wiki/FF_layout" title="FF layout" class="mw-redirect">front wheel drive</a>. However, luxury carmakers such as BMW, <a href="/wiki/Infiniti" title="Infiniti">Infiniti</a>, and Mercedes (which also includes a Jeep WK2 derivative underpinning the 2011–present Grand Cherokee) use the <a href="/wiki/FR_layout" title="FR layout" class="mw-redirect">rear wheel drive</a> setup. In both cases, <a href="/wiki/All-wheel_drive" title="All-wheel drive" class="mw-redirect">all-wheel drive</a> is usally an option and often standard equipment depending on the market. Crossovers are typically designed for only light off-road capability, if any at all.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crossover_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crossover_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg/220px-1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg/330px-1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg/440px-1977_Simca_Matra_Rancho.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The first car later to be recognized as crossover SUV was the French <a href="/wiki/Matra_Rancho" title="Matra Rancho">Matra-Simca Rancho</a>, introduced in 1977, two years before the demise of its owner <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Europe" title="Chrysler Europe">Chrysler Europe</a>, after which it was rebranded to Talbot-Matra Rancho until its production ended in 1984. It looked like a 4x4 off-road vehicle, but was in fact based on the pick-up version of the <a href="/wiki/Simca_1100" title="Simca 1100">Simca 1100</a> family saloon and had only front wheel drive.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The crossover term was used as a <a href="/wiki/Market_segmentation" title="Market segmentation">market segment</a> description and one of the reasons <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> purchased <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors Corporation</a> (AMC) in 1987. The automaker acquired the famous <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> brand and vehicles from AMC to add to its successful minivans, giving Chrysler a "solid position in the so-called 'crossover' market, which is somewhere 'between' cars and trucks and the fastest-growing segment in the entire industry."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some sources indicate the term crossover began as a marketing term,<sup id="cite_ref-intelli_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intelli-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and a 2008 <a href="/wiki/CNNMoney.com" title="CNNMoney.com">CNNMoney</a> article indicated that "many consumers cannot tell the difference between an SUV and a crossover."<sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A January 2008 <i>Wall Street Journal</i> blog article called crossovers "wagons that look like sport utility vehicles but ride like cars."<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Though crossovers have become a defined market, they are not an entirely new concept. The <a href="/wiki/Willys-Overland_Jeepster" title="Willys-Overland Jeepster">Willys-Overland Jeepster</a> convertible coupe offered many of the features that define the crossover.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A more direct crossover antecedent is the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Eagle" title="AMC Eagle">AMC Eagle</a>, a vehicle that "pioneered the crossover SUV" category.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> As a precursor to today's models, AMC's "vehicles worked well and sold well" and the "surviving Eagles to look like the "early man" version of a CUV, sort of a missing link of the car world."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The market segment spans a wide range of vehicles. In some cases, manufacturers have marketed vehicles as <i>crossovers</i> simply to avoid calling them <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagons</a>, or have produced crossovers mainly because station wagons have fallen out of favor with buyers in a particular region such as the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.caranddriver.com/comparisons/2013-bmw-x3-xdrive28i-vs-2013-audi-q5-20t-2013-land-rover-range-rover-evoque-comparison-test">[1]</a></p>
<p>While crossover vehicles released in the early-2000s resembled traditional SUVs or wagons, others have prioritized <a href="/wiki/Sport_sedan" title="Sport sedan" class="mw-redirect">sportiness</a> over utility—such as the <a href="/wiki/Infiniti_FX" title="Infiniti FX">Infiniti FX</a> and <a href="/wiki/BMW_X6" title="BMW X6">BMW X6</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_2010_FX35_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds_2010_FX35-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cars.com_2009_FX35_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cars.com_2009_FX35-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By 2006, the segment came into strong visibility in the U.S., when crossover sales "made up more than 50% of the overall SUV market."<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday1-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Sales increased in 2007 by 16%.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Audi_Q5" title="Audi Q5">Audi Q5</a> has become their second best-selling vehicle in the United States market after the <a href="/wiki/Audi_A4" title="Audi A4">Audi A4</a> sedan.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.caranddriver.com/comparisons/2013-bmw-x3-xdrive28i-vs-2013-audi-q5-20t-2013-land-rover-range-rover-evoque-comparison-test">[2]</a> Around half of <a href="/wiki/Lexus" title="Lexus">Lexus</a>' sales volume come from its SUVs since the late 1990s, the big majority of which is the <a href="/wiki/Lexus_RX" title="Lexus RX">Lexus RX</a> crossover.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://money.cnn.com/galleries/2011/autos/1112/gallery.most-disliked-cars.fortune/5.html">[3]</a></p>
<p>In the U.S., domestic manufacturers were slow to switch from their emphasis on light truck-based SUVs, and foreign automakers developed crossovers targeting the U.S. market, as an alternative to station wagons that are unpopular there. But by the 2010 model year, domestic automakers had quickly caught up.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The segment has strong appeal to aging <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" title="Baby boomer">baby boomers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
