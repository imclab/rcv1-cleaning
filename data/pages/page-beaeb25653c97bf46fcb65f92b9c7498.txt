<h3 class='post-title'>
<a href='http://brandfailures.blogspot.com/2006/11/brand-culture-failures-car-names.html'>Brand culture failures: Car names translation problems</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
<div class='post-body'>
<p>Of all products, cars have had the most translation problems. When people chuckled at General Motors&#8217; Chevy Nova in Latin America, the automotive giant was perplexed. Until, that is, someone pointed out that &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">Nova</span>&#8217; means &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">It doesn&#8217;t go</span>&#8217; in Spanish.<br /><br />Then there was the Mitsubishi Pajero sport utility that caused embarrassment in Spain, where &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">pajero</span>&#8217; is slang for &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">masturbator</span>&#8217;.<br /><br />Toyota&#8217;s Fiera car proved controversial in Puerto Rico, where &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">fiera</span>&#8217; translates to &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">ugly old woman</span>&#8217;.<br /><br />Likewise few Germans were enthusiastic about owning Rolls-Royce&#8217;s &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">Silver Animal Droppings</span>&#8217; car. To the English speaking world it bears the more romantic name &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">Silver Mist</span>&#8217;.<br /><br />And finally, Ford didn&#8217;t have the reception they expected in Brazil when their &#8216;Pinto&#8217; car flopped. Then they discovered that in Brazilian Portuguese slang, &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">pinto</span>&#8217; means &#8216;<span style="font-style: italic;">small penis</span>&#8217;.<br /><br /><a style="font-weight: bold;" href="http://brandfailures.blogspot.com/2006/11/more-brand-culture-failures-part-1.html">Read more about most famous and funniest brand culture  failures</a></p>
