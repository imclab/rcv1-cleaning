            <p align="center"><b><font face="Verdana" size="2" color="#FFCC00">
			Car Buyer Beware: Shady Dealings&nbsp; </font></b></td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td width="100%" align="center" height="163" valign="top">
            <p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px"><b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="2">&nbsp;&nbsp;
			<img border="0" src="CoolRides%20Banners/cad48-72dpiSml-embos-vsml.jpg" width="409" height="121" align="center" alt="Old Caddy illustration by: Dr. Von Zuko 2004©"><br>
			</font><font face="Verdana" size="1"><font color="#808080">Shady 
			Dealings 1:&nbsp; </font><br>
			</font><font face="Verdana" size="2">Inflating Trade-In Value to Cover Negative Equity
			</font></b><font face="Verdana" size="2"><br><br>This shady tactic is frequently 
			used if you are Up-side Down in the loan on the car you are 
			trading in (meaning: you owe more than the car is worth). <br><br>To keep the deal moving forward on the car you intend to buy, the 
			dealer will frequently allow you to believe that they are valuing 
			your trade-in at the same amount that you owe on it. Now keep in 
			mind that no one is really going to cover you on the deal so you 
			come out even. Auto dealers are not exactly known for their charity.
			<br><br>On paper, it looks like the dealer is valuing your trade-in at what 
			you owe on it, and that you wont own any money to the original 
			lender on the old car. <br><br>The trick here is that they are actually giving you less than what 
			you owe on the car, and probably less then what the car is worth 
			(they are in business to make a profit). The difference between what 
			you owe on the old car and what the car is actually worth is loaded 
			into the purchase price of the new car (or the capitalization costs 
			of a leased car). Rest assured, you are the one who will be paying 
			that difference not the dealer.<br><br>By inflating the cash price or other costs of the newer car, you are 
			tricked into (illegally) paying more than the advertised price, more 
			in finance charges, and more in sales taxes and registration fees. A 
			similar illegal practice may occur when a lease balance is paid off. 
			Keep in mind, that even if the dealer tells you what they are doing, 
			these practices are still illegal.<br><br></font><b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="1" color="#808080">Shady Dealings 2:</font><font face="Verdana" size="2">
			<br>
			Manipulating the Advertised Price <br></font></b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="2"><br>According to the law, 
			dealerships can not sell a vehicle for more than the advertised 
			price (even when the customer is not aware of the advertised price.) 
			If a dealership inflates the selling price of a vehicle, whether to 
			improve the profit margin, cover other costs, or (presumably) help a 
			customer out of a prior loan, the resulting sale is at a higher 
			price than advertised and is a violation of the law. <br><br>An advertised price is broadly defined to include news paper, direct 
			mail, Internet, radio or TV advertising as well as the window 
			sticker on the vehicle.<br><br></font><b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="1" color="#808080">Shady Dealings 3: 
			</font><font face="Verdana" size="2"><font color="#808080">&nbsp;</font><br>
			Packing (inflated monthly payments) </font></b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="2"><br><br>Packing is a trick intended to 
			get you loaded up with a lot of added cost accessories and services 
			that carry a higher profit margin for the dealership.<br><br>How this works is that you are quoted an inflated monthly payment 
			and once you accept that monthly payment amount, the dealership goes 
			to work packing accessories, service contracts, GAP insurance, 
			paint or fabric protection, etc. into the deal, in order to reach 
			the inflated monthly amount. <br><br>They count on you not realizing that the accessories and services 
			are optional, instead you are led to believe the accessories are 
			included with vehicle or not told anything at all.<br><br>
			</font><b><font face="Verdana" size="1" color="#808080">Shady 
			Dealings 4.</font><font face="Verdana" size="2"><font color="#808080">&nbsp;
			</font><br>
			The Deferred Down Payment<br>
			</font></b><font face="Verdana" size="2"><br>
			Dealerships will sometimes encourage customers (who cant aford the 
			down payment at purchase time) to make their down payments in the 
			form of additional payments, referred to as deferred down payments. 
			The law does recognize payments of this type and requires that 
			deferred down payments be itemized, including the amount and date 
			due for the deferred down payments. Some dealership, rather than 
			disclosing deferred down payments, will have customers write all of 
			the checks for the deferred down payment with different dates on 
			them and then agree not to cash the checks until those agreed upon 
			dates.<br>
			<br>
			Whats wrong with this? As part of this transaction, the dealer and 
			the customer are creating terms and obligations that are not 
			included in the purchase agreement (the single document law).<br>
			<br>
			</font><b><font face="Verdana" size="1" color="#808080">Shady 
			Dealings 5.</font><font face="Verdana" size="2">&nbsp; <br>
			Full Disclosure on the Used Cars </font></b>
			<font face="Verdana" size="2"><br>
			<br>
			Are they telling you everything about that slightly used vehicle you 
			have your eye on?&nbsp; Are they telling you what a great buy it 
			would be?&nbsp; Ask questions!<br>
			<br>
			Dealerships are required by law to disclose material known facts 
			about any used vehicle they are offering for sale. They are also 
			prohibited from misrepresenting anything related to the facts of the 
			vehicle's history. Material known facts means: <br>
&nbsp;</font><ul>
				<li>
				<p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px">
				<font face="Verdana" size="2">Is the odometer reading for this 
				vehicle accurate or been replaced? </font></li>
				<li>
				<p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px">
				<font face="Verdana" size="2">Was the vehicle involved in an 
				accident that resulted in substantial damage? </font></li>
				<li>
				<p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px">
				<font face="Verdana" size="2">Was the vehicle ever a rental or 
				fleet vehicle such as a Taxi Cab, Police Car, etc.?</font></li>
				<li>
				<p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px">
				<font face="Verdana" size="2">Was the vehicle ever repurchased 
				by either the manufacturer or dealer under the Lemon Law?</font></li>
			</ul>
			<p align="left" style="margin-left: 8px; margin-right: 8px">
			<b><font face="Verdana" size="1" color="#808080">Shady Dealings 6.</font><font face="Verdana" size="2">&nbsp;
			<br>
			It Depends on How You Define the Term New or Used?</font></b><font face="Verdana" size="2"><br>
&nbsp;<br>
			Dealerships are required by law to describe the vehicle being 
			purchased as either &quot;new&quot; or &quot;used&quot;.&nbsp; Technically, the used 
			vehicle definition also includes a demonstrator (Demo) vehicle used 
			by dealership employees or manufacturer representatives.&nbsp; Often 
			the sales contract will state the vehicle is &quot;new&quot; with the dealer 
			arguing that it has never been &quot;titled to anyone&quot; but, it is a used 
			vehicle.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; <br>
			<br>
			Additionally, there are vehicles that were previously sold but 
			returned due to failed financing, or some other reason.&nbsp; It is 
			not uncommon for a dealer to&nbsp; attempt to list this vehicle as 
			new.&nbsp; The dealer will want someone else to pay for the 
			depreciation on vehicles in their inventory.<br>
			<br>
			<br>

					<td>
					<p style="margin-left: 6px; margin-right: 6px">
					<font face="Verdana" size="1"><b>Quick Tip 1: </b><br>
					Remember you only pay sales tax on the actual purchase price 
					of a vehicle.&nbsp; <br>
					<br>
					If someone tries to convince you that you pay sales tax on 
					the list price or MSRP . . . you are being scammed!</font></p>
					<p style="margin-left: 6px; margin-right: 6px">
					<font face="Verdana" size="1"><b>Quick Tip 2:</b><br>
					Never tell a car salesman how much you think you can afford 
					on a monthly payment. <br>
					<br>
					Instead, let them do the work of qualifying you and 
					determining what you can get approved for on a vehicle loan.<br>
					<br>
					<b>Quick Tip 3:<br>
					</b>If you buy a car or truck from a private seller, 
					&quot;ALWAYS&quot; make sure to write down the VIN (Vehicle Identity 
					Number) and get a Vehicle History Report &quot;BEFORE&quot;<b> </b>
					buying the vehicle.&nbsp; <br>
					<br>
					A vehicle history report will tell you important things 
					about that vehicle like has it ever been wrecked, in a 
					flood, or a rental car, or a whole host of things that could 
					save you grief . . . such as, does it have a clean title?
					<br>
					<b><br>
					Quick Tip 3:</b><br>
					Cosigning on a loan is serious business.&nbsp; Think hard 
					before you cosign for someone.&nbsp; If they default in any 
					way, you are legally required to pay off their loan.<br>
					<br>
					And yes! It will affect your credit score.</font></td>
