      <p>While the automotive industry suffers, the after market is experiencing a boom it has not seen since the mid fifties when young men with time on their hands and money in their pockets dreamed of having the fastest, most furious hot road around. Back then, <a href="http://cars.helium.com/topic/7239-car-shows" class="sumLink">car shows</a> were everywhere, and drag racing was just being born out of the stop light to stop light sprints that gave every traffic cop in America headaches.</p><div class="googleArticleAd">
    <div class="adSpacer">
<script type="text/javascript">
    
      GA_googleFillSlotWithSize(HELAD_publishercode, "ArticleATFMiddleArticle300x250", 300, 250);

  </div><p>The only real difference between then and now is that back then, most of the after market business involved making jalopies presentable. Anything fresh from Detroit usually found its way to an adult population satisfied with car makers' efforts to provide a safe, sturdy, stylish vehicle that lasted just long enough for owners to save enough money to buy a new version in a couple of years. The backbone of the after market business were young men who inherited automobiles that were certainly less than perfect, and great amounts of money went toward getting these worn old iron horses back on the road.</p><p>These days the primary source of after market success comes from young owners of new machines, and those cars need not be anything special like the old <a href="http://classic-cars.helium.com/topic/6253-muscle-cars" class="sumLink">muscle cars</a> or the gas guzzling showboats that begged customization. Now it's common to see a dressed out Toyota or Nissan as some classic American iron brought back from the junk heap. Young men and women are accessorizing their brand <a href="http://buying-cars.helium.com/topic/4275-new-cars" class="sumLink">new cars</a> with spinning rims, rumbling exhausts, and ultra-thin tires, and the factory paint hardly has tile to dry before it's replaced with something with flash. After market <a href="http://auto-repair.helium.com/topic/3219-auto-parts" class="sumLink">auto parts stores</a> are packed with dazzling goodies to dress up even the most factory-perfect yuppie-mobiles. Neither BMW nor Audi are safe from the trend to make a car a personal expression of its owner.</p><p>The young driver is not the only force behind the continued economic success of the after market. Baby boomers who grew up with a deep appreciation of the old marques are powering a revival of the fifties, restoring the virtually every old car they get their hands on regardless of its original reputation. Among the <a href="http://classic-cars.helium.com/topic/6253-muscle-cars" class="sumLink">muscle cars</a> and former Detroit masterpieces, <a href="http://cars.helium.com/topic/7239-car-shows" class="sumLink">car shows</a> now play home to almost any old make brought back to its original showroom grace. For every classic Mustang, there is a Plymouth Valiant; for each handsome Corvette, a Ford Country Squire station wagon.</p><p>Even the minivan is not immune to the car enthusiast. It won't be too long before they, too, will find their place among the classic cars circling</p>
