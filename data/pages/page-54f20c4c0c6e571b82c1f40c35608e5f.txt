<span class="menu_arts">Arts &amp; Entertainment</span><br>
<span class="serif_index">PURE LOVE</span><br>
<span class="small_bold">Tear-Jerking Love Stories Enthrall Japanese Women (October 14, 2004)</span><br>
<br>

<div class="image">
<table cellspacing="0" cellpadding="0" border="0" width="150">
<tr>
<td width="150" colspan="2">
<table width="150" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="0" border="0">
<tr>
<td width="145"><img src="images/art041014.jpg" width="145" height="200" alt="Yong-sama"></td>
<td width="5" bgcolor="#7c0066"><img src="../images/spacer.gif" width="5" height="1" alt=""></td></tr></table></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td width="133" class="small">Bae has won legions of fans in Japan. (Jiji)</td>
<td width="17" valign="top"><a href="#" onClick="MM_openBrWindow('images/l_art041014.jpg','newwindowh','width=260,height=350')"><img src="../images/zoom.gif" width="17" height="16" border="0"></a></td>

Japanese women are being swept up in an unprecedented craze 
for jun'ai (pure, innocent love). A South Korean TV drama series titled <span class="italic">Winter 
Sonata</span> and the novel <span class="italic">Sekai no Chushin de, Ai o Sakebu</span> 
(Crying for Love at the Heart of the World) by Katayama Kyoichi are among the 
catalysts for this boom. Middle-aged women are said to be the most passionate 
devotees of <span class="italic">Winter Sonata,</span> while middle school and 
high school girls are the most hardcore fans of <span class="italic">Sekai.</span> 
Both have earned abbreviated nicknames, a sure sign of popularity in Japan: <span class="italic">Winter 
Sonata</span> is familiarly referred to as Fuyusona, and <span class="italic">Sekai</span> 
as Sekachu. Why are people so hungry for &quot;pure&quot; love? Discussions about 
why this theme has struck such a chord with Japanese women have been filling the 
columns and airwaves of Japan's media.
<p>
<span class="articlebold">Winter Sonata Draws Unusually High Ratings</span><br>
<span class="italic">Winter Sonata</span> (<span class="italic">Fuyu no Sonata</span> 
in Japanese) was produced in South Korea in 2002. It was aired on Saturday nights 
at 11 pm on the NHK (Japan Broadcasting Corporation) General terrestrial channel 
from April to August 2004, and despite being shown at such a late hour it quickly 
became a hit, achieving viewer ratings of over 20%. Such was the show's impact 
that when the star of the series, Bae Yong-joon, visited Japan, hordes of mostly 
middle-aged women crowded the airport to greet him. Nicknamed &quot;Yong-sama&quot; 
by his fans, the actor's extraordinary popularity has become known as the &quot;Yong-sama 
phenomenon.&quot;</p>
<p>
NHK actually first began broadcasting <span class="italic">Winter Sonata</span> 
in April 2003 on its Satellite 2 channel. But seeing the show's popularity gradually 
rise, it decided to put the series on its flagship terrestrial channel, a move 
that ignited the Fuyusona frenzy. Fans have been snapping up videotapes, DVDs, 
and books about the drama, and this demand helped NHK to reverse the decline in 
sales and profits it experienced in fiscal 2002 with an increase in both of these 
indicators in fiscal 2003 (April 2003-March 2004). NHK's operating revenue (excluding 
subscriber fees) rose &yen;4.6 billion ($41.8 million at &yen;110 to the dollar) 
over the previous year to &yen;105.4 billion ($958 million), of which revenues 
from <span class="italic">Winter Sonata</span> accounted for &yen;3.5 billion 
($31.8 million).</p>
<p>
The book <span class="italic">Sekai no Chushin de, Ai o Sakebu</span> has sold 
more than 3 million copies, becoming the bestselling Japanese novel of all time. 
The film version of the book drew 5 million people to theaters, and a TV adaptation 
aired from July to September 2004 registered high viewer ratings as well.</p>
<p>
Both stories are about pure and innocent love. In <span class="italic">Winter 
Sonata</span> a young woman meets a man who looks just like her first love, who 
died years before in an accident. <span class="italic">Sekai,</span> meanwhile, 
is the tale of a man who for 17 years cannot overcome the loss of his sweetheart, 
who died of an illness in his high school days.</p>
<p>
<span class="articlebold">Reminders of Yesteryear</span><br>
Other novels dealing with pure love, such as <span class="italic">Wasure Yuki</span> 
(Snow of Oblivion) by Shindo Fuyuki and <span class="italic">Ima, Ai ni Yukimasu</span> 
(I'm Coming to See You Now) by Ichikawa Takuji, are also enjoying brisk sales. 
NHK earlier aired another South Korean romance series starring Choi Ji-woo, who 
played the heroine in <span class="italic">Winter Sonata,</span> on Satellite 
2. In the hope of recreating the <span class="italic">Winter Sonata</span> boom, 
NHK broadcasts the program on terrestrial TV starting in October.</p>
<p>
So, why pure love, and why now? Katayama Kyoichi, the author of <span class="italic">Sekai,</span> 
has this to say: &quot;Romantic affairs are fundamentally about committing oneself 
to another person with one's heart and soul. It may be that my novel reached the 
hearts of young people who are not content with superficial relationships.&quot; 
In regard to South Korean drama series, one critic observes that &quot;the characters 
use beautiful language, are polite, care about their families, and maintain platonic 
relationships. Perhaps they remind today's Japanese of something they have left 
behind.&quot;</p>
<p>
As Japanese women increasingly enter the workforce and build careers, it may be 
that some have found they have little time for the ultra-romantic pursuits portrayed 
in <span class="italic">jun'ai</span> TV series and books. These refreshing stories 
appear to be providing a welcome outlet for their romantic feelings.</p>
