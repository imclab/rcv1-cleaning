									<div class="entry-content">
										<p>﻿“Avenue Q” is like “Sesame Street” for grown-ups. The kind of
grown-ups who think it’s hilarious when puppets lampoon fringe
topics like Internet porn, racism and high-decibel sex.</p>
<p>The racy puppet show, playing in Billings on March 9, is one of
the freshest productions to score on Broadway in recent years. It
earned three Tony Awards in 2004 before closing in 2009 after 2,534
shows only to open six weeks later as an off-Broadway production.
Teenagers and people in their early 20s have especially embraced
the show’s counter-culture banter and bawdy humor. But one of the
creators, Robert Lopez, points out that senior citizens laugh just
as hard because, after all, “in order to get to 75, you have to be
25.”</p>
<p>Another “Avenue Q” creator, Jeff Marx, interned at “Sesame
Street” early in his career and some of the original cast members
were “Sesame Street” performers. So the comparisons between the two
puppet shows are deserving, if unnerving.</p>
<p>Two “Avenue Q” puppets, Rod and Nicky, are reminiscent of Sesame
Street’s Bert and Ernie and Trekki Monster’s voice and disposition
are pure Cookie Monster, only without the cookie fetish. But
“Avenue Q” puppets are singing songs with titles like “If You Were
Gay,” “Everyone’s a Little Bit Racist” and “I’m Not Wearing
Underwear Today.”</p>
<p>While “Sesame Street” uses simplistic characters and melodies to
punctuate lessons learned by children, “Avenue Q” features young
adults bumbling through real-life problems where there are no
clear-cut solutions.</p>
<p>Performer Lisa Helmi Johanson, who portrays one of three human
characters in the touring production of “Avenue Q,” told The
Gazette in a recent telephone interview that the show has more
depth than puppets making dirty jokes.</p>
<p>“There are heartfelt moments that are touching and beautiful
because of the message they send,” Johanson said. “Trekki Monster
has a few of my favorite comedic moments, but because we have new
actors this time around, different situations and different lines
tickle me in a new ways.”</p>
<p>Johanson’s character is Christmas Eve, an Asian woman who
struggles with English. She is also a therapist without any
clients. Johanson, who is of Finnish and Korean heritage, said
“Avenue Q” travels well across North America where Americans and
Canadians both relate to the humor. But in Japan, where the show
toured last year, humor was lost in translation or maybe the
audience was too polite to laugh.</p>
<p>“They gave us presents, they took pictures, they came back
multiple times and brought the pictures, but I’m not sure they
understood the show,” said Johanson, 25. “It made me realize how
American this show is.”</p>
<p>The show’s creators invented characters like themselves who were
searching for purpose in life while they encountered adult
problems. Part of the show’s humor stems from knowing that in
“Sesame Street” and other children’s television shows everything
turns out, but in “Avenue Q” and in real life, that isn’t always
the case.</p>
<p>Lopez is now working with the creators of “South Park,” Trey
Parker and Matt Stone, to produce the comedic musical “The Book of
Mormon” on Broadway. It opens this month.</p>
<p>One reviewer has already dubbed it another “smart, crudely
provocative hit that bucks the tradition of the Great White
Way.”</p>
