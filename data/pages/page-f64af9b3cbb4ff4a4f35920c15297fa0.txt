<p>She emigrated to Germany in 1987, after being dismissed from her job in Romania during the 1970s due to her refusal to co-operate with the regime's secret police. </p><p>Her first collection of German language short stories, published in 1982, was censored in Romania. </p><p>Mueller's initial works were smuggled out of the country, while in later years she was awarded several literary prizes, including the Irish Republic's Impac Award in 1998. </p><p>One of her later books, 2001's The Appointment, goes into great detail about living under a stagnated dictatorship. </p><p>Only a few of the author's works have been translated into English, including The Passport (1986), The Land of Green Plums (1994) and The Appointment. </p><p>Mueller will receive a prize of 10 million Swedish Kronor (&pound;892,000) along with her Nobel honour, which will be presented at a ceremony in Stockholm on 10 December. </p><p>German Chancellor Angela Merkel said the author &quot;more than deserved&quot; the prize. </p><p>&quot;Especially now, 20 years after the fall of the Berlin Wall, it's a beautiful signal that such high quality literature and this life experience are being honoured,&quot; she said. </p><p>&quot;We are of course happy that Herta Mueller has found a home in Germany and I congratulate her from the bottom of my heart.&quot; </p><!-- E BO -->
