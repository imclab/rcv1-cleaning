                            <div id="story-body-text">
                                
                                
                                
                                
                                
                                    
                                        
                                        <p>A couple of major ironies are folded into the title of the big new architecture exhibition at the <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="ORCUL00073" title="Los Angeles County Museum of Art" href="/topic/arts-culture/museums/los-angeles-county-museum-of-art-ORCUL00073.topic">Los Angeles County Museum of Art</a>, "The Presence of the Past: <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PECLB0015261" title="Peter Zumthor" href="/topic/arts-culture/architecture/peter-zumthor-PECLB0015261.topic">Peter Zumthor</a> Reconsiders LACMA."</p>
                                            <div class="p402_premium">
                                            <p>The first irony is that the title itself rings with echoes of architectural history. In 1980, when Paolo Portoghesi curated the first Venice Architecture Biennale, he called his show "The Presence of the Past," and he used it to explore the rising interest in history and ornament among the talented emerging architects of the day, including <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PECLB0011149" title="Frank Gehry" href="/topic/arts-culture/architecture/frank-gehry-PECLB0011149.topic">Frank Gehry</a> and Arata Isozaki.</p>
<p>The second irony is a more obvious and cutting one, especially for preservationists and architectural historians in Los Angeles.</p>
                                        
                                        
                                        
                                        <p><a href="http://www.latimes.com/entertainment/arts/culture/la-ca-0721-old-lacma-pictures,0,5918144.photogallery" target="_blank"><strong>PHOTOS: A fresh perspective on LACMA's 'old' buildings</strong></a></p>
<p>A main goal of the exhibition is to build support for a dramatic $650-million plan by Zumthor, the acclaimed Swiss architect, to remake LACMA. And making room for a massive, undulating new structure by Zumthor along Wilshire Boulevard would require knocking down four of the oldest buildings on the museum's campus, including three designed in the early 1960s by prominent L.A. modernist William Pereira.</p>
<p>So much, those preservationists might mutter, for the presence of the past.</p>
<p>I'm already on record as an early supporter of Zumthor's plan, which I think has the potential to be the most thrilling piece of civic architecture built in Los Angeles since Gehry's Walt Disney Concert Hall. But I also think we owe it to the older LACMA buildings, as they face possible demolition, to understand them as deeply as we can &mdash; to recover a clear sense of what their architects hoped they would say to and about Los Angeles.</p>
<p>And so earlier this month, I spent the bulk of three (very hot) weekdays in and around the three Pereira buildings and a 1986 addition by the New York firm Hardy Holzman Pfeiffer Associates (HHPA). I studied the details of the four buildings and the ways they'd been modified over the years.</p>
<p>I read, took notes and sent emails while sitting at one of the small round tables in Times Mirror Court, a 40,000-square-foot plaza between the Pereira and HHPA buildings. I walked over to <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PEHST001570" title="Renzo Piano" href="/topic/arts-culture/architecture/renzo-piano-PEHST001570.topic">Renzo Piano</a>'s Resnick Pavilion, where the "Presence of the Past" is installed, to look at the Zumthor proposal, then back to the eastern side of the campus to see how the new building would fit on the site.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.latimes.com/entertainment/arts/culture/la-et-cm-times-arts-culture-2012-pictures,0,2715761.photogallery"><strong>PHOTOS: Arts and culture in pictures by The Times</strong></a></p>
<p>What did I discover? For starters, that the potentially doomed buildings work better as an ensemble than individually.<strong> </strong>Also, surprisingly, that the 1986 addition, originally the Robert O. Anderson building and now called the Art of the Americas wing, is a piece of architecture with more verve &mdash; and a better sense of humor &mdash; than I'd realized.</p>
<p>The story of the original LACMA buildings goes back to Exposition Park, near USC, where the Los Angeles County Museum of History, Science and Art opened in 1913. It was no coincidence &mdash; to get back to the symbolism of names &mdash; that the museum listed "art" after "history" and "science." Important paintings were tough to find in the original museum.</p>
<p>Over time, it expanded its art holdings, a process that accelerated once <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PEHST000001268" title="William Randolph Hearst" href="/topic/economy-business-finance/media-industry/news-agency/william-randolph-hearst-PEHST000001268.topic">William Randolph Hearst</a> became a major donor in the 1940s. By 1956, Richard Fargo Brown, who would become LACMA's first director, was pushing L.A. County officials to consider a new, separate site for an art museum.</p>
<p>After the county agreed to donate seven acres on Wilshire immediately west of the La Brea Tar Pits, the search for an architect began. Brown favored German modernist <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PEHST002279" title="Ludwig Mies van der Rohe" href="/topic/arts-culture/architecture/ludwig-mies-van-der-rohe-PEHST002279.topic">Ludwig Mies van der Rohe</a>, perhaps the most esteemed architect in the world at the time.</p>
<p>Howard Ahmanson, the project's lead donor, liked Edward Durell Stone, an American. The board also considered <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PECLB0015231" title="Philip Johnson" href="/topic/arts-culture/architecture/philip-johnson-PECLB0015231.topic">Philip Johnson</a>, <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="PEHST000318" title="Philip, Duke of Edinburgh" href="/topic/human-interest/imperial-royal-matters/philip-duke-of-edinburgh-PEHST000318.topic">Gordon Bunshaft</a>, Eero Saarinen and Edward Larrabee Barnes before choosing the supremely well-connected Pereira, who in the course of a prolific career also designed the master plan for <a class="taxInlineTagLink" id="OREDU00000198" title="University of California, Irvine" href="/topic/education/colleges-universities/university-of-california-irvine-OREDU00000198.topic">UC Irvine</a> and San Francisco's Transamerica Pyramid.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.latimes.com/entertainment/arts/culture/la-panorama-lacma-pano-20130718,0,508217.htmlstory" target="_blank"><strong>PANORAMA: Remaking LACMA</strong></a></p>
<p>Pereira's design for LACMA consisted of three separate and largely windowless buildings; faced in split-face marble tile, they were set back from Wilshire and lifted above a series of reflecting pools. The biggest, the Ahamanson Building, was to the west of a central plaza, with the Lytton Gallery &mdash; renamed the Hammer Building in 1968 &mdash; to the north and the Bing Center to the east.</p>
<p>Time magazine called the new museum the "temple on the tar pits." Stylistically, it was typical of 1960s civic architecture in America. It was faithful to the basic rules of modernism &mdash; flat roofs, spare forms, lean silhouettes &mdash; but it also reflected growing impatience among leading architects with the strict rules of the International Style.</p>
<p>As with Stone, Saarinen or World Trade Center architect Minoru Yamasaki, Pereira was looking in the 1960s to establish a new direction for modernism, one in which some limited ornament and nods to history were no longer taboo. The most obvious reflection of this attitude in the LACMA buildings comes in the slender concrete columns that give rhythm to their exteriors.</p>
<p>On the whole, however, Pereira's work was always more careful, less fluid or decorative, than buildings by those other architects.</p>
