		<h1 class="title">
							<a href="/arts-entertainment/mad-about-the-movies/38869-shailene-woodley-s-new-short-hair-for-a-role-and-excellent-cause" title="See Shailene Woodley's short hair, for TFIOS role and excellent cause ">See Shailene Woodley's short hair, for TFIOS role and excellent cause </a>
					</h1>
	
				<p class="meta">
	
			Written by Barbara Vancheri on <time datetime="2013-08-25" pubdate>Sunday, 25 August 2013 1:52 pm</time>. 	
		
		</p>
		
	</header>
	
	
	<div class="content clearfix">
		<p>Photo o&nbsp;</p>
<div style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img src="/2013Barb/gushazelblogpic.jpg" alt="gushazelblogpic" style="border: 1px solid #000000; margin: 7px auto; vertical-align: top; display: block;" /></span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">
<div>Hypable.com has posted this photo of the leads of “The Fault In Our Stars” which shows<strong> Shailene Woodley</strong>’s new short haircut. She stars alongside <strong>Ansel Elgort</strong> in the adaptation of the novel.</div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div>It wasn’t a fashion statement but an attempt to help her play a teenage girl struggling with cancer. Woodley donated her hair to a charitable organization, Children With Hair Loss, which helps all children with medically related hair loss. &nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>It was founded by a woman whose niece, at age 3, was diagnosed with acute lymphoblastic leukemia and underwent chemo for 5 years. She started the group so children could have access to fashionable, human-hair wigs if they wanted.</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>In other news, s<span style="font-size: 16px;">ome suburban Pittsburghers have been notified about shooting that will take place at a nearby church, apparently the home of the support group where Hazel Grace and Gus meet. They were given three dates in September and one in October.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>Author <strong>John Green</strong> recently told the Indianapolis Star that he planned to make a cameo in the movie, even if it was being shot in Pittsburgh rather than its original setting of Indianapolis.&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>The Star’s<strong> David Lindquist</strong> quoted Green as saying Pittsburgh was chosen as the main site for the movie (some scenes will be shot in Amsterdam, which cannot be faked here — easily) due to Pennsylvania’s tax credit for filmmakers.&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>“[It’s] frustrating for me,” Green said. “I wish this movie could be filmed in Indianapolis, because I think it would be great for the city.”</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>He was offered to chance for a cameo in the movie and he took it. If filming hasn’t started, it should by tomorrow, Aug. 26.</div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>

		<h1 class="title">

					</h1>
	
				<p class="meta">
	
			Written by Barbara Vancheri on <time datetime="2013-08-25" pubdate>Sunday, 25 August 2013 11:31 am</time>. 	
		
		</p>
		
	</header>
	
	
	<div class="content clearfix">
		<p>&nbsp;</p>
<div><img src="/2013Barb/butlerposter.jpg" alt="butlerposter" style="border: 1px solid #000000; margin: 7px auto; vertical-align: top; display: block;" /></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“The Butler” was the top grossing movie in North America for the second week in a row.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">It picked up another $17,018,000, for $52,274,813, according to early estimates from hollywood.com.</span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>

		<h1 class="title">
							<a href="/arts-entertainment/mad-about-the-movies/38867-julie-harris-appeared-on-pittsburgh-stage-and-made-tv-theatrical-movies" title="Julie Harris appeared on Pittsburgh stage and made TV, theatrical movies">Julie Harris appeared on Pittsburgh stage and made TV, theatrical movies</a>
					</h1>
	
				<p class="meta">
	
			Written by Barbara Vancheri on <time datetime="2013-08-25" pubdate>Sunday, 25 August 2013 10:30 am</time>. 	
		
		</p>
		
	</header>
	
	
	<div class="content clearfix">
		<p>&nbsp;</p>
<div><img src="/2013Barb/julieharrisbvlblog.jpg" alt="julieharrisbvlblog" style="border: 1px solid #000000; margin: 7px auto; vertical-align: top; display: block;" /></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Actress<strong> Julie Harris</strong> has died.</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Mark Kennedy</strong>, drama writer for the Associated Press, reports the 87-year-old actress died at her West Chatham, Mass., home of congestive heart failure, actress and family friend <strong>Francesca James</strong> said.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">She won five Tony Awards for best actress in a play and was honored with a sixth for lifetime achievement.<em> (AP photo above)</em></span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">I spoke to Harris by phone for “The Gin Game” which stopped in Pittsburgh with <strong>Charles Durning</strong> in early 1999. And I met her when she came to town in 1996 to make a movie called “The Christmas Tree.” It marked <strong>Sally Field</strong>’s directing debut and I visited the set in Hartwood Acres one day.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">I waited for hours as the pleasant autumn day turned chilly — but I was rewarded with some time in Harris’ trailer.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">If you’re a moviegoer, you also may remember Harris played a professor in <strong>George A. Romero</strong>’s “The Dark Half” starring <strong>Timothy Hutton</strong> and <strong>Amy Madigan.&nbsp;</strong></span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;<strong> JULIE HARRIS BRINGS A DANCE TO POIGNANT TWO-CHARACTER PLAY ‘THE GIN GAME’&nbsp;</strong></span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img src="/2013Barb/gingame.gif" alt="gingame" style="border: 1px solid #000000; margin: 7px; float: left;" /></span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Credit Julie Harris for the stage moment when she and Charles Durning dance. It’s a sentimental scene that wasn’t there when “The Gin Game” debuted on Broadway two decades ago with<strong> Hume Cronyn</strong> and the late<strong> Jessica Tandy.&nbsp;</strong></span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>&nbsp;</strong></span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“I said to our director, <strong>Charles Nelson Reilly</strong>, we have this wonderful, championship dancer in Charles Durning, and wouldn’t it be wonderful if there was a scene somewhere along the line where they could dance together,” Harris recalls. When Reilly spoke to playwright<strong> D.L. Coburn</strong>, the response was: “Well, the play has been successful for 20 years, and I’m not about to change it.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">But then Coburn thought about it, embraced the idea and provided a scene for Harris and Durning, who revived the play on Broadway in April 1997 and are now taking it on the road. “The Gin Game,” a two-character play about inhabitants of a seedy retirement home, arrives in Pittsburgh on Tuesday for a 12-day run at the Byham Theater.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“It’s a great play,” the 73-year-old Harris says, by phone from Detroit. “The added touch of the little moment when they dance together is very beautiful. It’s touching.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">She puts “The Gin Game” in grand company. “To me, it has the perfection of 'The Glass Menagerie,' “a window into the lives of the characters that is most illuminating. It has the sort of “perfection of memory” that provides vivid snapshots of life.</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">It’s a compressed “three weeks in the lives of these two people who meet for the first time in a retirement home and go through his cataclysmic encounter,” with the gin game a metaphor for life. </span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“Life deals you the cards, and you have to do the best you can with them,” suggests Harris, whose cards in life included a 1945 Broadway debut that led to a record 10 Tony nominations and five Tony Awards.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">She keeps them in a “little cabinet at home. Some are on stands, and I think three are just the medallions before they put them on stands.”</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Harris also has two Emmys plus an Oscar nomination for the 1952 film “The Member of the Wedding.” At the time, Harris said movies didn’t exactly flatter her figure. “Pictures make me look like a 12-year-old boy who flunked his body-building course.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Since we are entering the heart of the awards season, it’s natural to ask what she thinks of such ceremonies. “I know that when I was nominated, I wasn’t always able to go because I was working on the road somewhere. I’ m not really 100 percent for saying there’s one best performance.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“For instance, last year, <strong>Judi Dench</strong> was nominated for an Oscar for ` Mrs. Brown’ and <strong>Helen Hunt</strong> was nominated for `As Good As It Gets.’ I thought that Judi Dench should have gotten the prize, and Helen Hunt thought she should have,” too, even noting in her acceptance speech that she saw “Mrs. Brown” three times.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“And truly, you really shouldn’t have to choose between those two; they’re equally wonderful,” she adds, mentioning the year when <strong>Barbra Streisand</strong> and <strong>Katharine Hepburn</strong> enjoyed a rare tie in the best actress category. “I wish they’d nominate five people and give them five Oscars,” a scenario that would reduce the nervousness and likely the worldwide audience, too.</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The Oscar nominations aren’t due until Feb. 9 but Harris, like moviegoers everywhere, has some favorites. (Of course hers count, since she is a member of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences.) Among them: “Life Is Beautiful,” “Gods and Monsters” - “I think <strong>Lynn Redgrave</strong>’s performance in that is astonishing, and Ian McKellen is wonderful” - “Central Station” with an extraordinary turn by <strong>Fernanda Montenegro</strong> and the leading ladies in “Stepmom.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Harris, who was last in Pittsburgh in 1996 to make the ABC movie “The Christmas Tree,” is looking forward to her return. She hopes to take a friend to see Fallingwater, which she found lovely the first time around, get to the Carnegie Museum of Art, look up her “Dark Half” director<strong> George Romero</strong>, and perhaps ride the incline up to Mount Washington again.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Although touring has changed since the days when the cast traveled by train instead of plane, Harris is still thrilled to visit or revisit cities and their attractions. A recent stop in Dallas, for instance, afforded a side trip to the Kimball Museum in Fort Worth. Harris, who confesses she rarely travels light, always brings along scripts and correspondence from fans and friends, in addition to her books and stash of vitamins.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“I got a letter from a fan who said I would like you to answer these questions about leadership, what makes a good leader,” says Harris, who usually fields inquiries about her own work or “East of Eden” co-star <strong>James Dean</strong>. “I simply answered by saying a leader has to be truthful, I think, you have to trust them and they should have humor. I always think of Mr. Lincoln when I think of a leader.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">When “The Gin Game” tour closes in May, Harris plans to star in a new play called “Winter” in Chicago. Written by <strong>Claudia Allen</strong>, it will co-star Chicago-based actor<strong> Mike Nussbaum.</strong> “I play a sort of frisky lady in the beginning, in the first act, who gets sick and has to go into a retirement home.”&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">After that, Harris has two solo plays she would like to do, one about <strong>Alice B. Toklas, Gertrude Stein</strong>’s companion, and the other about Canadian artist <strong>Emily Carr</strong> (1871-1945), noted for her expressionist paintings of landscapes, forests and Indians of British Columbia. Also an author, Carr weathered the lean times by making carpets and pottery with Indian designs. Once rejected in her native land, she later become memorialized on a Canadian stamp.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The play remains the thing for the actress who ha</span><span style="font-size: 12pt;">s stepped into the fascinating shoes of everyone from<strong> Emily Dickinson</strong> and <strong>Mary Todd Lincoln</strong> to <strong>Sally Bowles.</strong> Even as a newcomer playing a hapless kleptomaniac in “The Young and the Fair” in 1948, her first-night performance stopped the show dead in its tracks with a near-ovation.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The stage “is terribly fulfilling for the actor, because you can start at the beginning and go to the end and no one says cut, we have to do that over. I mean, for the two hours or so, you’re on your own, and that’s very exciting.”</span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><strong><span style="font-size: 12pt;">ACTRESS MAKES DEBUT AS DIRECTOR WITH CHRISTMAS MOVIE AT HARTWOOD&nbsp;</span></strong></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><strong><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></strong></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img src="/2013Barb/xmastreejharris.jpg" alt="xmastreejharris" style="border: 1px solid #000000; margin: 7px; float: right;" />First Forrest Gump, now his Mama.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong>Tom Hanks</strong> made his directorial debut with “That Thing You Do!’’ and <strong>Sally Field</strong> is doing likewise in Pittsburgh with a holiday movie called “The Christmas Tree.’’ Unlike Hanks, she’s not acting in the film, and hers is for TV, but she is working with well-known faces: Julie Harris and <strong>Andrew McCarthy</strong>.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">In keeping with the usual breakneck pace of TV movies, they’re getting up early and working till the sun sets, as well as trudging through the snow at Hartwood Acres, but they don’t seem to mind. Snow, in 60-degree weather?&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Yes, it snowed at Hartwood this week, thanks to a snow man who works movie magic with ice, soapy foam, cotton and an orange tank labeled “Unreal Snow’’ that sprays an authentic-looking white powder that can be washed away.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">To hear everyone tell it, it’s a wonderful life on the set of “The Christmas Tree.’’ It’s the kind of place where the word “lovely’’ is frequently evoked, even as Field is waiting for a droning airplane to pass or bees are circling the Coke cans or a crew member is bellowing, “All nuns on deck!’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The weather has been, admittedly, splendid this week. Field is “really lovely to the crew, and it’s a really nice story,’’ McCarthy says. Harris is not only a dignified veteran of stage, film and TV but she’s playing an orphan who becomes a nun and has a special place in her heart for Tree (yes, capital T), for heaven’s sake. As a child she found solace in it and the two literally grew up together.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">In other words, when ABC airs this Disney movie Dec. 15, it won’t need a parental advisory. It will be family entertainment all the way.</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">It’s based on the slender, just-published novel “The Christmas Tree’’ by former Wall Street Journal reporter <strong>Julie Salamon</strong> and has charming illustrations by <strong>Jill Weber.</strong> It tells how the search for the perfect tree for Rockefeller Center changes the lives of the nun who has cherished an evergreen at Brush Creek convent since childhood and the man who wants to share it with the world.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Harris plays Sister Anthony, and McCarthy is Richard, the driven landscape architect who ends up learning some lessons about life and love.<strong> Trini Alvarado</strong> is his girlfriend in a role created for the movie by Field, who fleshed out the sweet but slight story and wrote the screenplay with <strong>Janet Brownell.</strong>&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“It’s nice to come to work. I’ve been on jobs where it’s not fun to come to work. Everybody here is really good at their job, and they like being here,’’ says McCarthy, 33, who made his movie debut in “Class’’ and graduated to such films as “St. Elmo’s Fire,’’ “Less Than Zero,’’ “Year of the Gun’’ and “Mulholland Falls.’’ He also livened up the corpse comedies “Weekend at Bernie’s’’ and its sequel.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">He calls Field, a two-time Oscar winner for “Norma Rae’’ and “Places in the Heart,’’ the best director in terms of working with actors that he’s ever seen. By far.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“I’ve loved Sally’s and Julie’s work ever since before I can remember,’’ says the youthful McCarthy, plopping into a canvas director’s chair between takes. “To get a chance to work with people like that, you have to do it,’’ he adds.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The story is “sentimental in that nice way, in a really truthful way,’’ and an actress of Harris’ caliber brings a measure of integrity to the tale. “I just kind of stand back and watch her half the time. Most of the time, actually.’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The mansion at Hartwood, a rambling English tudor built in the late 1920s, doubles as the convent and is the main reason the production came to Pittsburgh rather than Canada. Allegheny County agreed to waive a location fee, although the production must pay for extras such as electricity or additional security.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“It’s almost like the story was written for this house,’’ says executive producer <strong>Wendy Japhet.</strong> “It’s a perfect environment for this story.’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The title character is “played” by three Norway spruces, transported to a picturesque meadow at Hartwood with the help of Eisler Nurseries in Butler. (In fact, you can look for<strong> Peter</strong> and<strong> Sherry Kyne</strong> from the Butler business and their two young children, Peter and Amanda, as extras in the film.)&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Two smaller trees, found at Butler Country Club, were brought in with their roots intact and will remain at Hartwood. Eisler bought the largest, a 60-footer that looks as if it could grace a public square, from Greenlawn Cemetary. He cut it, moved it and sunk it about five feet into the ground with cement. When its color started to fade to brown, it was sprayed green. Such are the secrets of moviemaking.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The novel describes the fir this way: “This tree was a star. Everything about it said so: its rich color, the regal way it held itself - even where it stood, just apart from a whole group of evergreens, as if it was special. . . . It seemed to have that improbable combination I was looking for - the size of King Kong and the suppleness of Giselle.’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">In addition to importing a few trees, the production constructed a greenhouse at Hartwood. With slightly fogged and stained windows, greenery inside and a weathered water pump out front, it looks appropriately aged.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The temperature may be almost 70, but McCarthy and Harris have to feign frigid air. As they walk out of the greenhouse, Harris tugs at her scarf and slips on her gloves. When a fall breeze adds an unscripted moment, Field keeps the camera rolling and announces, “I love the fact that the door blew open.’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Field, 49, who appears as slender as in her teen-age TV days, has the look of a director down pat, although it’s unlikely Hanks or Spielberg would wear a pink T-shirt with white hearts. A ponytail pokes out of a black baseball cap that has “Sundance’’ emblazoned over the bill, and she dons or doffs a flannel shirt, worn with jeans and sturdy shoes, as the temperature rises and falls.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The first-time director is juggling a budget of around $3 million and the challenge of working with children (7-year-old <strong>Suzi Hofrichter</strong> of Cecil, Washington County, plays Harris as a child), Christmas, period flashbacks and seasonal changes. Everyone knows it’s her maiden voyage, so to speak, and is cooperating, says Japhet. “This has been a tremendous experience. . . . The movie’s going to look beautiful.’’&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The final shots will be done at the real Rockefeller Center Dec. 3.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Field is concentrating on her work and doing no interviews while shooting, but others are happy to go on the record now. That includes Harris, who ends a long work day that required her to endure some pointed questioning from McCarthy’s character about her past, navigate her way through a tangle of snowy tree limbs and logs and run up to her beloved tree to check on its condition after a big storm.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">As she sheds the endless outer layers of clothing that constitute her nun’s habit, she says, “I love the story, and I love the company, and I love Sally’s direction. I think she’s very gifted. I think that’s sort of a winning combination.’’ The five-time Tony winner, who earned an Oscar nomination for her screen debut in 1952’s “The Member of the Wedding,’’ also has a good gut feeling about this project.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">As someone who has nurtured a 20-year-old begonia and recently returned a bonsai plant for fear she would kill it, Harris can identity with the bond Sister Anthony feels for the tree. “Indians used to say if you stand and lean up against a tree, you get a kind of energy,’’ Harris says, demonstrating on an unyielding wall in her trailer.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Harris and Field worked on a 1972 made-for-TV yuletide movie of a different sort: “Home for the Holidays’’ (also titled “Deadly Desires’’) about four daughters trying to protect<strong> Walter Brennan f</strong>rom a maniac during a Christmas reunion.&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">“I haven’t seen her over these years when she’s done all these really tremendous things, but she’s like a heroine to me,’’ says Harris. “When I saw her on our first day when we were rehearsing, Andrew and I almost broke into tears. It was so wonderful.’’</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></div>
<div style="margin-left: 30px;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Lovely, no doubt.</span></div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>

		<header>

				<div class="icon email"><a href="/component/mailto/?tmpl=component&amp;template=pg_master&amp;link=09ca218d45ec89e0e93d8d095145b3e78bb0ee47" title="Email" onclick="window.open(this.href,'win2','width=400,height=350,menubar=yes,resizable=yes'); return false;"><img src="/media/system/images/emailButton.png" alt="Email"  /></a></div>
			
				<div class="icon print"><a href="/arts-entertainment/mad-about-the-movies/38857-ben-affleck-as-batman-just-remember-no-one-thought-keaton-could-pull-it-offe-braod?tmpl=component&amp;print=1&amp;layout=default&amp;page=" title="Print" onclick="window.open(this.href,'win2','status=no,toolbar=no,scrollbars=yes,titlebar=no,menubar=no,resizable=yes,width=640,height=480,directories=no,location=no'); return false;" rel="nofollow"><img src="/media/system/images/printButton.png" alt="Print"  /></a></div>
			
		<h1 class="title">
							<a href="/arts-entertainment/mad-about-the-movies/38857-ben-affleck-as-batman-just-remember-no-one-thought-keaton-could-pull-it-offe-braod" title="Ben Affleck as Batman? Just remember, no one thought Keaton could pull it off">Ben Affleck as Batman? Just remember, no one thought Keaton could pull it off</a>
					</h1>
	
				<p class="meta">
	
			Written by Barbara Vancheri on <time datetime="2013-08-23" pubdate>Friday, 23 August 2013 3:48 pm</time>. 	
		
		</p>
		
	</header>
	
	
	<div class="content clearfix">
		<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Here is the official Warner Bros. release about Ben Affleck as Batman. Just remember, no one thought Michael Keaton could pull off the Dark Knight until he did. Twice. And never underestimate how smart Affleck is. Besides, he already played George Reeves/Superman in "Hollywoodland." &nbsp;</span></strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span class="Apple-style-span" style="font-size: 16px;"><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">BEN AFFLECK REVEALED AS BATMAN IN WARNER BROS. PICTURES’</span></strong></span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">NEW SUPER HERO FEATURE FILM, NOW SLATED TO OPEN&nbsp;<span class="aBn" data-term="goog_1755174746" tabindex="0" style="border-bottom-width: 1px; border-bottom-style: dashed; border-bottom-color: #cccccc; position: relative; top: -2px; z-index: 0;"><span class="aQJ" style="position: relative; top: 2px; z-index: -1;">JULY 17, 2015</span></span></span></strong></span><br /><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span><br /><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><em><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">The Oscar</span></strong><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">-winning star joins Henry Cavill in the first ever onscreen<br />match-up of DC Comics’ most iconic characters.</span></strong></em></span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span><br /><span style="font-size: 12pt;"><img src="/2013Barb/smallerbenaffleck.jpg" alt="smallerbenaffleck" style="float: left; margin: 7px; border: 1px solid #000000;" />BURBANK, CA, August 22, 2013 – Ending weeks of speculation, <strong>Ben Affleck</strong> has been set to star as Batman, a.k.a. Bruce Wayne.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;"> Affleck and filmmaker <strong>Zack Snyder</strong> will create an entirely new incarnation of the character in Snyder’s as-yet-untitled project—bringing Batman and Superman together for the first time on the big screen and continuing the director’s vision of their universe, which he established in “Man of Steel.”&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;"> The announcement was made today by <strong>Greg Silverman</strong>, president, Creative Development and Worldwide Production, and<strong> Sue Kroll</strong>, president, Worldwide Marketing and International Distribution, Warner Bros. Pictures.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The studio has slated the film to open worldwide on&nbsp;<span class="aBn" data-term="goog_1755174747" tabindex="0" style="border-bottom-width: 1px; border-bottom-style: dashed; border-bottom-color: #cccccc; position: relative; top: -2px; z-index: 0;"><span class="aQJ" style="position: relative; top: 2px; z-index: -1;">July 17, 2015.</span></span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Last month’s surprise announcement of the new movie featuring both Superman and Batman created a wave of excitement and immediately fueled discussion and debate—among fans as well as in the media—about who would put on the cape and cowl of Bruce Wayne’s alter ego.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Snyder successfully re-imagined the origin of Clark Kent/Superman in the worldwide blockbuster “Man of Steel,” which has earned more than $650 million worldwide to date, and climbing.&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;"> The director will now create an original vision of Batman and his world for the film that brings the two DC Comics icons together.&nbsp;Affleck will star opposite <strong>Henry Cavill</strong>, who will reprise the role of Superman/Clark Kent.&nbsp; </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The film will also reunite “Man of Steel” stars <strong>Amy Adams, Laurence Fishburne</strong> and<strong> Diane Lane</strong>. In the announcement, Silverman stated, “We knew we needed an extraordinary actor to take on one of DC Comics’ most enduringly popular superheroes, and Ben Affleck certainly fits that bill, and then some.&nbsp; His outstanding career is a testament to his talent and we know he and Zack will bring new dimension to the duality of this character.”</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Snyder also expressed his excitement about the casting of Affleck, noting, “Ben provides an interesting counter-balance to Henry’s Superman.&nbsp; He has the acting chops to create a layered portrayal of a man who is older and wiser than Clark Kent and bears the scars of a seasoned crime fighter, but retain the charm that the world sees in billionaire Bruce Wayne.&nbsp; I can’t wait to work with him.”</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Kroll added, “We are so thrilled that Ben is continuing Warner Bros.’ remarkable legacy with the character of Batman.&nbsp; He is a tremendously gifted actor who will make this role his own in this already much-anticipated pairing of these two beloved heroes.”</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Affleck recently starred in the Academy Award-winning Best Picture “Argo,” which he also directed and produced, earning acclaim and a BAFTA Award nomination for his performance in the film, as well as a number of directing honors.&nbsp; </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">In 2010, he starred in and directed the hit crime thriller “The Town.”&nbsp; His recent acting work also includes “The Company Men,” “State of Play,” and “Hollywoodland,” for which he received a Golden Globe nomination for Best Actor. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Earlier in his career, Affleck starred in and co-wrote (with <strong>Matt Damon</strong>) “Good Will Hunting,” for which he won an Oscar&nbsp;for Best Original Screenplay.&nbsp;</span><br /><span style="font-size: 12pt;">&nbsp;</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 12pt;"></span><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The new superhero film is being scripted by<strong> David S. Goyer</strong> from a story he co-created with Zack Snyder.&nbsp; <strong>Charles Roven</strong> and <strong>Deborah Snyder</strong> are producing, with <strong>Benjamin Melniker</strong>, <strong>Michael E. Uslan</strong> and <strong>Wesley Coller</strong> serving as executive producers.&nbsp;</span><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Production is expected to begin in 2014.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><span style="font-size: 12pt;">The film is based on Superman characters created by <strong>Jerry Siegel</strong> &amp; <strong>Joe Shuster</strong>, and Batman characters created by <strong>Bob Kane</strong>, published by DC Entertainment.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: left;"><em><span style="font-size: 12pt;">Photo of Affleck on Oscar night 2013 by Matt Petit, AMPAS.</span></em></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>	</div>
