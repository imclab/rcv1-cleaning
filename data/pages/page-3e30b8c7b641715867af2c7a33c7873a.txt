<p><b>Erotic literature</b> comprises fictional and factual stories and accounts which sexually arouse the reader, whether written with that intention or not. Such <a href="/Erotica" title="Erotica">erotica</a> takes the form of novels, short stories, poetry, <a href="/Erotic_memoirs" title="Erotic memoirs">true-life memoirs</a>, and <a href="/Sex_manuals" title="Sex manuals">sex manuals</a>. Erotic literature has often been subject to censorship and legal restraints on publication.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Erotic verse">edit</a>]</div><a name="Erotic_verse"></a><h2>Erotic verse</h2>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Erotic_verse" title="Erotic verse">erotic verse</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early periods">edit</a>]</div><a name="Early_periods"></a><h3>Early periods</h3>
<p>Many erotic poems have survived from <a href="/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> and <a href="/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>, the authors including the Greeks <a href="/Straton_of_Sardis" title="Straton of Sardis">Straton of Sardis</a>, <a href="/Sappho_of_Lesbos" title="Sappho of Lesbos">Sappho of Lesbos</a> (lyrics); and the Romans <a href="?title=Automedon_%28poet%29&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Automedon (poet)">Automedon</a> (<i>The Professional</i> and <i>Demetrius the Fortunate</i>),  <a href="/Philodemus" title="Philodemus">Philodemus</a> (<i>Charito</i>), <a href="?title=Marcus_Argentarius&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Marcus Argentarius">Marcus Argentarius</a>, <a href="/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a>, <a href="/Propertius" title="Propertius">Propertius</a>, <a href="/Tibullus" title="Tibullus">Tibullus</a>, <a href="/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, <a href="/Martial" title="Martial">Martial</a> and <a href="/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a> and the anonymous <a href="/Priapeia" title="Priapeia">Priapeia</a> Some later Latin authors also wrote erotic verse, e.g. <a href="/Joannes_Secundus" title="Joannes Secundus">Joannes Secundus</a>. In the Renaissance period many poems were not written for publication and merely circulated in manuscript among a relatively limited readership. Such were the <i><a href="/Sonnets" title="Sonnets">Sonnets</a></i> of <a href="/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> who also wrote the erotic poems <i><a href="/Venus_and_Adonis_%28Shakespeare_poem%29" title="Venus and Adonis (Shakespeare poem)">Venus and Adonis</a></i> and <i><a href="/The_Rape_of_Lucrece" title="The Rape of Lucrece">The Rape of Lucrece</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">edit</a>]</div><a name="17th_and_18th_centuries"></a><h3>17th and 18th centuries</h3>
<p>In the 17th century, <a href="/John_Wilmot%2C_Earl_of_Rochester" title="John Wilmot, Earl of Rochester">John Wilmot, Earl of Rochester</a> (1647–80) was notorious for obscene verses, many of which were published posthumously in compendiums of poetry by him and other <a href="/Restoration_rake" title="Restoration rake">Restoration rakes</a> such as <a href="/Charles_Sedley" title="Charles Sedley">Sir Charles Sedley</a>, <a href="/Charles_Sackville%2C_6th_Earl_of_Dorset" title="Charles Sackville, 6th Earl of Dorset">Charles Sackville, 6th Earl of Dorset</a>, and <a href="/George_Etherege" title="George Etherege">George Etherege</a>. Though many of the poems attributed to Rochester were actually by other authors, his reputation as a libertine was such that his name was used as a selling point by publishers of collections of erotic verse for centuries after. One poem which definitely was by him was "A Ramble in <a href="/St._James%27s_Park" title="St. James's Park">St. James's Park</a>" in which the protagonist's quest for healthy exercise in the park uncovers instead "Bugg'ries, Rapes and Incest" on ground polluted by debauchery from the time when "Ancient Pict began to Whore". This poem was being censored from collections of Rochester's poetry as late as 1953, though, in line with a general change in attitudes to sexuality, it was dramatised as a scene in the film <i><a href="/The_Libertine_%282004_film%29" title="The Libertine (2004 film)">The Libertine</a></i> about his life based on an existing play.
</p><p>English collections of erotic verse by various hands, include the <i>Drollery</i> collections of the 17th century; <i><a href="/Pills_to_Purge_Melancholy" title="Pills to Purge Melancholy">Pills to Purge Melancholy</a></i> (1698–1720); the <i><a href="/Roxburghe_Ballads" title="Roxburghe Ballads">Roxburghe Ballads</a></i>; <i><a href="/Bishop_Percy%27s_Folio" title="Bishop Percy's Folio">Bishop Percy's Folio</a></i>; <i>The Musical Miscellany</i>; <i>National Ballad and Song: Merry Songs and Ballads Prior to the Year AD 1800</i> (1895-7) edited by J. S. Farmer; the three volume <i>Poetica Erotica</i> (1921) and its more obscene supplement the <i>Immortalia</i> (1927) both edited by T. R. Smith. French collections include <i>Les Muses gaillardes</i> (1606) <i>Le Cabinet satyrique</i> (1618) and <i>La Parnasse des poetes satyriques</i> (1622).
</p><p>A famous collection of four erotic poems, was published in England in 1763, called <i><a href="/An_Essay_on_Woman" title="An Essay on Woman">An Essay on Woman</a></i>. This included the title piece, an obscene parody of <a href="/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>'s "<a href="/An_Essay_on_Man" title="An Essay on Man">An Essay on Man</a>"; "<a href="/Veni_Creator:_or%2C_The_Maid%27s_Prayer" title="Veni Creator: or, The Maid's Prayer">Veni Creator: or, The Maid's Prayer</a>", which is original; the "<a href="/Universal_Prayer" title="Universal Prayer">Universal Prayer</a>", an obscene parody of Pope's poem of the same name, and "<a href="/The_Dying_Lover_to_his_Prick" title="The Dying Lover to his Prick">The Dying Lover to his Prick</a>", which parodies "<a href="/A_Dying_Christian_to_his_Soul" title="A Dying Christian to his Soul">A Dying Christian to his Soul</a>" by Pope. These poems have been attributed to <a href="/John_Wilkes" title="John Wilkes">John Wilkes</a> and/or Thomas Potter and receive the  distinction of being the only works of erotic literature ever read out loud, in their entirety in the <a href="/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>—before being declared obscene and blasphemous by that august body and the supposed author, Wilkes, declared an <a href="/Outlaw" title="Outlaw">outlaw</a>.
</p><p><a href="/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> worked to collect and preserve Scottish folk songs, sometimes revising, expanding, and adapting them. One of the better known of these collections is <i>The Merry Muses of Caledonia</i> (the title is not by Burns), a collection of bawdy lyrics that were popular in the music halls of Scotland as late as the 20th century.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a>]</div><a name="19th_century"></a><h3>19th century</h3>
<p>One of the 19th century's foremost poets--<a href="/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a>—devoted much of his considerable talent to erotic verse, producing, inter alia, twelve eclogues on <a href="/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">flagellation</a> titled <i>The Flogging Block</i> "by Rufus Rodworthy, annotated by Barebum Birchingly"; more was published anonymously in <i><a href="/The_Whippingham_Papers" title="The Whippingham Papers">The Whippingham Papers</a></i> (c. 1888). Another notorious anonymous 19th century poem on the same subject is <i><a href="/The_Rodiad" title="The Rodiad">The Rodiad</a></i>, ascribed (seemingly falsely and in jest) to <a href="?title=George_Colman_the_Younger&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="George Colman the Younger">George Colman the Younger</a>. <a href="/John_Camden_Hotten" title="John Camden Hotten">John Camden Hotten</a> even wrote a pornographic comic opera, <i><a href="?title=Lady_Bumtickler%E2%80%99s_Revels&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Lady Bumtickler’s Revels">Lady Bumtickler’s Revels</a></i>, on the theme of <a href="/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">flagellation</a> in 1872.
</p><p><a href="/Pierre_Lou%C3%BFs" title="Pierre Louÿs">Pierre Louÿs</a> helped found a literary review, <i>La Conque</i> in 1891, where he proceeded to publish <i><a href="/Astarte" title="Astarte">Astarte</a></i>—an early collection of erotic verse already marked by his distinctive elegance and refinement of style. He followed up in 1894 with another erotic collection in 143 prose poems--<i><a href="/Songs_of_Bilitis" title="Songs of Bilitis">Songs of Bilitis</a> (Les Chansons de Bilitis)</i>, this time with strong lesbian themes.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a>]</div><a name="20th_century"></a><h3>20th century</h3>
<p>Although <a href="/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a> could be regarded as a writer of love poems, he usually dealt in the less romantic aspects of love such as sexual frustration or the sex act itself. <a href="/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> in his <i>Literary Essays</i> complained of Lawrence's interest in his own "disagreeable sensations" but praised him for his "low-life narrative." This is a reference to Lawrence's dialect poems akin to the Scots poems of <a href="/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>, in which he reproduced the language and concerns of the people of <a href="?title=Nottinghamshire&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> from his youth. He called one collection of poems <i>Pansies</i> partly for the simple ephemeral nature of the verse but also a pun on the French word <i>panser</i>, to dress or bandage a wound. "The Noble Englishman" and "Don't Look at Me" were removed from the official edition of <i>Pansies</i> on the grounds of obscenity, which he felt wounded by.
</p><p>From the age of 17, <a href="?title=Gavin_Ewart&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Gavin Ewart">Gavin Ewart</a> acquired a reputation for wit and accomplishment through such works as "Phallus in Wonderland" and "Poems and Songs", which appeared in 1939 and was his first collection. The intelligence and casually flamboyant virtuosity with which he framed his often humorous commentaries on human behaviour made his work invariably entertaining and interesting. The irreverent eroticism for which his poetry is noted resulted in <a href="?title=W_H_Smith&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="W H Smith">W H Smith</a>'s banning of his "The Pleasures of the Flesh" (1966) from their shops.
</p><p>Canadian poet <a href="?title=John_Glassco&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="John Glassco">John Glassco</a> wrote <i><a href="?title=Squire_Hardman_%28poem%29&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Squire Hardman (poem)">Squire Hardman</a></i> (1967), a long poem in <a href="/Heroic_couplets" title="Heroic couplets">heroic couplets</a>, purporting to be a reprint of an 18th century poem by <a href="?title=George_Colman_the_Younger&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="George Colman the Younger">George Colman the Younger</a>, on the theme of flagellation.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Erotic fiction">edit</a>]</div><a name="Erotic_fiction"></a><h2>Erotic fiction</h2>
<p><b>Erotic fiction</b> is the name given to <a href="/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a> that deals with sex or sexual themes, generally in a more literary or serious way than the fiction seen in <a href="/Pornographic_magazine" title="Pornographic magazine">pornographic magazines</a> and sometimes including elements of <a href="/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a> or <a href="/Social_criticism" title="Social criticism">social criticism</a>. Such works have frequently been banned by the government or religious authorities. It should be noted, however, that apparently non-fictional works dealing with sex or sexual themes may contain fictional elements; calling an erotic book 'a memoir' is a literary device that is common in this genre. For reasons similar to those that make pseudonyms both commonplace and often deviously set up, the boundary between fiction and non-fiction is often very diffuse.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History of western erotic fiction">edit</a>]</div><a name="History_of_western_erotic_fiction"></a><h3>History of western erotic fiction</h3>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ancient times">edit</a>]</div><a name="Ancient_times"></a><h4>Ancient times</h4>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Ancient_erotica" title="Ancient erotica">ancient erotica</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/Classic_erotica" title="Classic erotica">Classic erotica</a> from the Ancient World includes the <a href="/Song_of_Songs" title="Song of Songs">Song of Songs</a> from the <a href="/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> and the Roman <i><a href="/Satyricon" title="Satyricon">Satyricon</a></i> of <a href="/Petronius_Arbiter" title="Petronius Arbiter">Petronius Arbiter</a> (later made into a <a href="/Satyricon_%28film%29" title="Satyricon (film)">film</a> by <a href="/Fellini" title="Fellini">Fellini</a>).
</p><p><a href="/Sex_manual" title="Sex manual">Sex manuals</a> such as the <a href="/Kama_Sutra" title="Kama Sutra">Kama Sutra</a> are some of the best known works of erotic literature. The <a href="/Ananga_Ranga" title="Ananga Ranga">Ananga Ranga</a> is a lesser known one, aimed specifically at preventing the separation of a husband and wife.
</p><p>Erotic fiction from the <a href="/Roman_literature" title="Roman literature">Roman period</a>- more appropriately called <a href="/Ribaldry" title="Ribaldry">ribaldry</a> - includes the <i><a href="/Satyricon" title="Satyricon">Satyricon</a></i> of <a href="/Petronius" title="Petronius">Petronius</a> (later made into a <a href="/Satyricon_%28film%29" title="Satyricon (film)">film</a> by <a href="/Fellini" title="Fellini">Fellini</a>. The output of literary eroticism during the Roman era featured more poetry than prose, see <a href="/Latin_profanity" title="Latin profanity">Latin profanity</a>.

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a>]</div><a name="Middle_Ages"></a><h4>Middle Ages</h4>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Medieval_erotica" title="Medieval erotica">medieval erotica</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p>From the Medieval period we have the <i><a href="/Decameron" title="Decameron">Decameron</a></i> (1353) by the Italian , <a href="/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a> (made into a <a href="/The_Decameron_%281970_film%29" title="The Decameron (1970 film)">film</a> by <a href="/Pasolini" title="Pasolini">Pasolini</a>) which features tales of <a href="/Lechery" title="Lechery">lechery</a> by monks and the <a href="/Nunsploitation" title="Nunsploitation">seduction of nuns</a> from convents. This book was banned in many countries. Even five centuries after publication copies were seized and destroyed by the authorities in the USA and the UK. For instance between 1954 and 1958 eight orders for destruction of the book were made by English magistrates. 
</p><p><br />
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a>]</div><a name="Renaissance"></a><h4>Renaissance</h4>

</p><p>The sixteenth century was notable for the <i><a href="/Heptameron" title="Heptameron">Heptameron</a></i> of <a href="/Marguerite_de_Navarre" title="Marguerite de Navarre">Marguerite de Navarre</a> (1558), inspired by Boccaccio's <i>Decameron</i>.

<p>One striking aspect of pre-industrial European <strong>erotic literature</strong> is the preponderance of female characters. Two early 17th century French works, <i><a href="/L%27%C3%89cole_des_filles" title="L'École des filles">L'École des filles </a></i> and <i><a href="/L%E2%80%99Academie_des_Dames" title="L’Academie des Dames">L’Academie des Dames</a></i>, were written as female dialogues — a literary device that was to be repeated many times over the next century in works such as <a href="/John_Cleland" title="John Cleland">John Cleland</a>’s <i><a href="/Fanny_Hill" title="Fanny Hill">Fanny Hill</a></i> and the <a href="/Marquis_de_Sade" title="Marquis de Sade">Marquis de Sade</a>’s <i><a href="/L%27Histoire_de_Juliette" title="L'Histoire de Juliette">Juliette</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Enlightenment">edit</a>]</div><a name="Enlightenment"></a><h4>Enlightenment</h4>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/18th_century_literature" title="18th century literature">18th century literature</a>, <a href="/18th_century_erotica" title="18th century erotica">18th century erotica</a>, <a href="/Libertine_novel" title="Libertine novel">libertine novel</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p>An early pioneer of the publication of erotic works in England was <a href="/Edmund_Curll" title="Edmund Curll">Edmund Curll</a> (1675-1747). The rise of the novel in 18th century England provided a new medium for erotica. One of the most famous in this new genre was <i><a href="/Fanny_Hill" title="Fanny Hill">Fanny Hill</a></i> by <a href="/John_Cleland" title="John Cleland">John Cleland</a>. This book set a new standard in literary smut and has often been adapted for the cinema in the 20th century.
</p><p>The rise of the novel in 18th century England provided a new medium for erotica. One of the most famous in this new genre was <i><a href="/Fanny_Hill" title="Fanny Hill">Fanny Hill</a></i> by <a href="/John_Cleland" title="John Cleland">John Cleland</a>. This book set a new standard in literary smut and has often been adapted for the cinema in the 20th century.
</p><p>French writers at this time also wrote erotica. A famous example is <i><a href="/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_Philosophe" title="Thérèse Philosophe">Thérèse Philosophe</a></i> (1748) by <a href="/Jean-Baptiste_de_Boyer%2C_Marquis_d%27Argens" title="Jean-Baptiste de Boyer, Marquis d'Argens">Jean-Baptiste de Boyer, Marquis d'Argens</a> which describes a girl's inititation into the secrets of both <a href="/Philosophy_and_sex" title="Philosophy and sex">philosophy and sex</a>. Another example is <i><a href="/The_Lifted_Curtain_or_Laura%27s_Education" title="The Lifted Curtain or Laura's Education">The Lifted Curtain or Laura's Education</a></i>, about a young girl's sexual initiation by her father, written by the <a href="/Honor%C3%A9_Gabriel_Riqueti%2C_comte_de_Mirabeau" title="Honoré Gabriel Riqueti, comte de Mirabeau">Comte de Mirabeau</a>; also <i><a href="/Les_Liaisons_dangereuses" title="Les Liaisons dangereuses">Les Liaisons dangereuses</a></i> (Dangerous Liaisons) by <a href="/Pierre_Choderlos_de_Laclos" title="Pierre Choderlos de Laclos">Pierre Choderlos de Laclos</a>, first published in 1782.
</p><p>In the late 18th century the theme of <a href="/Sado-masochism" title="Sado-masochism">sado-masochism</a> was explored by the <a href="/Marquis_de_Sade" title="Marquis de Sade">Marquis de Sade</a> in such works as  <i><a href="/Justine%2C_or_the_Misfortunes_of_Virtue" title="Justine, or the Misfortunes of Virtue">Justine, or the Misfortunes of Virtue</a></i> and <i><a href="/120_Days_of_Sodom" title="120 Days of Sodom">120 Days of Sodom</a></i>. The Marquis de Sade's work was very influential on later erotica and he (together with the later writer Sacher-Masoch) lent his name to the sexual acts which he describes in his fiction.
Directories of <a href="/Prostitutes" title="Prostitutes">prostitutes</a> and their services have also historically served as a sexual education in print, such as <i><a href="/Harris%27s_List_of_Covent_Garden_Ladies" title="Harris's List of Covent Garden Ladies">Harris's List of Covent Garden Ladies</a></i> (<a href="/1757" title="1757">1757</a>-<a href="/1795" title="1795">1795</a>). The rise of the novel in 18th century England provided a new medium for erotica. One of the most famous in this new genre was <i><a href="/Fanny_Hill" title="Fanny Hill">Fanny Hill</a></i> by <a href="/John_Cleland" title="John Cleland">John Cleland</a>. This book has often been adapted for the cinema in the 20th century.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rousseau coins &quot;books to read with one single hand&quot;">edit</a>]</div><a name="Rousseau_coins_.22books_to_read_with_one_single_hand.22"></a><h5>Rousseau coins "books to read with one single hand"</h5>
<p>The expression "<a href="/Livre_%C3%A0_lire_d%27une_seule_main" title="Livre à lire d'une seule main">livre à lire d'une seule main</a>" (English: books to read with one single hand) is coined by <a href="/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a>. It is often used to refer to <strong>erotic literature</strong>, or "<a href="/Stroke_book" title="Stroke book">stroke books</a>".
</p>
<dl><dd>"Though my taste had not preserved me from silly, unmeaning books, by good fortune I was a stranger to licentious or obscene ones; not that La Tribu (who was very accommodating) made any scruple of lending these&nbsp;; on the contrary, to enhance their worth, she spoke of them with an air of mystery which produced an effect she had not foreseen, for both shame and disgust made me constantly refuse them. Chance so well seconded my bashful disposition, that I was past the age of thirty before I saw any of those <b>dangerous compositions, to which a fine lady of fashion has no other objection than that they must be read with one hand.</b> 
</dd></dl>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 19th century era">edit</a>]</div><a name="19th_century_era"></a><h4>19th century era</h4>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Victorian_erotica" title="Victorian erotica">Victorian erotica</a>, <a href="/19th_century_erotica" title="19th century erotica">19th century erotica</a>, <a href="/19th_century_literature" title="19th century literature">19th century literature</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p>In the <a href="/Victorian_erotica" title="Victorian erotica">Victorian period</a>, the quality of erotic fiction was much below that of the previous century&nbsp;— it was written by '<a href="/Hack" title="Hack">hacks</a>'. Some works, however, borrowed from established literary models, such as <a href="/Dickens" title="Dickens">Dickens</a>. It also featured a curious form of <a href="/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">social stratification</a>. Even in the throes of orgasm, the social distinctions between master and servant (including form of address) were scrupulously observed. Significant elements of <a href="/Sado-masochism" title="Sado-masochism">sado-masochism</a> were present in some examples, perhaps reflecting the influence of the English <a href="/Public_school" title="Public school">public school</a>. These works were often anonymous, and undated, and include such titles as  <i><a href="/The_Lustful_Turk" title="The Lustful Turk">The Lustful Turk</a></i> (1828); <i><a href="/The_Way_of_a_Man_with_a_Maid" title="The Way of a Man with a Maid">The Way of a Man with a Maid</a></i>; <i><a href="?title=A_Weekend_Visit&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="A Weekend Visit">A Weekend Visit</a></i>, <i><a href="/Romance_of_Lust" title="Romance of Lust">The Romance of Lust</a></i> (1873) and <i><a href="/The_Autobiography_of_a_Flea" title="The Autobiography of a Flea">The Autobiography of a Flea</a></i> (1887).    
</p><p>Clandestine erotic periodicals of this period include <i><a href="/The_Pearl_%28erotica%29" title="The Pearl (erotica)">The Pearl</a></i> a collection of erotic tales, rhymes, songs and parodies published in London between 1879 to 1880.
</p><p>In 1870 the erotic novella <i><a href="/Venus_in_Furs" title="Venus in Furs">Venus in Furs</a></i> by <a href="/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> author <a href="/Leopold_von_Sacher-Masoch" title="Leopold von Sacher-Masoch">Leopold von Sacher-Masoch</a>, brought the attention of the world to phenomenon of <a href="/Masochism" title="Masochism">masochism</a>, named after the author.
</p><p>Towards the end of the century, a more "cultured" form of erotica began to appear by such as the poet <a href="/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> who pursued themes of paganism, lesbianism and sado-masochism in such works as <i><a href="/Lesbia_Brandon" title="Lesbia Brandon">Lesbia Brandon</a></i> and in contributions to <i><a href="/The_Whippingham_Papers" title="The Whippingham Papers">The Whippingham Papers</a></i> edited by <a href="?title=St_George_Stock&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="St George Stock">St George Stock</a>, author of <i><a href="/The_Romance_of_Chastisement" title="The Romance of Chastisement">The Romance of Chastisement</a></i>. This was associated with the <a href="/Decadent_movement" title="Decadent movement">Decadent movement</a>, in particular, with <a href="/Aubrey_Beardsley" title="Aubrey Beardsley">Aubrey Beardsley</a> and the <a href="/Yellow_Book" title="Yellow Book">Yellow Book</a>. But it was also to be found in France, amongst such writers as <a href="/Pierre_Louys" title="Pierre Louys">Pierre Louys</a>, author of <i><a href="/Les_chansons_de_Bilitis" title="Les chansons de Bilitis">Les chansons de Bilitis</a></i> (1894) (a celebration of lesbianism and sexual awakening).
</p>

<dl><dd><i><a href="/20th_century_erotica" title="20th century erotica">20th century erotica</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p>Twentieth century erotic fiction includes such classics of the genre as: <i><a href="?title=Maudie&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Maudie">Maudie</a></i> by Anon; <i><a href="/Sadopaideia" title="Sadopaideia">Sadopaideia</a></i> (1907) by Anon; <i><a href="/Trois_Filles_de_Leur_M%C3%A9re" title="Trois Filles de Leur Mére">Trois Filles de Leur Mére</a></i> (1926) by Pierre Louys; <i><a href="/Story_of_the_Eye" title="Story of the Eye">Story of the Eye</a></i> (1928) by <a href="/Georges_Bataille" title="Georges Bataille">Georges Bataille</a>; <i><a href="/Tropic_of_Cancer_%28novel%29" title="Tropic of Cancer (novel)">Tropic of Cancer</a></i> (1934) by <a href="/Henry_Miller" title="Henry Miller">Henry Miller</a>; The <i><a href="/Story_of_O" title="Story of O">Story of O</a></i> (1954) by <a href="/Pauline_R%C3%A9age" title="Pauline Réage">Pauline Réage</a>; <i><a href="/Lolita" title="Lolita">Lolita</a></i> (1955) and <i><a href="/Ada_or_Ardor:_A_Family_Chronicle" title="Ada or Ardor: A Family Chronicle">Ada, or Ardor</a></i> (1969) by <a href="/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>; <i><a href="/Delta_of_Venus" title="Delta of Venus">Delta of Venus</a></i> (1978) by <a href="/Ana%C3%AFs_Nin" title="Anaïs Nin">Anaïs Nin</a> and <i><a href="/The_Bicycle_Rider" title="The Bicycle Rider">The Bicycle Rider</a></i> (1985) by <a href="/Guy_Davenport" title="Guy Davenport">Guy Davenport</a>.
</p><p><i>Lolita</i> and <i>The Story of O</i> were published by <a href="/Olympia_Press" title="Olympia Press">Olympia Press</a>, a <a href="/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>-based <a href="/Publisher" title="Publisher">publisher</a>, launched in 1953 by <a href="/Maurice_Girodias" title="Maurice Girodias">Maurice Girodias</a> as a rebadged version of the <a href="/Obelisk_Press" title="Obelisk Press">Obelisk Press</a> he inherited from his father <a href="/Jack_Kahane" title="Jack Kahane">Jack Kahane</a>. It published a mix of erotic fiction and <a href="/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> <a href="/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">literary</a> works.
</p><p>From around the late <a href="/1970s" title="1970s">1970s</a>, many sex manuals have been published and openly sold in the western world, notably <i><a href="/The_Joy_of_Sex" title="The Joy of Sex">The Joy of Sex</a></i>. Sex manuals specifically written for sexual minorities are also now published.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Erotic memoirs and other accounts">edit</a>]</div><a name="Erotic_memoirs_and_other_accounts"></a><h2>Erotic memoirs and other accounts </h2>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Erotic_memoirs" title="Erotic memoirs">erotic memoirs</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">Prostitution</a> was the focus of much of the earliest erotic works. The very term "<a href="/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a>" is derived from the Greek <i>pornographos</i> meaning "the writing of prostitutes", originally denoting descriptions of the lives and manners of prostitutes and their customers in <a href="/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>. According to <a href="/Athenaeus" title="Athenaeus">Athenaeus</a> in <i><a href="/The_Deipnosophists" title="The Deipnosophists">The Deipnosophists</a></i> these constituted a considerable genre, with many lubricious treatises, stories and dramas on the subject.
</p><p>Accounts of prostitution have continued as a major part of the genre of erotic literature. 
</p><p>In the eighteenth century directories of <a href="/Prostitutes" title="Prostitutes">prostitutes</a> and their services, such as <i><a href="/Harris%27s_List_of_Covent_Garden_Ladies" title="Harris's List of Covent Garden Ladies">Harris's List of Covent Garden Ladies</a></i> (1757–1795), provided both entertainment and instruction.
</p><p>In the nineteenth century the sensational journalism of <a href="/William_Thomas_Stead" title="William Thomas Stead">W.T. Stead</a>'s <i><a href="/The_Maiden_Tribute_of_Modern_Babylon" title="The Maiden Tribute of Modern Babylon">The Maiden Tribute of Modern Babylon</a></i> (1885) about the procuring of underage girls into the brothels of Victorian London provided a stimulus for the erotic imagination. Stead's account was widely translated and the revelation of "padded rooms for the purpose of stifling the cries of the tortured victims of lust and brutality" and the symbolic  figure of "<a href="/The_Minotaur_of_London" title="The Minotaur of London">The Minotaur of London</a>"  confirmed European observers worst imaginings about "<a href="/Le_Sadisme_anglais" title="Le Sadisme anglais">Le Sadisme anglais</a>" and inspired erotic writers to write of similar scenes set in London or involving sadistic English gentlemen. Such writers include  <a href="/D%27Annunzio" title="D'Annunzio">D'Annunzio</a> in <i>Il Piacere</i>, <a href="/Paul-Jean_Toulet" title="Paul-Jean Toulet">Paul-Jean Toulet</a> in <i>Monsieur de Paur</i> (1898), <a href="/Octave_Mirbeau" title="Octave Mirbeau">Octave Mirbeau</a> in <i><a href="/The_Torture_Garden_%28novel%29" title="The Torture Garden (novel)">Jardin des Supplices</a></i> (1899) and <a href="/Jean_Lorrain" title="Jean Lorrain">Jean Lorrain</a> in <i><a href="/Monsieur_de_Phocas" title="Monsieur de Phocas">Monsieur de Phocas</a></i> (1901).
</p><p>A very recent work in this genre is <i><a href="/The_Intimate_Adventures_of_a_London_Call_Girl" title="The Intimate Adventures of a London Call Girl">The Intimate Adventures of a London Call Girl</a></i> (2005) by <a href="/Belle_de_Jour_%28writer%29" title="Belle de Jour (writer)">Belle de Jour</a>.
</p><p>Erotic memoirs include <a href="/Casanova" title="Casanova">Casanova</a>'s  <i><a href="/Histoire_de_ma_vie" title="Histoire de ma vie">Histoire de ma vie</a></i>, from the eighteenth century. Notable English works of this genre from the nineteenth century include <i>The Ups and Downs of Life</i> (1867) by <a href="/Edward_Sellon" title="Edward Sellon">Edward Sellon</a> and <i><a href="/My_Secret_Life" title="My Secret Life">My Secret Life</a></i> by "Walter". Edward Sellon was a writer, translator and illustrator of erotic literature who wrote erotica for the pornographic publisher William Dugdale, including such works as <i><a href="/The_New_Epicurean" title="The New Epicurean">The New Epicurean</a></i> (1865). The true identity of "Walter" is very mysterious.  Ian Gibson, in <i>The Erotomaniac</i> speculates that <i>My Secret Life</i> was really written by Henry Spencer Ashbee and therefore it is possible that "Walter" is a fiction. A famous German erotic work of this time, published in two parts in 1868 and 1875 entitled <i>Pauline the Prima Donna</i> purports to be the memoirs of the opera singer <a href="/Wilhelmine_Schr%C3%B6der-Devrient" title="Wilhelmine Schröder-Devrient">Wilhelmine Schröder-Devrient</a>. Various discrepencies with known facts of the singer's life however have led many to doubt the veracity of this book and the erotic adventures contained in the second volume, at least, appear to be very implausible. These include the authoress indulging in lesbian sadomasochism, group sex, sodomy, bestiality, <a href="/Scatology" title="Scatology">scatology</a>, <a href="/Necrophilia" title="Necrophilia">necrophilia</a>, prostitution and <a href="/Vampirism" title="Vampirism">vampirism</a>: all before she had reached the age of 27. Twentieth century contributions to the genre include <a href="/Frank_Harris" title="Frank Harris">Frank Harris</a>'s <i><a href="/My_Life_and_Loves" title="My Life and Loves">My Life and Loves</a></i> (1922–27) and from the twenty-first we have <i><a href="/One_Hundred_Strokes_of_the_Brush_Before_Bed" title="One Hundred Strokes of the Brush Before Bed">One Hundred Strokes of the Brush Before Bed</a></i> (2004) by <a href="?title=Melissa_P&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Melissa P">Melissa P</a>.
</p><p>In the twenty-first century, memoirs of sexual abuse, known as <a href="/Misery_memoirs" title="Misery memoirs">Misery memoirs</a> or <a href="/Misery_porn" title="Misery porn">Misery porn</a>, have become very popular, though doubts have been cast on the motivation and veracity of some of the writers.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sex manuals">edit</a>]</div><a name="Sex_manuals"></a><h2>Sex manuals</h2>
<p>Early <a href="/Sex_manuals" title="Sex manuals">sex manuals</a>, as well as being instructive, are often, also, great works of literature. They include from the classical world, the lost works of <a href="/Elephantis" title="Elephantis">Elephantis</a> and <a href="/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>'s <i><a href="/Ars_Amatoria" title="Ars Amatoria">Ars Amatoria</a></i> The Indian <i><a href="/Kama_Sutra" title="Kama Sutra">Kama Sutra</a></i> is one of the world's best known works of this type. The <i><a href="/Ananga_Ranga" title="Ananga Ranga">Ananga Ranga</a></i>  a 12th century collection of <a href="/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> erotic works, is a lesser known one. Also very famous, and often reprinted and translated, is <i><a href="/The_Perfumed_Garden" title="The Perfumed Garden">The Perfumed Garden for the Soul's Recreation</a></i>, a 16th century <a href="/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> work by <a href="/Sheikh_Nefzaoui" title="Sheikh Nefzaoui">Sheikh Nefzaoui</a>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a>]</div><a name="Legal_status"></a><h2>Legal status</h2>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Early legislation">edit</a>]</div><a name="Early_legislation"></a><h3>Early legislation</h3>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: To 1857">edit</a>]</div><a name="To_1857"></a><h4>To 1857</h4>
<p>Erotic or pornographic works have often been prosecuted, censored and destroyed by the authorities on grounds of obscenity. Originally, in England, erotic or pornographic publications were the concern of the ecclesiastical courts. After the Reformation the jurisdiction of these courts declined in favour of the Crown which licensed every printed book. Prosecutions of books for their erotic content alone were rare and works which attacked the church or state gave much more concern to the authorities than erotica or '<a href="/Obscene_libel" title="Obscene libel">obscene libel</a>' as it was then known. For instance the Licensing Act of 1662 was aimed generally at "heretical, seditious, schismatical or offensive books of pamphlets" rather than just erotica per se. Even this Licensing Act was allowed to lapse in 1695 and no attempt made to renew it. 
</p><p>The first conviction for <a href="/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a> in England occurred in 1727, when <a href="/Edmund_Curll" title="Edmund Curll">Edmund Curll</a> was fined for the publication of <i><a href="/Venus_in_the_Cloister" title="Venus in the Cloister">Venus in the Cloister or The Nun in her Smock</a></i> under the <a href="/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> offence of <a href="/Disturbing_the_peace" title="Disturbing the peace">disturbing the</a> <a href="/Queen%27s_peace" title="Queen's peace">King's peace</a>. This set a <a href="/Legal_precedent" title="Legal precedent">legal precedent</a> for other convictions. The publication of other books by Curll, however, considered seditious and blasphemous, such as <i><a href="/The_Memoirs_of_John_Ker" title="The Memoirs of John Ker">The Memoirs of John Ker</a></i>, apparently most offended the authorities. Prosecutions of erotica later in the eighteenth century were rare and were most often taken because of the admixture of seditious and blasphemous material with the porn. For instance, no proceedings were taken against the publishers of Cleland's notorious <i>Fanny Hill</i> (1763).
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="?title=Erotic_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: 1857 - 1959">edit</a>]</div><a name="1857_-_1959"></a><h4>1857 - 1959</h4>
<p>It was the <a href="/Obscene_Publications_Act_1857" title="Obscene Publications Act 1857">Obscene Publications Act 1857</a> which made the sale of obscene material a statutory offence, for the first time, giving the courts power to seize and destroy offending material. The origins of the Act itself were in a trial for the sale of pornography presided over by the <a href="/Lord_Chief_Justice" title="Lord Chief Justice">Lord Chief Justice</a>, <a href="/John_Campbell%2C_1st_Baron_Campbell" title="John Campbell, 1st Baron Campbell">Lord Campbell</a>, at the same time as a debate in the House of Lords over a bill aiming to restrict the sale of poisons. Campbell was taken by the analogy between the two situations, famously referring to the London pornography trade as "<a href="/A_sale_of_poison_more_deadly_than_prussic_acid%2C_strychnine_or_arsenic" title="A sale of poison more deadly than prussic acid, strychnine or arsenic">a sale of poison more deadly than prussic acid, strychnine or arsenic</a>" (Perhaps the earliest known appearance of this ever-popular analogy; compare "I would rather give a healthy boy or a healthy girl a phial of prussic acid than this novel," describing <i><a href="/The_Well_of_Loneliness" title="The Well of Loneliness">The Well of Loneliness</a></i> in 1928), and proposed a bill to restrict the sale of pornography; giving statutory powers of destruction would allow for a much more effective degree of prosecution. The bill was controversial at the time, receiving strong opposition from both <a href="/Houses_of_Parliament" title="Houses of Parliament">Houses of Parliament</a>, and was passed on the assurance by the Lord Chief Justice that it was "... intended to apply exclusively to works written for the single purpose of corrupting the morals of youth and of a nature calculated to shock the common feelings of decency in any well-regulated mind." The House of Commons successfully amended it so as not to apply to Scotland, on the grounds that Scottish common law was sufficiently stringent.
</p><p>The Act provided for the seizure and destruction of any material deemed to be obscene, and held for sale or distribution, following information being laid before a "court of summary jurisdiction" (<a href="/Magistrates%27_court" title="Magistrates' court">Magistrates' court</a>). The Act required that following evidence of a common-law offence being committed - for example, on the report of a plain-clothes policeman who had successfully purchased the material - the court could issue a warrant for the premises to be searched and the material seized. The proprietor then would be called upon to attend court and give reason why the material should not be destroyed. Critically, the Act did not define "obscene," leaving this to the will of the courts.
</p><p>Whilst the Act itself did not change, the scope of the work affected by it did. In 1868 <a href="/Sir_Alexander_Cockburn%2C_12th_Baronet" title="Sir Alexander Cockburn, 12th Baronet">Sir Alexander Cockburn</a>, Campbell's successor as Lord Chief Justice, held in an appeal that the test of obscenity was "...whether the tendency of the matter charged as obscenity is to deprave and corrupt those whose minds are open to such immoral influences and into whose hands a publication of this sort may fall." This was clearly a major change from Campbell's opinion only ten years before - the test now being the effect on someone open to corruption who obtained a copy, not whether the material was <i>intended</i> to corrupt or offend.
</p><p>Cockburn's declaration remained in force for several decades, and most of the high profile seizures under the Act relied on this interpretation. Known as the <a href="/Hicklin_test" title="Hicklin test">Hicklin test</a> no cognisance was taken of the <a href="/Literary_merit" title="Literary merit">literary merit</a> of a book or on the extent of the offending text within the book in question. The widened scope of the original legislation led to the subsequent notorious targeting of now acknowledged classics of world literature by such authors as <a href="/Zola" title="Zola">Zola</a>, <a href="/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> and <a href="/D.H._Lawrence" title="D.H. Lawrence">D.H. Lawrence</a> plus medical textbooks by such as <a href="/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a> rather than the blatant erotica which was the original target of this law.
</p><p>In contrast to England, where actions against obscene literature were the preserve of the magistrates, in America such actions were the responsibility of the Postal Inspection Service, embodied in the federal and state <a href="/Comstock_laws" title="Comstock laws">Comstock laws</a>, named after the postal officer and anti-obscenity crusader <a href="/Anthony_Comstock" title="Anthony Comstock">Anthony Comstock</a> who proved himself officious in the work of suppression both in his official capacity and through his <a href="/New_York_Society_for_the_Suppression_of_Vice" title="New York Society for the Suppression of Vice">New York Society for the Suppression of Vice</a>. The first such law was the Comstock Act, (ch. 258 <a href="?title=Template:USStat&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Template:USStat">Template:USStat</a> enacted March 3, 1873) which made it illegal to send any "obscene, lewd, and/or lascivious" materials through the mail. Twenty-four states passed similar prohibitions on materials distributed within the states.
</p>
