	<p>I may be on a slippery slope here, but I thought maybe I should clarify some key points on diagnostic charges.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I hear it often: "Why is the diagnostic so expensive?" Or, "If I have the work performed, is the diagnostic fee waived," and when the answer is, "No," some people get angry, or give us this look:</p>
<p><img id="img-1376150625287" src="/Portals/94047/images/are you kidding me.jpg" alt="are you kidding me" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Here's one way to look at it: If you go to the doctor with a problem, and your doctor can't figure out what's wrong with you, do you walk out of there free of charge? Unlikely. How about if he finds out you need open heart surgery? Is your "diagnostic" free? Also unlikely.</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Family-doctor-resized-600.jpg" alt="family doctor" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Do you know how expensive medical school is? That doctor spent thousands of dollars and years of his/her life on specialized training and education on how the human body works.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>A similar story could be spun about auto technicians. Back in the day, they were called auto mechanics. Most of what a mechanic did, involved a lot of removal and replacement, and "diagnosing" a car was a piece of cake. Running poorly? Points/plugs/wires/carb overhaul/timing adjustment. Check engine light, ABS light, trac off light? Wheel speed sensor, mass air flow sensor, MAP sensor, air bag impact sensor, computer?</p>
<p><img id="img-1376152216127" src="/Portals/94047/images/ChuckTesta(1)-resized-600.jpg" alt="nope chuck testa" height="304" border="0" width="320"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Which vehicle do you think is easier to diagnose and repair? This beautiful 1976 Corvette Stingray:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/76 Corvette-resized-600.JPG" alt="76 corvette" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>...or this 2003 Infiniti G35x sedan:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/03 G35x-resized-600.JPG" alt="03 G35x" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>How about if I show you their engines?</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/76 350-resized-600.JPG" alt="76 350" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/G35x V6-resized-600.JPG" alt="G35x 3.5 V6" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I'll give you a hint: It's not the 'vette.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Auto technicians today need a ton of specialized and continuous training, and while the education isn't nearly as expensive as medical school, the equipment is. Talk to any good technician that's done it for any length of time. Ask them how much money they have into their tools.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Tool box-resized-600.JPG" alt="tool box" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Sitting in there ^^, between what you can and can't see, is around $100,000...at least.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/modis scan tool-resized-600.jpg" alt="snap-on solus" border="0"></p>
<p>Technicians must spend this kind of money to be able to perform their job both effectively and efficiently. This scanner, which gathers information from your car's various computers, is roughly $6,000.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>While it sucks having to pay the diagnostic fees, it is totally worth it once you look at the big picture. Try to take this into consideration the next time someone tells you that it'll cost $115 to find out why your engine is running poorly.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/themoreyouknow.jpg" alt="the more you know" border="0"></p>

	<p>Well, summer finally arrived in Minnesota and so far it's been pretty brutal. Just the past few days have been near 100° F with insane humidity. Step outside and into your car that's been sitting outside and you feel like...</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/face melting-resized-600.jpg" alt="face melting" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>As you start your car and kick on the A/C, you feel like it isn't as cold as it should be. Well, on days like this it won't be. The air is far too dense for the A/C system in your ride to work 100% as it should.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>However, on slightly cooler days if it's not blowing as cold as it should, but is working (the compressor is cycling on and off) the common thought process is that you're low on freon. While this COULD be the case, it's not always so.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There are many things that factor in to how effectively your air conditioning system works, but here's a couple things that could cause the issue:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/cooling fans-resized-600.jpg" alt="cooling fans" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;Most cars have two cooling fans mounted on the radiator. One is the primary fan used for drawing air into the radiator to cool off the coolant/anti-freeze when the car isn't moving fast enough for the air to be forced through naturally.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The second one is meant to come on with your A/C to draw air through the condenser and lower your A/C pressures. My Jeep's doesn't come on, so the A/C doesn't work worth a darn at idle, but when the truck is moving it's nice and cold. If your condenser fan isn't working properly, this can give you poor A/C.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/ac condenser-resized-600.jpg" alt="ac condenser" border="0"></p>
<p>Plugged or dirty A/C condenser. See the left side of the one pictured? Debris from the road over time gets deposited on the fins that make up the condenser, which won't allow the proper air flow through the fins giving your poor performance. Make sure these are nice and clean.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1374171724630" src="/Portals/94047/images/ac compressor-resized-600.jpg" alt="ac compressor" border="0"></p>
<p>If your car is just blowing warm air, the compressor may not be cycling due to low refrigerant, a bad switch, wiring issue, etc. You'll want to look at it with the A/C turned on and engine running to see if the center of the pulley (the clutch) is spinning or not.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>If the compressor is cycling, but doing so rapidly, chances are that the refrigerant is indeed low. "Topping it off" or "bumping it" is generally not a good idea. You're better off having the system completely evacuated and recharged at which point a ultraviolet dye is usually added to the system to easily detect future leaks.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the event that your air conditioning isn't working very well and you're tired of your "four-fifty-five" (four windows down, 55 miles per hour) air, bring your vehicle to Auto Works for an A/C Quick Check which will check for any obvious leaks or debris problems, and check A/C pressures.</p>

	<p>Let me set the stage for you:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Summer time. Minnesota. 2007 Chevrolet Tahoe 4x4.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1371573056602" src="/Portals/94047/images/08 Tahoe-resized-600.JPG" alt="2007 Tahoe" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Wait, I did say, "Summer," and "Minnesota," didn't I?</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1371573043272" src="/Portals/94047/images/08 Tahoe 2-resized-600.JPG" alt="2007 Tahoe" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There we go. Much more fitting.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/picard-facepalm.jpg" alt="picard" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>So, this Tahoe has the 5.3L V8 Flex-Fuel engine with 109,591 miles and the JL4 RPO code, which is the active brake control/Stabilitrak option.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We had done some brake work on the rear of it and performed a suggested brake fluid flush with it. After the flush, we had no brake pedal. What the H-E-double hockey stick is going on here?</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>As it turns out, bleeding the brakes on this truck (and most newer GM trucks) can be quite challenging if air is introduced into the brake system.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>First, you must bleed out the ABS (Anti-Lock Braking System), which requires the use of a scan tool which engages the ABS pump in a sequence as you pump the brake pedal, like this one:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Modis scan tool-resized-600.jpg" alt="Snap-On Modis" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I've stumbled across a secondary method which remains untested at our shop:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>Fill the master cylinder and pressure bleed the brakes like always, in this order: Right rear, left rear, right front, left front.</li>
<li>Then take a short test drive over 15 mph, and brake hard enough to make the ABS engage during braking. ABS all the way to the stop. A gravel road works well for this, as does the snow if it's winter time, or even wet grass. Do that 3 times in a row, then return to your shop/garage/carport/driveway/whatever.</li>
<li>Refill the master cylinder again, and pressure bleed the brakes again, in the same order.</li>
<li>If your brakes are still spongy, go do the ABS stops again, and bleed the brakes again.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, after we bled the ABS using the scan tool, we still had a very spongy brake pedal, and a lot of air in the front lines.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>What worked best for us, was to have the truck running and the master cylinder cap removed. We then bled the brakes as the above procedure stated and ended up with a firm brake pedal and no MILs (Malfunction Indicator Lamps) after over an hour (total time including ABS bleeding). You can imagine how great my right leg/hip felt the next day.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, I'll "brake" down...get it, BRAKE instead of BREAK...</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/tumbleweed-resized-600.jpg" alt="tumbleweed" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>...how to manually (pressure) bleed your brakes:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>First, start by making sure your master cylinder reservoir is full of brake fluid.</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/adding brake fluid-resized-600.jpg" alt="adding brake fluid" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the interest of not making a mess, you can get yourself a neat little bleeding tool like this:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/bleeding brakes-resized-600.jpg" alt="bleeding brakes" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The hose fits onto the bleeder screw so that the brake fluid that comes out will go into that reservoir and not on your driveway/garage floor.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Have a friend/neighbor/spouse/sibling/offspring (someone that reach and depress the brake pedal) sit in the car.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>You'll want to start at the wheel furthest away from the master cylinder, which will be the right (passenger) rear.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Have whomever is in the car press and hold the brake pedal, then open your bleeder screw (either on the brake caliper for disc brakes or behind your wheel cylinder for drum brakes) until the pedal is all the way to the floor, then close the bleeder screw.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Have them pump the brake pedal back up three times, and hold it down as you open the bleeder screw again. Do this until you see a steady stream of brake fluid with no bubbles/foam in it.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Repeat the process for the remaining wheels in this order: left rear, right front, left front.</p>
<p><span style="color: #ff0000;">Note: It's a good idea to randomly check the fluid level in your master cylinder reservoir as to not let it go below the "MIN" (minimum) line, because you could then introduce air into the system, negating everything you've been trying to do thus far.</span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>All said and done, you now have a nice, firm brake pedal that doesn't fade away when you hold pressure on the pedal. If the pedal fades off, you've got a leak somewhere. If the pedal doesn't get firm, there's still air in the system or you may have a bad master cylinder.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

	<p>Why should I flush my coolant every 30,000 miles? Can I add just water to my cooling system? The answer to these two questions can be answered with one picture:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Rusty coolant-resized-600.JPG" alt="Rusty coolant" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>You may be thinking that this looks semi-normal, and kind of resembles GM's Dex-Cool (orange) coolant. It's not, on either count. That is rusty water.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We had a 2002 Mercury Sable with a 3.0L V6 OHV (VIN U) and 88k miles in that had a nasty coolant leak from the radiator. Because the coolant level got very low, the engine overheated and blew out one or both of the head gaskets, failing the block test (a test to see if hydrocarbons from the exhaust are making their way into the cooling system).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The assumption here is that the vehicle got low on coolant, the customer didn't have any coolant, so they just threw some water into it each time it got low. Bad deal.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Coolant/antifreeze either has propylene (newer style, less toxic) or ethylene (early style) glycol and corrosion/rust inhibitors, among other additives, which all serve a major role in keeping your cooling system functioning properly.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>So what have we learned here?</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/no water-resized-600.jpg" alt="no water" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Straight water in your cooling system gets you the proverbial slap on the wrist and a good scolding. You just don't want to do it.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Furthermore, some of you may have seen the (hilarious) movie Sex Drive with Seth Greene and few other young actors many of us have never heard of.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1370272471180" src="/Portals/94047/images/sex-drive.jpg" alt="sex drive movie" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There's a scene in the movie where the main character's brother's 1969 Pontiac GTO Judge overheats, and they decide that the best plan of action is to urinate into the radiator to cool it off. Let me just say that this is not a really good idea, either.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>So in review, flush your cooling system at the suggested intervals because the additives in your coolant will break down and stop doing their jobs, never add straight water (except in a pinch, and only to get you to the nearest auto repair shop to have your leak/overheating issue addressed followed immediately by a coolant flush), and don't pee into your radiator.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

	<p>Almost everyone I know has had this happen:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1368469815338" src="/Portals/94047/images/wet cell-resized-600.jpg" alt="wet cell phone" border="0"></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/wet cell 2.jpg" alt="wet cell phone" border="0"></p>

<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/mega rage-resized-600.jpg" alt="mega rage" border="0"></p>

<p>...followed by...</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/sad_face-resized-600.jpg" alt="sad face" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, all is not lost. This can USUALLY be fixed. There is a trick you can try at home to dry out your moisture ridden electronics, whether it's a cell phone, laptop, tablet, or even a part of your car that has a circuit board like a control module.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/mercedes-benz gl450-resized-600.jpg" alt="mercedes benz gl450" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We recently had a 2008 Mercedes-Benz GL450 in with a tail/brake light issue. After some extensive diagnostic and back-probing on different circuits, it was determined that the lighting control module had gotten moisture in it from water running down the rear hatch.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Maybe you've heard of the rice trick? You'll need a few things:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li>Plain white rice</li>
<li>Large plastic tub or Ziplok bag</li>
<li>Patience</li>
<li>Little bit of luck</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We filled a large plastic tub with a lot of white rice.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Rice 1-resized-600.JPG" alt="rice tub" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We then set the control module in the rice and let it sit for a few hours. The longer you can keep the product in the rice, the better your chances are of the rice sucking up all of the moisture in the product. Cell phones can usually be taken apart (rear cover, battery) so do this with all the parts to the product that can be removed and submerge all of the pieces into the rice.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Rice 2-resized-600.JPG" alt="rice tub " border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Be sure you get all of the rice out of any places a tiny piece of rice can get into (shake it out well), and turn the unit on/plug it in to test the operation.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

	<p>We had our second BMW within a month come in with this same problem, so I figured it was worth posting about. The first one was on a 2001 X5, and the second was a 2005 545i, both with a 4.4L V8 engine. This post is about the 545i.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/bmw 545i-resized-600.jpg" alt="bmw 545i" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The aforementioned 545i had 120k miles on it, and a history with us of fluid leaks, usually oil. In this case, our customer brought the car to us with a major coolant leak. When I say major, I mean this engine is spraying coolant out of the front of it with the velocity of a garden hose.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We could tell the leak was directly in the center of the engine in the valley, but in an area that you couldn't tell precisely where it was coming from.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/545i engine-resized-600.JPG" alt="545i Engine" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Side note: This was two weeks ago. See the snow? April. In Minnesota. Neat.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/opener.jpg" alt="opener" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Anyway, since we knew the coolant leak on this BMW was in the valley, we advised the customer that we had to remove the intake manifold and valley cover to see what had happened. Here's what we found:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Bad oring-resized-600.JPG" alt="Crossover pipe oring" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>That is the factory crossover pipe, which has an o-ring on either end of it where it attaches to the block. The o-ring is supposed to be part of the pipe, and it had corroded apart and become disconnected from the crossover pipe, causing the coolant leak.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Further research yielded an interesting and terrifying fact: To repair this at the dealer, the engine is removed and dismantled. We're talking a 15-30 hour job, somewhere around $9,000-$12,000 just in labor alone!</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Luckily for this customer, there is an aftermarket solution available which is far less expensive for the customer and much less time consuming for the technician. The factory crossover pipe is a solid pipe. The aftermarket replacement is a telescoping pipe with a threaded shaft. You basically cut the factory one in half, remove the halves, lay this replacement pipe in the valley, and thread it outword until it seals. All that's left after that is replacement of all the other gaskets and seals that you had to remove to get to the valley area.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There are a few places online where you can said pipe and gasket kit, and here's what the (better) pipe itself looks like:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/n62 crossover pipe-resized-600.jpg" alt="n62 crossover pipe" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Here's part of the kit that we were given by the customer:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Repair kit-resized-600.JPG" alt="repair kit" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>...and what it looked like installed:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1367520963975" src="/Portals/94047/images/Pipe installed-resized-600.JPG" alt="Pipe installed" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>All told, this customer shelled out $2,400 for this repair, which sounds like a lot, but not when compared to the 15+ hours it could've been. It instead was under 8 hours of labor.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/winning-resized-600.jpg" alt="Winning" border="0"></p>

	<p>So let's say you own a 2005-2007 Honda Odyssey Touring minivan like this one:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Odyssey Touring-resized-600.jpg" alt="Odyssey Touring" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Ever gone shopping for tires on it, or even looked at your tire size? We had one of these vans in today with low tread, so I was going to quote out some tires for it. I looked at the sidewall, and then had this look:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Bewildered.jpg" alt="Bewildered" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Right there on the side of the tire, was 235-710R460A. I'm used to seeing something like P235/65R16. As it turns out, this van, along with 2006-2007 Nissan Quest RL &amp; RS models, 2006-2007 Acura RL models, and the Rolls Royce Phantom &amp; Bugatti Veyrons are equipped with this Michelin PAX Tire System.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>~~~&gt; <a href="http://www.michelinman.com/automotive-tires/michelin-pax.page" title="Link to Michelin's page" target="_blank">Link to Michelin's page</a> &lt;~~~</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Basically, it's a self-sealing run-flat tire, so you don't need a spare tire. You can go up to 125 miles (at 55 mph) on a flat tire. These tires are designed to never separate from the rim. Here's what it looks like:</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/PAX System-resized-600.jpg" alt="PAX System" border="0"></p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Pax System 2-resized-600.jpg" alt="PAX System" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>How can you go wrong? I personally think it's pretty neat, but there is a downside. Call your PAX Certified tire facility (find one aside from the dealer, call it a challenge) and ask how much a set of these bad boys are.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This, like the electric car, may become more and more popular and widespread, so costs may eventually come down and we may start seeing this in more and more new cars. This will eliminate dangerous flat tire swap situations on busy interstates, and also virtually eliminate the need for your roadside assistance.</p>

	<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/06-expedition-1-resized-600.jpg" alt="06 Expedition" border="0"><br><br>So, you have a newer (2004-2008) Ford Expedition/F-Series pickup 5.4L V8 and you go out to start it one day and you have a crank no start condition (it just cranks and cranks and cranks, but it won't fire). You turn into Samir from the movie Office Space momentarily. If you haven't seen it, search "Samir Office Space Traffic" on youtube. There's swear words in it, pretty hilariously mashed together ones at that, so there's your warning.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Now, your neighbor, or buddy, or brother in-law "mechanic" tells you it seems like your fuel pump is bad. So, you have said "mechanic" install a fuel pump in your driveway for a semi-hefty fee. You turn the key, and you become Samir again because your truck still won't start.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/ipad_23032_pac_man_keep_calm_and_om_nom-resized-600.jpg" alt="keep calm and om nom" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This was the unfortunate situation one of our customers ran into recently with his 2006 Expedition with 115,562 miles. He'd had someone put a fuel pump in the truck thinking it would fix the problem, but it didn't. He had it towed to us and paid us to diagnose and repair the problem. This truck has been gone for over a week, but I just did a Google search to look for a part location and found that it's a VERY common problem in newer Ford trucks.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The problem? A fuel pump driver module. This is what commands the fuel pump to engage when it's needed. The other problem? It's made of aluminum and mounted in a place where salt sits on the frame and corrodes the bottom side of the module, allowing moisture into the driver module, shorting it out.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Here's where it sits on F-Series trucks:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Driver Location-resized-600.jpg" alt="Driver Module Location" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The part is circled in red, the spare tire cable/mount is circled in blue. Drop the spare tires and you'll see this part. Now, the side that corrodes is the part that's touching the crossmember so you may have to remove the module (2 bolts) to see the any damage. Our Expedition's module was near the fuel tank along the frame rail on the driver's side.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This is what ours looked like from the top:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Fuel Pump Driver-resized-600.JPG" alt="fuel pump driver module top" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>...and underneath:</p>
<p><img src="/Portals/94047/images/Fuel Pump Driver 2-resized-600.JPG" alt="fuel pump driver module bottom" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Let me tell you, that thing smelled TERRIBLE. Similar to how the turn signal module on my Harley smelled when it self-destructed 84 miles from home...but that's another story.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>This fuel pump driver module is available through your local Ford dealership, or some parts stores have the Dorman aftermarket part available.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>How do you prevent this? You can't, really. One thing that may help is regularly washing your truck and making sure the underbody is cleaned as well. Most "Deluxe" car washes at the gas station have an underbody wash. Salt = corrosion. Car wash &gt; salt.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><img id="img-1363904762672" src="/Portals/94047/images/themoreyouknow.jpg" alt="the more you know" border="0"></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
