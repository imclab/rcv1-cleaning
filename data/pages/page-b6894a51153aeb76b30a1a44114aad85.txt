		<h2><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/b/2013/08/27/snafu-day.htm" zT="1/1jS">Snafu Days - My Bookish Life</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Tuesday August 27, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry"><p><img class="alignright" src="http://0.tqn.com/d/classiclit/1/0/s/o/2/iStock_000001570016XJosepArdiacaRodriguez.jpg" alt="Book" height="200" />I woke up this morning thinking about a quote from <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_marktwain.htm" zT="1/1UH">Mark Twain</a>: "Good friends, good books and a sleepy conscience: this is the ideal life."</p>

<p>I guess it was one of those days, when thoughts like that pop to mind--in the sleepy half-awake moments, as I stare over at my rows of books and begin to wonder what the day will bring me.</p>

<p>Of course, seconds after that be-dazed wondering, I realized that I hadn't heard the alarm (set to play NPR). I also hadn't heard the back-up alarm. And, my kid was minutes away from being late for school. On days like this, there's really no time to browse through a book, or have poetic musings. It's all about: kids up, dressed, breakfasted, given meds, teeth brushed... then there's the mad-dash out the door. It <em>really</em> would not do to be late on the Friday of the first week of school!</p>

<p>In case you're curious to know... we made it with time to spare.</p>

<p><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_marktwain.htm" zT="1/1UH">Mark Twain</a> wrote in his autobiography: "It is human life. We are blown upon the world; we float buoyantly upon the summer air a little while, complacently showing off our grace of form and our dainty iridescent colors; then we vanish with a little puff, leaving nothing behind but a memory--and sometimes not even that..."</p>

<p>Stafu days--they happen. Have you felt like you're in the midst of a whirlwind--just tossed around? How do you get through it?</p></div>

		<h2><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/b/2013/08/26/the-color-yellow.htm" zT="1/1jS">The Yellow Wallpaper</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Monday August 26, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry"><p><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/yellowwallpapergilman/gr/The-Yellow-Wallpaper-Summary.htm" zT="1/1UH"><img class="alignright" src="http://0.tqn.com/h/classiclit/1/3/b/q/2/YellowWallpaperCover.jpg" alt="The Yellow Wallpaper" width="121" height="200" /></a><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_cpgilman.htm" zT="1/1UH">Charlotte Perkins Gilman</a> writes: "While we flatter ourselves that things remain the same, they are  changing under our very eyes from year to year, from day to day."</p>

<p>In <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/yellowwallpapergilman/gr/The-Yellow-Wallpaper-Summary.htm" zT="1/1UH">The Yellow Wallpaper</a>, <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_cpgilman.htm" zT="1/1UH">Gilman</a> draws from personal experience, locked in a yellow-papered room, without the saving graces of writing or reading. Loneliness and boredom give way to paranoia and psychosis. This work of Gothic fiction is a series of journal entries, written in an epistolary style. The story is an early example of feminist literature, condemning the androcentric hegemony in the medical profession.</p>

<p>Trapped in a locked room with yellow wallpaper, the walls come alive. <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_cpgilman.htm" zT="1/1UH">Gilman</a> <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/yellowwallpapergilman/a/The-Yellow-Wallpaper-Quotes.htm" zT="1/1UH">writes</a>: "There are things in that wallpaper that nobody knows about but me, or ever will."</p>

<p>The young female narrator finally finds escape, with her own insanity (as the mad woman in the attic in <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/janeeyre/fr/aa_janeeyre.htm" zT="1/1UH">Jane Eyre</a>). In the end, she accepts her solitary confinement. "For outside you have to creep on the ground, and everything is green  instead of yellow. But here I can creep smoothly on the floor, and my  shoulder just fits in that long smooch around the wall, so I cannot lose  my way."</p>

<p><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_cpgilman.htm" zT="1/1UH">Gilman</a> calls for recovery, for health, and for survival. She was able to rise above the "rest cure." She left the narrator in the yellow room, but <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/cs/profileswriters/p/aa_cpgilman.htm" zT="1/1UH">Gilman</a> walked out of the room. She escaped her depression, isolation and loneliness. She left her marriage. And, she embraced work. As she <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/yellowwallpapergilman/a/Why-I-Wrote-The-Yellow-Wallpaper.htm" zT="1/1UH">explains</a>, "I cast the noted specialist's advice to the winds and went to work  again--work, the normal life of every human being; work, in which is joy  and growth and service, without which one is a pauper and a  parasite--ultimately recovering some measure of power."</p>

<p>Now, she offers escape for others...</p>

		<h2><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/b/2013/08/26/succeed-in-your-literature-class-heres-how.htm" zT="1/1jS">Succeed in Your Literature Class? Here's how...</a></h2>

		<h2><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/b/2013/08/24/jane-eyre-arrives-on-the-literary-scene.htm" zT="1/1jS">Jane Eyre Arrives on the Literary Scene</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Saturday August 24, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry"><p><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/bbooktitles/fr/aafpr_bronte.htm" zT="1/1UH"><img src="http://classiclit.about.com/library/graphics/0375412778_bronte.gif" alt="Bronte - romance literature" align="right" /></a><a href="http://classiclit.about.com/library/bl-etexts/cbronte/bl-cbronte-jan-pre.htm" zT="1/1UH">Jane Eyre</a> is still a great favorite to readers around the world. That's why it's interesting to note that on August 24, 1847, Charlotte Bronte sent off her famous work to the publishing house, Smith Elder &amp; Company. She was using the assumed name of Currer Bell, and the work would elicit strong feelings and controversy.</p>

<p>To learn more about <em>Jane Eyre</em>, read the <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/library/bl-etexts/cbronte/bl-cbronte-jan-pre.htm" zT="1/1UH">complete text</a>, discover <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/janeeyre/tp/aatp_janeeyre.htm" zT="1/1UH">top books</a>, learn about <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/library/bl-etexts/egaskell/bl-egaskell-cbronte-1.htm" zT="1/1UH">The Life of Charlotte Brontë</a>, and take the <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/library/bl-quiz/romantic/bronte_c/janeeyre/bl-start.htm" zT="1/1UH">Jane Eyre Quiz</a>.</p>

<p>Also take a look at a few <a href="http://classiclit.about.com/od/janeeyre/a/aa_janeeyrequ.htm" zT="1/1UH">quotes</a> from the novel. And, join our discussion: <a href="http://forums.about.com/ab-classiclit/messages?lgnF=y&amp;msg=844.1" zT="1/1UH">Favorite Quote from Jane Eyre</a>.</p></div>
