                        <h1><a href="http://autodesign.com/2012/01/history-car-design-history-by-the-people-who-wrote-it/" class="title">REARVIEW | Car Design History by the People Who Wrote It</a><a href="http://autodesign.com/2012/01/history-car-design-history-by-the-people-who-wrote-it/#respond" class="comment_counter"  title="Comment on REARVIEW | Car Design History by the People Who Wrote It">0</a></h1>
                        <h3>Posted In <span><a href="http://autodesign.com/category/car-culture/" title="View all posts in Car Culture" rel="category tag">Car Culture</a>,<a href="http://autodesign.com/category/uncategorized/" title="View all posts in Uncategorized" rel="category tag">Uncategorized</a></span> by <a href="http://autodesign.com/author/ryan/" title="Posts by Ryan Paul" rel="author">Ryan Paul</a></h3>
                    </div><!--  END "title_holder"  //-->
                    <div class="entry_content">
                             <p><img class="alignnone" src="http://deansgarage.com/wp-content/gallery/cache/1019__650x_irv-semple-aicover.jpg" alt="" width="554" height="408" /></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>While digging up some history for an upcoming post, I came across this amazing set of interviews, all done in the mid-eighties by the <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/">Automobile in American Life and Society</a> project at the University of Michigan.  Transcripts of sort-of-meandering conversations filled with fascinating bites of the history of American car design straight from the mouths of the people who directed it.  There&#8217;s  <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/Design/Mitchell/mitchellinterview.htm">Bill Mitchell</a>, who succeeded Harley Earl at GM, and <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/Design/Rybicki_interview.htm">Irv Rybicki</a> who followed him.  <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/Design/Bordinat_interview.htm">Gene Bordinat&#8217;s</a>, who ran post-war Ford&#8217;s design, excuse me: <em>styling</em> department for 19 years is particularly interesting (and long), full of anecdotes about the inner workings of the upper echelon of Ford Motor Company in the &#8217;50s and &#8217;60s. Plenty of others, too, like  <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/Design/Exner_interview.htm">Virgil Exner, Jr. </a>talking about both his and his father&#8217;s careers and a lot of first-person stories about working for Raymond Loewy, and  <a href="http://www.autolife.umd.umich.edu/Design/Buehrig_interview.htm">Gordon Buehrig</a>, who talks extensively about the processes involved in coachbuilt cars like the Duesenbergs and Auburns he was responsible for, complete with a great little one about how Auburn&#8217;s chief engineer tuned their eight-carb V-12 in the dark, by checking the color of the flames coming out of the exhaust.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There&#8217;s just a ton of fascinating history in these stories, and even though they&#8217;re clunky in their presentation, and quite long, they&#8217;re worth every word. If you have any interest in design history, you should check &#8216;em out.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
