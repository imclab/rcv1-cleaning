<p><b>A&amp;E</b> (an <a href="/wiki/Acronym#Nomenclature" title="Acronym">initialism</a> for its former name, the <b>Arts &amp; Entertainment Network</b><sup id="cite_ref-Carmody.2C_John_1995_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carmody.2C_John_1995-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>), is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a> channel that serves as the flagship television property of <a href="/wiki/A%2BE_Networks" title="A+E Networks">A+E Networks</a>, a joint venture between the <a href="/wiki/Hearst_Corporation" title="Hearst Corporation">Hearst Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> (both of which maintain a 50% ownership interest). The channel is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and operates offices in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago, Illinois</a>; <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit, Michigan</a>; <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London, England</a>; <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stamford,_Connecticut" title="Stamford, Connecticut">Stamford, Connecticut</a>. International versions of A&amp;E are also available in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>.</p>
<p>The channel, which originally focused programming on <a href="/wiki/Biography" title="Biography">biographies</a>, documentaries, and <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> series (especially crime dramas and mysteries), has expanded to include <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a> programming; A&amp;E's programming reaches more than 85 million homes in the United States and Canada.</p>
<p>As of August 2013, approximately 98,302,000 American households (86.08% of households with television) receive the A&amp;E channel.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1984.E2.80.932002">1984–2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1984–2002">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1984–2002" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A&amp;E launched on February 1, 1984, initially available to 9.3 million <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> homes in the U.S. and Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The network is a result of the 1984 merger of Hearst/<a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Repertory_Television_Service" title="Alpha Repertory Television Service">Alpha Repertory Television Service</a> (ARTS) and (pre–<a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> merger) <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">RCA</a>-owned The Entertainment Channel.<sup id="cite_ref-freud_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freud-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> When A&amp;E debuted, the channel took over the satellite transponder timeslot that ARTS occupied from its launch in 1981. Children's television channel <a href="/wiki/Nickelodeon" title="Nickelodeon">Nickelodeon</a> signed off just before 9 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">Eastern Time</a>, and A&amp;E took over at 9 p.m. with a three-hour programming block, which was repeated at 9 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Time_Zone" title="Pacific Time Zone">Pacific Time</a>. In January 1985, A&amp;E moved to its own dedicated transponder and began delivering its programming 24 hours a day, while Nickelodeon replaced the vacated A&amp;E programming with a classic television block called <a href="/wiki/Nick_at_Nite" title="Nick at Nite">Nick at Nite</a> on July 1 of that year. However, some cable providers continued to carry Nickelodeon and A&amp;E on the same channel and would usually switch over A&amp;E at 8 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">Eastern</a>. It was not until the early 1990s that these companies found separate channels for both networks.</p>
<p>A&amp;E was envisioned as a commercial counterpart to PBS, and in its early days focused on such PBS-style programming as the Leonard Bernstein <i><a href="/wiki/Fidelio" title="Fidelio">Fidelio</a></i>, filmed in 1978. Later it began to add programming originally seen on commercial networks, such as reruns of <i><a href="/wiki/Columbo_(TV_series)" title="Columbo (TV series)" class="mw-redirect">Columbo</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Breaking_Away_(TV_series)" title="Breaking Away (TV series)">Breaking Away</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Quincy_(TV_series)" title="Quincy (TV series)" class="mw-redirect">Quincy</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Equalizer" title="The Equalizer">The Equalizer</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order" title="Law &amp; Order">Law &amp; Order</a></i>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Court" title="Night Court">Night Court</a></i>. Highbrow British mysteries including <i><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie%27s_Poirot" title="Agatha Christie's Poirot">Agatha Christie's Poirot</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Cracker_(UK_TV_series)" title="Cracker (UK TV series)">Cracker</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Dalziel_and_Pascoe_(BBC_TV_series)" title="Dalziel and Pascoe (BBC TV series)">Dalziel and Pascoe</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Inspector_Morse_(TV_series)" title="Inspector Morse (TV series)">Inspector Morse</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Lovejoy" title="Lovejoy">Lovejoy</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Midsomer_Murders" title="Midsomer Murders">Midsomer Murders</a></i>, the Joan Hickson <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Marple" title="Miss Marple">Miss Marple</a></i> series and <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(1984_TV_series)" title="Sherlock Holmes (1984 TV series)">Sherlock Holmes</a></i> were also featured; several of these series were produced in association with A&amp;E. By 1990, A&amp;E's original programming accounted for 35 to 40 percent of the network's program content.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A&amp;E's signature show was <i><a href="/wiki/Biography_(TV_series)" title="Biography (TV series)">Biography</a></i>, a one-hour documentary series that A&amp;E revived in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1994, airings of <i>Biography</i> went from weekly broadcasts to airing five nights a week, which helped boost A&amp;E's ratings to record levels.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The nightly series became A&amp;E's top-rated show and one of cable television's most notable successes.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Biography</i> received primetime <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a> in 1999 and 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1995, the channel's name officially changed to the A&amp;E Network,<sup id="cite_ref-Carmody.2C_John_1995_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carmody.2C_John_1995-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> to reflect its declining focus on arts and entertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1997, the network had branded itself as simply A&amp;E, and was using the slogans "Time Well Spent" and "Escape the Ordinary."</p>
<p>"The word 'arts,' in regard to television, has associations such as 'sometimes elitist,' 'sometimes boring,' 'sometimes overly refined' and 'doesn't translate well to TV,'" Whitney Goit, executive vice president for sales and marketing, stated. "Even the arts patron often finds arts on TV not as satisfying as it should be … And the word 'entertainment' is too vague. Therefore, much like ESPN uses its letters rather than what they stand for – Entertainment Sports (Programming) Network – we decided to go to just A&amp;E." Of the network's tagline, Goit said, "Intellectually, 'Time well spent' defines a comparison between those who view a lot of television as a wasteland, and their acknowledgment that there are good things on TV and that they'd like to watch more thought-provoking TV."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A&amp;E commissioned <i><a href="/wiki/Hornblower_(TV_series)" title="Hornblower (TV series)">Horatio Hornblower</a></i> (1999), winner of two Emmy Awards, and the seven subsequent dramas in the series; <i><a href="/wiki/Dash_and_Lilly" title="Dash and Lilly">Dash and Lilly</a></i> (1999), which received nine Emmy nominations; and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crossing_(2000_film)" title="The Crossing (2000 film)">The Crossing</a></i> (2000), which won the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a>. The network created two original weekly drama series, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lumet" title="Sidney Lumet">Sidney Lumet</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/100_Centre_Street" title="100 Centre Street">100 Centre Street</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Nero_Wolfe_Mystery" title="A Nero Wolfe Mystery">Nero Wolfe</a></i> (both of which lasted from 2001 to 2002).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002.E2.80.93present">2002–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2002–present">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2002–present" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the summer of 2002, A&amp;E underwent an overhaul in management which moved the network's focus toward <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a> in order to attract a younger demographic<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> and cancelled the network's two original scripted series. In May 2003, A&amp;E launched a marketing campaign with the network's new tagline, "The Art of Entertainment."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Between <a href="/wiki/2003_in_television" title="2003 in television">2003</a> and <a href="/wiki/2007_in_television" title="2007 in television">2007</a>, the channel gradually retired several long-running series, moving its classic mysteries to <a href="/wiki/The_Biography_Channel" title="The Biography Channel">The Biography Channel</a> and cancelling <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_with_the_Arts" title="Breakfast with the Arts">Breakfast with the Arts</a></i>, in favor of reality programming such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dog_the_Bounty_Hunter" title="Dog the Bounty Hunter">Dog the Bounty Hunter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gene_Simmons_Family_Jewels" title="Gene Simmons Family Jewels">Gene Simmons Family Jewels</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Growing_Up_Gotti" title="Growing Up Gotti">Growing Up Gotti</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Family_Plots" title="Family Plots">Family Plots</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Airline_(UK_TV_series)" title="Airline (UK TV series)">Airline</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Inked" title="Inked">Inked</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_Cars" title="King of Cars">King of Cars</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Criss_Angel_Mindfreak" title="Criss Angel Mindfreak">Criss Angel Mindfreak</a></i>. In addition, A&amp;E had garnered favorable notice for true-crime documentary series such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cold_Case_Files" title="Cold Case Files">Cold Case Files</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Justice" title="American Justice">American Justice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/City_Confidential" title="City Confidential">City Confidential</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Investigative_Reports&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Investigative Reports (page does not exist)">Investigative Reports</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_First_48" title="The First 48">The First 48</a></i>. The network also cut back on its broadcasts of <i><a href="/wiki/Biography_(TV_series)" title="Biography (TV series)">Biography</a></i> from originally twice daily to weekend mornings only.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The changes were criticized as causing A&amp;E to become an aberration of its original focus on <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine arts</a> programming. For example, <a href="/wiki/Maury_Chaykin" title="Maury Chaykin">Maury Chaykin</a> reflected on the cancellation of the A&amp;E original series <i><a href="/wiki/A_Nero_Wolfe_Mystery" title="A Nero Wolfe Mystery">A Nero Wolfe Mystery</a></i> in a 2008 interview: "I'm a bit jaded and cynical about which shows succeed on television. I worked on a fantastic show once called <i>Nero Wolfe</i>, but at the time A&amp;E was transforming from the premiere intellectual cable network in America to one that airs <i><a href="/wiki/Dog_the_Bounty_Hunter" title="Dog the Bounty Hunter">Dog the Bounty Hunter</a></i> on repeat, so it was never promoted and eventually went off the air."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A&amp;E's most-watched program was the <a href="/wiki/Docudrama" title="Docudrama">docudrama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_93_(TV_film)" title="Flight 93 (TV film)">Flight 93</a></i>, about the hijacking of the plane which crashed in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> during the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Media_Research" title="Nielsen Media Research">Nielsen</a>, the program attracted 5.9 million viewers for its initial telecast on January 30, 2006. The previous record-holder for the network was a <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> docudrama, <i>Ike: Countdown to D-Day</i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Selleck" title="Tom Selleck">Tom Selleck</a> and broadcast in <a href="/wiki/2004_in_television" title="2004 in television">2004</a>, with 5.5 million viewers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> A&amp;E later acquired rights to rerun the critically acclaimed <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sopranos" title="The Sopranos">The Sopranos</a></i>; its A&amp;E premiere on January 10, 2007, averaged 3.86 million viewers, making it the most-watched premiere of a rerun off-network series in cable television history.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The series has continued to perform well for A&amp;E, and the network now regularly ranks in the top ten basic U.S. cable channels in prime time ratings.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">May 26</a>, 2008 in conjunction with the premiere of the original film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain_(TV_miniseries)" title="The Andromeda Strain (TV miniseries)">The Andromeda Strain</a></i>, A&amp;E rebranded with a new logo and slogan, <i>Real Life. Drama.</i>, with the new logo being an altered version of the older logo, along with a larger <a href="/wiki/Ampersand" title="Ampersand">ampersand</a> equal to the letters.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time, A&amp;E began a heavy cross-promotional campaign of on-screen graphics promoting other programs as well as various features of the A&amp;E website. Each segment of each program aired on A&amp;E now contains one program and one website cross-promotional graphic.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As part of its continuing efforts to include more scripted shows, A&amp;E ordered several dramas for Fall 2009. Among them were projects from <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Bruckheimer" title="Jerry Bruckheimer">Jerry Bruckheimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Ryan" title="Shawn Ryan">Shawn Ryan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Obst" title="Lynda Obst">Lynda Obst</a>, and a western miniseries from <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a>. Several unscripted series were also renewed or ordered for fall, including <i><a href="/wiki/Intervention_(TV_series)" title="Intervention (TV series)">Intervention</a></i>, <i>The First 48</i>, <i>Gene Simmons Family Jewels</i>, <i>Dog the Bounty Hunter</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Crime_360" title="Crime 360">Crime 360</a></i>, <i>Criss Angel Mindfreak</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Paranormal_State" title="Paranormal State">Paranormal State</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Manhunters" title="Manhunters">Manhunters</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Storage_Wars_(TV_series)" title="Storage Wars (TV series)" class="mw-redirect">Storage Wars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parking_Wars" title="Parking Wars">Parking Wars</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Shipping_Wars" title="Shipping Wars">Shipping Wars</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Live_Feed_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Live_Feed-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2009, it was reported that A&amp;E Television Networks was in discussions to acquire <a href="/wiki/Lifetime_(TV_network)" title="Lifetime (TV network)">Lifetime</a> (then jointly owned by two of A&amp;E's then-three corporate parents, Hearst and Disney).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The transaction was eventually consummated on August 27, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, A&amp;E introduced a brand new series called <i>Beyond Scared Straight</i>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Scared_Straight!" title="Scared Straight!">original 1978 documentary film</a>, which follows troubled juveniles on a jail tour, followed by updates on their post-prison behavior. In July 2012, <a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a> sold its 15% stake in network parent A+E Networks to Hearst and Disney (which each owned a 42.5% interest in the company), making the two companies 50/50 partners in the joint venture.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming">Programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Programming">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A%26E_(TV_channel)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Programming" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_programs_broadcast_by_A%26E_Network" title="List of programs broadcast by A&amp;E Network">List of programs broadcast by A&amp;E Network</a></div>
<p>A&amp;E is presently known for reality series such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dog_the_Bounty_Hunter" title="Dog the Bounty Hunter">Dog the Bounty Hunter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Intervention_(TV_series)" title="Intervention (TV series)">Intervention</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Storage_Wars" title="Storage Wars">Storage Wars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Duck_Dynasty" title="Duck Dynasty">Duck Dynasty</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Criss_Angel_Mindfreak" title="Criss Angel Mindfreak">Criss Angel Mindfreak</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paranormal_State" title="Paranormal State">Paranormal State</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> It also broadcasts a limited amount of scripted original programming including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glades" title="The Glades">The Glades</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bates_Motel" title="Bates Motel">Bates Motel</a></i>.</p>
<p>In its original format, the network had often shown programming from abroad, particularly <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> network productions from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-freud_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freud-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Examples of British programming frequently broadcast on the channel included the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Freud_(mini-series)" title="Freud (mini-series)" class="mw-redirect">Freud</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-freud_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freud-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the broadcast of British programming on A&amp;E has diminished greatly since it began incorporating more reality shows onto its schedule. For example, the network waited almost a year and a half to show the fourth series of <i><a href="/wiki/Spooks" title="Spooks">Spooks</a></i>, retitled as <i>MI-5</i>, first airing it after prime time on Friday nights at 11 p.m. <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">Eastern</a>, before pulling the series after only two episodes, it later aired the rest of the season's episodes in a day-long marathon on October 21, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Its fine arts programming have also been completely removed from the channel's schedule. Thursday nights once featured an anthology series called <i>A&amp;E Stage</i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Grimes" title="Tammy Grimes">Tammy Grimes</a> and later <a href="/wiki/John_Mauceri" title="John Mauceri">John Mauceri</a>, which featured telecasts of notable plays, concerts, full-length documentaries related to the arts, and complete operas, although shown with commercials. Such programs as <a href="/wiki/Otto_Schenk" title="Otto Schenk">Otto Schenk</a>'s 1978 production of <i>Fidelio</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a> conducting, were rebroadcast on this anthology, as well as an adaption of <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Spider%27s_Web_(play)" title="Spider's Web (play)">Spider's Web</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Keith" title="Penelope Keith">Penelope Keith</a>, originally broadcast in the UK on December 26, 1982. The final fine arts-related show to air on the network, <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_with_the_Arts" title="Breakfast with the Arts">Breakfast with the Arts</a></i>, once featured a higher quantity of classical music than in its final years, and fewer interviews. The show was cancelled in July 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><b>A&amp;E HD</b> is a <a href="/wiki/720p" title="720p">720p</a> <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high definition</a> <a href="/wiki/Simulcast" title="Simulcast">simulcast</a> feed of A&amp;E that launched on September 4, 2006. It has found a home on many cable and satellite systems in Canada but was until recently rare in the U.S. However, A&amp;E HD is in the process of becoming more widely available in the U.S., since Comcast, the largest US cable provider, is expanding the number of systems including A&amp;E HD in its lineup in preparation of greater competition from HD satellite service. A&amp;E HD is notable for being one of several cable networks stretching standard definition content horizontally to fill the display area of widescreen HDTVs, rather than preserving the original aspect ratio of 4:3.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
