              <div class="content">
      <div class="field-body">
                             <div>Whether you’re already car-free or just car-free curious, seeing Diane Meyer’s new photo exhibit, <strong><a target="_blank" href="http://18thstreet.org/almost%20utopia/bob%20sane/wanted.html">Without a Car in the World <em>(100 Car-less Angelinos Tell Stories of Living in Los Angeles)</em></a></strong>, is sure to be revelatory.</div>
<div> </div>
<div><em>Without a Car</em> pairs photo portraits of 100 L.A.-area residents (including me!) with brief quotes from their interviews about car-free living. Far from a simple hurrah for automobile-free living, the exhibit features interviews both from those who are proud of their car-free lifestyles to others who sound deeply unhappy about a car-lessness that’s been imposed on them, whether due to financial concerns, disability, or other reasons.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>
<div id="photoImgDiv4026103651" style="width: 502px;" class="photoImgDiv"><img width="500" height="252" alt="IMG_7909 by you." title="" onload="show_notes_initially();" class="reflect" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2545/4026103651_b0fdcba07d.jpg" /></div>
</div>
<div> </div>
<div>In fact, the juxtaposition of these points of view is what makes <em>Without a Car </em>especially poignant. One Angeleno talks about how taking public transportation’s so much easier and convenient than people think it is. Another expounds on the difficulties of getting around by bus — how long it takes, how unreliable the system seems.</div>
<div> </div>
<div><em>Without a Car</em> shows a diverse group of Angelenos from all walks of life, and the simple, short quotes from the car-free people quite effectively expose and explore L.A.’s race and class issues, whether it’s a mother who rents a car because her daughter’s too embarrassed to show up to take a standardized test via the bus, or a girl who proudly rides her unicycle in Santa Monica.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>
<div id="photoImgDiv4026103367" style="width: 502px;" class="photoImgDiv"><img width="500" height="375" alt="IMG_7907 by you." title="" onload="show_notes_initially();" class="reflect" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2745/4026103367_09e6dc056c.jpg" /></div>
</div>
<div> </div>
<div>What the exhibit makes clear is that going car-free is an extremely individual experience — and that race and class play heavily into how pleasant that experience is going to be. It’s one thing to be a relatively lucky freelance writer who first moved to a nice part of Santa Monica where everything — including an Enterprise rental office — is within walking distance before getting rid of her car (that’s me — although I now have a car again). It’s quite another to be a teenager from a working-class family living in a relatively dangerous part of L.A., who has no choice but to transfer on multiple buses to get to school and work before relying on a late night, infrequent bus line to get her back home.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>The artist Diane Meyer, who’s an assistant professor of photography at Loyola Marymount University, is herself car-free. See the exhibit yourself at the 18th Street Arts Center, 1639 18th St., Santa Monica, before it closes on Dec. 11, 2009. A number of public programs — including a discussion about walking in L.A. with DJ Waldie and a panel discussion on the future of transportation in L.A. are planned. Check the <a href="http://18thstreet.org/almost%20utopia/bob%20sane/wanted.html">18th Street Arts Center calendar</a> for details, as dates and times of the events — including the panel I’m on, currently planned for Nov. 14 — will likely change.</div>
