<p>What are <b>Homeschool language arts lesson plans based on literature?</b></p><p>What does it look like and how do you write them?</p><p><b>Learning Language arts through literature is using literature as a base to teach handwriting, spelling, writing techniques, grammar and punctuation</b>. This is done by selecting a model of excellent literature and drawing all the aspects of language arts from this. This can be used as a complete homeschool language arts curriculum which you create.</p>
<div class="half-width-box-right">
<h2 style="text-align: center">Ten Day Outline</h2>
<ul><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#copywork" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Copywork</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#spelling" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Spelling</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#grammar" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Grammar</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#dictation" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Dictation</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#keyword" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">KeyWord Outline</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#words" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Working with Words</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#style" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Working with Style</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#proofreading" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Proof-reading</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#rewriting" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Re-writing</a></li><li style="text-align: left;"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-literature.html#finalcopy" onclick="return FIX.track(this);">Final Copy</a> - Present and Publish</li></ul>

<p>In order to write Language Arts lesson plans based on literature, you first need to select the finest literature as a model. An excerpt from a childrenâs classic book is a wonderful place to begin. What is a classic?</p><p>It can be defined as a book which enriches the childâs imagination, stirs the emotions, and places them in another time and place. It is one which lives out the lives, thoughts, motives and dilemmas of interesting characters. It is one which is written beautifully, creating characters of depth, places of wonder, and worlds that are dreamt about. It is a classic when it deserves to read and re-read, and enjoyed each time.</p><p><b>Children's classic</b> books are a model of excellent literature;<br/>they model for us what good writing should look like; they model depth of character; they model exciting plots and they use a range of interesting literary devices. For this reason, the classic books&#xa0;are an excellent starting point for any and every author.</p>
<div class="ImageBlock ImageBlockCenter"><a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-the-hobbit.html" onclick="window.open('http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/language-arts-lesson-plans-the-hobbit.html'); return false;" title="Go to Language Arts Lesson Plans The Hobbit - literature for writing lessons"><img src="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/images/001the-hobbit.jpg" width="470" alt="language arts lesson plans" title="language arts lesson plans"></a></div>
<p>In choosing literature to use as a model, I would encourage you not to choose a random excerpt, but rather <b>choose a book as a whole</b>. Read the book aloud, savour and enjoy it. Then, and only then, should you look at the wonderful devices you have found in the book which you love.</p><p>There's nothing worse than reading one good passage from a book and pulling it apart without knowing it in its entirety. I would hate to ruin excellent literature by doing this and I know that from my own schooling, our English Literature studies was far more about pulling an excerpt apart and critiquing it, without ever enjoying it.</p><p>Please, take care not to do this! How? <b>Enjoy it first!</b> Read it; have fun with the character voices; live with the characters; laugh and cry with them. Then, challenge your child to look at the writing in "wonder."</p><p>"How did Mr Tolkien write so well? Let's see how he did it and let's try to copy what he did!"</p><p>Once you have enjoyed the book, then choose some pieces of literature from it. Choose them for their literary devices (descriptions, poetry, use of dialogue, use of metaphors).</p><p><b>Now....</b> how do you write language arts lesson plans based on these excerpts?</p><p>I have written them as "Days", but sometimes the work involved in the language arts activities will take longer than a day.</p>
<a name="copywork"></a>
<h2>Day One: Copywork</h2>
<p>Copy the selection carefully. Use cursive or printing. Decorate it if you wish.</p>
<a name="spelling"></a>
<h2>Day Two: Spelling</h2>
<p>Choose appropriate spelling words from the copied paragraph or selection. List spelling rules which the words follow. Find&#xa0;other words which also use the same rule.</p>
<a name="grammar"></a>
<h2>Day Three: Grammar</h2>
<p>Discuss the grammar of the passage. Use different colours to identify nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs and so on.</p><p>You may also wish to diagram the sentences.</p><p>Does the author use prepositional phrases? Write the grammar definitions. Also look at the punctuation in the passage. (How is the dialogue punctuated? How does the author write a list? How is each sentence begun and finished?)</p>
<a name="dictation"></a>
<h2>Day Four : Dictation</h2>
<p>Use the copywork from Day One as a dictation exercise.</p>
<a name="keyword"></a>
<h2>Day Five: KeyWord Outline</h2>
<p>Remove all the flowery, descriptive parts out of the passage so you are left with the barebones of the passage.</p><p>Write an outline of the plot or description. Use only a few keywords to capture what the main points are.</p>
<a name="words"></a>
<h2>Day Six: Working with Words</h2>
<p>Now, build on the keyword outline from Day Five. Add interesting verbs, dual verbs, nouns, pronouns, adjectives, adverbs. Create an interest filled paragraph. You can either choose to stay as close to the original as possible, or choose to make it into your own creative work - using your own characters etc.</p>
<a name="style"></a>
<h2>Day Seven: Working with Style</h2>
<p>Review your paragraph from Day Six and focus on improving sentence openers, using prepositional phrases, using adverbial&#xa0;and adjectival clauses, using metaphors and similes. You may add dialogue.</p>
<a name="proofreading"></a>
<h2>Day Eight: Proofreading</h2>
<p>Review your work. Does it need refining? Have you chosen words rich with meaning? Does it create a picture in your reader's mind? Check punctuation of sentences?</p>
<a name="rewriting"></a>
<h2>Day Nine: Re-writing</h2>
<p>Copy the final paragraph neatly in cursive or printing. Where will you display this? Do you have a special folder or book?</p>
<a name="finalcopy"></a>
<h2>Day Ten: Final Copy</h2>
<p>Show your final copy. Read it aloud to your family. Publish it. Why not add it to the <a href="http://www.design-your-homeschool.com/kids-writing.html">Homeschool Gallery</a> on my site?&#xa0;</p><p>We'd love to see your work.</p>
