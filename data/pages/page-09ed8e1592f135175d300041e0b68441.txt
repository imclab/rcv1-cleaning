									<div class="entry-content">
										<p>Scripts are generally easier to read than novels or essays. Maybe that’s part of the reason that “Waiting for Godot” by Samuel Beckett and “No Exit” by Jean-Paul Sartre have proved so influential in the United States.<!--p:BC body copy 1st graph--></p>
<p>If you studied French, those scripts were proof that you had finally learned enough to read something important, something with intellectual heft, something “real.”</p>
<p>And if they seemed more like philosophical treatises than playable theater, you were probably inclined to blame yourself and your subpar French.</p>
<p>At the St. Louis Actors’ Studio, director Bobby Miller stages “Waiting for Godot” in English, of course. It is still a challenge; among the things that this “play about nothing” lacks are points of emotional entry.</p>
<p>But Miller wrings every ounce of vitality and bleak humor from this story of two bums waiting for someone who never shows up. We can guess who it is, however: Just look at that name.</p>
<p>As the bums Estragon and Vladimir, Terry Meddows and Gary Wayne Barker hint at a luckless, old-movie duo. Laurel and Hardy? It’s not out of the question. Meddows looks hollowed-out, blank behind the eyes, while Barker, a bigger man, seems puffy, like a balloon. (This is especially obvious when, in a hurry to relieve himself, Vladimir practically sails right off the stage.)</p>
<p>The sadness that often shadows those old comedies, with characters whose meager ambitions are compassed by a place to sleep or something to eat, is here as well. But as they wait, the bums fill their time with small tasks. Estragon tries to remove a painfully tight boot; Vladimir frets about sleeping arrangements; they consider suicide.</p>
<p>Eager for the presence of anyone else at all, they are thrilled by the arrival of Pozzo (Greg Johnston, the arrogant personification of entitlement) who treats his servant, the ironically named Lucky (Aaron Orion Baker), like a dog — if you hated dogs. There’s also a boy (Hayden Benbenek) who carries messages from Godot. The news isn’t good.</p>
<p>Written in the 1940s, the same decade that produced “No Exit,” “Waiting for Godot” is shadowed by war, by Auschwitz and Hiroshima, by a profound sense of loss and abandonment. Yet the bums have each other, and their moment-to-moment way of living has led Miller to a brilliant approach to the play.</p>
<p>Taken as a whole, it’s pretty grim. But Miller clearly seems to have approached “Godot” as a series of short beats — tiny plays that address only one subject at a time. There’s a boot play, a relieve-yourself play, a brutal-master play, a messenger play. None is exactly a joy, but none is too much to bear by itself. Miller’s pacing lets us appreciate the bums’ resolve because he never makes them carry more weight than they can manage.</p>
<p>By the end, when Estragon and Vladimir are alone beneath the tree that is their entire world (impressively fashioned and lit by Patrick Huber), they can barely recall events that have just passed. Forgetfulness is one of their few blessings; another is the bond between them.</p>
<p>Maybe “Godot” is the opposite of “No Exit”: Life is other people.</p>
