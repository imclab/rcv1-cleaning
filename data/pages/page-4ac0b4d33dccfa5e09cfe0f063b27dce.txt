    <p class="i1">
        
<span class="dateline"><a href="http://www.bing.com/maps/?v=2&where1=GEORGETOWN, Ky.&sty=h&form=msdate" target="_blank">GEORGETOWN, Ky.</a>&#160;&#8212; </span>Later this year, Toyota could overthrow General Motors to become king of the world's automakers by selling more vehicles than the once-mighty American giant. 
    </p>
    
<p>Analysts say the keys to Toyota’s success aren't just design and reliability, but the culture that exists inside their plants. </p>
<p>The cars may be high-tech at Toyota, but employees like Howard Artrip are expected to find simpler ways to build them.</p>
<p>One idea is using plastic totes to separate parts by the car model, not by the type of part.</p>
<p>“We actually bought these from Wal-Mart down the street," says Artrip.</p>
<p>The Japanese call this kind of thinking "kaizen," or continuous improvement. Toyota has embraced it wholeheartedly, just about everywhere you look at its seven north American assembly plants.</p>
<p>Take motorized carts for example. Toyota used to buy them. But it was discovered that car parts could be used instead, steered by a power-window motor. By building these carts themselves, Toyota has cut its costs in half.</p>

<p>And savings of $3,000 per cart is just a small part of Toyota’s overall good fortune, which continues to elude their American competitors.</p>
<p>“It's not just the Toyota culture,” says AutoWeek Editor and Associate Publisher Dutch Mandel.  “It's the Japanese culture that they've brought to corporate America. And they've melded in such a way that takes the benefits of both.”</p>
<p>Those benefits led to record sales last year at Toyota. But don't expect a big party here.</p>
<p>“It's not to say that Toyota doesn't recognize its accomplishments,” said Chad Buckner, a paint shop engineering manager. “But there's a healthy sense of paranoia that that's not enough.”</p>
<p>That's why every worker is empowered to yank the line to a halt if they see a problem.</p>
<p>“In the Toyota system, no problem equals a problem,” said John Robinson, an assembly engineering manager. “So we want to expose problems.”</p>
<p>Problems expose opportunities for solutions, which employees seem eager to find.</p>
<p>“At the end of the day if you see a change, and my job is better, my process has improved, it makes you want to come back,” said Robinson.</p>
<p>Back, that is, to keep Toyota moving forward toward No. 1 in the world.</p>
