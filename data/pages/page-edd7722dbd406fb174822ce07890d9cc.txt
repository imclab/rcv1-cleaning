	<h1>LA Auto Show: the car market in America today</h1>
		<h2>
Setting the scene for this week's LA Auto Show, Andrew English considers the 
  current state of the American car market. 

</h2>
	</div>
<div class="oneHalf gutter">

	<div id="mainBodyArea" >

	
			  	
					<div class="firstPar"><p>
As Arnie the governator bows out in favour of his replacement, Jerry Brown, 
  California is facing tough economic conditions and some stark financial 
  choices &ndash; just like the rest of us, then. 
</p></div><div class="secondPar">
<p>
For the motor industry, the darkest days, where the lights were literally 
  turned off at the LA Auto Show, seem to be receding. With a 14.6 per cent 
  rise in the first nine months of 2010, California&rsquo;s new car sales have 
  bounced off the recession faster than the rest of the US. 
</p></div><div class="thirdPar">
<p>
Top selling cars are still Hondas and Toyotas, but the Japanese have made 
  heavy going of maintaining their position, and Toyota&rsquo;s latest 1.5 million 
  strong recall for brake-fluid leaks has helped the US domestic industry to 
  fill the gap. 
</p></div><div class="fourthPar">
<p>
Volkswagen is hoping its $4 billion investment in products and its new 
  Chattanooga plant to build the mid-sized sedan will allow it to fill some of 
  that gap. No surprise that new VW USA boss, Jonathan Browning, was in 
  bullish mood. VW America&rsquo;s top job has been something of a poisoned chalice 
  in the past, but Browning says he&rsquo;s confident that the VW board are 
  determined to &ldquo;make VW a significant volume player in the US market.&rdquo; He 
  denies that he&rsquo;s buried the chalice under the basement of his new Washington 
  home. 
</p></div><div class="fifthPar">
<p>
Reflecting the financial climate, is a new reality on electric cars. VW has 
  always been cautious and Dr Ulrich Hackenberg, board member responsible for 
  research and development, remains so. &ldquo;Electric cars will need to be 
  affordable and practical and in widespread daily use before we can state 
  that we are in the electric age,&rdquo; he said. VW&rsquo;s two electric contenders, the 
  tiny Up and the Golf, will not be on sale until 2013 and Hackenberg 
  confirmed that <strong><a href="http://www.telegraph.co.uk/motoring/green-motoring/7986932/Battery-electric-Volkswagen-Golf-review.html">the 
  battery Golf</a></strong> will be based on the Mark VII model rather than the 
  current Mark VI. 
</p></div><div id="tmg-related-links" class="related_links_inline">

	<div class="body">
<p>
October&rsquo;s JD Power report reflected that caution, predicting combined global 
  sales of hybrid electric and battery electric vehicles of no more than 5.2 
  million units in 2020, or just 7.3 per cent of the predicted 70.9 million 
  passenger vehicles. These figures are predicated on a stable oil price, 
  which as we all know is something of a unicorn state of affairs. 
</p>
<p>
While US gas prices remain at around $3.20 a US gallon (58 pence per litre) 
  there&rsquo;s a big memory loss about the days of $4 plus a US gallon. And while 
  the Nymex price of West Texas Intermediate remains at under $85 a barrel, it 
  doesn&rsquo;t take much for the market to run scared. Whether that&rsquo;s for genuine 
  reasons of supply and demand or not is immaterial - the fact is the oil 
  price is a momentarily slumbering giant and when it wakes again we&rsquo;ll all be 
  looking at the myriad alternatives. 
</p>
<p>
And the one prediction you can take to the bank is that they&rsquo;ll all be more 
  expensive.
