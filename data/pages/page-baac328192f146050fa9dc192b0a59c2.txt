<p><b>1950s American automobile culture</b> has had an enduring influence on the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_States" title="Culture of the United States">culture of the United States</a>, as reflected in popular music, major trends from the <a href="/wiki/1950s" title="1950s">1950s</a> and mainstream acceptance of the "<a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rod</a>" culture. The American manufacturing economy switched from producing <a href="/wiki/War_economy" title="War economy">war-related items</a> to <a href="/wiki/Consumer_goods" title="Consumer goods" class="mw-redirect">consumer goods</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and by the end of the 1950s, one in six working Americans were employed either directly or indirectly in the <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive industry</a>. The United States became the world's largest manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a>'s goal of 40 years earlier—that any man with a good job should be able to afford an automobile—was achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeiss200334_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeiss200334-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A new generation of service businesses focusing on customers with their automobiles sprang up during the decade, including <a href="/wiki/Drive-through" title="Drive-through">drive-through</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drive-in" title="Drive-in">drive-in</a> restaurants and more <a href="/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">drive-in theaters</a> (cinemas).</p>
<p>The decade began with 25 million <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_register" title="Vehicle register">registered</a> automobiles on the road, most of which predated World War II and were in poor condition; no automobiles or parts were produced during the war owing to <a href="/wiki/Rationing#United_States" title="Rationing">rationing</a> and restrictions. By 1950, most factories had made the transition to a consumer-based economy, and more than 8 million cars were produced that year alone. By 1958, there were more than 67 million cars registered in the United States, more than twice the number at the start of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-natpetnews_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natpetnews-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As part of the U.S. national defenses, to support military transport, the <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_System_(United_States)" title="National Highway System (United States)">National Highway System</a> was expanded with Interstate highways, beginning in 1955, across many parts of the U.S. The wider, multi-lane highways allowed traffic to move at faster speeds, with few or no <a href="/wiki/Stoplight" title="Stoplight" class="mw-redirect">stoplights</a> on the way. The wide-open spaces along the highways became a basis for numerous <a href="/wiki/Billboard" title="Billboard">billboards</a> showing advertisements.</p>
<p>The dawning of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Race#Early_Space_Race:_1950s" title="Space Race">Space Race</a> were reflected in contemporary American automotive styling. Large <a href="/wiki/Car_tailfin" title="Car tailfin">tailfins</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGunnell200415_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGunnell200415-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> flowing designs reminiscent of rockets, and <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">radio antennas</a> that imitated <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> were common, owing to the efforts of design pioneers such as <a href="/wiki/Harley_Earl" title="Harley Earl">Harley Earl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-century2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-century2-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstate_Highway_System">Interstate Highway System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interstate Highway System">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interstate Highway System" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Map" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg/220px-Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg/330px-Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg/440px-Interstate_Highway_plan_September_1955.jpg 2x" /></a>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a></div>
<p>The Dwight D. Eisenhower National System of Interstate and Defense Highways (commonly called the Interstate system or simply the Interstate) is a network of <a href="/wiki/Freeway" title="Freeway" class="mw-redirect">freeways</a> that forms a part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_System_(United_States)" title="National Highway System (United States)">National Highway System</a> of the United States. While serving as <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Commander_of_the_Allied_Expeditionary_Force" title="Supreme Commander of the Allied Expeditionary Force" class="mw-redirect">Supreme Commander</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> in Europe during World War II, <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> had gained an appreciation of the German <a href="/wiki/German_autobahns" title="German autobahns">Autobahn</a> network as an essential component of a national defense system, providing transport routes for military supplies and troop deployments.<sup id="cite_ref-Petroski_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petroski-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Construction was authorized by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aid_Highway_Act_of_1956" title="Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956">Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956</a>, and the original portion was completed 35 years later. The system has contributed in shaping the United States into a world economic superpower and a highly industrialized nation.<sup id="cite_ref-Genie_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Genie-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Interstate grew quickly, along with the automobile industry, allowing a new-found mobility that permeated ways of American life and culture. The automobile and the Interstate became the American symbol of individuality and freedom,<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> and, for the first time, automobile buyers accepted that the automobile they drove indicated their social standing and level of affluence. It became a statement of their personality and an extension of their self.<sup id="cite_ref-siedu_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siedu-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suburbanization">Suburbanization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Suburbanization">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Suburbanization" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States' investment in infrastructure such as highways and bridges coincided with the increasing availability of cars more suited to the higher speeds that better roads made possible, allowing people to live beyond the confines of major cities, and instead commute to and from work.<sup id="cite_ref-Magazines1958_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Magazines1958-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>After World War II, land developers began to buy land just outside the city limits of larger cities to build mass quantities of inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Tract_housing" title="Tract housing">tract houses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-historychannel_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historychannel-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> One of the first examples of planned suburbanization is <a href="/wiki/Levittown,_Pennsylvania" title="Levittown, Pennsylvania">Levittown, Pennsylvania</a>, which was developed by <a href="/wiki/William_Levitt" title="William Levitt">William Levitt</a> beginning in 1951 as a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-natpetnews_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natpetnews-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The promise of their own <a href="/wiki/Single-family_house" title="Single-family house" class="mw-redirect">single-family</a> home on their own land,<sup id="cite_ref-waltermartin_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waltermartin-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> together with a free college education and low-interest loans given to returning soldiers to purchase homes under the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Bill" title="G.I. Bill">G.I. Bill</a>, drove demand for new homes to an unprecedented level. Additionally, 4 million babies were born every year during the 1950s. By the end of the baby boom era in 1964, almost 77 million "baby boomers" had been born,<sup id="cite_ref-historychannel_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historychannel-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> fueling the need for more suburban housing, and automobiles to commute them to and from the city centers for work and shopping.</p>
<p>By the end of the 1950s, one-third of Americans lived in the suburbs. Eleven of the United States's twelve largest cities recorded a declining population during the decade, with a consequent loss in tax revenues and city culture. Only <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, a center for the car culture, gained population.<sup id="cite_ref-schmoop_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schmoop-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Economist Richard Porter commented that "The automobile made suburbia possible, and the suburbs made the automobile essential."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_the_inner_city">Decline of the inner city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decline of the inner city">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decline of the inner city" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More people joined the <a href="/wiki/Middle-class" title="Middle-class" class="mw-redirect">middle-class</a> in the 1950s, with more money to spend, and the availability of consumer goods expanded along with the economy, including the automobile.<sup id="cite_ref-historychannel_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historychannel-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Americans were spending more time in their automobiles and viewing them as an extension of their identity, which helped to fuel a boom in automobile sales.<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Most businesses directly or indirectly related to the auto industry saw tremendous growth during the decade. New designs and innovations appealed to a generation tuned into fashion and glamour,<sup id="cite_ref-siedu_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siedu-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and the new-found freedom and way of life in the suburbs had several unforeseen consequences for the inner cities. The 1950s saw the beginning of <a href="/wiki/White_flight" title="White flight">white flight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, driven by increasing automobile ownership. Many local and national transportation laws encouraged suburbanization, which in time ended up damaging the cities economically.<sup id="cite_ref-siedu2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-siedu2-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg/170px-Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg" width="170" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg/255px-Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg/340px-Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Car_of_the_Future_1950.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Auto-related magazines became common in the 1950s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As more middle-class and affluent people fled the city to the relative quiet and open spaces of the suburbs, the urban centers deteriorated and lost population.<sup id="cite_ref-GreenbergWatts2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenbergWatts2008-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time that cities were experiencing a lower tax base due to the flight of higher income earners, pressures from <a href="/wiki/The_New_Deal" title="The New Deal" class="mw-redirect">The New Deal</a> forced them to offer pensions and other benefits, increasing the average cost of benefits per employee by 1,629&#160;percent. This was in addition to hiring an average of 20&#160;percent more employees to serve the ever shrinking cities.<sup id="cite_ref-GreenbergWatts2008_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenbergWatts2008-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> More Americans were driving cars and fewer were using public transportation, and it was not practical to extend to the suburbs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> At the same time, the number of surface roads exploded to serve the ever increasing numbers of individually owned cars, further burdening city and country resources. During this time, the perception of using public transportation turned more negative. In what is arguably the most extreme example, <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, the fifth largest city in the U.S. in 1950 with 1,849,568 residents,<sup id="cite_ref-detroit1950_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-detroit1950-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> had shrunk to 706,585 by 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-PopEstCities_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopEstCities-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> a reduction of 62 percent.</p>
<p>In some instances, the automotive industry and others were directly responsible for the decline of public transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDauvergne200838-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_American_streetcar_scandal" title="Great American streetcar scandal" class="mw-redirect">Great American streetcar scandal</a> saw GM, <a href="/wiki/Firestone_Tire" title="Firestone Tire" class="mw-redirect">Firestone Tire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil_of_California" title="Standard Oil of California" class="mw-redirect">Standard Oil of California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Petroleum" title="Phillips Petroleum" class="mw-redirect">Phillips Petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mack_Trucks" title="Mack Trucks">Mack Trucks</a> and other companies purchase a number of streetcars and electric trains in the 1930s and 1940s, such that 90&#160;percent of city trolleys had been dismantled by 1950. It was argued that this was a deliberate destruction of streetcars as part of a larger strategy to push the United States into <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Snell1974_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snell1974-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i>United States v. National City Lines, Inc.</i>, many were found guilty of antitrust violations.<sup id="cite_ref-seventhcircuit_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seventhcircuit-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The story has been explored several times in print, film and other media, for example in <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Framed_Roger_Rabbit" title="Who Framed Roger Rabbit">Who Framed Roger Rabbit</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Taken_for_a_Ride_(film)" title="Taken for a Ride (film)" class="mw-redirect">Taken for a Ride</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_End_of_Suburbia" title="The End of Suburbia">The End of Suburbia</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_rights">Women's rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Women's rights">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Women's rights" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The automobile unions played a leading role in advancing the cause of <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a>. In 1955, the <a href="/wiki/UAW" title="UAW" class="mw-redirect">United Auto Workers Union</a> (UAW) organized the UAW Women's Department to strengthen women's role in the union and encourage participation in the union's elected bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-womenuaw_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-womenuaw-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In a move that was met with some hostility by Teamsters leaders, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">U.S. Division of Transport Personnel</a> had in 1943 instructed <a href="/wiki/Teamsters_Union" title="Teamsters Union" class="mw-redirect">Teamsters Union</a> officials that women should be allowed full employment as <a href="/wiki/Truck_driver" title="Truck driver">truck drivers</a>. That proved to be only a temporary wartime measure,<sup id="cite_ref-vosko_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vosko-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> but a change of heart among Teamsters leadership by the mid-1950s led to the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Pay_Act_of_1963" title="Equal Pay Act of 1963">Equal Pay Act of 1963</a>. Women in the auto industry were considered leaders in the movement for women's rights.<sup id="cite_ref-umichwomen_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umichwomen-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorsports">Motorsports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Motorsports">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Motorsports" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_rodding">Hot rodding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hot rodding">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hot rodding" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">Hot rod</a></div>
<p>The increasing popularity of hot rodding cars (modifying them to increase performance) is reflected in part by the creation of special-interest magazines catering to this culture. <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Rod_(magazine)" title="Hot Rod (magazine)"><i>Hot Rod</i></a></i> is the oldest such magazine, with first editor <a href="/wiki/Wally_Parks" title="Wally Parks">Wally Parks</a><sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag3-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup>, and founded by <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Petersen" title="Robert E. Petersen">Robert E. Petersen</a> in 1948, with original publication by his Petersen Publishing Company.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag3-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Hot Rod</i> has licensed affiliation with <a href="/wiki/Universal_Technical_Institute" title="Universal Technical Institute">Universal Technical Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag4_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag4-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg/220px-Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg/330px-Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg/440px-Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hot_Rods_2_db.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">Hot rod</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The relative abundance and inexpensive nature of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Ford Model T</a> and other cars from the 1920s to 1940s helped fuel the hot rod culture that developed, which was focused on getting the most linear speed out of these older automobiles. The origin of the term "hot rod" is unclear, but the culture blossomed in the post-war culture of the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGenatCoxParks200341_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGenatCoxParks200341-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Hot Rod</i> magazine's November 1950 cover announced the first hot rod to exceed 200&#160;mph. The hand-crafted car used an Edelbrock-built <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(automobile)" title="Mercury (automobile)">Mercury</a> flathead V8 and set the record at the <a href="/wiki/Bonneville_Salt_Flats" title="Bonneville Salt Flats">Bonneville Salt Flats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag2-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> This 30,000-acre (47&#160;sq&#160;mi) region<sup id="cite_ref-flats_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flats-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> has been called the "Holy Grail of American Hot Rodding", and is often used for <a href="/wiki/Land_speed_record" title="Land speed record">land speed racing</a>, a tradition that grew rapidly in the 1950s and continues today.<sup id="cite_ref-flats2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flats2-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hot rodding was about more than raw power. <a href="/wiki/Kustom_Kulture" title="Kustom Kulture">Kustom Kulture</a> started in the 1950s,<sup id="cite_ref-kustom_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kustom-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> when artists such as <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Howard" title="Kenny Howard">Von Dutch</a> transformed automobile <a href="/wiki/Pin_striping" title="Pin striping" class="mw-redirect">pin striping</a> from a seldom-used accent that followed the lines of the car into a freestyle art form. Von Dutch was as famous for his "flying eyeball"<sup id="cite_ref-kustom_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kustom-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> as he was for his intricate spider-web designs.<sup id="cite_ref-pinstripe_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pinstripe-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> As the decade began, hand-drawn pin striping was almost unheard of, but by 1958 it had become a popular method of customizing the looks of the hot rod.<sup id="cite_ref-pinstripe_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pinstripe-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> As the decade progressed, hot rodding became a popular hobby for a growing number of teenagers as the sport literally came to Main Street.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrod2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrod2-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drag_racing">Drag racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Drag racing">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Drag racing" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drag_racing" title="Drag racing">Drag racing</a> has existed since the first cars, but it was not until the 1950s that it started to become mainstream, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Santa_Ana_Drags" title="Santa Ana Drags">Santa Ana Drags</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Dragstrip" title="Dragstrip">drag strip</a> in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-nhra_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhra-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The strip was founded by C. J. "Pappy" Hart, Creighton Hunter and Frank Stillwell at the <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne_Airport" title="John Wayne Airport">Orange County Airport</a> auxiliary runway in southern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and was operational from 1950 until June 21, 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-wedidit_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wedidit-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Hot Rod</i> editor Wally Parks created the <a href="/wiki/National_Hot_Rod_Association" title="National Hot Rod Association">National Hot Rod Association</a> in 1951, and it is still the largest governing body in the popular sport.<sup id="cite_ref-nhra_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhra-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> As of October 2012, there are at least 139 professional drag strips operational in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-dragtimes_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dragtimes-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> One of the most powerful racing fuels ever developed is <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a>, which dramatically debuted as a racing fuel in 1950, and continues as the primary component used in <a href="/wiki/Top_Fuel" title="Top Fuel">Top Fuel</a> drag racing today.<sup id="cite_ref-dragtimes2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dragtimes2-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASCAR">NASCAR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: NASCAR">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: NASCAR" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="NASCAR winner &quot;The Fabulous Hudson Hornet&quot;" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg/220px-Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg/330px-Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tim_FlockFabulousHudsonHornet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> winner "The Fabulous Hudson Hornet"</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a></div>
<p>The National Association for Stock Car Auto Racing (NASCAR) is the second most popular spectator sports in the United States behind the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL).<sup id="cite_ref-forbes_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> It was incorporated on February 21, 1948 by <a href="/wiki/Bill_France,_Sr." title="Bill France, Sr.">Bill France, Sr.</a> and built its roots in the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-nascar_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nascar-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Two years later in 1950 the first asphalt "superspeedway", <a href="/wiki/Darlington_Speedway" title="Darlington Speedway" class="mw-redirect">Darlington Speedway</a>, was opened in South Carolina, and the sport saw dramatic growth during the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-nascar_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nascar-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Because of the tremendous success of Darlington, construction began of a 2.5-mile, high-banked superspeedway near Daytona Beach, which is still in use.<sup id="cite_ref-nascar_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nascar-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/NASCAR_Cup_Series" title="NASCAR Cup Series" class="mw-redirect">Cup Series</a> was started in 1949, with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Roper" title="Jim Roper">Jim Roper</a> winning the first series.<sup id="cite_ref-nascar_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nascar-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> By 2008, the most prestigious race in the series, the <a href="/wiki/Daytona_500" title="Daytona 500">Daytona 500</a> had attracted more than 17 million television viewers.<sup id="cite_ref-jayski_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jayski-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Dynasties were born in the 1950s with racers like <a href="/wiki/Lee_Petty" title="Lee Petty">Lee Petty</a> (father of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Petty" title="Richard Petty">Richard Petty</a>, grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Petty" title="Kyle Petty">Kyle Petty</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Buck_Baker" title="Buck Baker">Buck Baker</a> (father of <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Baker" title="Buddy Baker">Buddy Baker</a>).</p>
<p>NASCAR, and stock car racing in general, has its roots in <a href="/wiki/Rum-running" title="Rum-running">bootlegging</a> during <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">Prohibition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bootleg_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bootleg-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Junior_Johnson" title="Junior Johnson">Junior Johnson</a> was one of many bootleggers who took part in the sport during the 1950s, equally well known for his arrest in 1955 for operating his father's <a href="/wiki/Moonshine" title="Moonshine">moonshine</a> <a href="/wiki/Distillery" title="Distillery" class="mw-redirect">still</a> as he is for his racing success.<sup id="cite_ref-esquire_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esquire-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> He ended up spending a year in an Ohio prison,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMenzer200159_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMenzer200159-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> but soon returned to the sport before retiring as a driver in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-bootleg_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bootleg-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_business_models">New business models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New business models">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New business models" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Faster_food">Faster food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Faster food">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Faster food" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Drive-in restaurant menu board" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg/220px-DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg/330px-DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg/440px-DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DRIVE-IN_RESTAURANT_-_NARA_-_547855.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Drive-in" title="Drive-in">Drive-ins</a> take orders over an intercom system, with a <a href="/wiki/Carhop" title="Carhop">carhop</a> accepting payment and delivering the order on a tray that attaches to the car window.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As more Americans began driving cars, entirely new categories of businesses came into being to allow them to enjoy their products and services without having to leave their cars. This includes the <a href="/wiki/Drive-in" title="Drive-in">drive-in</a> restaurant, and later the drive-through window. Even into the 2010s, the <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Drive-In" title="Sonic Drive-In">Sonic Drive-In</a> restaurant chain has provided primarily drive-in service by <a href="/wiki/Carhop" title="Carhop">carhop</a> in 3,561 restaurants within 43 <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a>, serving approximately 3 million customers per day.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bg909_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bg909-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Known for its use of carhops on <a href="/wiki/Roller_skates" title="Roller skates">roller skates</a>, the company annually hosts a competition to determine the top skating carhop in its system.<sup id="cite_ref-vegassun_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vegassun-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A number of other successful "drive up" businesses have their roots in the 1950s, including <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> (expanded c.1955), which had no dine-in facilities, requiring customers to park and walk up to the window, taking their order "to go". Automation and the lack of dining facilities allowed McDonald's to sell burgers for 15 cents each, instead of the typical 35 cents, and people were buying them by the bagful. By 1948, they had fired their carhops, installed larger grills, reduced their menu and radically changed the industry by introducing assembly-line methods of food production, similar to the auto industry, dubbing it the "Speedee Service System".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchlosser200119.E2.80.9320_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlosser200119.E2.80.9320-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> They redesigned their sign specifically to make it easier to see from the road, creating the now familiar yellow double-arch structure.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchlosser200120_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlosser200120-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Businessman <a href="/wiki/Ray_Kroc" title="Ray Kroc">Ray Kroc</a> joined McDonald's as a franchise agent in 1955. He subsequently purchased the chain from the McDonald brothers and oversaw its worldwide growth.<sup id="cite_ref-mcdonalds_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcdonalds-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other chains were created to serve the increasingly mobile patron. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Karcher" title="Carl Karcher">Carl Karcher</a> opened his first <a href="/wiki/Carl%27s_Jr." title="Carl's Jr.">Carl's Jr.</a> in 1956, and rapidly expanded, locating his restaurants near California's new freeway off-ramps.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchlosser200121.E2.80.9322_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlosser200121.E2.80.9322-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> These restaurant models initially relied on the new and ubiquitous ownership of automobiles, and the willingness of patrons to dine in their automobiles, and today drive-through service accounts for 65&#160;percent of their profits.<sup id="cite_ref-slate_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-slate-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drive-in_movies">Drive-in movies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drive-in movies">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drive-in movies" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">Drive-in theater</a></div>
<p>The drive-in theater is a form of <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">cinema</a> structure consisting of a large outdoor <a href="/wiki/Movie_screen" title="Movie screen" class="mw-redirect">movie screen</a>, a projection booth, a <a href="/wiki/Concession_stand" title="Concession stand">concession stand</a> and a large <a href="/wiki/Parking_lot" title="Parking lot">parking area</a> for automobiles, where patrons view the movie from the comfort of their cars and listen via an electric speaker placed at each parking spot.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="View of screen from back row" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg/220px-ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg/330px-ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg/440px-ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ForkUnionDriveIn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">Drive-in theater</a> just before the movie starts</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although drive-in movies first appeared in 1933,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESandersSanders2003162_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESandersSanders2003162-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> it was not until well after the post-war era that they became popular, enjoying their greatest success in the 1950s, reaching a peak of more than 4,000 theaters in the United States alone.<sup id="cite_ref-cnbcdrive_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnbcdrive-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tropes_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tropes-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Drive-in theaters have been romanticized in popular culture with the movie <a href="/wiki/American_Graffiti" title="American Graffiti"><i>American Graffiti</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Grease_(film)" title="Grease (film)"><i>Grease</i></a> and the television show <a href="/wiki/Happy_Days" title="Happy Days"><i>Happy Days</i></a>. They developed a reputation for showing <a href="/wiki/B_movie" title="B movie">B movies</a>, typically monster or horror films, and as "passion pits", a place for teenagers to <a href="/wiki/Kissing" title="Kissing" class="mw-redirect">kiss</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tropes_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tropes-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> While drive-in theaters are rarer today with only 366 remaining<sup id="cite_ref-cnbcdrive_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnbcdrive-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> and no longer unique to America, they are still associated as part of the 1950s' American car culture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESandersSanders20034_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESandersSanders20034-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Drive-in movies have seen somewhat of a resurgence in popularity in the 21st century, due in part to <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" title="Baby boomer">baby boomer</a> nostalgia.<sup id="cite_ref-tropes_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tropes-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Schuller" title="Robert Schuller" class="mw-redirect">Robert Schuller</a> started the nation's first drive-in church in 1955 in <a href="/wiki/Garden_Grove,_California" title="Garden Grove, California">Garden Grove, California</a>. After his regular 9:30&#160;am service in the chapel four miles away, he would travel to the drive-in for a second Sunday service.<sup id="cite_ref-penner1992_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penner1992-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Worshipers listened to his sermon from the comfort of their cars, using the movie theater's speaker boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchlosser200119_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlosser200119-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><br style="clear:both;" /></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malls">Malls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Malls">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Malls" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="interior, center courtyard and stairs" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg/220px-2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg/330px-2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg/440px-2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2009-0611-003-Southdale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Southdale_Center" title="Southdale Center">Southdale Center</a> in 2009</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first modern shopping malls were built in the 1950s,<sup id="cite_ref-newmall_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmall-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/The_Outlets_at_Bergen_Town_Center" title="The Outlets at Bergen Town Center">Bergen Mall</a>, which was the first to use the term "mall" to describe the business model. Other early malls moved retailing away from the dense, commercial downtowns into the largely residential suburbs. <a href="/wiki/Northgate_Mall_(Seattle)" title="Northgate Mall (Seattle)">Northgate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> is credited as being the first modern mall design, with two rows of businesses facing each other and a walkway separating them. It opened in 1950. <a href="/wiki/Shopper%27s_World" title="Shopper's World">Shopper's World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Framingham,_Massachusetts" title="Framingham, Massachusetts">Framingham, Massachusetts</a> was the two-story mall, and opened a year later.<sup id="cite_ref-newmall_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmall-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The design was modified again in 1954 when <a href="/wiki/Northland_Center" title="Northland Center">Northland Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" title="Detroit, Michigan" class="mw-redirect">Detroit, Michigan</a>, used a centralized design with an anchor store in the middle of the mall, ringed by other stores. This was the first mall to have the parking lot completely surrounding the shopping center, and to provide <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">central heat and air-conditioning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newmall_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmall-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1956, <a href="/wiki/Southdale_Center" title="Southdale Center">Southdale Center</a> opened in <a href="/wiki/Edina,_Minnesota" title="Edina, Minnesota">Edina, Minnesota</a>, just outside of Minneapolis. It was the first to combine all these modern elements, being enclosed with a two-story design, central heat and air-conditioning plus a comfortable common area. It also featured two large department stores as anchors. Most industry professionals consider Southdale Center to be the first modern regional mall.<sup id="cite_ref-newmall_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmall-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This formula (enclosed space with stores attached, away from downtown and accessible only by automobile) became a popular way to build <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retail</a> across the world. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gruen" title="Victor Gruen">Victor Gruen</a>, one of the pioneers in mall design,<sup id="cite_ref-victormall_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victormall-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> came to abhor this effect of his new design; he decried the creation of enormous "land wasting seas of parking" and the spread of suburban sprawl.<sup id="cite_ref-deadmall_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deadmall-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermarket">Aftermarket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aftermarket">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aftermarket" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1950s jump started an industry of aftermarket add-ons for cars that continues today. The oldest aftermarket wheel company, <a href="/wiki/American_Racing" title="American Racing">American Racing</a>, started in 1956 and still builds "mag wheels" (<a href="/wiki/Alloy_wheel" title="Alloy wheel">alloy wheels</a>) for almost every car made.<sup id="cite_ref-amracing_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amracing-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Holley_Performance_Products" title="Holley Performance Products">Holley</a> introduced the first modular four-barrel carburetor, which Ford offered in the 1957 Ford Thunderbird,<sup id="cite_ref-holley_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holley-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> and versions are still used by performance enthusiasts. <a href="/wiki/Edelbrock" title="Edelbrock">Edelbrock</a> started during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and expanded after the war. They provided a variety of high performance parts for the new hot rodders, which was popular equipment for setting speed records at Bonneville Salt Flats.<sup id="cite_ref-Edelbrock_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edelbrock-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> Owners were no longer restricted to the original equipment provided by manufacturers, helping not only create the hot rod culture<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELucsko2008105_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELucsko2008105-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> but also the foundation for cosmetic modifications. The creation and rapid expansion of the aftermarket made it possible for enthusiasts to personalize their automobiles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDregniWilliams201035_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDregniWilliams201035-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auto_industry_changes">Auto industry changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Auto industry changes">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Auto industry changes" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg" class="image"><img alt="White car and refrigerator on display" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg/220px-1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg/330px-1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg/440px-1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1949_Crosley_Hotshot_%26_Appliance_Display.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf11/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Crosley" title="Crosley">Crosley</a> autos were sold in a number of appliance stores in the early 1950s.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most new cars were sold through <a href="/wiki/Car_dealership" title="Car dealership">automobile dealerships</a> in the 1950s, but <a href="/wiki/Crosley" title="Crosley">Crosley</a> automobiles were still on sale at any number of appliance or department stores,<sup id="cite_ref-crosleyapp_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crosleyapp-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt2007164_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt2007164-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Allstate_(automobile)" title="Allstate (automobile)">Allstate</a> (a rebadged <a href="/wiki/Henry_J" title="Henry J">Henry J</a>) could be ordered at any <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears and Roebuck</a> in 1952 and 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt2007165_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt2007165-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> By mid-decade, these outlets had vanished and the automobile dealer became the sole source of new automobiles.</p>
<p>Starting in the mid-1950s, new car introductions in the fall once again became an anticipated event, as all dealers would reveal the <a href="/wiki/Car_model" title="Car model">models</a> for the upcoming year each October. In this era before information technology, the primary source of information on new models was the dealer.<sup id="cite_ref-tstar_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tstar-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The idea was originally suggested in the 1930s by President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> during the Great Depression, as a way of stimulating the economy by creating demand. The idea was reintroduced by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> Dwight Eisenhower for the same reasons, and this method of introducing next year's models in the preceding autumn lasted well into the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-tstar_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tstar-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During the decade, many smaller manufacturers could not compete with the <a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(automobile_manufacturers)" title="Big Three (automobile manufacturers)">Big Three</a> and either went out of business<sup id="cite_ref-outofbiz_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-outofbiz-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> or merged. In 1954, <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a> was formed when <a href="/wiki/Hudson_(automobile)" title="Hudson (automobile)" class="mw-redirect">Hudson</a> merged with <a href="/wiki/Nash-Kelvinator_Corporation" title="Nash-Kelvinator Corporation" class="mw-redirect">Nash-Kelvinator Corporation</a> in a deal worth almost $200 million, the largest corporate merger in U.S. history at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-amcstart_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amcstart-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscle_cars">Muscle cars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Muscle cars">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Muscle cars" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Muscle_car" title="Muscle car">muscle-car</a> era is deeply rooted in the 1950s,<sup id="cite_ref-dealership_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dealership-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> although there is some debate as to the exact beginning. <i>Old Cars Weekly</i> claims it started with the introduction of the original Chrysler <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Hemi_engine#331" title="Chrysler Hemi engine">"Firepower" hemi</a> V8 engine in 1951,<sup id="cite_ref-Weekly2010_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weekly2010-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> while others such as <i>Hot Rod</i> magazine consider the first overhead valve engine by Chevrolet, the 265 cid V8, as the "heir apparent to Ford flathead's position as the staple of racing", in 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_small-block_engine" title="Chevrolet small-block engine">"small block Chevy"</a> itself developed its own subculture that exists today.<sup id="cite_ref-Mueller2005_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mueller2005-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Other contenders include the 1949 <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_V8_engine" title="Oldsmobile V8 engine">Oldsmobile V8 engine</a>, the first in a long line of such powerful V8 engines,<sup id="cite_ref-Mueller2004_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mueller2004-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AmbrogioLuckerman2006_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AmbrogioLuckerman2006-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_V8_engine#OHV" title="Cadillac V8 engine">Cadillac V8</a> of the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-amercar_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amercar-76"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Regardless how it is credited, the <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a> race centered around the V8 engine<sup id="cite_ref-dealership_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dealership-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-time_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-77"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> and the muscle-car era lasted until new smog regulations forced dramatic changes in OEM engine design in the early 1970s. This in turn opened up new opportunities for aftermarket manufacturers like Edelbrock.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodmag_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodmag-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Each year brought larger engines and/or increases in horsepower,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt20077_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt20077-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> providing a catalyst for customers to upgrade to newer models. Automobile executives also deliberately updated the body designs yearly, in the name of "planned obsolescence"<sup id="cite_ref-planned_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planned-79"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-planned2_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-planned2-80"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> and added newly developed or improved features such as automatic transmissions, power steering, power brakes and cruise control, in an effort to make the previous models seem outdated and facilitate the long drive from the suburbs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt20077_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGenatNewhardt20077-78"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Record sales made the decade arguably the "golden era" of automobile manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-century3_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-century3-81"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Harley Earl and Bill France Sr. popularized the saying "Race on Sunday, sell on Monday",<sup id="cite_ref-century_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-century-82"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup> a mantra still heard today in motorsports, particularly within NASCAR. During the muscle-car era, manufacturers not only sponsored the drivers, but designed <a href="/wiki/Stock_car_racing" title="Stock car racing">stock cars</a> specifically to compete in the fast-growing and highly popular sport.<sup id="cite_ref-dealership_70-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dealership-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs_celebrating_the_automobile">Songs celebrating the automobile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Songs celebrating the automobile">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1950s_American_automobile_culture&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Songs celebrating the automobile" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the automobile became more and more an extension of the individual, it was natural for this to be reflected in popular culture. America's love affair with the automobile was most evident in the music of the era.</p>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Rocket_88" title="Rocket 88">Rocket 88</a>" was first recorded in 1951 and originally credited to <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Brenston" title="Jackie Brenston">Jackie Brenston</a> and his Delta Cats, although it was later discovered to be the work of <a href="/wiki/Ike_Turner" title="Ike Turner">Ike Turner</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Rhythm" title="Kings of Rhythm">Kings of Rhythm</a>. It is often credited as the first <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> song ever produced<sup id="cite_ref-jahl_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jahl-83"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> and has been covered by other artists.</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Hot_Rod_Lincoln" title="Hot Rod Lincoln">Hot Rod Lincoln</a>" was first recorded in 1955 by Charlie Ryan,<sup id="cite_ref-charlie_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charlie-84"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-charlie2_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charlie2-85"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> and has since been recorded by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Miller" title="Roger Miller">Roger Miller</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-rmiller_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rmiller-86"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> the 1960 <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Bond" title="Johnny Bond">Johnny Bond</a> version charted at number 26 on Billboard Hot 100. Comedian <a href="/wiki/Jim_Varney" title="Jim Varney">Jim Varney</a> produced a version with <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Skaggs" title="Ricky Skaggs">Ricky Skaggs</a> for the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beverly_Hillbillies_(film)" title="The Beverly Hillbillies (film)">The Beverly Hillbillies</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-varney_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-varney-87"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> The song is still a popular live song for artists such as <a href="/wiki/Asleep_at_the_Wheel" title="Asleep at the Wheel">Asleep at the Wheel</a><sup id="cite_ref-allmusicasleep_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicasleep-88"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Junior_Brown" title="Junior Brown">Junior Brown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hotrodjr_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hotrodjr-89"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Maybellene" title="Maybellene">Maybellene</a>", released by <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Berry" title="Chuck Berry">Chuck Berry</a> in 1955, is an uptempo rocker describing a hot rod race between a jilted lover and his unfaithful inamorata. It was a #5 hit and was described by <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> as the starting point of rock &amp; roll guitar.<sup id="cite_ref-rs-maybellene_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-maybellene-90"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Wake_Up_Little_Susie" title="Wake Up Little Susie">Wake Up Little Susie</a>" recorded by <a href="/wiki/The_Everly_Brothers" title="The Everly Brothers">The Everly Brothers</a>, reached number one on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a>&#160; Pop chart, despite having been <a href="/wiki/Banned_in_Boston" title="Banned in Boston">banned</a> from Boston radio-stations for lyrics about elaborating "our reputation is shot" because the narrator and his date slept through a drive-through movie date and missed their <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">curfew</a> by six hours.<sup id="cite_ref-rstone_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rstone-91"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Teen_Angel_(song)" title="Teen Angel (song)">Teen Angel</a>" was released in 1959 and initially met with resistance by radio stations because of its dark message about a young girl who dies in an automobile/train accident.</li>
</ul>
<p>Other songs recorded during the decade also reflect the automobile's place in American culture, such as "<a href="/wiki/Brand_New_Cadillac" title="Brand New Cadillac">Brand New Cadillac</a>", <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Burgess" title="Sonny Burgess">Sonny Burgess</a>'s "Thunderbird" and <a href="/wiki/Bo_Diddley" title="Bo Diddley">Bo Diddley</a>'s "Cadillac". A 1955 Oldsmobile was celebrated in the nostalgic "<a href="/wiki/Ol%27_%2755" title="Ol' '55">Ol' '55</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Waits" title="Tom Waits">Tom Waits</a> (1973).</p>
