 	<h2><a href="http://jewishentertainment.net/the-art-of-the-kvetch-jewish-humor-as-secularism-the-new-school/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to The Art of the Kvetch: Jewish Humor as Secularism | The New School">The Art of the Kvetch: Jewish Humor as Secularism | The New School</a></h2>
	<div class="meta">Posted by Vera Ferris on May 6th, 2011 and filed under <a href="http://jewishentertainment.net/category/jewish-art/" title="View all posts in Jewish Art" rel="category tag">Jewish Art</a> <strong>|</strong>  <span>Comments Off</span></div>
	<div class="story">
		<p><img src="http://i.ytimg.com/vi/LnlXUlRZqWA/0.jpg" align="left">Jewish Studies at Eugene Lang College The New School for Liberal Arts | http://www.newschool.edu/lang/jewish-studies</p>
<p>The modern tradition of Jewish humor emerged from the same ideological ferment that produced Zionism, Liberal Judaism, Modern Orthodoxy, and a wide variety of secular forms of Jewish identity. To this day, Jewish humor reinforces the bond between Jews even as globalization has brought it to millions of non-Jews. Is this lively tradition the result of a seed of humor planted deep in Jewish textual traditions, or is Jewish humor an exercise in self- loathing by an insecure minority? Having transcended its Yiddish forms in American comedy (from Lenny Bruce to Larry David), is Jewish humor a way for Jews to be Jews in a genre at once particular and universally accessible?</p>
<p>Jewish Cultural Studies | http://www.newschool.edu/jewishculture<br />
THE NEW SCHOOL FOR GENERAL STUDIES | http://www.newschool.edu/generalstudies</p>
<p>Participants include:</p>
<p>Michael Wex, author of Just Say Nu: Yiddish for Every Occasion (When English Just Won&#8217;t Do) and Born to Kvetch; Noah Isenberg, author of Between Redemption and Doom: The Strains of German-Jewish Modernism and editor and translator of The Face of East European Jewry by Arnold Zweig; Jeffrey Israel, author of a dissertation on Jewish humor and politics; and Val Vinokur, author of The Trace of Judaism: Dostoevsky, Mandelstam, Babel, Levinas.</p>
<p>Eugene Lang College The New School for Liberal Arts | http://www.newschool.edu/lang</p>
<p>Presented by Jewish Studies at Eugene Lang College The New School for Liberal Arts and the Jewish Cultural Studies Program.</p>
