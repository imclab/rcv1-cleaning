	 <div class="parent chrome5 single1"><h2>March 26, 2013</h2>

 <div class="child c1 first">
<div class="entry" id="entry-6a00e54f9164528834017d424d7413970c">
	
		<h1 class="entry-header spacedmargin h1-post">2013 New York auto show goes crossover crazy</h1>
	
	<div class="entry-content">
		<div class="entry-body">
			<p>
<a class="asset-img-link" href="http://sneil.typepad.com/.a/6a00e54f9164528834017d42512de4970c-popup" onclick="window.open( this.href, &#39;_blank&#39;, &#39;width=640,height=480,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0&#39; ); return false"><img alt="Acura MDX Prototype" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00e54f9164528834017d42512de4970c" src="http://sneil.typepad.com/.a/6a00e54f9164528834017d42512de4970c-500wi" style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" title="Acura MDX Prototype" /></a><br /><br /></p>
<p><em>By John LeBlanc for MSN Autos</em>
</p>
<p>Among the annual “big” international auto shows,
stereotypically, the Los Angeles gig is about “green” cars, the Detroit circus
is about “power”, the Geneva salon is about “exotics” and the New York event
focuses on “traditional American luxury.” And while both Cadillac (with its new
2014 CTS) and Buick (with its refreshed Regal and LaCrosse) will be showing off
new versions of their traditional American luxury sedans, the relatively fresh genre
of crossovers will dominate the debuts during this week&#39;s Big Apple media days.
</p>
		</div>
		
			<div class="entry-more">
				

<p>All-new 2014 models we&#39;ll see for this frist in New York include a more conservatively drawn
Acura MDX (seen above in &quot;prototype&quot; form at this year&#39;s Detroit show), the Evoque-inspired Range Rover Sport and one of the smallest crossovers
you can buy: the Kia Soul. Updated — or “refreshed” — crossovers include the Dodge
Durango and Toyota Highlander. While the growing niche of gasoline-electric
hybrid crossovers will expand with the introduction of the Nissan Pathfinder Hybrid and its Infiniti QX60
Hybrid (nee JX) platform-mate, as well as the Subaru XV Crostrek Hybrid — the
first hybrid from the Japanese automaker.</p>
<p>As many <em>MSN Autos</em> readers know, the appeal of
crossovers is enticing. The vehicles’ Frankenstein mix of SUV styling, minivan people-moving
capabilities and sedan driving characteristics makes the genre one of the fastest
growing in the industry worldwide. However, every dog has its day. So just as minivans replaced
station wagons in the 1980s, and SUVs usurped minivans in the 1990s, crossovers are replacing
both SUVs and minivans today. 
</p>
<p>Yet despite the onslaught of new models we’ll see in New York
this week (and I suspect at every other auto show going forward for the next
few years) how long do you think the crossover trend last? Looking into your crystal ball, is there
a type of automotive body style you think could replace today’s crossovers?</p>
			</div>
		
	</div>
