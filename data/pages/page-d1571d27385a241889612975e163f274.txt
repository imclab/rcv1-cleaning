				<td><font color="#CC0000" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif"><b>CAR BUYERS BEWARE:</b></font><p><b>Scammers advertise very rare or 
				expensive classic cars for sale on a web site with a price that seems to good to be true.</b><br><b>Characteristics:</b><br><br>
				1. Come from overseas. <br>2. Have an asking price that seems to good to be true. <br>3. Request a deposit be sent immediately. <br>
				4. Express a sense of urgency in making the transaction. <br>5. The use of free web-based email accounts, especially Yahoo, MSN, 
				hotmail, etc.</p>
<p><em><b>Example:</b></em></p>
<p>The SCAMMER advertises a really nice car, usually for a really cheap price, but not necessarily&nbsp; When you contact the 
Seller, they insist that you place a &quot;Deposit&quot; with them to hold the car for you, and that the deposit is refundable if you don't like the car when you 
inspect it. They usually request a western union money transfer (for the &quot;Deposit&quot;), to a location outside the USA.</p>
<p>If you see any of the above symptoms in a transaction you are about to make, <b>BEWARE</b>, because it's probably a scam!</p>
<p align="left"><font color="#CC0000" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif"><b>CAR SELLERS BEWARE:</b></font></p>
<p align="left"><b>Scammers offer to pay you by arranging to send you a check and asking you to wire some of the money back.</b><br><b>Characteristics:</b></p>
<p align="left">1. Want to pay with a &quot;cashier's check&quot; or money order that is written for <u>more than</u> the car(s) they &quot;want 
to purchase&quot;.<br>2. Asks few (or no) questions about the vehicle of interest.<br>3.&nbsp; Buyer is usually from 
overseas.<br>4. A sense of urgency is conveyed in making the transaction as soon as possible.<br>5. 
Arrangements for delivery somewhere other than the inquirer's location&nbsp; - to &quot;client&quot; etc. </p>
<p align="left"><em><b>Example:</b></em></p>
<p align="left">The SCAMMER offers to send a cashier's check for the full asking price of one or more vehicles, plus an additional 
amount intended as a loan payment, or shipping fee, etc. The person responding to the ad later asks to have any excess funds, refunded to him or a third party. 
The original cashier's check turns out to be non-negotiable or bogus.</p>
<p align="left">Most financial institutions require at least 10-15 business days before even a cashier's check will fully clear, so if 
in doubt..... wait it out.&nbsp; If it's a real buyer, they will understand.<p align="left">
				Read the CBS News Report on this story <b><font face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif" size="2"><a style="border-bottom-style: groove;" href="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2002/11/01/eveningnews/main527883.shtml">
				<font color="#0000FF">Click Here</font></a></font></b> 
<p align="left"><font color="#CC0000" face="Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif"><b>BUYING FROM A CLASSIC CAR DEALERSHIP, AN AUCTION WEB SITE OR INDIVIDUAL:<br></b></font>Keep in mind that there are 
people <b>(Dealers and private sellers)</b> who will intentionally misrepresent the condition of a car, or attempt to pass a clone off as an original, just to 
get a sale.&nbsp; My suggestions: </p>
<p align="left">1. Always inspect a car before putting a deposit on it (and know how to identify: a clone from an original, engine size, etc.).<br>2. Always get 
a bill of sale or receipt.<br>3. Ask to see the title: To make sure they actually have the title and they know where it is, and to verify the VIN numbers 
match the model, specifications, options, etc. as expected.</p>
<p align="left"><b>I </b> <u><b>STRONGLY</b></u><b> discourage anyone giving me a deposit on any of my cars, unless they have personally stopped by my car lot 
and closely inspected the car they are interested in.&nbsp;</b></p>
