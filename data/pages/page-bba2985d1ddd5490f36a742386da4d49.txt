<div class="copy">Looking for a decent year-round convertible which can comfortably fit two adults and two children. <br>

<div style="border-top:1px dotted #777;margin-top:6px;padding-right:20px;">

<br> Trying to keep the cost under $15K (US) .  So far I've found quite a few Volvo C70s which meet my criteria (mostly 2003s and 2004s turbo models) but am beginning to think I should also consider Saab 93s of similar vintage.  The Volvo seems reliable enough but there are many complaints of harsh ride and body flex.  I will probably keep my old Jeep as well so extreme winter weather (I'm in New England) is not much of a concern.<br>
Other suggestions (though BMWs appear to be out of my price range), First/second hand knowledge and opinions appreciated.</div>

<a name="1519363"></a><div class="comments" id="c1519363">I LOVED LOVED LOVED my 92 Saab convertible. Very comfortable, very safe, and exceedingly fun to drive. Saabs can be quirky, but they run forever with proper care. I would certainly consider a newer one when my budget allows.<br>
<br>
One advantage of the Saab convertible (at least for the older years) was that they were built from the ground up, in a special factory, to be convertibles, not just sedans with the top chopped off. This means the frame and body were not &quot;twisty&quot; like some convertibles, and the windshield frame was actually a roll-bar. The car was great in snow and cold weather as well. No drafts, and the top had an extra layer for insulation. <br>

<a name="1519578"></a><div class="comments" id="c1519578">How important are gas prices to you?<br>
<br>
I ask because if you want to get a <i>real</i> four-seat convertible (not to be confused with the <i>fake</i> four-seat convertible that is only equipped to handle two adults and two double amputees) and you don't care too much about performance, well... nothing beats old American iron.  Plenty (and I do mean <i>plenty</i>) of American manufacturers produced <i>proper</i> four-seat (four-door) convertibles.  These aren't sporty like the European saloons you've mentioned, but they're comfortable as hell and will get you from point A to point B with the sun shining on your head the whole way.<br>
<br>
The advantage of going with an older fixed-up American car is that <i>anybody</i> can work on them.  There is the slight disadvantage of not having modern (arguably) safety features like air-bags, but I believe these are more than offset by the fact that your vehicle weighs as much as many SUVs (with comparable gas mileage) only is wrapped in steel and chrome instead of plastic and crumple-zones.<br>
<br>
Finding good candidates can be tricky as well, but you'd be surprised what $15,000 can get you.  In most cases the person you're buying from will have spent at least that much getting it restored.  Beware of Craigslist scams, though.<br>

<a name="1519881"></a><div class="comments" id="c1519881">I've owned a few old Caddy droptops in sterling condition, specifically the '72 Eldo and 69 Fleetwood. They are gorgeous, comfortable, very powerful, well equipped (climate control, 4 wheel disk brakes, power seats and windows... in 1969!) and always breaking down.<br>
<br>
Always. Breaking. Down.<br>
<br>
Before Honda and Toyota figured it out in the late '80s, cars that weren't made by Rolls Royce or Volvo were only good for 75-80k miles, and it was expected you would have serious maintenance work done before then. (Valve jobs, tranny rebuilds, carb rebuilds, suspension work, electrical work... my '69 was flawless for the 50k miles I had it, except for the damn electricals. It would eat voltage converters like pop-tarts. I had to have one in the glovebox at all times, with a spare alternator in the trunk for good measure, or I'd be stranded on the side of the highway.)<br>
<br>
A top-down restoration of an American droptop from the golden age ('49 to '73) is do-able... but it will only give you a car as durable as the original. You can expect a major repair every 30k-40k mi, and you'll need to restore it all over again once you hit 100k.   <br>
<br>
It's not a GM problem, either - I owned and loved a '72 Mustang Landau and a '74 Super Beetle. I did all my own work on them, went for reliability and restoration rather than performance in my upgrades, performed regular maintenance religiously with the highest quality components I could find, and they were always breaking down. (VW Motto: No part is heavier than fifty pounds or more than fifty bucks!) <br>
<br>
