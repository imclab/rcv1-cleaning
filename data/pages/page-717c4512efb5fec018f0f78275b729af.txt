<p><b>Car classification</b> can be defined by a number of different criteria and objectives. However, comprehensive classification is elusive, because a car or other vehicle may fit into multiple categories, or not completely satisfy the requirements for any. This article details the commonly used classifications and definitions.</p>
<p>Part of the classification process takes into account the vehicle's <b>body style</b>. They may or may not correlate to a car's price, size or intended market classification. The same car model might be available in multiple body styles comprising a model range. Some distinctions, as with four-wheel drive vs. SUV models or minivan vs. MPV models, the distinction between body style and classification can be particularly narrow. While body styles have historical and technical definitions, in common usage such definitions are broad and may be ambiguous.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_methods">Classification methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification methods">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification methods" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous ways of categorizing passenger vehicles. Where applicable, the equivalent <a href="/wiki/Euro_NCAP" title="Euro NCAP">Euro NCAP</a> classifications are shown. Numerous jurisdictions establish vehicle classification systems for tax reasons, most often according to the car's construction, engine, weight, type of fuel and emissions, as well as the purpose for which they are used.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Car-related taxation is based on a sustainable environmental policy known as the user pays principle and there are many differences between different nations and jurisdictions regarding vehicle class taxes and fees.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, the power of classification was used by cities in the 1920s to exclude taxation of electric-powered vehicles because officials believed they did not cause "substantial wear upon the pavements."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Car rental companies often use<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2012)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/ACRISS_Car_Classification_Code" title="ACRISS Car Classification Code">ACRISS Car Classification Code</a>.</p>
<p>For research into safety, the Highway Loss Data Institute takes into account a combination of both vehicle size and other vehicle features with all passenger cars that do not fit the definition of either "sports" or "luxury", are classified on the basis of both vehicle length and <a href="/wiki/Wheelbase" title="Wheelbase">wheelbase</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Another vehicle classification scheme has been developed by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">Federal Highway Administration</a> for automatically calculating road use <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">tolls</a>. <div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Microcar" title="Microcar">Microcar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bubble_car" title="Bubble car">Bubble car</a></div>
<p>Straddling the boundary between car and motorbike, these vehicles have engines under 1.0 litre, typically seat only two passengers, and are sometimes unorthodox in construction. Some microcars are three-wheelers, while the majority have four wheels. Microcars were popular in post-war Europe, where their appearance led them to be called "<a href="/wiki/Bubble_car" title="Bubble car">Bubble cars</a>". More recent microcars are often electric powered.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/REVAi" title="REVAi">REVAi</a>/G-Wiz</li>

<p>In 2012, Japan's Transport and Tourism Ministry will allow local government to use ultracompact cars as transport for residents and tourists in their limiting areas. The size of ultracompact cars will be less than minicars, but have engine greater than 50cc displacement and able to transport 1 or 2 persons. Ultracompact cars cannot use minicars standard, because of strict safety standards for minicars. The regulation about running capacity and safety performance of ultracompact cars will be published in early autumn. Today, there are cars smaller than ultracompact cars, called category-1 motorized vehicles which it has 50cc displacement or less and only one seat for the driver.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/City_car" title="City car">City car</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kei_car" title="Kei car">Kei car</a></div>
<p>A city car is a small automobile intended for use in urban areas. Unlike microcars, a city car's greater speed, capacity and (in perception at least) occupant protection are safer in mixed traffic environments and weather conditions. While city cars can reach <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highway</a> speeds, that is not their intended use. In Japan, city cars are called <a href="/wiki/Kei_car" title="Kei car">kei cars</a>. Kei cars have to meet strict size and engine requirements: engines have a maximum displacement of 660&#160;cc and the car's length must be under 3400&#160;mm.</p>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Supermini_car" title="Supermini car" class="mw-redirect">Supermini car</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subcompact_car" title="Subcompact car">Subcompact car</a></div>
<p>This class is known as <b>supermini</b> in Europe, <b>subcompact</b> in North America. Superminis have three, four or five doors and are designed to seat four passengers comfortably. Current supermini hatchbacks are approximately 3900&#160;mm long, while saloons and estate cars are around 4200&#160;mm long. Currently (2013) sedan variants are generally not available in Europe and are marketed at a lower price than hatchback models in North America.</p>
<p>In Europe, the first superminis were the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_500" title="Fiat 500">Fiat 500</a> of 1957 and the <a href="/wiki/Mini" title="Mini">Austin Mini</a> of 1959. Today, superminis are some of the best selling vehicles in Europe.</p>
<p>In Australia, the motoring press tends to distinguish between a <b>light car</b> such as the <a href="/wiki/Daihatsu_Charade" title="Daihatsu Charade">Daihatsu Charade</a> or early models of the <a href="/wiki/Holden_Barina" title="Holden Barina">Holden Barina</a>, and slightly larger models such as the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a> which is considered to be a <b>small car.</b> As the general size of vehicles in this class has gradually increased, the category of <b>light car</b> has almost disappeared.</p>
<p>Examples of superminis/subcompact cars:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Opel_Corsa" title="Opel Corsa">Opel Corsa</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tata_Indica_Vista" title="Tata Indica Vista" class="mw-redirect">Tata Indica Vista</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Volkswagen Polo</a></li>
</ul>
<p>This category is equivalent to the EuroNCAP class "Superminis".</p>

<p>Small family/compact cars refer to the hatchbacks and shortest saloons and estate cars with similar size. Compact cars have room for five adults and usually have engines between 1.4 and 2.2 litres, but some have engines of up to 2.5 litres. These are the most popular vehicles in most developed countries.</p>

<p>A <b>station wagon</b> (also known as an <b>estate</b> or <b>estate car</b>) is an automobile with a <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">body style</a> variant of a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan/saloon</a> with its roof extended rearward over a shared passenger/cargo volume with access at the back via a third or fifth door (the <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a> or <a href="#Tailgate_evolution">tailgate</a>), instead of a trunk lid. The body style transforms a standard <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three-box</a> design into a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling#One-box_and_Two-Box_design" title="Three-box styling">two-box</a> design—to include an <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A, B, and C-pillar</a>, as well as a D-pillar. Station wagons can flexibly reconfigure their interior volume via fold-down rear seats to prioritize either passenger or cargo volume.</p>

<p>The term "sports car" does not appear to have a clear definition.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> It is commonly used to describe vehicles which prioritise acceleration and handling, however some people claim it is also defined as a vehicle with two seats.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><br />
A <b>sports car</b> (sportscar or sport car) is a small, usually two seat, two door <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> designed for spirited performance and nimble handling.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Sports cars may be spartan or luxurious but high maneuverability and minimum weight are requisite.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Van.2C_camper.2C_RV.2C_minibus_etc.">Van, camper, RV, minibus etc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Van, camper, RV, minibus etc.">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Van, camper, RV, minibus etc." class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG/220px-Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG/330px-Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG/440px-Dodge_Ram_Van.JPG 2x" /></a>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">Van</a></div>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ConversionVan-Interior.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/ConversionVan-Interior.JPG/220px-ConversionVan-Interior.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/ConversionVan-Interior.JPG/330px-ConversionVan-Interior.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/36/ConversionVan-Interior.JPG/440px-ConversionVan-Interior.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ConversionVan-Interior.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>In some countries, the term "van" can refer to a small <i>panel van</i> based on a passenger car design (often the estate model / station wagon); it also refers to light trucks, which themselves are sometimes based on SUVs or MPVs. (But note that those retaining seats and windows, while being larger and more utilitarian than MPVs, may be called "<a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibuses</a>".) The term is also used in the term "camper van" (or just "camper") — equivalent to a North American <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">recreational vehicle</a> (RV).</p>
<p>In the United States, the term "van" refers to vehicles that, like European minibuses, are even larger than large MPVs and are rarely seen being driven for domestic purposes — except for "conversion vans". These possess extremely large interior space and are often more intended for hauling cargo than people. Most vans use body-on-frame construction and are thus suitable for extensive modification and coachwork, known as conversion. Conversion vans are often quite luxurious, boasting comfortable seats, soft rides, built-in support for electronics such as television sets, and other amenities. The more elaborate conversion vans straddle the line between cars and recreational vehicles.</p>
<p>Examples of North American "vans":</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dodge_Ram_Van" title="Dodge Ram Van">Dodge Ram Van</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ford_E-Series#1992.E2.80.93present" title="Ford E-Series">Ford E-Series</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/GMC_Savana" title="GMC Savana" class="mw-redirect">GMC Savana</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Examples of European "vans":</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ford_Transit" title="Ford Transit">Ford Transit</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Transporter" title="Volkswagen Transporter">Volkswagen Transporter</a></li>

<dl>
<dt>Bakkie&#160;</dt>
<dd>A generic <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South African</a> term for light pickup truck.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Buggy_(automobile)" title="Buggy (automobile)">Buggy</a></dt>
<dd>A Buggy is an automobile with wheels that project beyond the vehicle body.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cabrio_coach" title="Cabrio coach">Cabrio coach</a> or Semi-convertible</dt>
<dd>A form of car roof, where a retractable textile cover amounts to a large <a href="/wiki/Sunroof" title="Sunroof">sunroof</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Convertible" title="Convertible">Convertible</a> or cabriolet</dt>
<dd>A body style with a flexible textile folding roof or rigid retracting roof — of highly variable design detail — that can <i>convert</i> between open-air and enclosed modes.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">Coupé</a></dt>
<dd>A 2-door, 2- or 4-seat car with a fixed roof. Its doors are often longer than those of an equivalent sedan and the rear passenger area smaller; the roof may also be low. In cases where the rear seats are very small and not intended for regular use it is called a <a href="/wiki/2_plus_2" title="2 plus 2" class="mw-redirect">2+2</a> (pronounced <i>"two plus two"</i>). Originally, a coupé was required to have only one side window per side, but this consideration has not been used for many years.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Convertible coupé</dt>
<dd>A 2 door roadster with both a cloth convertable top, and a removable hardtop, coupé roof. (technically not a coupe, but some car companies try and cash in on the cachet the word coupe brings)</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Coupé utility</dt>
<dd>A passenger-car derived vehicle with an integral cargo tray.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Corniche" title="Corniche">Corniche</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd>Sometimes used to describe a <i>luxury sedan</i> or <i>town car</i>. Actually a trade mark of <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" title="Rolls-Royce plc" class="mw-redirect">Rolls-Royce</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Crossover_(automobile)" title="Crossover (automobile)">Crossover</a> (or CUV)</dt>
<dd>A loose marketing term to describe a vehicle that blends features of a <a href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV" class="mw-redirect">SUV</a> with features of a car — especially forgoing the <a href="/wiki/Body_on_frame" title="Body on frame" class="mw-redirect">body on frame</a> construction of the SUV in favor of the car's <a href="/wiki/Unibody" title="Unibody" class="mw-redirect">unibody</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> construction. Crossovers usually borrow drivetrains and other parts from traditional cars in the same manufacturer's line.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Drop Head Coupe</dt>
<dd>Generally a European term referring to a 2 door, 4 place automobile with a retractable canvas / cloth top with <i>both</i> a padded headliner and rollup windows (as opposed to side curtains).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">Estate</a></dt>
<dd>British name for a <i>station wagon</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">Fastback</a></dt>
<dd>A design where the roof slopes at a smooth angle to the tail of the car, but the rear window does not open as a separate "door".</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Flower Car</dt>
<dd>in US, similar to ute in Australia, i.e. generic for Chevy El Camino, Ford Ranchero, GMC Sprint/Diablo, etc.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>;<a href="/wiki/Hearse" title="Hearse">Hearse</a>: A converted car to transport the deceased.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">Hatchback</a></dt>
<dd>Incorporates a shared passenger and <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a> volume, with rearmost accessibility via a rear third or fifth door, typically a top-hinged <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a>—and features such as fold-down rear seats to enable flexibility within the shared passenger/cargo volume. As a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">two-box design</a>, the body style typically includes A, B and C-<a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">pillars</a>, and may include a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">D-pillar</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">Hardtop</a></dt>
<dd>Originally a removable solid roof on a <i>convertible</i>; later, also a fixed-roof car whose doors have no fixed window frames, which is designed to resemble such a convertible.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Hearse" title="Hearse">Hearse</a></dt>
<dd>A converted car (often a station wagon), light truck or minivan usually used to transport the dead. Often longer and heavier than the vehicle on which they are usually based. Can sometimes double up as an <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">ambulance</a> in some countries, such as the United States, especially in rural areas.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Kammback" title="Kammback">Kammback</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd>Originally, a car with a tapered rear that cuts off abruptly.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Landaulet" title="Landaulet">Landaulet</a></dt>
<dd>A limousine with the passenger section covered by a convertible top.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Leisure_activity_vehicle" title="Leisure activity vehicle">Leisure activity vehicle</a></dt>
<dd>A small <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">van</a>, generally related to a <a href="/wiki/Supermini_car" title="Supermini car" class="mw-redirect">supermini</a>, with a second or even a third seat row, and a large, tall <a href="/wiki/Car_boot" title="Car boot">boot</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Liftback" title="Liftback" class="mw-redirect">Liftback</a></dt>
<dd>A broad marketing term for a hatchback, which incorporates a shared passenger and cargo volume, with rearmost accessibility via a top-hinged <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a>.</dd>
</dl>

<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">Limousine</a></dt>
<dd>By definition, a <a href="/wiki/Chauffeur" title="Chauffeur">chauffeur</a>-driven car with a (normally glass-windowed) division between the front seats and the rear. In German, the term simply means a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Microvan" title="Microvan">Microvan</a></dt>
<dd>Term for a boxy wagon-type of car that is smaller than a conventional minivan.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Microvan" title="Microvan">Microvan</a></dt>
<dd>Term for a boxy wagon-type of car that is smaller than a conventional minivan; often without rear sliding door(s). Examples are <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C3#C3_Picasso" title="Citroën C3">Citroën Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renault_Sc%C3%A9nic" title="Renault Scénic">Renault Scénic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Yaris_Verso" title="Toyota Yaris Verso">Toyota Yaris Verso</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_A-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz A-Class">Mercedes-Benz A-Class</a>. In Japan, this term is used for <a href="/wiki/Kei_car" title="Kei car">Kei car</a> based vans.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">Minibus</a></dt>
<dd>Designed to carry fewer people than a full-size bus, generally up to 16 people in multiple rows of seats. Passenger access in normally via a sliding door on one side of the vehicle. One example of a van with a minibus version available is the Ford Transit.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Multi-purpose_vehicle" title="Multi-purpose vehicle" class="mw-redirect">MPV</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd>Multi-purpose vehicle, a large car or small bus designed to be used on and off-road and easily convertible to facilitate loading of goods from facilitating carrying people.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Notchback" title="Notchback">Notchback</a></dt>
<dd>A configuration where the third box of a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three-box styling</a> configuration is less pronounced — especially where the rear deck (third box) is short or where the rear window is upright.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/People_carrier" title="People carrier" class="mw-redirect">People carrier</a> or people mover</dt>
<dd>European name to describe what is usually referred to in North America as a <a href="/wiki/Minivan" title="Minivan">Minivan</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Phaeton_body" title="Phaeton body">Phaeton</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd>A Phaeton is a style of open car or carriage without proper weather protection for passengers.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">Pickup truck</a> (or <i>pickup</i>)</dt>
<dd>A light-duty, open-bed truck.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Pillarless</dt>
<dd>Usually a prefix to <i>coupé</i>, <i>fastback</i>, or <i>hardtop</i>; completely open at the sides when the windows are down, without a central <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">pillar</a>, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Sunbeam_Rapier#Sunbeam_Rapier_Fastback_coup.C3.A9" title="Sunbeam Rapier">Sunbeam Rapier</a> fastback coupé.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Ragtop</dt>
<dd>Originally an open car like a <i>roadster</i>, but with a soft top (cloth top) that can be raised or lowered. Unlike a <a href="/wiki/Convertible_(car)" title="Convertible (car)" class="mw-redirect">convertible</a>, it had no roll-up side windows.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Now often used as slang for a convertible.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Retractable_Hardtop" title="Retractable Hardtop" class="mw-redirect">Retractable Hardtop</a></dt>
<dd>aka Coupé convertible or Coupé Cabriolet. A type of convertible forgoing a foldable textile roof in favor of a multi-segment rigid roof retracts into the lower bodywork.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">Roadster</a></dt>
<dd>Originally a two-seat open car with minimal weather protection — without top or side glass — though possibly with optional hard or soft top and side curtains (i.e., without roll-up glass windows). In modern usage, the term means simply a two-seat <a href="/wiki/Sports_car" title="Sports car">sports car</a> <i>convertible</i>, a variation of <i>spyder</i>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">Sedan</a></dt>
<dd>A car seating four or more with a fixed roof that is full-height up to the rear window. Known in British English as a saloon. Sedans can have 2 or 4-doors. This is the most common body style<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In the U.S., this term has been used<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> to denote a car with fixed window frames, as opposed to the <i>hardtop</i> style wherein the sash, if any, winds down with the glass.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sedan_delivery" title="Sedan delivery">Sedan delivery</a></dt>
<dd>North American term for a vehicle similar to a <i>wagon</i> but without side windows, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Panel_truck" title="Panel truck">panel truck</a> but with two doors (one on each side), and one or two rear doors<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">Sport utility vehicle</a> (SUV)</dt>
<dd>Derivative of a pickup truck or 4-wheel-drive vehicle, but with fully enclosed passenger cabin interior and carlike levels of interior equipment.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Spyder (or Spider)</dt>
<dd>Similar to a <i>roadster</i> but originally with less weather protection.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>Nowadays it simply means a convertible.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Shooting-brake" title="Shooting-brake">Shooting-brake</a></dt>
<dd>Initially a vehicle used to carry shooting parties with their equipment and <a href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">game</a>; later used to describe custom-built wagons by high-end coachbuilders, subsequently synonymous with <i><a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">estate</a></i>; and in contemporary usage a three or five-door wagons combining features of a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">wagon</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">Station wagon</a></dt>
<dd>A variant of a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">saloon</a>, (also known as estate or estate car) or with its roof extended rearward over a shared passenger/cargo volume; access at the back via a third or fifth door instead of a trunk lid; flexible configurations to vary passenger or cargo volume; and two or three rows of seating — in a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling#One-box_and_Two-Box_design" title="Three-box styling">two-box</a> design with a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A, B &amp; C-pillar</a>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">D pillar</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/T-top" title="T-top">T-top</a></dt>
<dd>A derivative of the Targa top, called a T-bar roof, this fixed-roof design has two removable panels and retains a central narrow roof section along the front to back axis of the car (<i>e.g.</i> <a href="/wiki/Toyota_MR2" title="Toyota MR2">Toyota MR2</a> Mark I.)</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Targa_top" title="Targa top">Targa top</a></dt>
<dd>A semi-convertible style used on some sports cars, featuring a fully removable hard top roof panel which leaves the A and B <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">pillars</a> in place on the car body.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Town_car" title="Town car">Town car (US)</a></dt>
<dd>Essentially the inverse of the <a href="/wiki/Landaulet" title="Landaulet">landaulet</a>, a historical body style in which the front seats were open and the rear compartment closed, normally with a removable top to cover the front chauffeur's compartment. In Europe the style is also known as Sedanca de Ville, often shortened to Sedanca or de Ville. Note that the modern <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Town_Car" title="Lincoln Town Car">Lincoln Town Car</a> derives its name, but nothing else, from this style.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Ute_(vehicle)" title="Ute (vehicle)">Ute</a></dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian</a>/<a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand English</a> term for the vehicles with a cargo bed at the rear.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sedan_delivery" title="Sedan delivery">Wagon delivery</a></dt>
<dd>North American term (mainly U.S. and Canada). Similar to a <i>sedan delivery</i>, with four doors.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">Van</a></dt>
<dd>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> "van" refers to a truck-based commercial vehicle of the wagon style, whether used for passenger or commercial use. Usually a van has no windows at the side rear (panel van), although for passenger use, side windows are included. In other parts of the world, 'van' denotes a passenger-based wagon with no rear side windows.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-English_terms">Non-English terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Non-English terms">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Car_classification&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Non-English terms" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some non-<a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> terms are familiar from their use on imported vehicles in English-speaking nations even though the terms have not been adopted into English.</p>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Barchetta" title="Barchetta">Barchetta</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a>. The name means, roughly, "small boat".</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">Berlina</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>.</dd>
<dt>Berline&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Berlinetta" title="Berlinetta">Berlinetta</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> term for a sport <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Brake_(carriage)" title="Brake (carriage)">Break</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>.</dd>
<dt>Camioneta&#160;</dt>
<dd>Brazilian Portuguese term for a station wagon (specially in the state of Rio de Janeiro).</dd>
<dt>Carrinha&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>. Not used in <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Espada" title="Espada" class="mw-redirect">Espada</a>&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> nickname for a <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a> (the same word for <i>Sword</i> - long piece of metal). Not used in <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a>.</dd>
<dt>Furgoneta&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">van</a>, in the latter language almost always used in its diminutive form <i>furgonetka</i>.</dd>
<dt>Furgão&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> alternative term (less used) for a <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">van</a>. Used in <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a>, most often for vans but sometimes for panel van variants of passenger cars.</dd>
<dt>Kombi&#160;</dt>
<dd>is a <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> abbreviation of "Kombinationswagen" (Combination Car) and it is German name for <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>. Since <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> is a major producer of cars for many European countries, the term Kombi in this meaning is also used in <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovenian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_language" title="Bosnian language">Bosnian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans_language" title="Afrikaans language" class="mw-redirect">Afrikaans</a> and in Australia, Kombi is also used to refer to a <a href="/wiki/VW_Type_2" title="VW Type 2" class="mw-redirect">Volkswagen Microbus</a>. In Brazil the word specifically refers to the VW Microbus.</dd>
<dt>Perua</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a> term either designating a <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">van</a> (especially as spoken in the city of São Paulo) or a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a> (in the city of Rio de Janeiro).</dd>
<dt>Turismo&#160;</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> term for a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>. Literally means <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, used mostly in Latin American countries.</dd>
</dl>
