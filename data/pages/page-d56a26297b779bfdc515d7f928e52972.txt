<p>Over the course of the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a> rapidly developed from an expensive toy for the rich into the <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> standard for passenger <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> in most <a href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries" class="mw-redirect">developed countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-carsystem_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carsystem-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries" class="mw-redirect">developing countries</a>, the effects of the automobile have lagged, but are emulating the impacts of developed nations. The development of the automobile built upon the transport revolution started by <a href="/wiki/Railways" title="Railways" class="mw-redirect">railways</a>, and like the railways, introduced sweeping changes in employment patterns, social interactions, <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and goods distribution.</p>
<p>The effects of the automobile on everyday life have been a subject of controversy. While the introduction of the mass-produced automobile represented a revolution in mobility and convenience, the modern consequences of heavy automotive use contribute to the use of <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_fuel" title="Non-renewable fuel" class="mw-redirect">non-renewable fuels</a>, a dramatic increase in the rate of <a href="/wiki/Accidental_death" title="Accidental death" class="mw-redirect">accidental death</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_isolation" title="Social isolation">social isolation</a>, the disconnection of community, the rise in <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, the generation of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urban_decay" title="Urban decay">urban decay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-asphalt_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asphalt-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_and_convenience">Access and convenience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Access and convenience">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Access and convenience" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Worldwide, the automobile has allowed easier access to remote places. However, average journey times to regularly visited places have increased in large cities, especially in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>, as a result of widespread automobile adoption. This is due to <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a> and the increased distances between home and work brought about by urban sprawl.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Examples of automobile access issues in underdeveloped countries are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Paving of <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Federal_Highway_1" title="Mexican Federal Highway 1">Mexican Federal Highway 1</a> through <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>, completing the connection of <a href="/wiki/Cabo_San_Lucas" title="Cabo San Lucas">Cabo San Lucas</a> to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and convenient access to the outside world for villagers along the route. (occurred in the 1950s)</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, approximately 30 percent of the population does not have access to reliable all weather roads.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, 184 towns and 54,000 villages have no motor road (or roads at all)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The origin of <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> explosion has been hypothesized by <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control" title="Centers for Disease Control" class="mw-redirect">CDC</a> researchers to derive in part from more intensive social interactions afforded by new road networks in <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a> allowing more frequent travel from villagers to cities and higher density development of many African cities in the period 1950 to 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>The development of the automobile has contributed to changes in employment distribution, shopping patterns, social interactions, manufacturing priorities and <a href="/wiki/City_planning" title="City planning" class="mw-redirect">city planning</a>; increasing use of automobiles has reduced the roles of <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad" class="mw-redirect">railroads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancaster_suburbs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ancaster_suburbs.jpg/220px-Ancaster_suburbs.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ancaster_suburbs.jpg/330px-Ancaster_suburbs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Ancaster_suburbs.jpg/440px-Ancaster_suburbs.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancaster_suburbs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Sidewalkless</div>

<p>In addition to federal, state, and local dollars for roadway construction, car use was also encouraged through new <a href="/wiki/Zoning_laws" title="Zoning laws" class="mw-redirect">zoning laws</a> that required that any new business construct a certain amount of parking based on the size and type of facility. The effect of this was to create a massive quantity of free <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">parking</a> spaces and to push businesses further back from the road. Many shopping centers and <a href="/wiki/Suburbs" title="Suburbs" class="mw-redirect">suburbs</a> abandoned sidewalks altogether,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> making pedestrian access dangerous. This had the effect of encouraging people to drive, even for short trips that might have been walkable, thus increasing and solidifying American auto-dependency.<sup id="cite_ref-parking_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parking-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> As a result of this change, employment opportunities for people who were not wealthy enough to own a car and for people who could not drive, due to age or physical disabilities, became severely limited.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_changes">Cultural changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural changes">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural changes" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the appearance of the automobile, horses, walking and <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">streetcars</a> were the major <a href="/wiki/Modes_of_transportation" title="Modes of transportation" class="mw-redirect">modes of transportation</a> within cities.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Horses require a large amount of care, and were therefore kept in public facilities that were usually far from residences. The wealthy could afford to keep horses for private use, hence the term <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a> trade referred to elite patronage.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Horse <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a> left on the streets also created a <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> problem.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The automobile made regular medium-distance travel more convenient and affordable, especially in areas without railways. Because automobiles did not require rest, were faster than horse-drawn conveyances, and soon had a lower <a href="/wiki/Total_cost_of_ownership" title="Total cost of ownership">total cost of ownership</a>, more people were routinely able to travel farther than in earlier times. The construction of highways half a century later continued this revolution in mobility. Some experts suggest that many of these changes began during the earlier <i><a href="/wiki/Bicycle_craze" title="Bicycle craze" class="mw-redirect">Golden age of the bicycle</a></i>, from 1880—1915.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_to_urban_society">Changes to urban society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Changes to urban society">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Changes to urban society" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG/220px-Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG/330px-Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG/440px-Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG 2x" /></a>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ngaajyunlapgaau.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>Beginning in the 1940s, most urban environments in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> lost their <a href="/wiki/Streetcars" title="Streetcars" class="mw-redirect">streetcars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cable_car_(railway)" title="Cable car (railway)">cable cars</a>, and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a>, to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>-burning motor coaches or <a href="/wiki/Buses" title="Buses" class="mw-redirect">buses</a>. Many of these have never returned, though some urban communities eventually installed <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">subways</a>.</p>
<p>Another change brought about by the automobile is that modern urban pedestrians must be more alert than their ancestors. In the past, a pedestrian had to worry about relatively slow-moving streetcars or other obstacles of travel. With the proliferation of the automobile, a pedestrian has to anticipate safety risks of automobiles traveling at high speeds because they can cause serious injuries to a human and can be fatal,<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> unlike in previous times when traffic deaths were usually due to horses escaping control.</p>
<p>According to many social scientists, the loss of <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrian</a>-scale villages has also disconnected communities. Many people in developed countries have less contact with their neighbors and rarely walk unless they place a high value on <a href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise" class="mw-redirect">exercise</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fromhighway_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fromhighway-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advent_of_suburban_society">Advent of suburban society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advent of suburban society">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advent of suburban society" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improved transport accelerated the outward growth of cities and the development of <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a> beyond an earlier era's <a href="/wiki/Streetcar_suburb" title="Streetcar suburb">streetcar suburbs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Until the advent of the automobile, <a href="/wiki/Factory_worker" title="Factory worker" class="mw-redirect">factory workers</a> lived either close to the factory or in high density communities farther away, connected to the factory by <a href="/wiki/Streetcar" title="Streetcar" class="mw-redirect">streetcar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a>. The automobile and the federal <a href="/wiki/Subsidy#Infrastructure_subsidies" title="Subsidy">subsidies</a> for roads and suburban development that supported car culture allowed people to live in low density <a href="/wiki/Residential_area" title="Residential area">residential areas</a> even farther from the <a href="/wiki/City_center" title="City center" class="mw-redirect">city center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">integrated</a> city neighborhoods.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Few suburbs were <a href="/wiki/Factory_town" title="Factory town" class="mw-redirect">factory towns</a>, due in part to <a href="/wiki/Single_use_zoning" title="Single use zoning" class="mw-redirect">single use zoning</a>. Hence, they created few local jobs and residents commuted longer distances to work each day as the suburbs continued to expand.<sup id="cite_ref-asphalt_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asphalt-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Car_culture">Car culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Car culture">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Car culture" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The car had a significant effect on the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a>. As other vehicles had been, automobiles were incorporated into artworks including music, books and movies. Between 1905 and 1908, more than 120 songs were written in which the automobile was the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-crabgrass_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crabgrass-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Novels celebrating the political effects of motorization included <a href="/wiki/Free_Air" title="Free Air">Free Air</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sinclair_Lewis" title="Sinclair Lewis">Sinclair Lewis</a>, which followed in the tracks of earlier <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_touring#Social_significance" title="Bicycle touring">bicycle touring</a> novels. Where 19th century mass media had made heroes of <a href="/wiki/Casey_Jones" title="Casey Jones">Casey Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allan_Pinkerton" title="Allan Pinkerton">Allan Pinkerton</a> and other stalwart protectors of public transport, new <a href="/wiki/Road_movie" title="Road movie">road movies</a> offered heroes who found freedom and equality, rather than duty and hierarchy, on the open road.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WindmillSaabs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/WindmillSaabs.jpg/220px-WindmillSaabs.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/WindmillSaabs.jpg/330px-WindmillSaabs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/WindmillSaabs.jpg/440px-WindmillSaabs.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WindmillSaabs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
International Saab Club meeting in Latvia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> writes that widespread car culture has shifted voter's preference to the <a href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing" class="mw-redirect">right</a> of the political spectrum.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> He thinks that car culture has contributed to an increase in individualism and fewer social interactions between members of different socioeconomic classes.</p>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> became motorized, individuals, families and small groups were able to vacation in distant locations such as <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a>. Roads including the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Parkway" title="Blue Ridge Parkway">Blue Ridge Parkway</a> were built specifically to help the urban masses experience natural scenery previously seen only by a few. Cheap restaurants and <a href="/wiki/Motel" title="Motel">motels</a> appeared on favorite routes and provided wages for locals who were reluctant to join the trend to <a href="/wiki/Rural_depopulation" title="Rural depopulation" class="mw-redirect">rural depopulation</a>.</p>
<p>Since the early days of the automobile, the <a href="/wiki/American_Motor_League" title="American Motor League">American Motor League</a> promoted the making of more and better cars, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Automobile_Association" title="American Automobile Association" class="mw-redirect">American Automobile Association</a> joined the <a href="/wiki/Good_roads_movement" title="Good roads movement" class="mw-redirect">good roads movement</a> begun during the earlier <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_craze" title="Bicycle craze" class="mw-redirect">bicycle craze</a>. Fans of particular types and models formed <a href="/wiki/Car_club" title="Car club">car clubs</a> to celebrate and promote them. When manufacturers and petroleum fuel suppliers were well established, they also joined construction contractors in lobbying governments to build public roads.<sup id="cite_ref-asphalt_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asphalt-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Road building was sometimes also influenced by <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian-style</a> political ideologies. In Europe, massive freeway building programs were initiated by a number of <a href="/wiki/Social_democratic" title="Social democratic" class="mw-redirect">social democratic</a> governments after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, in an attempt to create jobs and make the automobile available to the working classes. From the 1970s, promotion of the automobile increasingly became a trait of some <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatives</a>. <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> mentioned a "great car economy" in the paper on <a href="/wiki/Roads_for_Prosperity" title="Roads for Prosperity">Roads for Prosperity</a>.</p>
<div class="rellink">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Automobile_folklore" title="Automobile folklore">Automobile folklore</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Safety">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Safety" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Motor vehicle <a href="/wiki/Accident" title="Accident">accidents</a> account for 37.5% of accidental deaths in the United States, making them the country's leading cause of accidental death.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Though travelers in cars suffer fewer deaths per journey, or per unit time or distance, than most other users of <a href="/wiki/Private_transport" title="Private transport">private transport</a> such as bicyclers or pedestrians, cars are also more used, making <a href="/wiki/Automobile_safety" title="Automobile safety">automobile safety</a> an important topic of study.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs">Costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Costs">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_the_automobile_on_societies&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Costs" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_automobile_usage" title="Economics of automobile usage">Economics of automobile usage</a></div>
<p>In countries such as the United States the <a href="/wiki/Transport_infrastructure" title="Transport infrastructure" class="mw-redirect">infrastructure</a> that makes car use possible, such as highways, roads and parking lots is funded by the government and supported through zoning and construction requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-Shoup_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shoup-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fuel_taxes_in_the_United_States" title="Fuel taxes in the United States">Fuel taxes in the United States</a> cover about 60% of highway construction and repair costs, but little of the cost to construct or repair local roads.<sup id="cite_ref-Vuchic_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vuchic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> Payments by motor-vehicle users fall short of government expenditures tied to motor-vehicle use by 20–70 cents per gallon of gas.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">Zoning</a> laws in many areas require that large, <a href="/wiki/Free_parking" title="Free parking" class="mw-redirect">free parking</a> lots accompany any new buildings. Municipal parking lots are often free or do not charge a <a href="/wiki/Market_rate" title="Market rate">market rate</a>. Hence, the cost of driving a car in the US is subsidized, supported by businesses and the government who cover the cost of roads and parking.<sup id="cite_ref-Shoup_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shoup-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This government support of the automobile through subsidies for infrastructure, the cost of <a href="/wiki/Highway_patrol" title="Highway patrol">highway patrol</a> enforcement, recovering <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle_theft" title="Motor vehicle theft">stolen cars</a>, and many other factors makes public transport a less economically competitive choice for commuters when considering <a href="/wiki/Out-of-pocket_expenses" title="Out-of-pocket expenses">Out-of-pocket expenses</a>. Consumers often make choices based on those costs, and underestimate the <a href="/wiki/Indirect_costs" title="Indirect costs">indirect costs</a> of car ownership, insurance and maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-Vuchic_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vuchic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> However, globally and in some US cities, tolls and parking fees partially offset these heavy subsidies for driving. <a href="/wiki/Transportation_planning" title="Transportation planning">Transportation planning</a> policy advocates often support tolls, increased fuel taxes, <a href="/wiki/Congestion_pricing" title="Congestion pricing">congestion pricing</a> and market-rate pricing for municipal parking as a means of balancing car use in urban centers with more efficient modes such as buses and trains.</p>
<p>When cities charge <a href="/wiki/Market_rates" title="Market rates" class="mw-redirect">market rates</a> for parking, and when bridges and tunnels are tolled, driving becomes less competitive in terms of out-of-pocket costs. When municipal parking is underpriced and roads are not tolled, most of the cost of vehicle usage is paid for by general government revenue, a subsidy for motor vehicle use. The size of this subsidy dwarfs the federal, state, and local subsidies for the maintenance of infrastructure and discounted fares for public transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-Vuchic_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vuchic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By contrast, although there are environmental and social costs for rail, there is a very small impact.<sup id="cite_ref-Vuchic_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vuchic-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, out of pocket expenses for car ownership can vary considerably based on the state in which you live. In 2013, annual car ownership costs including repair, insurance, gas and taxes were highest in Georgia ($4,233) and lowest in Oregon ($2,024) with a national average of $3,201. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Data provided by the AAA indicates <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_ownership" title="Cost of ownership" class="mw-redirect">cost of ownership</a> in the United States is rising about 2% per year. <sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
