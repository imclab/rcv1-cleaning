				<h1 class="entry-title">The Difference Between Art and Entertainment</h1> 
		
		<div class="post-info">by <span class="author vcard"><span class="fn"><a href="http://goinswriter.com/author/jeff-goins/" class="fn n" title="Jeff Goins" rel="author">Jeff Goins</a></span></span> | <span class="post-comments"><span class="post-comments"><a href="http://goinswriter.com/art-and-entertainment/#comments"><span class="dsq-postid" rel="6898 http://goinswriter.com/?p=6898">95 Comments</span></a></span></span>  | <a href="http://twitter.com/jeffgoins" class="blank">Twitter</a>, <a href="http://facebook.com/goinswriter" class="blank">Facebook</a>, <a href="http://gplus.to/jeffgoins" class="blank">Google+</a></div>		<div class="entry-content">
			<a id="dd_start"></a><p>Art, they say, is in the eye of the beholder. Which is a nice way of saying it&#8217;s whatever you want it to be. But I don&#8217;t believe that. Although I don&#8217;t have an objective perspective on art (nobody does), I believe there <em>is</em> such a thing as good and bad creative work.</p>
<div id="attachment_6994" class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 580px"><a href="http://goinswriter.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/art-and-entertainment.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-6994" title="Art and Entertainment" src="http://goinswriter.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/art-and-entertainment-570x427.jpg" alt="Art Institute in Chicago" width="570" height="427" /></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Photo credit: Mark Heard (Creative Commons)</p></div>
<p>But maybe that&#8217;s asking too much â for us to label art &#8220;good&#8221; or &#8220;bad.&#8221; And that&#8217;s fine, I guess. But what is <em>not</em> okay is calling something &#8220;art&#8221; when it&#8217;s not. When it&#8217;s something else.</p>
<p>My friend <a title="Stephen Proctor" href="http://twitter.com/worshipvj" target="_blank">Stephen Proctor</a> pointed this out to me recently (quoting Mako Fujimura, I think). The difference between art and entertainment is a subtle, but important one:</p>
<blockquote><p><em>Entertainment gives you a predictable pleasure&#8230; Art leads to transformation.</em></p></blockquote>
<p>If that&#8217;s true, then I&#8217;ve got a problem with a lot of what we call &#8220;art.&#8221; Because it&#8217;s not changing us. At best, it&#8217;s inebriating us to the pain of the world. Which is not the point.</p>
<p>Art is supposed to transforms us â here&#8217;s how:</p>
<ul>
<li>It surprises us.</li>
<li>It wounds us.</li>
<li>It changes us.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Entertainment makes us feel good.</strong> It doesn&#8217;t surprise us. It meets our expectations. And that&#8217;s why we like it: it coddles us. But the problem with entertainment is it leaves us unchanged. And we desperately need to change.</p>
<p><strong>Art, on the other hand, transforms us.</strong> How does it do that? It beautifully wounds us â breaks our hearts, causes us to cry, and shows us our own inadequacies. Art forces us to make a choice. It does exactly what we don&#8217;t expect.</p>
<p>And that&#8217;s how art changes us.</p>
<p>So the question, dear artist, is this: Are you creating predictable work that doesn&#8217;t surprise? That doesn&#8217;t wound? That ultimately doesn&#8217;t change <em>anything</em>? What, then, are you creating?</p>
<p>It may be propaganda. It may be advertising. It may even be entertainment. But it&#8217;s probably not art.</p>
<p><strong>What do you think?</strong> Share in the <a title="Comments" href="http://goinswriter.com/art-and-entertainment#disqus_thread" target="_blank">comments</a>.</p>

Entertainment is there to entertain, and while you&#8217;ll once in a while get pseudo-profound ideas in entertainment, none of the work of entertainment is designed to express those ideas. <p>So, when a writer, musician, painter, poet, choreographer, etc conceives of a work of art to express a certain emotion/idea and creates the characters/plot/colors/structure/composition/etc that transfers the point (the certain emotion/idea) to the audience, you get a work of art. How well the artist&#8217;s selection of character/plot/color/structure/composition convey the point determines how good the work of art ends up being.</p>
<p>As for the audience, not much can be said about them. They might understand the language of the art (ie figurative painting) or they might be confronted with a language they do not know, understand, or care to learnÂ (ie cubism).</p>

    <li class="comment even thread-odd thread-alt depth-1" id="dsq-comment-43993">
        <div id="dsq-comment-header-43993" class="dsq-comment-header">

        <div id="dsq-comment-body-43993" class="dsq-comment-body">
            <div id="dsq-comment-message-43993" class="dsq-comment-message"><p>Entertainment can certainly surprise, wound and change us. That&#8217;s all part of being entertained. This need to separate art from entertainment is pointless, let alone impossible. And to say that &#8220;we need to change&#8221; is pure opinion&#8211;not one I disagree with, but an opinion nonetheless and, therefore, irrelevant to this issue, seeing as we can only use objectivity to settle this matter.</p>
<p>The only difference between art and entertainment is the observer&#8217;s perspective. The piece in and of itself is neither art nor entertainment. It simply IS. The art/entertainment happens within the beholder. That&#8217;s the beauty of it. Even the artist&#8217;s intentions are irrelevant in the attempt to distinguish between art and entertainment as they are known only to the artist him/herself. Therefore, an objective distinction cannot be made.</p>
<p>I don&#8217;t know about you, but when Rachel got off the plane to stay with Ross I cried like a baby. I also cry like a baby every time I see Juliet cradle Romeo&#8217;s body and take her own life. People have to learn to appreciate things for WHAT THEY ARE. Friends is not trying to be Shakespeare. It&#8217;s trying to be Friends. Both Romeo and Juliet and Friends can be engaging. Both can make us laugh, cry, and even take a harder look at ourselves. </p>
<p>Anything can contain societal relevance, even if none was intended. It&#8217;s all a matter of the viewer&#8217;s perspective. Go to see any show on Broadway, be it Mamma Mia or A Streetcar Named Desire. You will not find a leaflet in either playbill classifying either piece as art or entertainment. No announcement will be made prior to the show. Why? Because there is no standard by which to measure what qualifies as art and what qualifies as entertainment. If there was a standard, then there wouldn&#8217;t be much relevant art post-Shakespeare because the man said just about everything there is for a human being to say more eloquently and thoroughly than anyone else could possibly say it. </p>
<p>Dramatic artists are particularly guilty of attempting to divide art from entertainment. It has been said that good drama pits two truths against each other in an effort to raise questions, and therefore social awareness in regards to a particular issue.  It is said to &#8220;illuminate the human condition&#8221;. First of all, while that may create great drama,  there is no such thing as &#8220;truth&#8221; as it relates to a societal point of view. That&#8217;s why they are viewpoints and not facts. Writers are just people. It is pretentious to claim that these people are the holders of great societal truths which we, the public, need to be taught. It implies a (nonexistent) separation between writer and the public. It also implies a general ignorance among the public, which I resent. The fact that someone has  had their words published in a book doesn&#8217;t make those words true.</p>
<p>Secondly, this phrase: &#8220;to illuminate the human condition&#8221; is a senseless, meaningless cliche&#8217; perpetuated by so called &#8220;artists&#8221; with inflated senses of self importance to convince themselves and others that  they are providing a noble service to society. We are all human and therefore do not need to be made aware of something which we are all, by nature, already privy to. I don&#8217;t need to see a play to know that people lie, cheat, steal, rape, murder, love, hate, etc. I don&#8217;t need a movie to reiterate that people have both good and evil within them. I don&#8217;t need to be made aware of the consequences of our actions through a piece of  crafted by an author with an agenda. I don&#8217;t even need &#8220;art&#8221; to imagine another person&#8217;s point of view. We know these things through our own observations. In fact, the human condition is all we know. It would be one thing if art could illuminate, say, the squirrel condition seeing as we have no subjective understanding of what it is to be a squirrel. But alas, even if another creature HAD the means to adequately express and communicate their experience, it would not bring us any closer to a subjective understanding as we are human and can only experience the human condition. Whether the artist intended us to see something human in their work or not, we WILL and we MUST because we only have our perspectives and experiences to compare it to. Therefore, it is senseless and irrelevant to state that art illuminates the human condition because the illumination of the human condition, intentional or otherwise, is present to some degree in anything and everything humans participate in. Even if you take a less literal approach and say that it illuminates different parts of the human condition to people who may not be aware, I will say to you that it is useless because, firstly if you can experience something I cannot, then you&#8217;re not illuminating anything by attempting to make me aware of it. Secondly, an audience&#8217;s preexisting knowledge of the human condition is REQUIRED in order to be engage that audience. Yes, I may not know what it is like to, say go to prison for 19 years for stealing a loaf of bread, but the particular circumstances of the piece (Les Miserables, in this particular case) are irrelevant. It is the emotional experience of the character that I am meant to relate to. If the circumstances were the primary means of engaging an audience, Les Miserables would not be the wildly acclaimed piece of literature it is, because even at the time of it&#8217;s publication, hardly anyone would be able to directly relate it. Clearly, this is not the case. I do not relate to Jean Valjean because I know what it&#8217;s like to be sent to prison by a harsh legal system or even what it is like to struggle financially. I relate to him because I can appreciate and subjectively understand the FEELINGS of desperation, rage, and heartache he experiences as a RESULT of the circumstances. </p>
<p>Art engages an audience. Entertainment engaged an audience. That is their primary function. Euclid stated that two things which are equal to the same thing are equal to each other. Therefore, I submit to you that art and entertainment are the same thing. We simply use two different words to describe it.</p>
<p>To try to make a distinction between art and entertainment is to deprive the individual of their subjective experience. We all have different preferences. Personally, I find Hamlet and Spider-Man equally engaging&#8211;that does not make me unenlightened, unintelligent, or uncultured. It means that I am willing to accept and enjoy things for what they are and for what I see in them.</p>
</div>
