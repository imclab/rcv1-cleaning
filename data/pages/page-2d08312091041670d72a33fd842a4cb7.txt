  <div class="title">
    <h1>Send in the Clowns: Humor Abuse Reveals the Man Behind the Rubber Nose</h1>
  </div>
  <div style="clear:right"></div>
</div>

<div class="resizable">

  <div class="meta">
    <div class="content-published-date" >
      Published Oct  6, 2011, 12:30pm
  </div>
</div>


      <div class="meta">
        <span id="byline">
          By <a href="/producers/laura-dannen">Laura Dannen</a>
      </span>
    </div>

</div>

    <div class="article-body resizable ">
  
<div class="inline-image-left inline-image mceNonEditable" data-image-id="12177" data-include-caption="true" data-layout="inline-image-left">
<div align="center">
  <a class="lightbox" caption="&lt;p&gt;Cirque du Soleil alum Lorenzo Pisoni stars in &lt;em&gt;Humor Abuse,&lt;/em&gt; now at Seattle Rep.&lt;/p&gt;" href="/data/images/2012/5/image/12177/HumorAbuse044r.jpg">
    <img alt="humor abuse" src="/images/change?src=%2Fdata%2Fimages%2F2012%2F5%2Fimage%2F12177%2FHumorAbuse044r.jpg&amp;cropify=400x600%2B0%2B0&amp;resize=200x%3E"></a>

    <div class="inline-image-caption" style="width: 200px">
      
        <p>Cirque du Soleil alum Lorenzo Pisoni stars in <em>Humor Abuse,</em> now at Seattle Rep.</p>
    </div>
</div>
</div>
<p>I know what you’re thinking: <strong>I don’t like clowns.</strong> Why would I like a play about the son of a professional clown? Because said son is 1) handsome, even when wearing a red rubber nose, 2) a master of falling down stairs/off ladders/through trapdoors and making it look easy, and 3) a talented Cirque du Soleil and Broadway alum with the kind of true story you can’t make up.</p>
<p>Sure, Lorenzo Pisoni’s solo show <a href="/arts-and-entertainment/find-an-event/#/expand:-1/filters:*/name:Humor%20Abuse/date:2011-09-07/info:72126/"><strong><em>Humor Abuse,</em></strong></a> his coming-of-age tale coadapted by friend and director Erica Schmidt, clowns around a bit, but each incredible pratfall is delivered with a sad smile and a sigh, as evidence of a life Pisoni can’t escape. <strong>“I was born to be my father’s straight man,”</strong> he says. Or his protege: At age 2, little Lorenzo wandered into the ring of the Pickle Family Circus at intermission and made his “uninvited debut.” By age 6, he was a contracted member of the clown show. He spent a childhood inside a trunk on his father’s back, dressing as a gorilla, and juggling clubs till his hands bled. <strong>He loved it.</strong> But it’s not always easy growing up with a father who thinks it’s funny to put a plastic banana in your lunchbox. Every day.</p>
<p>With the help of family photos projected onto a backdrop and a dry sense of humor about the “abuse” he sustained as a child, Pisoni wraps us up in his colorful past, and gets us to think about the man behind the clown makeup. Turns out that smile is painted on for a reason.</p>
<p><em>Humor Abuse is at Seattle Repertory Theatre through Oct 23.</em></p>
