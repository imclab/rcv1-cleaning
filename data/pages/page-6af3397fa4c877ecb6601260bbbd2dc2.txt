<h1>
<span>Car buying scams</span>
</h1>
<h2>
<span>Offers that are too good to be true usually are</span>
</h2>
<script src="/resources/js/social.js" type="text/javascript"></script>
<div>
<div style="float: left;" data-show-faces="false" data-width="107" data-layout="button_count" data-send="false" class="fb-like"></div>
<div>
<div data-size="medium" class="g-plusone"></div>
<a data-count="horizontal" data-lang="en" class="twitter-share-button" href="//twitter.com/share">Tweet</a>
</div>
</div>
<p>
<span></span>
</p>
<span><img class="displayBlock" src="/resources/images/motoring_advice/plastic-car-keys-440.jpg" title="If a deal looks too good to be true it probably is" alt="If a deal looks too good to be true it probably is"></span>
<p class="detail">
<span>If a deal looks too good to be true it probably is</span>
</p>
<div class="article-description"><p>You need to be alert to the possibility of being scammed when buying a car. Remember above all else that if a deal looks too good to be true it probably is. Car buying scams cost consumers around &pound;3 million a year.</p>
<!--
<p>Major car advertisers including Auto Trader, eBay Motors, and Exchange &amp; Mart have joined forces with the Metropolitan Police to form the <a href="http://www.vstag.co.uk/" mce_href="http://www.vstag.co.uk/">Vehicle Safe Trading Advisory Group</a> (VSTAG), an industry forum to combat vehicle related fraud.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.vstag.co.uk/node/22" mce_href="http://www.vstag.co.uk/node/22">Case histories and advice &raquo;</a></p>
-->
<p>Check the <a href="http://www.met.police.uk/fraudalert/">Metropolitan Police fraud alert</a> for the latest information on fraud and other economic crime to help you avoid becoming a victim.</p></div>
</div><div class="checkingPane">
<div class="left-column">
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><h3>Virtual vehicle scams</h3>
<p>The 'virtual vehicle' scam involves fake shipping websites that promise to handle and look after your money.</p>
<p>Once you respond and express interest in buying the car &ndash; which is currently located abroad &ndash; you are directed to a fake website for a shipping company that will handle the transfer of funds including shipping costs.</p>
<p>In theory the shipping company will transfer the funds to the vehicle seller when you have taken delivery and confirm that you're happy with the vehicle.</p>
<p>In practice, the funds may be transferred but the vehicle never appears.</p></div>
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><div class="highlightBox">
<h3>Advice - virtual vehicle scams</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>If an offer sounds too good to be true it probably is</strong></li>
<li>Don't hand over money to someone you don't know for a vehicle you've not seen<br />Always get a vehicle history check for peace of mind.</li>
<li>Put yourself in the sellers position: 
<ul>
<li>Would you price your vehicle well below the standard market value, and then - at additional expense - offer to ship it overseas, and - if the buyer doesn't like it - ship it back at your own expense?</li>
<li>Would you ask a complete stranger in the street to hold several thousands pounds of your money whilst you wait for another complete stranger to deliver a car to you?</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>'Paper cars' are another version of this &ndash; websites with copied adverts from elsewhere on the web, but with knock-down prices. Again, don't part with your money until you've seen the vehicle.</li>
<li>The Metropolitan Police and Western Union Bank both recommend that consumers use money transfers as a means to purchase goods only when sending money to someone they already know and trust</li>
</ul>
</div></div>
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><h3>Vehicle matching scams</h3>
<p>These work by approaching owners who are selling their cars and promising falsely to match them with 'definite buyers' &ndash; consumers lose nearly &pound;3 million a year according to the Office of Fair Trading (OFT)</p>
<p>Typically while advertising a car for sale in a magazine, newspaper or online you're cold-called by telephone. The caller promises that they already have buyers lined-up who are looking to buy the same model &ndash; all you have to do is pay a matcher's fee up-front before the buyer is introduced and the sale completed.</p>
<p>Often though <strong>there is no buyer, the contract with the vehicle matcher cannot be cancelled, and any money paid is lost</strong>.</p>
<p>In 2008, Consumer Direct received more than 1600 complaints about this type of scam from private sellers of second hand cars who had each lost between &pound;80 and &pound;99. The true number of victims is probably much larger.</p>
<p>The OFT has been working with the police, local authority Trading Standards Services and consumer bodies to crack down on car matching scammers.</p>
<p>The OFT is also working with online and print motoring publications and websites who are members of VSTAG to put in place clear and prominent warnings to car sellers about these scams.</p></div>
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><div class="highlightBox">
<h3>Advice - vehicle matching scams</h3>
<ul>
<li>Stop, think and be sceptical if you are <strong>cold called</strong> and asked for money in advance</li>
<li>Don't give your credit or debit card details to people you don't know</li>
<li>Stop, think and be sceptical if promises are made that give the impression that: 
<ul>
<li>there are immediate buyers for your car</li>
<li>finance has already been arranged for potential buyers</li>
<li>buyers are willing to pay your asking price or more</li>
<li>buyers are ready to view your car immediately, or</li>
<li>you will be offered a refund if the car does not sell</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Don't be pressured into anything - if in any doubt about a particular telephone call, hang up</li>
<li><strong>If it sounds too good to be true it probably is</strong></li>
</ul>
</div></div>
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><p>If you think you have been the victim of a vehicle matching scam, or you suspect a scam, <a href="http://www.adviceguide.org.uk">contact the Citizen's Advice Bureau</a> in the first instance for advice.</p></div>
<div class="displayInline rteContainer"><hr />
