<p><b>Humour</b> or <b>humor</b> (see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-our.2C_-or" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling differences</a>) is the tendency of particular cognitive experiences to provoke <a href="/wiki/Laughter" title="Laughter">laughter</a> and provide <a href="/wiki/Amusement" title="Amusement">amusement</a>. The term derives from the <a href="/wiki/Humorism" title="Humorism">humoural medicine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" title="Ancient Greeks" class="mw-redirect">ancient Greeks</a>, which taught that the balance of fluids in the human body, known as humours (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>humor</i>, "body fluid"), control human health and emotion.</p>
<p>People of all ages and cultures respond to humour. The majority of people are able to experience humour, i.e., to be amused, to laugh or smile at something funny, and thus they are considered to have a <b>sense of humour</b>. The hypothetical person lacking a sense of humour would likely find the behaviour induced by humour to be inexplicable, strange, or even irrational. Though ultimately decided by personal <a href="/wiki/Taste_(aesthetics)" title="Taste (aesthetics)" class="mw-redirect">taste</a>, the extent to which a person will find something humorous depends upon a host of variables, including <a href="/wiki/Geographical_location" title="Geographical location" class="mw-redirect">geographical location</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maturity_(psychological)" title="Maturity (psychological)">maturity</a>, level of <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligence</a> and <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/context" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:context">context</a>. For example, young children may favour <a href="/wiki/Slapstick" title="Slapstick">slapstick</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Punch_and_Judy" title="Punch and Judy">Punch and Judy</a> puppet shows or cartoons such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_and_Jerry" title="Tom and Jerry">Tom and Jerry</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">Satire</a> may rely more on understanding the target of the humour and thus tends to appeal to more mature audiences.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_of_humour">Theories of humour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories of humour">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories of humour" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theories_of_humour" title="Theories of humour" class="mw-redirect">Theories of humour</a></div>
<p>Many theories exist about what humour is and what social function it serves. The prevailing types of theories attempting to account for the existence of humour include <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychological</a> theories, the vast majority of which consider humour-induced behaviour to be very healthy; spiritual theories, which may, for instance, consider humour to be a "gift from God"; and theories which consider humour to be an unexplainable mystery, very much like a <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystical experience</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_humour">Views on humour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Views on humour">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Views on humour" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some claim that humour cannot or should not be explained. Author <a href="/wiki/E.B._White" title="E.B. White" class="mw-redirect">E.B. White</a> once said, "Humor can be dissected as a frog can, but the thing dies in the process and the innards are discouraging to any but the pure scientific mind."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> lamented the misuse of the term "humour" (a <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> from <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>) to mean any type of <a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedy</a>. However, both "humour" and "comic" are often used when theorising about the subject. The connotations of "humour" as opposed to "comic" are said to be that of response versus stimulus. Additionally, "humour" was thought to include a combination of ridiculousness and wit in an individual; the paradigmatic case being Shakespeare's Sir John Falstaff. The French were slow to adopt the term "humour"; in French, "humeur" and "humour" are still two different words, the former referring to a person's <a href="/wiki/Mood_(psychology)" title="Mood (psychology)">mood</a> or to the archaic concept of the four <a href="/wiki/Four_Temperaments" title="Four Temperaments" class="mw-redirect">humours</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Non-satirical humour can be specifically termed "droll humor" or "recreational drollery".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greece">Ancient Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Greece">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Greece" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Western humour theory begins with <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>, who attributed to <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">Socrates</a> (as a semi-historical dialogue character) in the <i><a href="/wiki/Philebus" title="Philebus">Philebus</a></i> (p.&#160;49b) the view that the essence of the ridiculous is an ignorance in the weak, who are thus unable to retaliate when ridiculed. Later, in Greek philosophy, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a></i> (1449a, pp.&#160;34–35), suggested that an ugliness that does not disgust is fundamental to humour.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: India">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: India" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In ancient <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_drama" title="Sanskrit drama">Sanskrit drama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bharata_Muni" title="Bharata Muni">Bharata Muni</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Natya_Shastra" title="Natya Shastra">Natya Shastra</a></i> defined humour (<i>hāsyam</i>) as one of the nine <i><a href="/wiki/Nava_rasas" title="Nava rasas" class="mw-redirect">nava rasas</a></i>, or principle <i><a href="/wiki/Rasa_(aesthetics)" title="Rasa (aesthetics)">rasas</a></i> (emotional responses), which can be inspired in the audience by <i>bhavas</i>, the imitations of emotions that the actors perform. Each <i>rasa</i> was associated with a specific <i><a href="/wiki/Bhava" title="Bhava">bhavas</a></i> portrayed on stage. In the case of humour, it was associated with mirth (<i>hasya</i>).<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Arabic_culture">In Arabic culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Arabic culture">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Arabic culture" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The terms "<a href="/wiki/Comedy" title="Comedy">comedy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a>" became synonymous after Aristotle's <i>Poetics</i> was translated into <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Islamic world</a>, where it was elaborated upon by <a href="/wiki/Arabic_literature" title="Arabic literature">Arabic writers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Islamic_philosophy" title="Early Islamic philosophy">Islamic philosophers</a> such as Abu Bischr, his pupil <a href="/wiki/Al-Farabi" title="Al-Farabi">Al-Farabi</a>, Persian <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>. Due to cultural differences, they disassociated comedy from <a href="/wiki/Greek_drama" title="Greek drama" class="mw-redirect">Greek dramatic</a> representation, and instead identified it with Arabic poetic themes and forms, such as <i>hija</i> (satirical poetry). They viewed comedy as simply the "art of reprehension" and made no reference to light and cheerful events or troublesome beginnings and happy endings associated with classical Greek comedy. After the <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">Latin translations of the 12th century</a>, the term "comedy" thus gained a new semantic meaning in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_literature" title="Medieval literature">Medieval literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caribbean" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mento" title="Mento">Mento</a> star <a href="/wiki/Lord_Flea" title="Lord Flea">Lord Flea</a>, stated in an 1957 interview that he thought that: "<a href="/wiki/West_Indians" title="West Indians" class="mw-redirect">West Indians</a> have the best sense of humour in the world. Even the most solemn song, like <i>Las Kean Fine</i> ["Lost and Can Not Be Found"], which tells of a boiler explosion on a sugar plantation that killed several of the workers, their natural wit and humor shine though."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_demographics">Social demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social demographics">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social demographics" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with any form of art, acceptance depends on social demographics and varies from person to person. Throughout history, comedy has been used as a form of entertainment all over the world, whether in the courts of the Western kings or the villages of the Far East. Both a social etiquette and a certain intelligence can be displayed through forms of wit and sarcasm. Eighteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> author <a href="/wiki/Georg_Lichtenberg" title="Georg Lichtenberg" class="mw-redirect">Georg Lichtenberg</a> said that "the more you know humour, the more you become demanding in fineness."<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour.2C_place_and_size">Behaviour, place and size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Behaviour, place and size">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Behaviour, place and size" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a> explains in his lecture in the documentary "<i><a href="/wiki/Funny_Business_(TV_series)" title="Funny Business (TV series)">Funny Business</a></i>"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> that an object or a person can become funny in three ways. They are:</p>
<ul>
<li>By behaving in an unusual way</li>
<li>By being in an unusual place</li>
<li>By being the wrong size</li>
</ul>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Sight_gag" title="Sight gag" class="mw-redirect">sight gags</a> fit into one or more of these categories.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exaggeration">Exaggeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Exaggeration">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Exaggeration" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exaggeration#Humour" title="Exaggeration">Exaggeration#Humour</a></div>
<p>"Some theoreticians of the comic consider exaggeration to be a universal comic device".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> It may take different forms in different genres, but all rely on the fact that "the easiest way to make things laughable is to exaggerate to the point of absurdity their salient traits".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humour_and_culture">Humour and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Humour and culture">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humour&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Humour and culture" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different cultures have different expectations of humour so comedy shows are not always successful when transplanted into another culture. For example, a 2004 <a href="/wiki/BBC_News" title="BBC News">BBC News</a> article discusses a stereotype among <a href="/wiki/British_humour" title="British humour">British comedians</a> that Americans and Germans do not understand <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a>, and therefore UK <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcoms</a> are not appreciated by them.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
