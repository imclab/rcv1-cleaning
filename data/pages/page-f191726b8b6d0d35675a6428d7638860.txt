<p>An <b>Ethernet crossover cable</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> cable used to connect computing devices together directly. Normal <i>straight through</i> or <i>patch</i> cables were used to connect from a host <a href="/wiki/Network_interface_controller" title="Network interface controller">network interface controller</a> (a computer or similar device) to a <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">network switch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Network_hub" title="Network hub" class="mw-redirect">hub</a> or <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a>. A cable with connections that "cross over" was used to connect two devices of the same type: two hosts or two switches to each other. Owing to the inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Auto-MDIX" title="Auto-MDIX" class="mw-redirect">Auto-MDIX</a> capability, modern implementations of the <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_over_twisted_pair" title="Ethernet over twisted pair">Ethernet over twisted pair</a> standards usually no longer require the use of crossover cables.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethernet_crossover_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethernet_crossover_cable&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG/220px-Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG/330px-Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG/440px-Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crossover_Adapter_DSC01805.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/10BASE-T" title="10BASE-T" class="mw-redirect">10BASE-T</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fast_ethernet#100BASE-TX" title="Fast ethernet" class="mw-redirect">100BASE-TX</a> Ethernet standards use one wire pair for transmission in each direction. By convention, one wire of the pair is designated "+" and the other "-". Following <a href="/wiki/TRS_connector" title="TRS connector" class="mw-redirect">traditional telephone</a> terminology, the + signal from each pair connects to the tip conductor, and the - signal is connected to the ring conductor. This requires that the transmit pair of each device be connected to the receive pair of the device on the other end. When a <a href="/wiki/Terminal_(telecommunication)" title="Terminal (telecommunication)">terminal</a> device is connected to a switch or hub, this crossover is done internally in the switch or hub. A standard <i>straight through</i> cable is used for this purpose where each pin of the connector on one end is connected to the corresponding pin on the other connector.</p>
<p>One <a href="/wiki/Network_interface_controller" title="Network interface controller">network interface controller</a> may be connected directly to another without the use of a switch or hub, but in that case the crossover must be done externally in the cable or modular crossover adapter in a manner similar to how the <a href="/wiki/Null_modem" title="Null modem">null modem</a> was used to directly connect two <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">teleprinters</a>. Since 10BASE-T and 100BASE-TX use pairs 2 and 3, these two pairs must be swapped in the cable. This is a <i>crossover cable</i>. A crossover cable was also used to connect two hubs or two switches on their <a href="/wiki/Upstream_(networking)" title="Upstream (networking)">upstream</a> ports .</p>
<p>Because the only difference between the <a href="/wiki/T568A" title="T568A" class="mw-redirect">T568A</a> and <a href="/wiki/T568B" title="T568B" class="mw-redirect">T568B</a> pin/pair assignments are that pairs 2 and 3 are swapped, a crossover cable may be envisioned as a cable with one <a href="/wiki/Modular_connector" title="Modular connector">modular connector</a> following T568A and the other T568B (see <a href="/wiki/Registered_jack#Crossover_Wiring" title="Registered jack">Jack crossover wiring</a>). Such a cable will work for 10BASE-T or 100BASE-TX. <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">Gigabit Ethernet</a> (and an early <a href="/wiki/Fast_Ethernet" title="Fast Ethernet">Fast Ethernet</a> variant, <a href="/wiki/Fast_ethernet#100BASE-T4" title="Fast ethernet" class="mw-redirect">100BASE-T4</a>) use all four pairs and also requires the other two pairs (1 and 4) to be swapped. This meant common crossover cables available in the retail market were usually <i>not</i> compatible with the Gigabit Ethernet convention, but newer crossover cables could be made that worked for all speeds. The polarity of each pair is not swapped, but the pairs crossed as a unit: the two wires within each pair are <i>not</i> crossed.<sup id="cite_ref-Spurgeon_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spurgeon-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethernet_MDI_crossover.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>

<p>In practice, it does not matter if non-crossover Ethernet cables are wired as T568A or T568B, just so long as both ends follow the same wiring format. Typical commercially available "pre-wired" cables can follow either format depending the manufacturer. What this means is that one manufacturer's cables are wired one way and another's the other way, yet both are correct and will work. In either case, T568A or T568B, a normal (un-crossed) cable will have <b>both</b> ends wired according to the layout in the <i>Connection 1</i> column.</p>
<p>Although the Gigabit crossover is defined in the Gigabit Ethernet standard,<sup id="cite_ref-gigx_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gigx-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> in practice all Gigabit PHYs feature an auto-MDIX capability and are designed for compatibility with the existing 100BASE-TX crossovers. The IEEE-specified Gigabit crossover is generally seen as unnecessary.</p>
<p>Certain equipment or installations, including those in which phone and/or power are mixed with data in the same cable, may require that the "non-data" pairs 1 and 4 (pins 4, 5, 7 and 8) remain un-crossed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Automatic_crossover">Automatic crossover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethernet_crossover_cable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Automatic crossover">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethernet_crossover_cable&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Automatic crossover" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Auto-MDIX" title="Auto-MDIX" class="mw-redirect">Auto-MDIX</a></div>
<p>Introduced in 1998, this made the distinction between uplink and normal ports and manual selector switches on older hubs and switches obsolete.<sup id="cite_ref-dove_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dove-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> If one or both of two connected devices has the automatic MDI/MDI-X configuration feature there is no need for crossover cables.</p>
<p>Although Auto-MDIX was specified as an optional feature in the <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">1000BASE-T standard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-gigx_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gigx-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> in practice it is implemented widely on most interfaces.</p>
<p>Besides the eventually agreed upon <i>Automatic MDI/MDI-X</i>, this feature may also be referred to by various vendor-specific terms including: <i>Auto uplink and trade</i>, <i>Universal Cable Recognition</i> and <i>Auto Sensing</i>.</p>
