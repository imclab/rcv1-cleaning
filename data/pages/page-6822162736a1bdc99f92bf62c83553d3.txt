<h3 class='post-title entry-title'>
Paint Complains: Has automotive paint gone down in quality?
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-1322642377716686287'>
<a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__9TTj0s27Vk/SdKVVlJcRZI/AAAAAAAAA7Q/mEOiPpY8xjQ/s1600-h/Paint+Complaints.jpg"><img style="display:block; margin:0px auto 10px; text-align:center;cursor:pointer; cursor:hand;width: 400px; height: 180px;" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/__9TTj0s27Vk/SdKVVlJcRZI/AAAAAAAAA7Q/mEOiPpY8xjQ/s400/Paint+Complaints.jpg" border="0" alt="Paint Complaints"id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5319478307859482002" /></a><br />Over the past, years, I seem to hear more gripes about thin, easily chipped paint on new cars. Toyotas, Honda, Suzuki, Subaru, etc.&#8212;I read paint complaints on various forums including Yarisworld.com, FitFreak.net, SX4club.com and others. So what gives? Has paint really gotten &#8220;thinner&#8221; as of late? Is it a different material? Is it worse quality than it used to be?<br /><br />I can vouch for having a lot of rock chips on both of my newer Japanese-made cars. My 2007 Toyota Yaris liftback and my brand-spakin&#8217;-new 2009 Suzuki SX4 are both chipped up. The Yaris rarely sees gravel/sand, but has still racked up a good amount of paint chips. The SX4, which has been on gravel-covered roads multiple times in the last month (I take it skiing), looks like it&#8217;s been pelted with gravel for a year or more. My 2005 Scion xB also was pitted badly. Then again, it had a decent amount of square frontal space to get chipped.<br /><br />A couple of searches on Google revealed that many (most?) auto manufacturers are now using a water-based paints instead of solvent-based finishes. The water-based paints release fewer VOCs, or Volatile Organic Compounds, which are bad for the environment. But are these water-based finishes less durable than solvent-based coatings? Just about every Web site I&#8217;ve found has said the water-based coatings are as durable as the older solvent-based finishes.  <br /><br />Most cars are painted with a base coat/clear coat system. This means a colored coat of paint is laid down, and then a clear coat of paint is put on top of that. It&#8217;s the clear coat that gives cars their deep, glossy finish. According to Canadiandriver.com, most automotive clear coats are still solvent-based. However, during the auto manufacturer&#8217;s painting process, it&#8217;s the base coat that gives off the most VOCs. <br /><br />So, allegedly water-based paints aren&#8217;t any less durable than solvent-based paints. So is it possible that the paint being applied to today&#8217;s cars is actually thinner than it used to be? Perhaps manufacturers aren&#8217;t putting as much paint on? There are ways to measure paint thickness using paint gauges, but I don't know if paint thicknesses have gotten progressively thinner over time. <br /><br />What about this: Could it be the shapes of today&#8217;s cars that are the culprit? Lower, more rounded front ends that possibly lend themselves to being pummeled my rocks? <br /><br />I don&#8217;t have an answer at this point. However, I&#8217;d like to hear from you: Does your paint chip easily? Do you know why it seems everyone complains about their new car&#8217;s paint job?<br /><br /><b>LINKS</b><br /><a href="http://www.jdpower.com/autos/articles/Automotive-Paint">Automotive Paint (JD Power)</a><br /><a href="http://www.canadiandriver.com/2008/02/13/auto-tech-automobile-painting.htm">Auto Tech: Automobile painting (CanadianDriver.com)</a><br /><br>
