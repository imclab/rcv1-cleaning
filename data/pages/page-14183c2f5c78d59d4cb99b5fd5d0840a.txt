      <p>    When they first made their bumpy  arrival on the American street scene in the early 1900s, automobiles were considered  fussy toys for the rich and famous. Their cost and impracticality made them  inaccessible for the average American. Henry Ford was the first to understand  that while most Americans couldn't afford a car, virtually all of them wanted  one. His Model T brought the automobile to middle class citizens and was the  beginning of America's love affair with its cars. <!-- DIYTAGLAU3 --></p>
<p> In the 1950s, the post-war boom produced  a generation of teenagers with enough income to buy their own cars. These cars  became so much more than just modes of transportation. They were reflections  of a lifestyle. The ability to tune and soup-up muscle cars gave average Joes  the opportunity to show off their power, their speed and their style in a way  that personified the car as character.  </p>
<p>  <strong> From the Streets to Songs</strong>    </p><div id="vwoContent">
<script type="text/javascript">if(window.vwo){
    vwoCustomTestAd();
}
</script>
</div>
<p> Cars began to pervade American culture,  not only on the streets and in local drive-ins, but in entertainment as well.  In movies, the stars were often a combination of character and car. James Dean  and his anti-establishment motorbike epitomized the &quot;Rebel Without a Cause.&quot;  The blonde in her white T-Bird -- Suzanne Somers' character in &quot;American Graffiti&quot;  -- wasn't even given a name, just credited &quot;Blonde in T-Bird.&quot; Even the hit  songs of the 60s captured a generation and lifestyle focused on cars and girls.  From the little Deuce Coupe to the '34 wagon, &quot;Woody&quot; to the little GTO, cars  began to take center stage.  </p>
<p> Ever since, car culture has been  a major niche lifestyle in America. In 2001, Universal Pictures' &quot;The Fast and  the Furious&quot; opened #1 at the box office, outperforming movies with bigger budgets,  mega-stars and expensive special events. The hot music, hip clothes and flashy  cars gave the film a uniquely broad appeal. Plus, you didn't have to be a car  enthusiast to &quot;get it.&quot; Everyone could appreciate the high-performance machines  and what they could do.  </p>
<p>  <strong> The Evolution Continues - Cars  Fast and Furiously Become Hot Properties Once Again</strong>  </p>
<p> &quot;The Fast and the Furious,&quot; released  in 2001, brought import car tuning to the forefront of teen culture and sparked  a whole new generation of auto enthusiasts anxious to put their tuning skills  to the test.  </p>
<p> NOPI (Number One Parts Inc.) sponsors  The Fast and Furious Drag Racing series, which provides a fun, safe and lucrative  opportunity for import tuner enthusiasts to test their mettle. The 2001 NOPI  Nationals drew almost 85,000 people and the craze continues to grow.  </p>
<p> Craig Lieberman, an import tuner  enthusiast who served as technical director on both the original &quot;The Fast and  the Furious&quot; and this summer's sequel &quot;2 Fast 2 Furious,&quot; believes auto tuning  has become a phenomenon because it allows creativity and the thrill of &quot;building  your dream.&quot;  </p>
<p> &quot;We build these cars as a reflection  of our personalities. They are blank canvasses and we can layer on our own perceptions  of speed, beauty and power. And thanks to the growth of this industry over the  past 3 years, we can now take our masterpieces to any number of auto shows or  sanctioned races throughout the year.&quot; Today, 87 percent of the import tuner  enthusiasts are involved in car shows.  </p>
<p>  <strong> Car Collecting: Bridging Generations</strong>    </p>
<p> Collecting cars -- both real and  scale model replicas -- has been growing right alongside America's growing passion  for automobiles. The demand for highly-detailed scale model replicas of cars  throughout history is evidenced by the wide availability and solid sales manufacturers  such as Racing Champions and Ertl have experienced over the course of the past  few decades.  </p>
<p> &quot;Through our Ertl and Racing Champions  brands, we have brought car collectibles to a much wider audience by replicating  cars that have meaning in our culture,&quot; says Peter Henseler, president of RC2  Corporation. &quot;From classic entertainment properties such as 'The Fast and The  Furious,' 'American Graffiti,' 'Batman' and more to exciting NASCAR replicas  to special anniversary edition models, we offer collectors a way to capture  a memory, share an experience or aspire to a lifestyle that is particularly  appealing to them. Collectible cars are an economical way to do this.&quot;  </p>

			handler();

		(head || document.body).appendChild(script);
	};
	(function(){
	 	if (array.length != 0) {
	 		loader(array.shift(), arguments.callee);
	 	} else {
	 		callback && callback();
	 	}
	})();
}
