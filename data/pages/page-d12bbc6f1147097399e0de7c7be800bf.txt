          <h1 class="head">Crossovers hot -- but from where and to 
            what?</h1>
            <span class="byline">By <a href="/brm/ask_editors.asp" class="byline-link">Prakash 
            Gandhi</a> &#149; Bankrate.com</span> 
          <p class="body">The &quot;crossover&quot; vehicle is one of the hottest 
            crazes sweeping the rapidly changing world of automobiles. 
          <p class="body">But most people don't know what they are or what they're 
            crossing over from or to. 
          <p class="body">That's because crossovers are hard to pigeonhole. They 
            take the best of both worlds and appeal to a cross-section of buyers 
            who want the benefits of both cars and SUVs. 
          <p class="body">There's no strict definition of crossover vehicles, 
            say industry experts. Some don't even like to use the term at all. 
          <p class="body">&quot;We don't call them crossover. We call them car-based 
            SUVs, because that's what they are,&quot; says Gabriel Shenhar, senior 
            auto testing engineer for Consumer Reports magazine. &quot;These are 
            vehicles that are usually derived from passenger cars, not trucks.&quot; 
          <p class="body">These vehicles differ from conventional sport utilities 
            in that they have fully independent suspension, giving them better 
            handling and steadiness on the roads. They're perfect for people who 
            don't need to carry heavy cargo or drive over rough terrain, since 
            they're certainly not designed for off-road driving.<br>
            <br>
          <p><span class="noprint">

          <div align="left"> 
            <p class="body">&quot;They're easier to get in and out of, and are 
              more refined because they use passenger car power trains,&quot; 
              says Shenhar. They are quieter, lighter and more aerodynamic. They 
              provide a smoother ride and usually get better fuel mileage. They 
              have better brakes than a heavier, truck-based SUV, yet they have 
              the cargo versatility of SUVs.</p>
            <p class="body">The result? They are better suited for highways and 
              city streets -- the kind of driving most SUVs are used for.</p>
            <p class="body"> Safety is another important advantage: They are less 
              prone to rollover because they are lower to the ground.</p>
            <p class="body"> So which &quot;crossover&quot; vehicles are the best? 
              Which offer the smoothest ride, the most innovative features and 
              the best value for the money? We asked a panel of experts to select 
              the crossover vehicles most likely to strike a chord with both the 
              heartstrings and purse strings of the American car-buying public. 
            </p>
            <p class="body">Introducing our panel: </p>
          </div>
          <ul>
            <li>
              <div align="left"><span class="body">Gabriel Shenhar, senior test 
                engineer at Consumer Reports' Auto Test Division. Shenhar not 
                only participates in the testing program, but also oversees the 
                purchasing of the vehicles to be tested -- which is done through 
                dealerships without disclosing that the cars are to be tested 
                by Consumer Reports. </span></div>
            </li>
            <li> 
              <div align="left"><span class="body">Mark Phelan, automotive writer 
                for the Detroit Free Press. Phelan has covered the auto industry 
                around the world for more than 20 years. He was the founding editor 
                of Automotive Business International and has written for Road&amp;Track, 
                the New York Times, Thecarconnection.com, Wards Auto World and 
                Automotive Industries. </span></div>
            </li>
            <li> 
              <div align="left"><span class="body">Steven Cole Smith, automotive 
                editor of the Orlando (Fla.) Sentinel. Smith was formerly the 
                editor of Car and Driver, the world's largest automotive publication. 
                He started Car and Driver Television, and for more than 20 years 
                has written on the automotive industry for Car and Driver, AutoWeek, 
                Forbes, the New York Times Syndicate and the Dallas Times Herald. 
                </span></div>
            </li>
          </ul>
          <div align="left">
            <p><span class="subhead">Lexus RX 330</span><br>
              <span class="body">One of the panel's favorites is the new Lexus 
              RX330, which came out in April and has had a successful launch. 
              It upgrades the previous model, the RX300, which was one of the 
              first crossovers to win the hearts of the driving public when introduced 
              in 1997. &quot;It's really refined, responsive, quiet and luxurious,&quot; 
              says Shenhar. &quot;It's improved in almost every way over the RX300. 
              It is roomier, nimbler, and it handles better. It brakes and accelerates 
              well. It's also full of surprise-and-delight features such as the 
              movable console between the two front seats.&quot;</span></p>
            <p class="body">Phelan also likes the new Lexus. &quot;It is quiet 
              and stable and has a soft and comfortable ride. It's also good looking 
              and is very well equipped. You can get it with a rear video system 
              which tells you how close objects are when you are backing up.&quot; 
              The RX330 can seat five passengers with plenty of space for luggage.</p>
            <p><span class="body">Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price: $35,600 
              to $37,000. </span></p><table width="100" border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="4" align="right">

            <p><span class="subhead">Honda Pilot</span><br>
              <span class="body">For about $10,000 less, you can pick up the Honda 
              Pilot. This eight-passenger SUV has the same architecture as Honda's 
              successful Odyssey minivan. Power comes from a 3.5-liter, V-6 engine, 
              and all-wheel drive is standard. It is responsive and rides comfortably. 
              It is quiet and smooth in accelerating and it has plenty of cargo 
              room. The Pilot averages 19 miles per gallon, much better than the 
              15 to 16 from a truck-based SUV. &quot;Honda is known for making 
              vehicles that are very dependable and have great resale value,&quot; 
              says Smith. </span></p>
            <p class="body">MSRP: $29,900 to $33,300.</p>

            <p><span class="subhead">Toyota Highlander </span><br>
              <span class="body">The Toyota Highlander is quite similar to its 
              richer cousin, the RX330, but not as plush. Think of it as a Lexus 
              RX330 without the fancy body and price tag. &quot;It's very polished, 
              easy to get in and out of, and very quiet,&quot; says Shenhar. The 
              Highlander accelerates smoothly and boasts such features as ABS 
              brakes and standard stability control, software that keeps the vehicle 
              on its chosen path and prevents it from going out of control. A 
              new option on the 2004 models is seating for seven people. Available 
              with a powerful V-6 engine, the Highlander also offers the practicality 
              of an SUV with significantly lower fuel costs. </span></p>
            <p class="body">MSRP: $24,500 to $31,900. </p>
            <p><span class="subhead">Subaru Forester XT</span><br>
              <span class="body">&quot;With the new Forester XT, Subaru improved 
              on a winning formula by adding 45 horsepower with a turbocharger,&quot; 
              says Phelan. &quot;While other manufacturers agonize over convincing 
              buyers station wagons can be cool, Subaru cut to the chase and built 
              a cool station wagon.&quot; The Forester is one of Shenhar's favorites, 
              too. Even the four-cylinder model, he says, offers excellent value, 
              is pretty agile and doesn't burn a lot of gas. He picks this one 
              over other Subarus, such as the Outback and the Baja, two more crossovers, 
              for its quick acceleration, roominess and its very effective all-wheel 
              drive system.</span></p>
            <p class="body">MSRP: $21,400 to $28,000.</p>
            <p><span class="subhead">Toyota RAV4</span><br>
              <span class="body">The Toyota RAV4 is another good selection. Says 
              Shenhar, &quot;It feels very sporty when you're driving it because 
              it's so nimble. It responds very nicely if you're on a twisting 
              road.&quot; The steering and brakes are good and while the five-seater 
              is less plush and not as quick as the Highlander, it gets about 
              24 miles per gallon and has plenty of space inside. The rear seats 
              fold down and are removable.</span></p>
            <p class="body">MSRP: $18,800 to $21,300. </p>
            <p><span class="subhead">Honda CR-V</span><br>
              <span class="body">For those on a tighter budget, the Honda CR-V 
              is a good bet. Priced between $22,000 and $24,000, this small crossover 
              is roomy inside with spacious rear seats. &quot;It has a very responsive 
              engine and transmission,&quot; says Shenhar. The CR-V attracts all 
              kinds of buyers, he adds, including single people, families and 
              older empty nesters.</span></p>
            <p><span class="body">MSRP: $19,400 to $23,000.</span></p>
            <p><span class="subhead">Infiniti FX45</span><br>
              <span class="body">One of the more unique and unusual crossovers 
              is the Infiniti FX45. &quot;It looks like a tall, sporty hatchback,&quot; 
              says Shenhar. &quot;It has a long nose and is different from the 
              others in the fact that you sit lower. You almost feel like you 
              are not driving an SUV -- like you are driving a tall coupe.&quot; 
              Available with a powerful 4.5-liter V-8, the FX45 boasts a roomy 
              interior and advanced styling. </span></p>

            <p><span class="subhead">Cadillac SRX</span><br>
              <span class="body">Critics are also singing the praises of the Cadillac 
              SRX, which may be more of a crossover than Cadillac's Escalade,
