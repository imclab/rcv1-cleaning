<p><!--wsa:gooold--><a title="Wikipedia: Homelessness" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homelessness"><strong>An estimated 100 million people worldwide are &#8220;homeless&#8221;</strong></a> &#8230; <em>but what is homelessness? </em>Is a squatter homeless? What about someone who stays in a shelter each night? A Web Urbanist article on <a title="Creatively Designed Homeless Dwellings" href="http://www.weburbanist.com/2007/08/01/creative-urban-furniture-convertible-inflatable-portable-homeless-shelters/">creatively designed homeless dwellings</a> and nomadic urbanism sparked a lively debate on this subject, which ended up being far longer than the article itself. However, each commenter seemed to understand the nature and problems of homelessness in a different way. Everyone knows that there are people on the streets without a choice as well as nomadic cultures and migrant workers. Few people realize, though, how many <em>nomadic urbanists</em> there are &#8211; those who <em>choose</em> atypical ways of dwelling (within or between cities) that involve no fixed residence.<br />
<span id="more-175"></span><br />
<a title="Urban Transients or Nomadic Urbanism?" href="http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/urban-transients-or-nomadic-urbanists.jpg">
		<span class="pibfi_pinterest">
		<img alt="Urban Transients or Nomadic Urbanism?" src="http://img.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/urban-transients-or-nomadic-urbanists.jpg" />
			<span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://weburbanist.com/2007/08/03/homeless-culture-nomadic-urbanism-from-car-living-to-couch-surfing/&media=http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/urban-transients-or-nomadic-urbanists.jpg&description=Homeless &#038; Nomad Culture: Car Living to Couch Surfing')">
			</span>
		</span></a></p>
<p><a title="NY Times: Car Living on the Rise" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2006/04/02/us/02cars.html?ex=1301634000&amp;en=caf7ec348adbce4d&amp;ei=5090&amp;partner=rssuserland&amp;emc=rss"><strong>Car Living</strong></a> is becoming increasingly popular out of choice or necessity as the cost of living increases in major urban areas. The above image was taken under a bridge in Seattle, and depicts one of the many semi-permanent dwellings found there. The graffiti on the side shows how the resident has essentially <strong>painted a &#8216;front yard&#8217; on the trailer</strong>, a symbol of &#8216;home.&#8217; Those living in this area appreciate the lack of rent and relative quiet of Seattle&#8217;s Industrial District. While the city officially discourages these nomadic dwellers, there are no residential neighbors to complain so they are largely left alone.</p>
<p><a title="Couch Surfing Around the World" href="http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/couch-surfing-around-the-world.jpg">
		<span class="pibfi_pinterest">
		<img alt="Couch Surfing Around the World" src="http://img.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/couch-surfing-around-the-world.jpg" />
			<span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://weburbanist.com/2007/08/03/homeless-culture-nomadic-urbanism-from-car-living-to-couch-surfing/&media=http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/couch-surfing-around-the-world.jpg&description=Homeless &#038; Nomad Culture: Car Living to Couch Surfing')">
			</span>
		</span></a></p>
<p><a title="Couch Surfing dot Com" href="http://couchsurfing.com"><strong>Couch Surfing</strong></a> is very different kind of urban nomadism. People from around the world use various <a title="StumbleUpon Tagged CouchSurfing Sites" href="http://www.stumbleupon.com/tag/couch-surfing/">related forums and websites</a> as they travel in order to stay free and meet new people. Couch Surfing dot Com has <strong>over 250,000 registered users</strong> and boasts available couches in virtually every major city in the world. In theory, a person could live from couch to couch indefinitely. Couch Surfers rely on reviews of one another and of places to stay, so maintaining good behavior is rewarded with better future couch surfing opportunities.</p>
<p><a title="The Future of Nomadism?" href="http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/the-future-of-nomadicism.jpg">
		<span class="pibfi_pinterest">
		<img alt="The Future of Nomadism?" src="http://img.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/the-future-of-nomadicism.jpg" />
			<span class="xc_pin" onclick="pin_this(event, 'http://pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=http://weburbanist.com/2007/08/03/homeless-culture-nomadic-urbanism-from-car-living-to-couch-surfing/&media=http://www.weburbanist.com/wp-content/uploads/2007/08/the-future-of-nomadicism.jpg&description=Homeless &#038; Nomad Culture: Car Living to Couch Surfing')">
			</span>
		</span></a></p>
<p>Architects, designers and urbanites are becoming increasingly interested in nomadic building design, from <a title="Shipping Container Museum Project" href="http://www.nyc-architecture.com/CHE/CHE-037.htm">shipping container museums</a> to <a title="Temporary and Nomadic Housing" href="http://www.openarchitecturenetwork.org/taxonomy/term/127">portable temporary housing</a>. Homelessness, in many ways, is a descriptive term that could be applied to more and more of the world&#8217;s population. There are certainly many people who did not choose and do not wish to remain homeless. Still, it is important to remember that the word &#8216;homeless&#8217; carries many connotations that may or may not apply to everyone without a home, such as car dwellers, couch surfers and other urban nomads.</p>

Living uprooted and frugally, while often uncomfortable, gave me a feeling of freedom that&#8217;s almost extinct in modern western society. I woke up each morning with no obligations, nowhere to be, no bills to pay. I was free to pick up and go, see new places, meet new people, essentially start a new life whenever I wanted. An older gentleman I met summed it up best when he said &#8216;When you travel, every night is a Friday, every day is a Saturday.&#8217;</p>
<p>Now, you might be thinking, &#8216;What a bum&#8230;&#8217;, but I think that mentality has been fed to us. We live in consumer societies that work because we buy, and we can buy because we work. A fine crack forms in its flawless walls when you suddenly find you don&#8217;t &#8216;need&#8217; any of those possessions. Therefore, it shouldn&#8217;t be all that surprising for there to be such a negative connotation to the word &#8216;homeless&#8217;.</p>
<p>In many ways the consumer society is great. It affords us the comforts of modern living, generally you don&#8217;t go hungry, you won&#8217;t get cold or overly hot, you&#8217;ll always have entertainment, but something bigger and more important can get lost along the way. What it means and feels like to be alive.</p>
