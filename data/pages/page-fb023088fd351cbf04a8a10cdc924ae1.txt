<h3 class="f-hc">
<a href="/tto/arts/visualarts/article3853748.ece">Art shoots for the stars</a>
</h3>
<span class="img-mid"><a href="/tto/arts/visualarts/article3853748.ece" title="Art shoots for the stars"><img src="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/multimedia/archive/00445/032afefa-0f3a-11e3-_445710h.jpg" alt="" width="140" height="93" /></a></span>
<p class=""><p>A second moon is about to be launched into space — or into airspace at least. On September 8, the artist Katie Paterson, working in partnership with the British Science Festival and UPS, the freight company, will dispatch a packaged fragment of lunar rock on a year-long journey . Over the next 12 months, this parcel will circle by aeroplane (with periodic touchdowns, of course) west to east around the planet, crossing oceans and continents, passing cities and deserts and forests, at twice the speed of the moon, looping the earth 30 times in total.</p>
<p>You can track the parcel’s progress across the skies via an app that will allow you to see not only the view from this “second moon” to Earth, but also the view from the real moon to our planet and your own place on the cosmological map as, zooming out on screen, you travel farther and farther into the outer reaches of the solar system. “Over the course of this project, you will see the movements of all the planets in relation to this second moon,” Paterson explains.</p>
<p>The Second Moon is the latest of many pieces in which Paterson has brought together the cosmological and the conceptual, the scientific and the poetic, the massive and the minute. She encourages her spectators to push their imaginations to their outermost reaches, to contemplate the mysteries of their place in the universe. She is the artist who transmitted Beethoven’s <i>Moonlight </i>Sonata to the lunar surface and back; installed a live phone line from a gallery to the groaning depths of an Icelandic glacier; mapped the locations of the 27,000 dead stars that are known to humanity (and continues to send a letter of condolence each time another one dies); took a grain of sand and got nanotechnological experts to carve it to just 0.00005mm across before casting it loose in the deserts of the Sahara.</p>
<p>Born in Glasgow in 1981, Paterson trained at Edinburgh College of Art and then the Slade. But it was a year spent in Iceland, in between, that transformed her practice. “I had no particular interest in the cosmos before that,” she says. “And I was definitely not scientifically minded.” In Iceland, however, under enormous skies glowing with northern lights, seeing the Earth’s energy bursting through its crust in geysers and sensing the incredible slowness of the glacial creep, she tapped into the vision that has been developing ever since, taking a year-long residency in University College London’s astrophysics department, peering through Hawaii’s Keck telescope into the universe’s first light.</p>
<p>Such experiences underpin her multi-disciplinary, essentially conceptual pieces which, at the same time as they probe the boundaries of scientific knowledge, remain simple. “What you see in my work might be subtle and minimal,” says Paterson, “but the imagination has to take a leap and it’s where that takes you that matters. Though it is very important to me that what I do is real — that there really was a microphone implanted in a glacier — where the mind can take you is completely open and unlimited.”</p>
<p>There is one place, certainly, that Paterson’s art should take her, and that is on to next year’s Turner Prize shortlist.</p></p>

<div class=" element artsTeaser">
<h3 class="f-hc">
<a href="/tto/arts/article3852770.ece">Gambon: ‘I got so frightened I collapsed’</a>
</h3>
<span class="img-mid"><a href="/tto/arts/article3852770.ece" title="Gambon: ‘I got so frightened I collapsed’"><img src="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/multimedia/archive/00445/7a036ccc-0e56-11e3-_445347h.jpg" alt="" width="140" height="93" /></a></span>
<p class=""><p>When I suggest to Sir Michael Gambon that his latest stage role must be a dream gig for an actor — it’s a one-man show in which he has no lines and has only to react to a pre-recorded voice for half an hour — I am being slightly flippant. And when he replies, spooning the bag out of his tea in a semi-trendy Edinburgh bar, I assume he is just being jocular. “Well,” he says. “I’m getting so old now that I can’t remember lines, so it’s like a dream being offered this part.”</p>
<p>After all, this 72-year-old giant of British acting ­— Dumbledore from the Harry Potter films, Philip Marlow from <i>The Singing Detective</i>, countless supporting roles in film and television and so many great lead roles on stage – has a reputation for being a bit of a prankster. And although his performance in Samuel Beckett’s short play <i>Eh Joe</i> is the linchpin of the Edinburgh International Festival’s current season of Beckett rarities, he first performed it at the Gate Theatre in Dublin in April 2006 and in the West End two months later. It’s a good part, but surely nothing like a dream.</p>
<p>As we talk more, it appears as if he’s in earnest. Over the past few years, he has developed a bit of a problem. He says that it is “accidental” that his recent stage work has consisted of one short Beckett play (<i>Krapp’s Last Tape</i> in the West End) and one Beckett play in which the cast hold their scripts because they are replicating a radio production (<i>All That Fall</i> with Dame Eileen Atkins, which ran in the West End earlier this year and goes to New York for five weeks just before Christmas). Yet when I mention that there are links between <i>Eh Joe </i>— in which Gambon’s Joe does nothing but react to a recording of Penelope Wilton as a woman taunting him about his failings — and <i>Krapp’s Last Tape</i> — in which Gambon’s Krapp spent much of the play reacting to old tape recordings he had made – he wants it known that there are big differences between the two.</p>
<p>“I spoke in <i>Krapp’s Last Tape</i> quite a bit,” he says, his voice quiet but unmistakeably wry and resonant; his craggy face somehow simultaneously impassive and enormously expressive. “But I must admit, I was sitting a lot of the time at a table, so I jotted a few of the lines down on top of the table. If I got into any trouble with memory I just flicked down and there it was.” So the memory problem is no joke? “No, no. But it’s all right, it’s just one of those things. I’ll suffer with it.” Does he know what it is? He shrugs.“Just getting old. It came on a couple of years ago and it’s dangerous for me to do stage plays.” He gives a rueful laugh, the confidential look. “I’ve never admitted this before, so all these producers don’t know. So I’m lucky in as much as I get away with it.”</p>
<p>The problem began, it turns out, when he was rehearsing for <i>The Habit of Art</i> at the Lyttelton at the National Theatre in November 2009. He was playing W.H. Auden opposite Alex Jennings as Benjamin Britten, a big role in what was Alan Bennett’s first play since <i>The History Boys</i> five years earlier. At the time it was reported only that Gambon had been ordered to rest because of “ill health”. In fact, he says, he was rushed to St Thomas’ Hospital during a rehearsal. “I got so frightened that I collapsed. They did all sorts of tests of me and they couldn’t find a thing.</p>
<p>“I think it’s linked to the memory,” he says. “It was a really major, big part — I’ve never told anyone this before — and I think I got so frightened that I collapsed.” So he thinks it was psychosomatic? “Oh, I’m sure it was. The doctors couldn’t find anything wrong with me. I then recovered, we started rehearsals again and I collapsed again. So the ambulance took me back to St Thomas’. It’s rather sad. And then they recast it [with Richard Griffiths]. I couldn’t do it. That is linked with what is wrong with me. It was overwhelming fear. And I am not exaggerating or lying. The two fast journeys to the hospital; I was completely out, I was gone, I was on a drip, everything.” He sighs. “I feel rather guilty about it, letting the National down.”</p>
<p>Physically, Gambon says he feels fine. His regime for looking after his voice is as relaxed as ever — “I never think about it, I smoke cigarettes and it just happens” — and he’s still busy with voiceover, film and television work. And as we see in <i>Eh Joe</i>, in which he is projected on to a strip of gauze at the front of the stage so that we can see every detail of his house-high face, he can still project his thoughts as powerfully as he can project his voice. This, he says, is not technique. He just sets out to listen, hard, and react as he sees fit. “It’s not easy, it’s real torment.”</p>
<p>He’s not any kind of Beckett scholar, he insists. “I just do what I’m told, I’m faithful to it and people seem to like that. But it’s brilliant stuff to do.” Part of the appeal of his Beckett splurge has been the chance these plays give him to reconnect with his Irishness. Most of his recent productions have started life at the Gate Theatre in Dublin, the city in which he was born in October 1940, before he and his parents moved to Camden in North London when he was 5. “The Irish accent is right in me; my mum and dad spoke it.” (As he goes outside for a cigarette after the interview, he ponders how he sounded to me. “I have three voices,” he says, “cockney, posh and Irish. I think I was somewhere between them.”)</p>
<p>Gambon started acting as a film-fixated teenager and found it was something he could do. After Rada, he worked for Laurence Olivier at the National Theatre in the 1960s. When he got fed up with playing small parts, he asked Olivier for bigger ones. Olivier said he couldn’t do that, but he helped him get his next job, playing Othello at the Birmingham Rep. “I’d been in Olivier’s <i>Othello</i> standing in the back with a spear, so I just copied him. I mean, you can’t resist it. I’m sure a lot of actors do that.”After that the leads kept coming. </p>
<p>He appeared in Alan Ayckbourn’s <i>The Norman Conquests</i> at Greenwich in 1974, one of many Ayckbourn roles over the years. In 1978 he played opposite Wilton in Harold Pinter’s <i>Betrayal</i> at the National. He had many more lead roles there, but it was his role in Dennis Potter’s television drama <i>The Singing Detective </i>in 1986 that made him a star. Since when he has worked constantly, at home and in Hollywood. “I’ve been an actor for more than 50 years and I can’t think what else I’d rather do. I’ve always enjoyed it. I’ve always gone to work. I just plod on.”</p>
<p>To himself, he’s just a working actor. But there are millions who think of him primarily as Harry Potter’s headmaster, Dumbledore, in the past five Potter films (he took over from the late Richard Harris). He claims to be at ease with the idea that 50 years of work might get condensed in the popular imagination into one twinkly wizard. “I don’t mind,” he says. “Those who know know, don’t they?” No, Harry Potter fans don’t besiege the stage door after Beckett dramas. But sometimes they stop him in the street.</p>
<p>“They say, ‘Are you Dumbledore?’ I say, yeah. And I sign an autograph. Mainly children. They love it. They’re sweet. I get tiny little children stopping me and it’s wonderful. I’ve had that a bit with older people with <i>The Singing Detective</i> or <i>Maigret</i>.” He’s struck by something funny. “But only very posh people stop me for Beckett.” Gambon doesn’t like to talk about his private life, but he has two young boys by his girlfriend Philippa Hart — Thomas, 6, and William, 4 — as well as a grown-up son, Fergus, by Anne, his wife of 51 years. Do his little ones know Dumbledore yet? He looks horrified. “Ooooh no, no! Keep them apart.” He won’t be able to do that for much longer though, will he? He shrugs. “Well, I’ll ignore it all.”</p>
<p>Gambon’s line-learning may be impaired and his age means he no longer pilots planes, but he’s still a mad-keen driver ­— he mentions proudly Gambon’s Corner, named by the <i>Top Gear</i> team in his honour after he went round that part of the test track on two wheels – and still goes to auction houses to add to his collection of antique guns. He’s amazingly sharp-witted; I’ve rarely met anyone quicker off the mark to answer a question, unhurriedly yet without pause. </p>
<p>He likes filming, but loves theatre. “The theatre starts every night at half past seven and I like the rhythm of going to the theatre, parking the car, going to the stage door; I’ve grown up with all of that.” Does he think that he won’t be able to do so much theatre from now on? “I’d love to do more theatre — I mean, I shouldn’t be telling the world that I can’t remember lines any more, but I find it more and more difficult, so I don’t know. If I could learn a technique or something maybe I would, but if not I’ll just plod on.” He has used earpiece prompts while filming, but doesn’t like the idea of using them on stage. “It would be a bit iffy. It would be frightening and the stage is frightening enough without all of that.”</p>
<p>What Gambon has in mind for himself, he says is supporting roles in films with great actors. “I’d like to be the man stood at the back,” he says, slipping gently into joker mode. “A gangster, something like that. De Niro is acting his part, but I’m standing always behind him. I don’t have much to do, but I’m on camera quite a bit, so they would recognise you in the street. ‘Were you that man with Robert De Niro?’ ‘Yes, I was actually.’ Sounds terrible, doesn’t it? Lazy bastard. That’s the sort of thing I’d like to do.” And this lugubrious rascal loses his composure to give an endearingly jovial cackle, leaving me to figure out whether he’s being very flippant, slightly flippant, or not flippant at all.<i>
