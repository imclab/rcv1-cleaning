<h3 class="f-hc">
<a href="/tto/arts/music/classical/article3851487.ece">Joseph Calleja: the next Pavarotti?</a>
</h3>
<span class="img-mid"><a href="/tto/arts/music/classical/article3851487.ece" title="Joseph Calleja: the next Pavarotti?"><img src="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/multimedia/archive/00444/82313362-0c25-11e3-_444694h.jpg" alt="" width="140" height="93" /></a></span>
<p class=""><p>Plácido Domingo once called Joseph Calleja “the baby tenor” but as Calleja has grown on to the world’s greatest opera stages, another moniker has attached itself: the new Pavarotti? Note the question mark. This title gets lobbed hopefully at plenty of new lyric tenors but there is reason to believe in Calleja.</p>
<p>I meet him on his home turf, Malta, where he still lives and was recently made the island’s first cultural ambassador. He is building a new home on even tinier Gozo — complete with a reef shark tank.</p>
<p>Calleja, now 35, has established his serious operatic credentials early. He made his debuts at the Royal Opera House and New York’s Metropolitan Opera before he was 28 and he’s next at the ROH in April to sing the title role in Gounod’s <i>Faust</i>. You hear Calleja and know you’re hearing a rare and thrilling talent: the soft vibrato, inflection, and honeyed timbre that fits Verdi and Puccini so beautifully.</p>
<p>Yet Calleja has more — namely an indefinable populist touch. Two of my friends — not classical fans — know of and like “the Maltese tenor”. Next week Calleja sings an all-Verdi programme at the BBC Proms, and then at Proms in the Park, performing with Dame Edna Everage, Nigel Kennedy and Bryan Ferry. His new album, <i>Amore</i>, is, he himself says, “unashamedly popular”. Calleja is more mainstream than bel canto’s Juan Diego Flórez, more everyman than German tenor Jonas Kaufmann and free of the vocal dramas of Rolando Villazón.</p>
<p>He is back in Malta to perform his annual spectacular concert — the hottest date in Malta’s calendar — and for his holiday-come-sabbatical. Malta’s first cultural ambassador wears smart civvies: jeans, a shirt the blue of his eyes, navy blazer, big cowboy boots and a broad smile. “Ideally I have two months to live like a normal human being, not worrying about having a glass of wine, a cigar . . .  opera singing is a vocation, it’s athleticism and the stress of being in front of incredible crowds.” </p>
<p>This year Calleja can only squeeze two weeks’ holiday with his family, six-year-old son Xandru, daughter Clara, eight, and girlfriend Sabine (he is separated from the mother of his children). “The last month I’ve done too much — my fault — thank God I have not paid for it physically.”</p>
<p>The tiny Mediterranean island is where Calleja learnt his craft, taught painstakingly by Paul Asciak — a tenor who sang regularly at Covent Garden in the 1950s. Malta is where Calleja had his first audition (he’s only ever had three; “young singers will hate me” he concedes) before winning Domingo’s international Operalia competition in 1999, aged 21.</p>
<p>Always singing pop and folk on the school bus, Calleja’s discovery of opera has become an interview legend. At 13 he saw a Mario Lanza film, <i>The Great Caruso</i>, and was captivated by the sound and style. When his aunt heard him singing along to <i>The Essential Pavarotti</i>she suggested that Calleja have lessons. Teacher Asciak told his parents that young Calleja had “a world-class instrument”. </p>
<p>“But you don’t tell a 15-year-old that he has a Ferrari in his throat.” Asciak only revealed the serious potential to Calleja after five months. There followed four years of meticulous study, listening to hours of golden era recordings, which explains Calleja’s timeless style. “Paul [now 90] is 85 per cent of what makes my voice special,” Calleja says, and still confers with him.</p>
<p>Although Calleja’s parents weren’t musical, they instilled a healthy discipline and ambition — and at 19 that drive took over. “I was a bit brash and rash at that time. I thought: I wanna go on stage and make some money. Show myself to the world. “It played out well but it could have been dangerous. Had I worked a bit less during the first two or three years of my career, perhaps it would have been slightly smarter. But I’ve never had a vocal crisis and that is testament to me being careful.” Calleja’s next new role will be in <i>Un ballo in maschera</i> at Covent Garden in 2015, but the heavies such as <i>Il trovatore</i>and <i>Aida</i> are eight or so years away.</p>
<p>There is no doubt, however, that Calleja can punch his way out of opera’s confines. “He’s credible but crossover,” says Chris Brogden, the commissioner from Sky Arts, which will broadcast his Malta concert later this year. This autumn Calleja will be starring alongside Marion Cotillard and Joaquin Phoenix as Caruso in <i>The Immigrant</i>, a Hollywood drama set in Twenties New York.</p>
<p>There’s perhaps more crossover than credibility to this jamboree for 35,000 (including Malta’s Prime Minister, and Joseph’s mother). <i>The X-Factor</i> runner-up Rebecca Ferguson, Malta’s Eurovision entry Gianluca and ageing Italian rocker Zucchero join Calleja on stage. Among the operatic nuggets, Calleja sings Verdi’s <i>La donna è mobile</i> with passages of breathtaking pianissimo. But when he duets with Ferguson on Elvis’s <i>Can’t Help Falling in Love</i>, I thank God it’s not on the new album.</p>
<p>Calleja’s fifth recording for Decca, <i>Amore</i>, more than teeters on the edge of crossover: <i>O Sole Mio</i>, <i>La Serenata</i>, <i>You Raise Me Up</i> . . . “It has all the old chestnuts but also some surprises,” Calleja says. He sings Russian for the first time in <i>Net, tolko tot, kto znal</i> (<i>None but the Lonely Heart)</i>, by Tchaikovsky. “We’re not reinventing the wheel but the result is exciting and it flatters the voice. Singing <i>La Vie en Rose</i>– it’s not something that Pavarotti would have done.”</p>
<p>Calleja also argues that having recorded the classic tenor aria albums early, only the heavier rep remains. “Vocally it will be presumptuous and a semi-suicide. I could record them now but not as well as if I wait eight to ten more years. So in the meantime, why not have fun?”</p>
<p>And Decca clearly believe the Calleja formula will deliver sales. “You can’t produce a record knowing it’s going to be a complete disaster,” says Calleja. “The public want new things but they want familiar versions of new things.”</p>
<p>At the concert after-party, Calleja’s holiday has officially begun. He hands out cigars – and smokes his first since December – and reserve years of Saint-Emilion emerge (Calleja is a wine connoisseur). He’s a relaxed and generous host, his jokes growing more raucous before he finally heads home at 5am.</p>
<p>Calleja says he doesn’t have “a press face, a stage face and a home face”. And it’s true, his easy-going manner is part of his strength on stage. Witness last year’s Last Night of the Proms when he jogged down the Royal Albert Hall steps in a Team GB tracksuit and Maltese-cross sweatshirt to sing <i>Rule, Britannia!</i>. </p>
<p>“Right before going on stage I just said: F*** it, I’m here. I’ve done everything I could: I’ve studied, I was professional, I rested. If I screw up, it’s chance,” he says. “Then when I go onstage, something else takes over: the joy of singing and the incredible rapport at the Proms.”</p>
<p>When Calleja sings at the Proms again this year, his performance will have a deeper personal significance. His father died in 2009 and his favourite film was Hitchcock’s <i>The Man Who Knew Too Much</i>, with the assassination scene set in the Royal Albert Hall. “My father never quite saw me perform there,” Calleja says, “but go tell him that his son a couple of years later would be singing time and time again in that place. It makes me emotional.”</p>
<p>
<b>Joseph Calleja sings in Prom 72, Sept 5 and at Proms in the Park, Sept 7. Royal Albert Hall, London SW7 (0845 401 5040). <i>Amore</i> is released on Decca on September 2</b>
</p></p>

<h3 class="f-hc">
<a href="/tto/life/celebrity/article3851458.ece">The Newsroom’s very British girl</a>
</h3>
<span class="img-mid"><a href="/tto/life/celebrity/article3851458.ece" title="The Newsroom’s very British girl"><img src="http://www.thetimes.co.uk/tto/multimedia/archive/00444/a8530a00-0c22-11e3-_444675h.jpg" alt="British actress Emily Mortimer" width="140" height="93" /></a></span>
<p class=""><p>When Emily Mortimer first went to Los Angeles she struggled to understand the natives. “I’m quite a good traveller,” she says. “I quite like having to fit in with foreign customs, you know what I mean? And it was like being in the most foreign place on earth.”</p>
<p>One of the customs of this place, she quickly discerned, was a tendency “to sort of be very enthusiastic all the time about your work”.</p>
<p>Being British, she naturally found this extremely difficult. “I remember at a party in LA when I was first there someone saying, ‘You are an actress?’, and me saying, ‘Yes, but not a very good one’, because I felt embarrassed… I hadn’t done anything really, I mean hardly very much. But he looked so totally horrified… it was like I’d said, ‘I eat babies’ or something. It was not charming in any way or funny or winning.”</p>
<p>Just over 10 years later she found herself standing outside an audition room at the top of a tower in midtown Manhattan, making the umpteenth audition for <i>The Newsroom</i>, a drama that would make her relatively famous in America. </p>
<p>Through the door was the executive producer Scott Rudin, Aaron Sorkin, the writer and creator, and several other men whose watches are permanently set to the big time. “I heard myself say the words ‘Failure is not an option’. I was pacing round this room on the top floor saying the words ‘Failure is not an option’. It was extremely high and frigid from air conditioning, as everywhere is, and I said those words. And I did laugh at those words saying them, but I did really mean them.”</p>
<p>Mortimer had gone native. She had become enthusiastic about her work. Now, like David Livingstone returned from the Zambezi, she is ready to talk about the place called Hollywood, a land of red carpets and high pressure luncheons and extremely competent assistants who can organise a massage for your labrador and a surprise 40th birthday party for your husband without dropping your goji berry smoothie. </p>
<p>At least this is how I imagine it is. We meet in the Greenwich Hotel on the west side of Manhattan and order tea. </p>
<p>“I’m still slightly taken with LA but I definitely got this panicky feeling that I needed to get out once we had a baby,” she says. “It felt too much like the edge of the universe.”</p>
<p>Whereas in New York you can hardly leave your apartment without engaging in a conversation with three people you have never met and a dog you only vaguely recognise, in Los Angeles “You can go for a week without actually speaking to anyone… you long, sometimes, for people to ring up with the wrong number because you’re so isolated.”</p>
<p>So she and her husband, the actor Alessandro Nivola, and their two children, escaped to Brooklyn. Then she got the job on <i>The Newsroom</i> playing MacKenzie McHale, the brilliant but wildly idealistic producer in Sorkin’s series about a nightly news show and she had to start shuttling back to LA for five months at a time.</p>
<p>Does her entourage travel with her? “My entourage doesn’t really exist,” she says, though she did have one while filming the show: an agent, a manager, a publicist, and – for the first time – an assistant too. </p>
<p>Her character has a lot to say, and it is all in Sorkinese – that rapid, elegant style of speech which requires characters to be both carried away with their own raging emotions and awkwardly self-conscious, often within the same sentence.</p>
<p>Driving to the studio she would attempt to rev herself up to Sorkin speed by listening to <i>Girl On Fire</i>, by Alicia Keys. “Looks like a girl, but she’s a flame / So bright, she can burn your eyes”, it goes, in the wind up to the chorus. But she couldn’t quite keep it up.</p>
<p>“I’m probably far too self-conscious to be an actress,” she says. “I try, and then I just think ‘Oh God, this is too much…’ You are really just saying the f****** lines over and over and over and over again, and walking out of the trailer just hoping it’s going to stay in your head until you get to the set and almost [think] if you just move your head a little bit it might fall out of one ear so you just walk very straight and hope that no one talks to you until you get there and you say it.”</p>
<p>Initially she was doing it all in an American accent, too. “Quite early on in the rehearsal period I said, ‘Is there any reason why she can’t be English?’ And everyone said, ‘No, no. I don’t think so.’ They were all so relieved that I wasn’t going to have to be struggling through this accent at 5 o’clock in the morning trying to talk at a million miles an hour.”</p>
<p>So Sorkin made her an American who was raised in England. “It suited his purposes in a way because she is, in some ways, the most patriotic.” It sounded funnier to hear impassioned speeches about truth and justice and the American way delivered in an English accent.</p>
<p>
<i>The Newsroom</i> is produced at a frenetic pace — “We film 10 pages a day of dialogue, and that’s extreme, it’s like extreme sports” – and her husband was away filming. “So I was there in LA on my own with our two kids.” So she had an assistant. </p>
<p>She found it an odd experience. “They are people who spend more time with you than your family, very often.” Talking about it to her friend, the comic actress Dolly Wells, the daughter of the late satirist John Wells, they “imagined what it would be like if your best childhood friend became your assistant”. They improvised twenty minutes of dialogue along those lines in her trailer and this became the starting point for <i>Doll and Em</i>, a six-episode comedy that will show on Sky next year. </p>
<p>The other thing that has surprised her about life as a Hollywood celebrity is quite how much money she needs to spend publicising her own achievements. She could understand the need for hair, make-up artists and stylists for all those brutal photo-shoots and red carpet promenades. “What I think people aren’t really nearly aware of enough is the racket of this whole publicity thing. For all these people who are on the front of these magazines and everything, they’ve all been paying people to get them on the front of the magazines.”</p>
<p>We may not be best placed, she and I, to expose this racket, given that we are ourselves engaged in an interview which was set up with the aid of a publicist. It is probably the equivalent of a drug dealer and a junkie pausing in the midst of a sale to jointly announce that crack kills. </p>
<p>“I hope you are not going to make me sound like a tool because I’m trying to be honest about this. I think that it’s interesting and everybody, <i>everybody</i> does it,” she says. “And then you do these interviews… and you come off like a total a******e. You just think I just spent however many grand trying to get into whatever this is and I look terrible and I sound like a total twit.”</p>
<p>It is also funny because I once interviewed her father, the late barrister and author Sir John Mortimer. Afterwards his representative contacted me to demand a fee, and I had to explain that we have a policy of not paying people to speak to us. Now, apparently, it’s the other way around.</p>
<p>Her father had described a life of letters that sounded so fabulous that I half wondered if he was making it up: rising at five in their house that his father built in the Chilterns to scribble furiously, completing his Rumpole stories in a single draft, all done by 10, then a glass of champagne.</p>
<p>“No, no, it was totally true,” she says. In the mornings they would find that he had written himself notes using her little sister’s fridge magnets. “Things like ‘For f***s sake write you f*****’.”</p>
<p>He died in January 2009. “These weird things keep coming back to me,” says Mortimer. “I always thought of mum as being the person who brought us up really… and he was just there with a glass of champagne making you feel that somehow it was all right. But then I was talking to my little boy the other day and suddenly this thing came into my head that… I remember my dad saying to me.”</p>
<p>Her son Sam, who is 10 next month, was telling her that life was deeply unfair, and she was replying that he was actually extremely lucky, without much success. Then she said, “My father always used to say childhood is the absolute worst time of your life, it’s misery, you’re forced to go to school for eight or nine hour days, unpaid labour… you’re treated like someone of no consequence… and Sam smiled, was relieved, somehow. And I thought God, these things that I look back on with such affection about my dad — actually, it was total lifeline, that way of thinking.”</p>
<p>Mortimer was a shy child with a strong interest in showbusiness, specifically: Torvill and Dean, Delia Smith and the dancing girls who featured on the BBC variety show <i>Summertime Special</i>. Sometimes her parents would sit and watch her acting out adverts for Persil Automatic. </p>
<p>“My brother-in-law always teases me that my secret fantasy is to wear a Confederate bikini and an ankle bracelet. Because I’ve got a thing for Kid Rock… because it’s so the opposite to the way that I was brought up.”</p>
<p>She once went to a gym – she insists this was a one-off occurrence – and “I saw Kid Rock on television on a speed boat swilling whisky with these girls in bikinis. I thought God I wish I could have had that sort of Americana-like teenage years, just sort of on a speed boat in a Confederate bikini.”</p>
<p>I say that must be how all Americans spend their teens. To be honest I’m still struggling to dispel this mental image of Mortimer gyrating on the speed boat. </p>
<p>“Now you know far too much about my weird fantasies,” she says. “I was always just really into dancing girls, they had this tragic dignity somehow.”</p>
<p>This dream has yet to come to pass. She went to Oxford, where she studied English and Russian and expected to be recruited as a spy. Instead an agent saw her in a student play and recruited her to drama. “I had this sort of longing for someone to say ‘Stop, don’t be ridiculous, go and make use of your education’.”</p>
<p>She looked to her father, but he shared her showbusiness fantasies. Not the business on the speed boat – “His fancies were slightly more elegant, he wanted to be Fred Astaire.”</p>
<p>She was in her late twenties when she arrived in LA and called attention to herself by taking on roles that people considered extremely brave. One of them, <i>Young Adam</i>, required her to be covered in custard and spanked by Ewan McGregor.</p>
<p>What really got her noticed was another film, <i>Lovely and Amazing</i>, in which she stood naked, without so much as a layer of Ambrosia Devon Custard to cover her dignity, and asked her boyfriend to point out her physical defects. </p>
<p>Still, none of these films gave her the profile she has gained from making <i>The Newsroom </i>opposite the veteran actor Jeff Daniels. When they finished filming the second series, she returned to New York to spend the rest of the year at home without an entourage. “It’s confusing going from one extreme to the next,” she says. “You find yourself walking from the fridge to the shopping bag and picking out the frozen peas… You get very self-conscious, you think ‘Oh, here I am walking to the fridge’.”</p>
<p>She gets recognised in the supermarket. In person she must seem, to these fans of <i>The Newsroom</i>, not unlike the character she plays: a very English woman who is busy articulating the American dream. </p>
