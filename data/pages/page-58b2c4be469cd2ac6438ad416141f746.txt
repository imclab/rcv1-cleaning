But Sam Toperoff’s new novel <em>Lillian &amp; Dash</em> takes a different approach to the iconic author and his long-time lover, playwright Lillian Hellman. Check out <a href="http://http://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/books/book-world-review-lillian-and-dash-by-sam-toperoff/2013/08/22/22748bde-044d-11e3-a07f-49ddc7417125_story.html" target="_blank">my review here</a>. — <a href="http://www.fallforthebook.org" target="_blank"><em>Art Taylor</em></a></p>

	<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1158640643g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1" /><h2 id="post-5865"><a href="http://artandliterature.wordpress.com/2013/07/22/upcoming-event-northern-virginia-writers-club-workshop/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Upcoming Event: Northern Virginia Writers Club&nbsp;Workshop">Upcoming Event: Northern Virginia Writers Club&nbsp;Workshop</a></h2>
	<small>July 22, 2013 <!-- by artandliterature --></small>

	<div class="entry">
		<p>This coming Saturday—July 27—from 11 a.m.-4 p.m.—I&#8217;ll be leading the workshop <a href="http://northernvirginiawriters.org/2013/07/19/art-taylor-workshop-this-time-its-for-real/" target="_blank">&#8220;Bring Life to Your Characters and Shape to Your Plot&#8221;</a> for the Northern Virginia Writers Club. The workshop distills a number of lessons that I&#8217;ve used at times for my semester-long fiction and creative nonfiction workshops at George Mason University: the benefits and drawbacks of character inventories; direct and indirection presentation of character; the direct relationships between character and plot; stories as conflict versus stories as connection/disconnection; linear versus modular storytelling&#8230;. While it&#8217;s likely going to be a lot to cover perhaps, my plan is to introduce concepts and idea for folks to both work on during the sessions themselves and then to develop later on their own. We&#8217;ll see how it goes!</p>
<p>Please note that the workshop is free for members of the Northern Virginia Writers Club, the regional chapter of the Virginia Writers Club, but registration has already closed. — <a href="http://www.arttaylorwriter.com" target="_blank"><em>Art Taylor</em></a></p>

	<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1158640643g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1" /><h2 id="post-5868"><a href="http://artandliterature.wordpress.com/2013/07/21/catching-up-on-my-reading/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Catching Up On My&nbsp;Reading&#8230;.">Catching Up On My&nbsp;Reading&#8230;.</a></h2>
	<small>July 21, 2013 <!-- by artandliterature --></small>

	<div class="entry">
		<p>Over the last week or so, I&#8217;ve been catching up on recent issues of <em>Ellery Queen&#8217;s Mystery Magazine</em>, taking some time to check out short stories by several friends—each of them a writer I admire tremendously. Just wanted to give a quick shout-out to those stories and to what stood out to me about each:</p>
<p><strong><a href="http://www.harleymazuk.com/" target="_blank">Harley Mazuk</a></strong>&#8216;s &#8220;The Road Les Traveled&#8221; (<em>EQMM</em>&#8216;s August 2013 issue) is another of the author&#8217;s Frank Swiver stories, about a PI in 1940s San Francisco. This one centers around a burlesque theater, a blacklisted screenwriter, and his wife—trouble with a capital T. While the plot is great fun, what I enjoyed most was Mazuk&#8217;s playfulness with details and language. When that troubled wife puts one some records and does a little shake and shimmy to lure Swiver into bed, he resists at first—and then comes a nice touch: &#8220;When Bessie Smith sang &#8216;Do Your Duty,&#8217; I took Mrs. St. George to bed.&#8221; Later, during a fight scene, comes another like bit of language play: &#8220;He swung the other arm at me like a leg of mutton, and he caught me on the chops.&#8221; And cleverness too in the quantity and variety of alcohol that permeates the story as well—definitely put to good use!</p>
<p>In the latest <em>EQMM</em>, the September/October issue (on newsstands now), a trio of friends and fine writers have really delivered the goods:</p>
<ul>
<li>In <strong><a href="http://www.danacameron.com/" target="_blank">Dana Cameron</a></strong>&#8216;s taut, tense &#8220;Dialing In,&#8221; a government-mandated assassination attempt goes badly wrong, leaving the shooter with lots of questions and some new missions of her own to carry through.</li>
<li>In <strong><a href="http://thethirteenthchild.com/about-david-dean/" target="_blank">David Dean</a></strong>&#8216;s &#8220;In a Dark Manner,&#8221; a confession of teenage misdeeds takes some surprising turns—with just a hint of light peeking through the darkness.</li>
<li>And <strong><a href="http://www.brendandubois.com/" target="_blank">Brendan DuBois</a></strong>&#8216; &#8220;Breaking the Box&#8221; follows the ups and downs of a high-stakes police interrogation—and then, in a bit of brilliant sleight-of-hand, takes a very unexpected turn.</li>
</ul>
<p>These tales reveal once more why these writers have long since been considered among the genre&#8217;s finest practitioners of the short story. Plenty more I&#8217;m still looking forward to reading in that issue as well, with stories by <strong>Doug Allyn</strong>, <strong>Jon L. Breen</strong>, and <strong>Ed Gorman</strong> and <strong>Ricky Sprague</strong>, among others—and so proud to be a part of it myself.</p>
<p>Beyond <em>EQMM</em>, I&#8217;ve also been sampling a few more of <strong>Cornell Woolrich</strong>&#8216;s tales from the new Centipede Press collection <em><a href="http://www.centipedepress.com/crime/speakmedeath.html" target="_blank">Speak to Me of Death</a>, </em>but I have to admit that I think I&#8217;m going to give up on pushing through the rest of the book. While stories like &#8220;From Dusk to Dawn&#8221; and &#8220;Marihuana&#8221; (the last couple I read) offer those surprise endings that seem to be the hallmark of so much of Woolrich&#8217;s work—satisfying endings, I should stress, often with a cruel twist—the prose itself strikes me as so overwritten, both stylistically and in terms of sheer quantity, that it&#8217;s often tough going. And too often the characters act less like real people than like symbols for an idea or theme or like cogs in a plot, slowly turning and turning relentlessly in the same direction because that&#8217;s what the machinery needs. Historical interest there, of course, but there are simply other, better things for me to be reading right now&#8230;. — <a href="http://www.arttaylorwriter.com"><em>Art Taylor</em></a></p>

	<small>July 9, 2013 <!-- by artandliterature --></small>

	<div class="entry">
		<p><a href="http://artandliterature.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/img004.jpg"><img class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-5861" alt="img004" src="http://artandliterature.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/img004.jpg?w=102&#038;h=150" width="102" height="150" /></a>I&#8217;m thrilled to have had two stories appearing in <em>Ellery Queen&#8217;s Mystery Magazine </em>this year—the second of which has just been published in the <em></em>September/October 2013 double issue. &#8220;Ithaca 37&#8243; takes its title from one of the guns that Michael Caine&#8217;s character used in the film <em>Get Carter</em>—a gun that this story&#8217;s narrator and film aficionado tries to put to good use as well and for some of the same reasons (or at least that&#8217;s what he thinks). This story is the first time I&#8217;ve tried to work pretty comprehensively with a less than reliable narrator, and I hope that the whole thing holds together straight through to the end.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m also pleased to be sharing space in this issue with some extraordinarily talented short story writers, including <strong>Doug Allyn</strong>, <a href="http://www.danacameron.com/" target="_blank"><strong>Dana Cameron</strong></a>, <a href="http://thethirteenthchild.com/about-david-dean/" target="_blank"><strong>David Dean</strong></a>, and <a href="http://www.brendandubois.com/" target="_blank"><strong>Brendan DuBois</strong></a>. Just got the chance last night to read David Dean&#8217;s contribution, &#8220;In a Dark Manner,&#8221; and it&#8217;s another terrific tale of his—marked both by keen suspense and sharp surprises and by the sense of moral weight that&#8217;s such a hallmark of his work. Looking forward to reading the rest of the issue soon too. — <a href="http://www.arttaylorwriter.com" target="_blank"><em>Art Taylor</em></a></p>

And so nice to see fellow Agatha nominees <a href="http://www.barbgoffman.com/" target="_blank">Barb Goffman</a> and <a href="http://www.bkstevensmysteries.com/" target="_blank">B.K. 	<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1158640643g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1" /><h2 id="post-5851"><a href="http://artandliterature.wordpress.com/2013/06/17/not-so-new-fiction-visions-and-revisions-at-redux/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Not-So-New Fiction: &#8220;Visions and Revisions&#8221; at&nbsp;Redux">Not-So-New Fiction: &#8220;Visions and Revisions&#8221; at&nbsp;<i>Redux</i></a></h2>
	<small>June 17, 2013 <!-- by artandliterature --></small>

	<div class="entry">
		<p>Nearly 10 years ago, I was fortunate to be a finalist for <em>North American Review</em>&#8216;s first Kurt Vonnegut Prize for my story &#8220;Visions and Revisions&#8221;—a little tale I&#8217;d tinkered with on and off over a period of about five years, as I recall. The story follows a woman who<em> </em>may be witnessing her new boyfriend abusing her daughter—or maybe not, as it turns out, and there&#8217;s the trouble she&#8217;s encountering: what to believe or not, and what to do about it. Here&#8217;s the opening couple of paragraphs:</p>
<blockquote><p><em>Tending the spaghetti, Sandra studied the cabinets on the wall beside her. Their whiteness, she knew, was proof that she had painted them, wasn’t it? After all, they had been brown once, surely. She remembered picking them out with her ex-husband before the house was built, choosing them from the builder’s ragged catalog, and later she had painted them white when the marriage was over, just to make them hers alone. One hand on the stirring spoon, she raised the other to smooth her fingers across the face of the nearest cabinet’s door. It was slightly wet with steam from the boiling pot, and there was a small smudge near the handle. She’d need to clean that up. These too—the cabinets, the steam, the smudge—were real.</em></p>
<p><em>Her eyes swept across the kitchen: the sink, porcelain not stainless (her choice); the sponge perched on the corner, sticky with soap and destined for the trashcan; the microwave, the toaster, the can opener; the pass-through window separating kitchen and living room, its ledge decorated with a small gathering of imitation Herend rabbits, and beyond that her boyfriend Curt slapping her nine-year-old daughter Wendy; a spice rack; a series of containers for sugar, flour, coffee and tea, ceramic containers whose surfaces were shaped like basketweave; the jolt of the cabinets as his hand struck her cheek; a stray red oven mitt, just out of reach.</em></p>
<p><em>Sandra gripped her thumb and forefinger around the counter&#8217;s edge, pressed it hard. Then she turned her attention back to stirring her spaghetti—turned her attention, in fact, more completely to its swirl and tumble in the big black pot&#8230;.</em></p></blockquote>
<p>&#8220;Visions and Revisions&#8221; appeared in <em>NAR</em>&#8216;s Summer 2004 issue, and this week it&#8217;s been given a fresh life, thanks to Leslie Pietrzyk, the editor at <em>Redux</em>, an “invitation-only literary journal of writers&#8217; favorite, previously published stories and poems, not found elsewhere on the web.” You can find <em>Redux</em>&#8216;s &#8220;reprint&#8221; of  <a href="http://www.reduxlitjournal.com/2013/06/86-visions-and-revisions-by-art-taylor.html" target="_blank">&#8220;Visions and Revisions&#8221; here.</a>  Hope you enjoy! — <a href="http://www.arttaylorwriter.com" target="_blank"><em>Art Taylor</em></a></p>
