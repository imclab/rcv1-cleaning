<br><B>Question</B><BR>I recently sold my 1999 Lexus and the buyer called me two days later to say the starter went out. I had no mechanical problems when I had the car and that is what told him when he test drove it. He says he is going to sue me for fraud...I say "buyer beware"-shouldn't he have had a mechanic check it out? <br><br>
<script type="text/javascript">if(zs>0){w('<span id="gBt">'+x2+'#b">Get the answer below</a><br></span><a name=b></a>');zSB(1,3);w('<br>')}</script><div id="p1" style="float:right"><g:plusone></g:plusone></div><B>Answer</B><br>As an individual, you are responsible to answer a prospective buyer's questions honestly, and you cannot deliberately hide or disguise a known problem (putting duct tape around a leaky fuel or brake line, for example). &nbsp;Other than that, it is "buyer beware" when purchasing a used car from an individual. &nbsp;He'd have a difficult time suing you because of this standard, and the fact that the problem was electrical makes his case even more dubious. &nbsp;Electrical items like starters and fuel pumps tend to work one moment and not the next; they don't always give warning that they're about to give out. &nbsp;And yes, he should have had a mechanic check the car over. &nbsp;If he had requested to take the car to one and you refused, then it should have been a warning to him to look elsewhere. &nbsp;If he never even asked, then it's his fault, not yours. &nbsp;I wouldn't lose a lot of sleep over this one &nbsp;<br>
