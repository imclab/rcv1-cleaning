<p>A <b>convertible</b> is a type of automobile of various <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">automobile body styles</a> that can <i>convert</i> from open-air mode to a provisional enclosed (roofed) mode.</p>
<p>Roof designs have varied widely and have evolved from the earliest models, where roofs were demountable and/or detachable. Contemporary roofs are often hinged to fold away, either into a recess behind the rear seats or into the boot or trunk of the vehicle. The roof may operate either manually or automatically via hydraulic or electrical actuators, and the roof itself may be constructed of soft or rigid material. Soft-tops are made of vinyl, canvas or other textile material, while <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hard-tops</a> are made of steel, aluminum, plastic, or other rigid materials.</p>
<p>Convertibles may also be called <i>drop top</i>, <i>rag top,</i> <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a>, <i>cabriolet</i>, or <i>cabrio</i>. Hard-top convertibles may be called <i>coupé cabriolet</i>, <i>coupé convertible</i>, <i>retractable hardtop</i> or, when equipped with two seats, <i>coupé roadster</i>/<i>roadster coupé</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flexible_or_rigid_roofs">Flexible or rigid roofs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Flexible or rigid roofs">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Flexible or rigid roofs" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roof designs vary widely, but a few characteristics are common to all convertibles. Roofs are affixed to the body of the vehicle and are usually not detachable. Instead the roof is hinged and folds away, either into a recess behind the rear seats or into the boot or trunk of the vehicle. The roof may operate either manually or automatically via hydraulic or electrical actuators; the roof itself may be constructed of soft or rigid material. Soft-tops are made of vinyl, canvas or other textile material; hard-top convertibles have roofs made from steel, aluminium, carbon fibre, or plastic materials.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions">Descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descriptions">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descriptions" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contemporary convertibles are known and marketed under several different terms due to the convergence of body styles over the years. A convertible may be described as a <i>drop-head coupé</i>, a <i>cabriolet</i> or <i>cabrio</i>, an open 2-seater without wind-up windows as a <i>spyder</i>, <i>spider</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a></i>. Retractable rigid roof (or hard-top) cars are marketed as <i>retractable hardtop</i> and French versions as <i>coupé cabriolet</i> or <i>coupé convertible</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folding_textile_roof">Folding textile roof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Folding textile roof">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Folding textile roof" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The collapsible textile roof section (of cloth or vinyl) over an articulated folding frame may include linings such as a sound-deadening layer or interior cosmetic headliner (to hide the frame) &#160;– or both&#160;– and may have electrical or electro-hydraulic mechanisms for raising the roof. The erected top secures to the windshield frame header with manual latches, semi-manual latches, or fully automatic latches. The folded convertible top is called the <i>stack</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cons">Cons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cons">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cons" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Convertibles offer the flexibility of an open top in trade for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Potentially reduced safety.</li>
<li>Poor break-in protection.</li>
<li>Deterioration and shrinkage of the sun-exposed textile fabric over time.</li>
<li>Problems with trunk floor pan rust-through due to leakage of a improperly maintained top.</li>
<li>A heavier vehicle and higher curb weight due to additional structure required to restore both torsion and flexure stiffness normally provided by a metal roof and (in some cases) door window frames, and additional weight from motorized mechanisms (where provided). Body-on-frame styles usually included an entire X-brace within the conventional ladder frame.</li>
<li>A narrower rear seat due to space required by the folded side rails.</li>
<li>Rear visibility deterioration if a plastic rear window is used.</li>
<li>Diminished rear visibility, from a large roof structure, small rear window, or obstructed rear window&#160;– or a combination of these: <small>e.g.,</small> <a href="/wiki/Mini_(BMW)#Mini_Convertible" title="Mini (BMW)" class="mw-redirect">Mini convertible</a>.</li>
<li>Generally poor <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">structural rigidity</a>. Contemporary engineering goes to great length to counteract the effects of removal of a car's roof. For example, a 2007 article in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times" class="mw-redirect">New York Times</a></i>, referring to the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Eos" title="Volkswagen Eos">Volkswagen Eos</a>, reported:<sup id="cite_ref-nyt2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt2-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<blockquote class="templatequote">

A beefed-up windshield frame of hot-stamped ultra-high-strength steel is connected directly to the floorpan’s reinforced frame rails. Steel tubing provides more stiffness behind the doors for an extra layer of safety. Partly as a consequence, rear seat passengers have about 10 inches less shoulder room than in the smaller Rabbit.</div>

</blockquote>
<ul>
<li>Specifically poor structural rigidity, such as pronounced <a href="/wiki/Scuttle_shake" title="Scuttle shake">scuttle shake</a>, a characteristic whereby the structural design of the bulkhead between engine and passenger compartment of a convertible suffers sufficiently poor rigidity to negatively impact ride or handling&#160;– or allow noticeable vibration, shudder or chassis-flexing into the passenger compartment.</li>
<li>Lesser handling qualities in sports models due to lower torsional stiffness – therefore convertibles require harder suspension set ups to achieve the equivalent roadholding, coming at the expense of poorer ride quality.</li>
<li>In many cases with a soft top, less aerodynamic than an equivalent coupe, especially with the top down.</li>
<li>In most cases, reduced cargo space.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tonneau_covers">Tonneau covers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tonneau covers">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tonneau covers" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_(4).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg/220px-MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg/330px-MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg/440px-MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_%284%29.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MG_TD_-_Flickr_-_exfordy_(4).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
MG TD with Tonneau cover over passenger seat and luggage well</div>

</div>
<p>Folding textile convertible tops often do not hide completely the mechanism of the folded top or can expose the vulnerable underside of the folded top to sun exposure and fading&#160;– in which case <a href="/wiki/Tonneau" title="Tonneau">tonneau</a> covers of various designs snap or secure into place to protect the folded roof and hide the mechanicals. Detachable foldable, rigid or semi-rigid covers require space-consuming storage inside the vehicle&#160;– and sometimes complicated installation from outside the stationary vehicle. Foldable vinyl and cloth covers can be prone to shrinkage, further complicating installation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_the_tonneau_cover">Evolution of the tonneau cover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evolution of the tonneau cover">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evolution of the tonneau cover" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Tonneau#Origin" title="Tonneau">tonneau</a> of a car is the rear part of an open car, usually passenger seats, other times just a luggage well. A tonneau cover protects that area when not in use. The tonneau cover may also extend over the folded roof and an unused front passenger seat and further extend over the entire passenger compartment particularly for an open car without side-weather-protection (windows sliding down into the doors). It then provides complete weather protection for the entire passenger compartment and some sense of security to the owner.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The MKI (first generation) <a href="/wiki/MG_MGB" title="MG MGB">MGB</a> (1963) <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a> could be supplied with a foldable vinyl tonneau cover, often supported by a light detachable tube installed to span behind the seats, the cover being attached to the car with a series of twenty press fit snaps. The standard cover was a permanent vinyl or cloth roof or convertible top&#160;– fixed to a relatively complex manually erected convertible frame. Weather-proof erection required the use of those same snaps from outside the car if a tonneau cover had just been removed.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Convertibles such as the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_LeBaron" title="Chrysler LeBaron">Chrysler LeBaron</a> (c. 1988) used sleeve and groove systems to anchor a foldable vinyl tonneau cover, again installed manually from outside the car. Later textile convertibles used semi-rigid plastic tonneau covers, e.g., the first generation <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">Audi TT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Allant%C3%A9" title="Cadillac Allanté">Cadillac Allanté</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Convertibles such as the fifth generation of the <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Eldorado#1971.E2.80.931978" title="Cadillac Eldorado">Cadillac Eldorado</a> featured a detachable two-part, fully rigid, manually installed tonneau sufficiently strong to support a seated person&#160;– also known as a <i>parade boot</i>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Convertibles such as the second generation <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes SL</a> popularized the integral manually operated self-storing rigid tonneau cover – in its case accompanied by a separate removable hardtop. In either case, the design required manual operation from outside the stationary vehicle.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Convertibles such as the first <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Boxster" title="Porsche Boxster">Porsche Boxster</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-concept_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concept-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Toyota_MR2" title="Toyota MR2">Toyota MR2</a> and third-generation <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MX5" title="Mazda MX5" class="mw-redirect">Mazda MX5 (NC)</a> featured <i>Z-fold</i> (aka zig-zag fold<sup id="cite_ref-concept_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concept-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>) tops, whereby the exterior of the neatly retracted fabric roof also protected the remaining roof from sun exposure&#160;– eliminating the aesthetic or protective need for a tonneau cover.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Convertibles such as the second-generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Thunderbird" title="Ford Thunderbird">Ford Thunderbird</a> (1958) convertible and the fourth-generation <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes SL</a> popularized the complex electro-hydraulic roof mechanism that automatically secured the folded top under a rigid tonneau&#160;– button activated by a seated driver&#160;– and later more routinely available on convertibles such as the <a href="/wiki/Volvo_C70" title="Volvo C70">Volvo C70</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Sebring_(convertible)" title="Chrysler Sebring (convertible)" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Sebring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Eclipse" title="Mitsubishi Eclipse">Mitsubishi Eclipse Spyder</a>.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>The contemporary <a href="/wiki/Retractable_hardtop" title="Retractable hardtop" class="mw-redirect">retractable hardtop</a> convertible such as the <a href="/wiki/BMW_3_Series_(E90)#Convertible_.28E93.29" title="BMW 3 Series (E90)">BMW E93 3-series</a> include tonneau covers that "self-store" the roof assembly.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detachable_hardtops">Detachable hardtops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Detachable hardtops">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Detachable hardtops" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Convertibles such as both the first and eleventh generation of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Thunderbird" title="Ford Thunderbird">Ford Thunderbird</a> and the second and third generations of the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes SL</a> featured as standard or optional equipment fully rigid, manually installable hardtops&#160;– later examples including heatable rear windows. These hardtops provided acoustic insulation but also required space-consuming off-season storage&#160;– and a cumbersome two-person installation. The optional aluminum (i.e., lightweight) detachable hardtop for a <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Boxster" title="Porsche Boxster">Porsche Boxster</a> weighed 51&#160;lb (23&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-concept_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concept-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Two current-day examples of vehicles with a detachable hardtop available are the <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Wrangler" title="Jeep Wrangler">Jeep Wrangler</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MX-5" title="Mazda MX-5">Mazda MX-5</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convertible_windows">Convertible windows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Convertible windows">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Convertible windows" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Side windows non-existent in open cars which may have detachable side screens, are manually or power operated glass side windows as in a saloon or sedan. Rear-windows have evolved similarly, with plastic rear-windows appearing as late as the first generation <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Boxster" title="Porsche Boxster">Porsche Boxster</a>. Contemporary convertibles and retractable hardtops feature <a href="/wiki/Heatable_Glass" title="Heatable Glass" class="mw-redirect">heatable glass</a> rear windows to maximize visibility&#160;– though rear windows often can compromise visibility by their size, as with the case of the very small rear window and restricted visibility of the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Eclipse" title="Mitsubishi Eclipse">Mitsubishi Eclipse Spyder</a>. Plastic windows can degrade, fade, yellow and crack over time, diminishing visibility.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Windblockers">Windblockers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Windblockers">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Windblockers" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Windblockers or wind deflectors minimize noise and rushing air reaching the occupants&#160;– specifically cold air (and the noise that comes with it) rushing from behind the passengers having been forced over the windshield then returning to the natural lower-pressure zone where the passengers sit.</p>
<p>Mazda pioneered the windblocker with its <a href="/wiki/Mazda_RX7" title="Mazda RX7" class="mw-redirect">Mazda RX7</a> convertible featuring an integral rigid opaque panel that folded up from behind the two seats. Current convertibles feature windblockers of various designs including detachable fold-up designs (<small>e.g.,</small> <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Camry_Solara" title="Toyota Camry Solara">Toyota Solara</a>), vertically retractable glass (<small>e.g.,</small> <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">Audi TT</a>), minimal flaps (<small>e.g.,</small> <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Miata" title="Mazda Miata" class="mw-redirect">Mazda Miata</a>)&#160;– or other integrated wind controlling systems.</p>
<p>Mercedes and Audi currently offer a heating duct to the neck area of the seat on <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SLK-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SLK-Class">SLK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">SL</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Audi_A5" title="Audi A5">A5</a>/<a href="/wiki/Audi_S5" title="Audi S5">S5</a> models, that is marketed as the "Air Scarf".</p>
<p>According to the chief engineer for the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Sebring_(convertible)" title="Chrysler Sebring (convertible)" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Sebring</a>, Jim Issner, the windblocker for the Sebring reduces "wind noise by approximately 11 to 12 <a href="/wiki/Decibels" title="Decibels" class="mw-redirect">decibels</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-sebringpress_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sebringpress-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contemporary convertible design may include such features as electrically heated glass rear window (for improved visibility), seat belt <a href="/wiki/Seat_belt#Pretensioners_and_webclamps" title="Seat belt">pre-tensioners</a>, boron steel reinforced A-pillars, front and side airbags, safety cage construction&#160;– a horseshoe like structure around the passenger compartment&#160;– and <a href="/wiki/Roll_over_protection_structure" title="Roll over protection structure">roll over protection structures or (ROPS)</a> with <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnically charged</a> <a href="/wiki/Roll_hoop" title="Roll hoop" class="mw-redirect">roll hoops</a> hidden behind the rear seats that deploy under roll-over conditions whether the roof is retracted or not.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Volvo_C70" title="Volvo C70">Volvo C70</a> retractable hardtop includes a door-mounted side impact protection inflatable curtain which inflates <i>upward</i> from the interior belt-line&#160;– vs. downward like the typical curtain airbag.<sup id="cite_ref-mweek_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mweek-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The curtain has an extra stiff construction with double rows of slats that are slightly offset from each other. This allows them to remain upright and offer effective head protection even an open window. The curtain also deflates slowly to provide protection should the car roll over.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Variations">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Variations" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Convertibles have offered numerous iterations that fall between the first mechanically simple but attention-demanding fabric tops to highly complex modern retractable hardtops:</p>
<p><i>Roadster:</i> A <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a> was an open two-seater possibly with a frame that required actual assembly (i.e., not retracting) and separately installable soft side "window" panels&#160;– offering little protection from inclement weather and often requiring time-consuming apparently complicated installation. Examples range from the very first cars to the vintage <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Speedster" title="Porsche Speedster" class="mw-redirect">Porsche Speedster</a> introduced in 1955, and the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XK120" title="Jaguar XK120">Jaguar XK120</a> Roadster unveiled in 1948 right up to the most recent Porsche Spyders. For most in the U.S., a contemporary roadster is a two-seater convertible such as the <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">Audi TT</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMW_Z8" title="BMW Z8" class="mw-redirect">BMW Z8</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Solstice" title="Pontiac Solstice">Pontiac Solstice</a>.</p>
<p><i>Landau &amp; Rigid Door:</i> <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a>'s 1948 <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_2CV" title="Citroën 2CV">Citroën 2CV</a> featured a sunroof that rolled back on itself, and extended to the rear bumper in place of a separate boot/trunk lid. This was for loading versatility that pre-dated <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchbacks</a>. Later models had a boot/trunk lid or an optional hatchback, and an internally opening sunroof, with a secured 'half-open' position. It had rigid body sides framing two doors on each side&#160;– followed in concept by such cars as the 1950 <a href="/wiki/Nash_Motors" title="Nash Motors">Nash</a> Rambler Convertible Coupe.</p>
<p>Citroën marketed the <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C3" title="Citroën C3">C3 Pluriel</a> (Pluriel is a <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with the English <i>plural</i>), which can be configured into five iterations, hence the name:<sup id="cite_ref-channel4_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-channel4-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>a hatchback with a multi-layer insulated top.<sup id="cite_ref-honest_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honest-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>a full-length "landau" sedan, operable partially or to the back window or any stage in between, with a buffet-minimizing wind deflector over the windshield.<sup id="cite_ref-honest_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honest-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>a semi-convertible, with the roof open to the back window, the roof assembly folds into a well in the trunk floor.<sup id="cite_ref-honest_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honest-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>a full convertible, whereby roof side rails are unlatched and removed.<sup id="cite_ref-honest_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honest-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>a roadster pick-up, where the back seats fold to a pickup-like bed with a drop-down tailgate.<sup id="cite_ref-honest_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honest-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><i>The Four-Door:</i> Buick advertised a Series 60 "Convertible Phaeton" body style in the 1934 Model Year, which was actually a four-Door convertible, <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> 1938-39 Roadmaster, and 1940-41 Super. Oldsmobile in the 98 Series 1941-47, and Cadillac in 1939 Series 61, and 1940-41 Series 62 models. The <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Continental" title="Lincoln Continental">Lincoln Continental</a> was available as a 4-door convertible in model years 1961 to 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The only current production 4-door convertible is the <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Wrangler" title="Jeep Wrangler">Jeep Wrangler</a> Unlimited.</p>
<p>Peugeot presented the a <a href="/wiki/Concept_car" title="Concept car">concept</a> four-door <a href="/wiki/Retractable_hardtop" title="Retractable hardtop" class="mw-redirect">retractable hardtop</a> convertible, the <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_407#407_concept_cars" title="Peugeot 407">Peugeot 407 Macarena</a> in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Produced by French coachbuilding specialist <a href="/wiki/Heuliez" title="Heuliez">Heuliez</a>, the Macarena's top can be folded in 60 seconds,<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> with a steel reinforcing beam behind the front seats incorporating LCD screens for the rear passengers into the crossmember.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Drophead Coupe, Coupé Cabriolet or Coupé Cabrio:</i> A type of convertible with only two doors,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and thereby recalling the <a href="/wiki/Cabriolet_(carriage)" title="Cabriolet (carriage)">cabriolet carriage</a>. With its <a href="/wiki/Mazda_RX7" title="Mazda RX7" class="mw-redirect">Mazda RX7</a> convertible, Mazda introduced a two-seater convertible with a removable rigid section over the passengers, removable independently of power operated textile section behind with heatable glass rear window. During the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_cars" title="Jaguar cars" class="mw-redirect">Jaguar</a> produced an XJ-SC with two removable panels over the front seats and a partial fold-down convertible section in the back. It retained the rear side windows of the coupe and had fixed cant rails above these and the door glass. This allowed an almost full convertible with roll-over safety. Going back in Jaguar history, during the 1950s the XK 120 Drophead Coupe (DHC) and later variants, provided open-air motoring with quite civilized fully lined insulated tops with the weather-protection of the hardtop models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_in_the_United_States">History in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History in the United States">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History in the United States" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the 1910 introduction by <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_(automobile)" title="Cadillac (automobile)" class="mw-redirect">Cadillac</a> of the United States' first closed-body car, the open or convertible car was the primary body style. U.S. automakers manufactured a broad range of models during the 1950s and 1960s&#160;– from economical compact-sized models such as the <a href="/wiki/Rambler_American" title="Rambler American">Rambler American</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Studebaker_Lark" title="Studebaker Lark">Studebaker Lark</a>, to the more expensive models such as the <a href="/wiki/Packard_Caribbean" title="Packard Caribbean">Packard Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_98" title="Oldsmobile 98">Oldsmobile 98</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_(automobile)" title="Imperial (automobile)">Imperial</a> by Chrysler.</p>
<p>Proposed major safety standards suggested during the mid-1970s for the 1980 model year included a 50-mile-per-hour (80&#160;km/h) crash to the front, at 25&#160;mph (40&#160;km/h) on the sides, as well as a rollover at 30 miles per hour (48&#160;km/h), a test that open top convertibles would unlikely be able to pass. Although the requirements were reduced, sales of convertible body styles were falling during the early-1970s. <a href="/wiki/Automobile_air_conditioning" title="Automobile air conditioning">Automobile air conditioning</a> systems were also becoming popular. In 1976, Cadillac marketed the <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_Eldorado" title="Cadillac Eldorado">Eldorado</a> as "the last of the American Convertibles". During this period of low convertible production, <a href="/wiki/T-top" title="T-top">T-tops</a> became an alternative for a few models.</p>
<p>Elsewhere globally, convertible production continued throughout this era with models such as the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class#1972_to_1989" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes SL</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">VW Beetle Cabriolet</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf#Golf_Mk1.2FRabbit_.28A1.2FTyp_17.2C_1974.E2.80.931984.29" title="Volkswagen Golf">VW Golf Cabriolet</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_E-type" title="Jaguar E-type" class="mw-redirect">Jaguar E-type</a>.</p>
<p>In the 1980s convertibles such as the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_LeBaron" title="Chrysler LeBaron">Chrysler LeBaron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saab_900" title="Saab 900">Saab 900</a> revived the body style in the United States&#160;– followed by models such as the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Miata" title="Mazda Miata" class="mw-redirect">Mazda Miata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Boxster" title="Porsche Boxster">Porsche Boxster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audi_TT" title="Audi TT">Audi TT</a>, and later <a href="#Retractable_hardtop_roof">retractable hardtop</a> models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_English">British English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: British English">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: British English" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Until the 1950s an open car was a tourer. A car that could convert from open to closed with wind-up windows and a fully lined roof was called an <b>all-weather tourer</b>. Tourers with side screens and simple light temporary roofs were the most common form of open car and generally the cheapest body for any car because of the lighter weight — less complication, less heavy glass and no metal roof.</p>
<p>Wind-up windows were expensive to provide on this type of body, and fully lined flexible fabric roofs were very expensive.</p>

<p>A <i>retractable hardtop</i>, also known as <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_coup%C3%A9_convertibles" title="List of coupé convertibles">coupé convertible</a></i> or <i>coupé cabriolet</i>, is a type of convertible that forgoes a folding textile roof in favor of an automatically operated, multi-part, self-storing <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> where the rigid roof sections are opaque, translucent, or independently operable.</p>
<p>An American named Ben P. Ellerbeck had innovated the first practical retractable hardtop system 1922 — a manually operated system on a Hudson coupe that never saw full production.<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The first <i>retractable hardtop</i> was from France when in 1934 Peugeot introduced the <i>601 Éclipse</i>, designed by Georges Paulin.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The retractable hardtop solves some issues with the soft-top convertible, but has its own compromises, namely mechanical complexity, expense and more often than not, reduced luggage capacity.</p>
<p>A 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times" class="mw-redirect">New York Times</a></i> article suggested the retractable hardtop may herald the demise of the textile-roofed convertible,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and a 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal" class="mw-redirect">Wall Street Journal</a></i> article suggested "more and more convertibles are eschewing soft cloth tops in favor of sophisticated folding metal roofs, making them practical in all climates, year-round."<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Construction">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Construction" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Retractable hardtops can vary in material (steel, plastic or aluminum), can vary from two to five in the number of rigid sections and often rely on complex dual-hinged trunk (<i>British: boot</i>) lids that enable the trunk lid to both receive the retracting top from the front and also receive parcels or luggage from the rear – along with complex trunk divider mechanisms to prevent loading of luggage that would conflict with the operation of the hardtop.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_variations">Construction variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Construction variations">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Construction variations" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Eos" title="Volkswagen Eos">Volkswagen Eos</a> features a five-segment retractable roof where one section is itself an independently sliding transparent sunroof.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_XLR" title="Cadillac XLR">Cadillac XLR</a> features a retractable aluminum hardtop requiring 6'-<span class="frac nowrap">10 <sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>" of vertical clearance during retraction, and manufactured by a supplier joint venture between <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mtrend_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtrend-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes SL</a> hardtop features a glass section that rotates during retraction to provide a more compact "stack."</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MX-5" title="Mazda MX-5">Mazda MX-5</a> has been available since the 2006 model year with an optional power retractable hardtop, in lieu of the standard folding-textile soft-top. The cost of the retractable hardtop is actually less than the price premium charged to a separate hardtop. Compared to the regular soft-top, the hardtop weighs 77&#160;lb (35&#160;kg) more yet has no reduction in cargo capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-autoweek.com_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autoweek.com-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> The MX-5 is one of the few cars offering both hardtop and soft-top convertible choices, so it is possible to make a direct performance comparison; the hardtop has slower acceleration but a higher top speed. The hardtop roof is constructed of polycarbonate and manufactured by the German firm Webasto.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds.com_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds.com-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Daihatsu" title="Daihatsu">Daihatsu</a> marketed the <a href="/wiki/Daihatsu_Copen" title="Daihatsu Copen">Copen</a> in the ultra-compact Japanese <a href="/wiki/Kei_car" title="Kei car">Kei</a> class.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Sebring" title="Chrysler Sebring">Chrysler Sebring</a>'s (and its successor the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_200" title="Chrysler 200">Chrysler 200</a>'s) retractable hardtop also is marketed alongside a soft-top. According to development engineer Dave Lauzun, during construction, the <a href="/wiki/Karmann" title="Karmann">Karmann-made</a> tops are dropped into a body that is largely identical: both soft-top and retractable feature the same automatic tonneau cover, luggage divider and luggage space.<sup id="cite_ref-youtube.com_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube.com-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The retractable does feature an underbody cross-brace not included in the softtop.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Volvo_C70" title="Volvo C70">Volvo C70</a>, its retractable hardtop manufactured by Webasto<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds.com_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds.com-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> includes a <i>global window switch</i> that allows simultaneous raising or lowering of all windows,<sup id="cite_ref-cars.com_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cars.com-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> and a button to power-activate the raising of the folded top stack within the trunk to access cargo below.<sup id="cite_ref-cars.com_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cars.com-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Opel_Astra_TwinTop" title="Opel Astra TwinTop" class="mw-redirect">Vauxhaul/Opel Astra TwinTop</a>, its three-piece retractable hardtop manufactured by Webasto<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds.com_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds.com-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> includes a <i>global window switch</i> that allows simultaneous raising or lowering of all windows and a button to power-activate the raising of the folded top stack within the trunk to access cargo below.<sup id="cite_ref-cars.com_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cars.com-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pros_and_cons">Pros and cons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pros and cons">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pros and cons" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The retractable hardtop convertible trades higher initial cost, mechanical complexity and, with rare exception, diminished trunk space – for increased acoustic insulation, durability, and break-in protection similar to that of a fixed roof coupe.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pros">Pros</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Pros">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Pros" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The retractable hardtop's advantages are:</p>
<ul>
<li>more practical in all climates year-round, compared to soft-tops which are more prone to leakage in heavy rain or winter. In addition, hardtops reduce wind noise and are more secure (the cloth of certain soft-tops can be easily slashed with a knife).<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-automobilemag.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-automobilemag.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>increased <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">structural rigidity</a> over a soft-top.</li>
<li>eliminates the need for a storage-consuming, manually-from-outside-the-vehicle-installable, separate or integral, rigid or foldable, <a href="#Tonneau_covers">tonneau cover</a> to conceal the mechanicals of a folded textile top.</li>
<li>eliminates the need to protect the vulnerable underside of a folded textile top from UV fading.</li>
<li>eliminates the need for a separate rigid hardtop requiring space-consuming off-season storage and a cumbersome twice-yearly, two-person manual installation and removal – a system popularized, for example, by the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz SL-Class">Mercedes-Benz SL-Class</a> of 1963 to 2001.</li>
<li>may enable consolidation/simplification of a manufacturer's car lineup; for instance the current <a href="/wiki/BMW_Z4" title="BMW Z4">BMW Z4</a> (<a href="/wiki/BMW_E89" title="BMW E89" class="mw-redirect">E89</a>) is offering only as a coupé-convertible (hardtop), compared to the preceding <a href="/wiki/BMW_E85" title="BMW E85" class="mw-redirect">E85</a> generation that had separate coupé and cabriolet (soft-top) variants.</li>
<li>part of a popular trend that automakers have been following in the late 2000s. Hardtops are nicknamed "metal origami" and top-up they are considered more attractive than soft-tops.<sup id="cite_ref-automobilemag.com_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-automobilemag.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> A 2006 <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times" class="mw-redirect">New York Times</a></i> article suggested the retractable hardtop may herald the demise of the textile-roofed convertible,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and a 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" title="Wall Street Journal" class="mw-redirect">Wall Street Journal</a></i> article suggested "more and more convertibles are eschewing soft cloth tops in favor of sophisticated folding metal roofs".<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Soft-top convertibles are often considered by the automotive press to not be in the "same league" as more complex hardtops.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Hard-tops generally have better visibility than soft-tops when the roof is up.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cons_2">Cons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cons">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cons" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG/220px-1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG/330px-1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG/440px-1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1958_Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner_roof.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner" title="Ford Fairlane 500 Skyliner">Ford Fairlane 500 Skyliner</a> during conversion</div>
</div>

<p>In addition to higher initial cost, increased mechanical complexity – and thereby potentially higher repair cost, hardtops have the general disadvantages of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Diminished passenger and trunk space compared to a soft-top convertible. Indeed some hardtops have a nearly unusable trunk once the top is lowered.<sup id="cite_ref-caranddriver.com_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caranddriver.com-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-motortrend.com_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-motortrend.com-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Even some soft-top models use the trunk for storage of top.</li>
<li>Hardtops generally have a higher weight and center of gravity which limits handling dynamics, so the Audi R8 Spyder, Porsche Boxster and 911 Cabriolet have retained soft-tops. Audi has yet to produce a hardtop convertible as of 2011, since they include <a href="/wiki/Quattro_(four-wheel-drive_system)" title="Quattro (four-wheel-drive system)">quattro</a> as standard equipment in their convertibles (after the discontinuation of the 2007 Audi A4 2.0T cabriolet) which already adds extra weight.<sup id="cite_ref-caranddriver.com_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caranddriver.com-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-motortrend.com_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-motortrend.com-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>decreased <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">structural rigidity</a> compared to a fixed-roof coupe. The 2010 Infiniti G37 Convertible tries to solve this problem with a folding steel roof, however this adds considerable mass to the vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-automobilemag.com_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-automobilemag.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The retractable hardtop may lift the articulating sections of the roof during retraction, requiring increased vertical clearance. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Volvo_C70" title="Volvo C70">Volvo C70</a> requires 6.5 feet (2.0&#160;m) of clearance during operation.<sup id="cite_ref-volvoownerdoc_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-volvoownerdoc-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_XLR" title="Cadillac XLR">Cadillac XLR</a> requires 6'-<span class="frac nowrap">10 <sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>" of vertical clearance.</li>
<li>The retractable hardtop may, such as in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_SLK" title="Mercedes SLK" class="mw-redirect">Mercedes SLK</a>, require additional rear clearance behind the car during operation of the top, the trunklid extending rearward while lowering or lifting the top.</li>
<li>The retractable hardtop frequently relies on battery power, and in the event of battery failure, can leave a partially retracted roof vulnerable to a downpour. Volvo includes an emergency roof cover with each <a href="/wiki/Volvo_C70" title="Volvo C70">Volvo C70</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-volvoownerdoc_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-volvoownerdoc-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> The Cadillac XLR owners manual contains seven pages of detailed instructions on how to manually lift the top. This problem is not unique to retractable hardtops since some soft-tops rely on battery power as well.</li>
<li>With numerous articulated sections, each joint or seal is an opportunity for water leakage.</li>
<li>Failure of hydraulics or electrical systems may result in substantial repair bills, though some models of ragtops have similar setbacks when they employ a powered top-down sequence.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: History">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convertible&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: History" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><b>1922</b> Ben P. Ellerbeck conceived the first practical retractable hardtop system in 1922 – a manually operated system on a <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Motor_Car_Company" title="Hudson Motor Car Company">Hudson</a> coupe that allowed unimpeded use of the <a href="/wiki/Rumble_seat" title="Rumble seat">rumble seat</a> even with the top down<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> – but never saw production.<sup id="cite_ref-retractableonline_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retractableonline-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>1935</b> <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> introduced the first production, power-operated retractable hardtop in 1935, the <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_402" title="Peugeot 402">402 Éclipse Décapotable</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> designed and patented by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Paulin" title="Georges Paulin">Georges Paulin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The French coachbuilder, <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Pourtout" title="Marcel Pourtout" class="mw-redirect">Marcel Pourtout</a>, custom-built other examples of Paulin's designs on a larger Peugeot chassis as well.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The first Eclipse 402s offered a power-retractable top, but in 1936 was replaced by a manually operated version on a stretched chassis, built in limited numbers until World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>1941</b> <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> presents the retractable hardtop <a href="/wiki/Concept_car" title="Concept car">concept car</a>, the Chrysler Thunderbolt.<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>1953</b> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> spent an estimated <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>2 million (US$17,161,692 in 2013 dollars<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-US_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup>) to engineer a <a href="/wiki/Continental_Mark_II" title="Continental Mark II">Continental Mark II</a> with a servo-operated retractable roof. The project was headed by Ben Smith, a 30-year-old draftsman.<sup id="cite_ref-greatcarstv_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greatcarstv-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> The concept was rejected for cost and marketing reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>1955</b> Brothers Ed and Jim Gaylord showed their first prototype at the 1955 Paris motor show,<sup id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> but the car failed to reach production.</p>
<p><b>1957</b> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fairlane_500_Skyliner" title="Ford Fairlane 500 Skyliner">Skyliner</a> in the United States. A total of 48,394 were built from 1957 to 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The retractable top was noted for its complexity and usually decent reliability<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> in the pre-transistor era. Its mechanism contained 10 power relays, 10 <a href="/wiki/Limit_switch" title="Limit switch">limit switches</a>, four lock motors, three drive motors, eight circuit breakers, as well as 610 feet (190&#160;m) of electrical wire,<sup id="cite_ref-drivingtoday_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drivingtoday-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> and could raise or lower the top in about 40 seconds. The Skyliner was a <a href="/wiki/Halo_car" title="Halo car" class="mw-redirect">halo car</a> with little luggage space (i.e., practicality), and cost twice that of a baseline Ford <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>.</p>
<p><b>1989</b> <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> introduced a modern retractable hardtop, the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Soarer" title="Toyota Soarer">MZ20 Soarer Aerocabin</a>. The car featured an electric folding hardtop and was marketed as a 2-seater with a cargo area behind the front seats. Production was 500 units.</p>
<p><b>1995</b> The <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_3000GT" title="Mitsubishi 3000GT" class="mw-redirect">Mitsubishi 3000GT Spyder</a> convertible by ASC was marketed in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-retractableonline_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retractableonline-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> The design was further popularized by such cars as the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_SLK" title="Mercedes-Benz SLK" class="mw-redirect">Mercedes-Benz SLK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> and 2001 <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_206" title="Peugeot 206">Peugeot 206 CC</a>.</p>
<p><b>2006</b> Peugeot presented a <a href="/wiki/Concept_car" title="Concept car">concept</a> four-door <a href="/wiki/Retractable_hardtop" title="Retractable hardtop" class="mw-redirect">retractable hardtop</a> convertible, the <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_407_Macarena" title="Peugeot 407 Macarena" class="mw-redirect">Peugeot 407 Macarena</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Produced by French coachbuilding specialist <a href="/wiki/Heuliez" title="Heuliez">Heuliez</a>, the Macarena's top can be folded in about 30 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> It has a reinforcing beam behind the front seats which incorporates LCD screens into the crossmember for the rear passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-edmunds_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmunds-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
