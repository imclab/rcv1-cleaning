show-boating, big car down the full length of Christopher Street from
Greenwich Avenue to West Street (West Side Highway) at the Hudson River
in Greenwich Village, New York -- and still do -- and many Gay and
other people yelled out -- and still do -- "The 'Stonewall Car'"! <br>

Those original participants and&nbsp;observers readily recognized the
cool, unique, stand-out, metallic blue, big Cadillac convertible,
one-and-only car impounded ("hijacked") by the New York City Police
Department ("NYCPD") on the first night of the Stonewall Rebellion (it was not a
'riot' -- no vandalism, no injuries, not one auto insurance claim) on Friday night, June 27th, 1969 at
about 2 a.m. &nbsp;The car with the top 'down' had been parked, as usual,
on the same block of the Stonewall Club on Christopher Street -- really
only a stone's throw from the club's entrance. &nbsp;The car was driven
(not towed, as many think) by&nbsp;two un-uniformed NYCPD police
officers to the old Sixth Precinct on Charles Street&nbsp;near the
Hudson River in Greenwich Village. &nbsp;Fortunately, the
event&nbsp;was a 'rebellion' and not a 'riot' or the car would have
been definitely damaged or possibly destroyed. &nbsp;There would have
also been a huge amount of other automobile&nbsp;insurance claims.
&nbsp;In reality, there were no insurance claims by anyone for anything
-- not even a scratch.<br>

      <td>A
quarter-of-a-century later on January 1, 1994, the 1969
"Stonewall Car" officially was bestowed with "vintage" automobile
status in accordance with the New York State Department of Motor
Vehicles' requirements and guidelines. &nbsp;A vehicle must be at least
25 years since production. &nbsp;It is ironic that this silver
milestone for the historic&nbsp;car occurred in the exact year
of&nbsp;&nbsp; "Stonewall 25". &nbsp;Remember, the car and the
rebellion were born in the same year! &nbsp;Throughout the Spring
of 1994, the "Stonewall Car", with actual veterans of the 1969
Stonewall Rebellion, was on display at seemingly endless events,
celebrations, commemorations and even Gay clubs and restaurants hosting
"SW-25" parties and dinners and churches memorializing this historic
anniversary. &nbsp;On "Stonewall Sunday", June 26, 1994, the "Stonewall
Car" was in the <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
New</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
York </span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
Sunday</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
News</span> (with a circulation of nearly one million), describing the
history of
the car vis-a-vis the Stonewall Rebellion. &nbsp;The only Gay newspaper
in New York City at that time was the <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
New </span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
York</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
Native</span>, which
assigned a reporter to the car, the Stonewall veterans, the "alternate"
march and the S.V.A. at the huge rally for the entire day. &nbsp;<span style="text-decoration: underline;">The</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
New</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
York</span> <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
Times</span> described the "Stonewall Car" as "... in mint condition".
&nbsp;That is the supreme automotive complement! </td>

      <td>The beautiful, blue acrylic lacquer 1969 "Stonewall Car" led
the 1994 "Spirit of Stonewall March" from the legendary Stonewall Club
in Greenwich Village, New York, through some narrow streets passing the
historic Washington Square Arch, with the huge Twin Towers of the World
Trade Center in the distant background, all the way up Fifth Avenue
with cheering spectators, to Central Park. &nbsp;At the Avenue of the
Americas and West 59th Street entrance to the park is where the two
huge marches came face to face. &nbsp;There was a loud street 'debate'
from the heads of both as to which march should enter first.
&nbsp;That, to a historically-correct, respectful, prideful person was
a no-brainer. &nbsp;Basically, we were just sitting a spell in the
sunny light with people gratefully taking pix of the car saying to each
other that "...ain't no one&nbsp;gonna be goin' into that park on this
glorious day before this classic&nbsp;and beautiful 1969 Cadillac
convertible "Stonewall Car". &nbsp;It was ultimately 'decided' that the
march with the historic 1969 "Stonewall Car", the
STONEWALL&nbsp;Veterans' Association,&nbsp;the Imperial QUEENS of New
York, et al. would go first. &nbsp;The decison was actually enforced
and put into action when a queen -- the Queen -- issued a royal
warning:&nbsp; Watch out! -- or you'll get run over by the
two-and-a-half ton "Stonewall Car". &nbsp;So, this famous car now lead
hundreds of thousands of GLBT people and their&nbsp;friends and
supporters into the park, where hundreds of thousands had already
congregated in anticipation of the biggest GLBT rally in history.
&nbsp;Have you ever brought your car to be part of an event with a real
million people? &nbsp;The "Stonewall Car" was the <i>
only </i>
car in the entire Central Park that glorious day -- along with about

For you car buffs, and other interested parties, here are some very
interesting factoids regarding the 1969 Cadillac DeVille convertible.
&nbsp;General Motors Corporation (GMC) described this Cadillac&nbsp;as
its "crown jewel". &nbsp;Right they were! &nbsp;Its weight is over
4,600 pounds (nearly 2 1/2 tons!); its length is 225 inches (nearly 19
feet long!); its width is 80 inches (over 6 1/2 feet wide); its height
is 55 inches (with convertible top up); its wheelbase (the distance
between axles) is 130 inches; and its massive, powerful,
fuel-efficient, 375 horsepower, V-8 engine is 472 cubic inches at 4,400
r.p.m. (revolutions per minute). &nbsp;It is the largest V-8 engine
ever produced to power a passenger car! &nbsp;The Cadillac&nbsp;has the
great&nbsp; standard-equipped power steering, power brakes, six-way
power bench seat, power windows, A.M./F.M. stereo radio, power radio
antenna and, most importantly, power convertible top. &nbsp;The
convertible top is made of white, triple-woven padded canvas with a
real glass rear window. &nbsp;One option the car has which we rarely
use is the "automatic climate control system". &nbsp;When is the top
up? &nbsp;Never! &nbsp;No, not even in winter. &nbsp; The car&nbsp;is
kept with the top down year-round and a custom canvas cover over it.
&nbsp;The seating areas are white sierra ostrich-grain leather.
&nbsp;The flooring is midnight black pile carpeting. &nbsp;The
dashboard, door panels and rear seating area side panels have inlaid,
designer grain wood. &nbsp;The entire car interior is original!
&nbsp;The custom Vogue tires are extra-wide whitewalls. &nbsp;The wheel
covers are solid chrome spokes. &nbsp;The fuel tank capacity is a
whopping 26 gallons (plus one gallon reserve). &nbsp;The locked gas cap
is hidden behind the rear license plate ("STONEWAL"). </td>

      <td>Over 16,000 of these classic Cadillac convertible cars -- a
very large number for a luxury convertible -- were produced by General
Motors Corporation with "Body by Fisher". &nbsp;The huge chrome bumpers
and massive front with cast aluminum "egg-crate" grille was used as a
design model for Cadillac grilles for the following two-plus decades
into the 1990s. &nbsp;The car front debuted the innovative and
safety-conscious wraparound cornering lights (now found, though less
grandly, on almost every car). &nbsp;1969 was the year that front seat
headrests (restraints) became mandatory by law. &nbsp;Seatbelts were
now required to have a push-button open/close mechanism. &nbsp;The 1969
Cadillac marked the public-wanted return of the dual horizontal
headlights (after four years of the vertical, un-Cadillac, 'stacked'
headlamps). &nbsp;(See "SW-30" on the S.V.A. website "Photo Album" icon
for pix of a 1966 blue Cadillac convertible,&nbsp; periodically with
the S.V.A. in the parades.) &nbsp; In 1959, back-tracking a decade,
Cadillac had the incredibly unique, "bullet" tail-lights. &nbsp;Ten
years later, it seems that they made a point to have the 1969 tail
lamps be unique. &nbsp;1969 was the only year that the Cadillac had the
distinctive "lantern" tail-lights. &nbsp;They complemented and were
built-in to the slowly sweeping, modified tail fin. &nbsp;For an
excellent foto of the tail lamps, see the Greenwich Village, New York
photo on Greenwich Avenue at Jackson Triangle Park&nbsp;below.
&nbsp;In fact, virtually the only difference between the 1969 and the
1970 Cadillacs (same body, same size, same stats, etc.) were the
tail-lights.
&nbsp;The 1970 returned to the Cadillac's well-known, long, vertical
tail-lights.
&nbsp;The 1969 and 1970 Cadillacs (98% identical twins) were widely
described as "muscle cars with tuxedos". &nbsp;It is most appropriate
that the 1969 is both distinctive and 'different'. &nbsp;The ad slogan
for the 1969 Cadillac was: &nbsp;"The pleasure of driving the only
luxury convertible built in America!" &nbsp;Over three decades later,
that horn slogan still blows true -- and with even greater
significance! </td>

      <td>The most
frequently asked question&nbsp;about the "Stonewall
Car" (SW-Car) is: &nbsp;"What is the mileage?" &nbsp;At the turn of the
millennium (2000), the&nbsp;SW-Car had an&nbsp;original mileage of
94,169
on New Year's Day. &nbsp;It continued to stay in the heated garage
until June! &nbsp;The mileage was only 94,894 original miles three
years later&nbsp;as of January 1, 2003, actually regarded as a very low
figure considering the vehicular age. &nbsp;At&nbsp;35 years, as of
April 1, 2004, the mileage was only 94,954. &nbsp;On&nbsp;June 11,
2005,&nbsp;the odometer read a&nbsp;low 95,169. &nbsp;Again, three
years later, at the end of June in 2008, the mileage is merely 95,233.
&nbsp;Since
the car did not do a few parades for various reasons -- including
predicted thunderstorms,&nbsp;lightning and rain downpours -- in the
past four years (as of mid-May 2013), the&nbsp;SW-Car&nbsp;drove less
than 40
miles! &nbsp;In mid-2013 after the annual New York City GLBT Pride
Parade, the original mileage is only 95,386. &nbsp;In&nbsp;the last
dozen years less than&nbsp;1,000 miles
have been
added to this restricted-mileage "historical" vehicle. &nbsp; <br>


























      <br>


























An interesting question asked is:&nbsp; Was the "Stonewall Car" held by
the police as 'evidence'? &nbsp;No! &nbsp;Evidence of what? -- other
than the fact that Williamson Henderson, the car's owner, was there.
&nbsp;After all, there was no crime and, thus, no 'evidence'. &nbsp;
Another often-asked querrie is how long did the NYCPD have (illegal)
possession of the car? &nbsp;It was for two nights (June 27 and 28,
1969) and the day in between. &nbsp;It was a "P.O.W."! &nbsp;Thankfully,
it was rescued and there was absolutely no damage to the beauty.
&nbsp;And, considering that the NYCPD are known as&nbsp;"the boys in
blue" and the car is police blue, we're lucky we ever got it back from
them! &nbsp;Is the engine the original? &nbsp;Yes, it sure is -- and
absolutely nothing overhauled. &nbsp;It even has the original glass
window cleaner bottle and mount adjacent to the engine! &nbsp;Needless
to say, the engine is huge! &nbsp; </td>
