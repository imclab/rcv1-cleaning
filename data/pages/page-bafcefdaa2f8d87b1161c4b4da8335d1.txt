		<H3>Japanese Classical Literature (up to 1868)</H3>
		<P>
		The oldest surviving literary works are the <B>Kojiki</B> (Record of Ancient Matters, c.712)
		and the <B>Nihon Shoki</B> (History Book of Ancient Japan, c.720). These works of history
		and mythology tell of the origins of the Japanese people and the
		formation of the state. While the Nihon Shoki is written almost
		entirely in Chinese, the Kojiki is written in Japanese using Chinese
		characters and is more familiar. The great anthology known as
		the <B>Man'yoshu</B> (Collection of Ten Thousand Leaves, c.770) contains around 4,500
		poems written by people from all walks of life, from emperors
		to peasants. There are two main forms of poem in the anthology,
		long and short, of which the short form, or <B>tanka</B>, survives to this day. Tanka consist of five lines with 5-7-5-7-7
		syllables. The foremost mood in literature of this period was
		personal sincerity.
		<P>
		The fairy tale <B>Taketori Monogatari</B> (The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter), written in the 9th century,
		is thought to be Japan's first novel. It is widely known today
		as <B>Kaguya-hime</B> (The Moon Princess) and is popular in children's picture books.
		It tells the tale of an old bamboo cutter who one day discovers
		a tiny child in a bamboo stem. He and his wife adopt her and the
		child grows into a beautiful woman in just a few months (just
		as bamboo grows phenomenally quickly). She attracts many suitors,
		including the emperor but sets impossible tasks for them to win
		her hand. Finally Kaguya-hime declares that she will return to
		her home - the moon. The emperor sends thousands of troops to
		stop her but she eludes them and leaves only a letter behind.
		The emperor orders the letter burned on the highest mountain in
		the land. Miraculously, the the letter continues producing smoke
		and as a result the mountain became known as Fuji, &quot;the immortal
		one&quot;.
		<P>
		<IMG SRC="/omnibus/pix/shikibu.jpg" WIDTH="286" HEIGHT="190" ALIGN="left" HSPACE="5">
		Undoubtedly the earliest great work of fiction was <B>Genji Monogatari</B> (The Tale
		of Genji) written in 1010 by <B>Murasaki Shikibu</B> (left), a lady of the Heian court (modern day Kyoto). It is a
		massive 54-volume tome that tells of the romantic adventures of
		noblemen, centering around the prince Genji, who has gone down
		in Japanese folklore as their version of Casanova or Don Juan.
		It also provides a glimpse into the daily life of 10th and 11th-century
		courtiers, as does <B>Makura-no-soshi</B> (The Pillow Book) by <B>Sei Shonagon</B>,
		another woman of the court. The literature of the Heian Period
		(794-1185) is characterized by <B>mono-no-aware</B>, or a feeling of being connected to nature and all things. This
		concept is still considered central to the Japanese psyche though
		it's not so easy to see in the Japanese of today.
		<P>
		Five centuries of great upheaval and almost constant war followed
		the <A HREF="/omnibus/history1.shtml">Battle of Dannoura</A> in 1185, in which the ruling Taira clan was defeated by the Minamoto
		clan (also known as the Genji). The battle became the subject
		of the most famous prose piece of the time, the <B>Heike Monogatari</B> (The Tales of the Taira Clan, c.1220) whose author is unknown.
		The mood of gloom and solitude that enveloped the country is reflected
		in the literature of the time. It is often described using the
		term yugen (mystery and depth). The most important people in Japanese
		society during this time, which spanned the <A HREF="/omnibus/history1.shtml">Kamakura</A>
		(1185-1333) and <A HREF="/omnibus/history1.shtml">Muromachi</A> (1333-1573) periods, were the samurai warriors, who lived a life
		of action, and the Buddhist priests, who spent their days in silent
		contemplation. Many of the great writers were priests. The <B>Shin Kokin-shu</B> (New Collection of Ancient and Modern Poems, 1205) is one of
		the greatest anthologies of poetry from this period. It was compiled
		by the poet <B>Fujiwara Teika</B> but one of the major figures included is the priest <B>Saigyo</B>. A form of poetry developed at this time was renga, or linked
		poems. Three or more poets cooperated in composing one long poem.
		The greatest renga masters, <B>Sogi</B>, <B>Shohaku</B>, and <B>Socho</B>, together made up
		the famous <B>Minase Sangin</B> (Three Poets at Minase) in the late 15th century.
		<P>
		After peace was restored to the land under the <B>Tokugawa</B> shogunate, who ruled from Edo (modern day Tokyo), new forms of
		culture evolved as Japan was closed to the outside world for over
		two centuries. The <A HREF="/omnibus/history2.shtml">Edo</A> period (1600-1868) saw the growth of the merchant class and with
		it the development of an earthier kind of popular culture. Still
		popular examples include <A HREF="kabuki.shtml">kabuki</A>, <A HREF="ukiyoe.shtml">ukiyo-e</A> (woodblock prints of actors and prostitutes) and novels like
		<B>Koshoku Ichidai Otoko</B> (Life of an Amourous Man) and <B>Koushoku Gonin Onna</B> (Five Women
		Who Loved Love) by <B>Ihara Saisaku</B>. These novels are bawdy tales equally about the love lives and
		the financial wheelings and dealings of the mercantile society.
		But at the same time, the Edo period also saw the development
		of the poetic form considered the finest achievement of Japanese
		literature - the <B>haiku</B>.
		<P>
		Perhaps most concisely described as the distilled essence of poetry,
		haiku are 17-syllable poems whose development was strongly influenced
		by the Zen Buddhism that was prevalent at the time. Though the
		current 5-7-5 syllable structure and mandatory use of a <B>kigo</B> ( a word to represent the season) were only introduced later
		in the <A HREF="/omnibus/history3.shtml">Meiji</A> period (1868-1912), the greatest exponent of haiku lived in the
		Edo period. <B>Basho Matsuo</B> (1644-94) was a Zen lay priest and his haiku often form part
		of travel journals and were written on the road, capturing his
		mood and surroundings in various parts of the country as he traced
		the footsteps of earlier literary greats such as Saigyo and Sogi.
		The best known work is <B>Oku no Hosomichi</B> (Narrow Road Through the Deep North - it is available in the
		English translation Narrow Road to Oku by famous Japan scholar
		<B>Donald Keene</B>). It tells of Basho's almost 1,500 mile pilgramage with his disciple
		Sora from Edo to northern Japan, undertaken when he was 48 and
		shortly before his death.The two other great haiku poets of this
		period were <B>Yosa Buson</B>, a painter and <B>Kobayashi Issa</B>, a peasant villager.
		<P>
		The shortest poetic form in the world, haiku work better in Japanese
		than in English and translations are particularly difficult. Anyway,
		here are some translations of haiku by Basho from Oku no Hosomichi.
		The photo on the left shows the last haiku inscribed on a huge
		stone next to the Mogami River.
