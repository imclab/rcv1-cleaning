									<div class="entry-content">
										<p>Heinz is a self-conscious guy who’ll do anything to win the
woman of his dreams — an airhead cocktail waitress he’s been
infatuated with forever. He seeks counsel from a mail-order
minister and a woman who works for an escort service to help him
“practice” for a big date.</p>
<p>He buys gifts for his beloved; fasts three days and three nights
so the Creator will answer his prayers; and gets not-always-helpful
advice from an unseen “Muse.” Heinz wonders why he can’t simply win
the girl and go home?</p>
<p>One reason is that the Playwright — the capital “P” is
deliberate — keeps putting obstacles in his way. The playwright in
real life is Jay Huling, whose “Hard Luck Sings the Blues” is this
year’s Premier Pennsylvania Production (original play) at Little
Theatre of Mechanicsburg.</p>
<p>“In a way this is a typical ‘boy meets girl, boy loses girl’
story, only Heinz is self-aware that he’s a character in a play,”
says Huling, who by day is creative director of the Hoffman Agency
in Jacksonville, Fla. “So are the other characters. Heinz’s
‘Creator’ is the playwright.”</p>
<p>If the plot sounds full of poignancy, it is, says Nancy Kraft,
who’s directing the LTM production. But it also has “a lot of silly
slapstick and double entendres, certainly raw humor. We’ve tried to
play up that angle. Heinz’s travails are really silly, which adds
to the pathos.”</p>
<p>If that’s how the director and cast are seeing the play, “they
seem to be getting it,” says Huling, who was a theater major in
college but segued into playwriting. It’s an art he feels is akin
to his day job, which involves writing short radio and TV spots to
sell products.</p>
<p>The playwright has injected similar themes into “Elvis of
Nazareth,” which was featured in “The Best American Short Plays:
2007-2008,” published by Applause Books — a play he says drives
home a message of faith but in a way that’s so humorous “some
people consider it almost blasphemous.”</p>
<p>But the work, which has won a few contests and played around the
country, is appearing in an anthology, “Best American Short Plays
of 2008,” to be published in August.</p>
<p>The humor and small cast were part of the draw for Kraft, who
has performed a great deal but hasn’t directed at LTM since
“Sleuth” in 1982. Kraft also likes working with an original play,
which she says allows you to “take advantage of something
fresh.”</p>
<p>“It’s a very compact and well-written script,” she comments.
“Some stuff is predictable, but what’s wrong with predictable? It
makes some audience members feel comfortable. And the comedy parts
here aren’t predictable.”</p>
<p>Kraft also has the pleasure of directing her son, Preston
Schreffler, as Heinz. Eric Brown is the Muse; Pam McMillin is
Linda; and Rachel Lewis is Penelope. Craig Stouffer plays Dr.
Marvin, the minister.</p>
<p>Emily Klein is understudying Lewis for the June 9
performance.</p>
<p>“It’s interesting to be funny and meaningful at the same time,”
Huling says. “The way it’s executed, ‘Hard Luck Sings the Blues’ is
a comedy that’s over the top screwball, but if you want to take
deep meaning out of it, it’s definitely there.”</p>
