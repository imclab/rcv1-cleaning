<h3 class='post-title entry-title'>
Auto-Culture: Miles per gallon ratings short-circuited by electric power
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-704384216773949903'>
<a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w1C44JQU7Pc"><img style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 400px; height: 300px;" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/_NVIyqlcHzR4/SwqmyPW8SCI/AAAAAAAAFp8/VcYfbnCFOnY/s400/2007TeslaRoadster-full-1.jpg" alt="" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5407317684658128930" border="0" /></a><span style="font-size:78%;">The Tesla Roadster is a fully electric sports car, and is the first car to be produced by electric car firm Tesla Motors. Tesla claims prototypes have been able to accelerate from 0-60 mph (97 km/h) in under 4 seconds, and reach a top speed of over 130 mph (210 km/h). Additionally, the car will be able to travel 221 miles (356 km) on a single charge of its lithium-ion battery. The Roadster's efficiency is reported as 133 Wh/km (4.7 mi/kWh), equivalent to 135 mpg&#8211;U.S [ctrl-click image to launch YouTube video]. Caption and Image Credit: ebaumsworld.com</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold;">Auto-Culture: Miles per gallon ratings short-circuited by electric power</span><br /><br />The ratings systems that determine the efficiency of a car's use of fuel output is becoming effectively outdated and unreflective in an alternative power world.<br /><br />Take, for example, a car that has as a power plant an all-electric engine ... like a Chevy Volt. If one were to use the old standard "Mile Per Gallon" ratings system, the new Chevy Volt electric car has a projected fuel economy equal to 230 mpg.<br /><br />A more realistic view upon which to comparatively judge the power effectiveness of a car, wither it be gasoline, hybrid, alternative-fuel, or electric would be a new system that focuses on an average monthly cost to a consumer based upon a defined usage parameter.<br /><br /><a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_NVIyqlcHzR4/SwqmySA1_NI/AAAAAAAAFqE/ACg9bvmPQfU/s1600/chevy-volt1-1.jpg"><img style="margin: 0px auto 10px; display: block; text-align: center; cursor: pointer; width: 400px; height: 283px;" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_NVIyqlcHzR4/SwqmySA1_NI/AAAAAAAAFqE/ACg9bvmPQfU/s400/chevy-volt1-1.jpg" alt="" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5407317685370748114" border="0" /></a><span style="font-size:78%;">In an Automotive News (April, 2009) interview, new GM CEO Fritz Henderson, admitted the obvious - that GM will not be able to make their first mass-produced electric car both affordable and profitable for a long time, saying that GM plans to lose money with &#8220;Gen-1 and 2&#8243; of the Chevy Volt. &#8220;We have been very clear with the task force, particularly in Gen-1 technology, like the Volt, the cost is high,&#8221; Henderson said. &#8220;And that means, it doesn&#8217;t necessarily pay the rent. It actually consumes rent when it&#8217;s launched.&#8221; Caption and Image Credit: EcoAutoNinja.com</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold;">This excerpted and edited from National Association of Convenience Stores -</span><br /><span style="font-size:130%;"><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Electric Cars Muddy the MPG Waters</span></span><br /><span style="font-style: italic;font-size:78%;" ><span style="font-weight: bold;">Alternative-fuel vehicles do not allow for the same miles per gallon measurements as gasoline-fueled cars</span><br />NACS Online - Posted: Nov 23, 2009</span><br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;">With more electric, fuel cell and hybrid vehicles on the road today, measuring miles per gallon isn&#8217;t as easy as it was in the old days.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;">Edmunds.com is asking the <a href="http://www.fueleconomy.gov/">Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)</a> to come up with fuel-cost ratings to assist buyers in comparing the cars. On Sept. 15, the agency asked for public comments on emissions and fuel economy standards for plug-in electric and hybrid cars.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;">&#8220;A consumer could reasonably assume, based on the way that they have been using window stickers now going back to 1975, that the Chevy Volt is over four times more efficient to operate than the <a href="http://www.toyota.com/prius-hybrid/">Toyota Prius</a>. And [that] would actually be completely wrong,&#8221; said Jeremy Anwyl, CEO of Edmunds.com.</span><br /><a href="http://www.edmunds.com/industry-car-news/"><br /></a><span style="font-style: italic;"><a href="http://www.edmunds.com/industry-car-news/">Edmunds.com</a> would like for the EPA to stop allowing car makers to promote vehicles based on mpg. &#8220;If car companies are building cars with very high monthly costs from a fuel perspective, that&#8217;s the sort of information that needs to be out there,&#8221; he said.</span><br /><a href="http://www.nacsonline.com/NACS/News/Daily/Pages/ND1123095.aspx">Reference Here</a>>><br /><br />Then there was this comment left at a forum site discussing all electric-powered automobiles ... "I wonder how many miles you'd have to drive one [an electric car] before you become sterile."<br /><br />... notes from <a href="http://theedje.blogspot.com/">The EDJE</a>
