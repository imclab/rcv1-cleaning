                </div><b><span class="srTitle"><a name="ref1036341" id="ref1036341"></a>Modernism</span></b><b>,</b>&nbsp;in <a href="/EBchecked/topic/36405/the-arts">the arts</a>, a radical break with the past and the concurrent search for new forms of expression. Modernism fostered a period of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/198475/experimentation">experimentation</a> in the arts from the late 19th to the mid-20th century, particularly in the years following World War I.</p>
 <!--[MEDIA-STRIP]-->
 <p>In an era characterized by <a href="/EBchecked/topic/287204/industrialization">industrialization</a>, rapid <a href="/EBchecked/topic/550924/social-change">social change</a>, and advances in <a href="/EBchecked/topic/528756/science">science</a> and the <a href="/EBchecked/topic/551385/social-science">social sciences</a> (e.g., Freudian theory), Modernists felt a growing alienation incompatible with Victorian morality, optimism, and convention. New ideas in psychology, philosophy, and political theory kindled a search for new modes of expression.</p>
</section>

<section id="toc282535" class="eb-topic-section" print-toc="toc-387266main-282535">
 <h2><span class="eb-topic-section-title">Modernism in <a href="/EBchecked/topic/343579/literature">literature</a></span></h2>
 <p>The Modernist impulse is fueled in various literatures by industrialization and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/619515/urbanization">urbanization</a> and by the search for an authentic response to a much-changed world. Although prewar works by <a href="/EBchecked/topic/299829/Henry-James">Henry James</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/133148/Joseph-Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, and other writers are considered Modernist, Modernism as a literary <a href="/EBchecked/topic/395185/movement">movement</a> is typically associated with the period after World War I. The enormity of the war had undermined humankind’s faith in the foundations of Western society and culture, and postwar Modernist literature reflected a sense of disillusionment and fragmentation. A primary <a href="/EBchecked/topic/590480/theme">theme</a> of <a name="ref1077163" id="ref1077163"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/184705/TS-Eliot">T.S. Eliot</a>’s long poem <em><a name="ref1077165" id="ref1077165"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/636643/The-Waste-Land">The Waste Land</a></em> (1922), a seminal Modernist work, is the search for redemption and renewal in a sterile and spiritually empty landscape. With its fragmentary images and obscure allusions, the poem is typical of Modernism in requiring the reader to take an active role in interpreting the text.</p>
 <p>The publication of the Irish writer <a name="ref1077166" id="ref1077166"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/306875/James-Joyce">James Joyce</a>’s <em><a name="ref1077167" id="ref1077167"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/613635/Ulysses">Ulysses</a></em> in 1922 was a landmark event in the development of Modernist literature. Dense, lengthy, and controversial, the novel details the events of one day in the life of three Dubliners through a <a href="/EBchecked/topic/585402/technique">technique</a> known as <a href="/EBchecked/topic/133295/stream-of-consciousness">stream of consciousness</a>, which commonly ignores orderly sentence <a href="/EBchecked/topic/569660/structure">structure</a> and incorporates fragments of thought in an attempt to capture the flow of characters’ mental processes. Portions of the book were considered obscene, and <em>Ulysses</em> was banned for many years in English-speaking countries. Other European and American Modernist authors whose works rejected chronological and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/403613/narrative">narrative</a> continuity include <a href="/EBchecked/topic/647786/Virginia-Woolf">Virginia Woolf</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/480557/Marcel-Proust">Marcel Proust</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/564945/Gertrude-Stein">Gertrude Stein</a>, and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/202695/William-Faulkner">William Faulkner</a>.</p>
 <p>The term Modernism is also used to refer to literary movements other than the European and American movement of the early to mid-20th century. In Latin American literature, <a name="ref1077170" id="ref1077170"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/387291/Modernismo">Modernismo</a> arose in the late 19th century in the works of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/249948/Manuel-Gutierrez-Najera">Manuel Guti&eacute;rrez N&aacute;jera</a> and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/366828/Jose-Julian-Marti">Jos&eacute; Mart&iacute;</a>. The movement, which continued into the early 20th century, reached its peak in the poetry of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/151576/Ruben-Dario">Rub&eacute;n Dar&iacute;o</a>. (<em>See also</em> <a href="/EBchecked/topic/19939/American-literature/42269/Poets-of-the-era">American literature</a>; <a href="/EBchecked/topic/331811/Latin-American-literature/236892/Modernismo">Latin American literature</a>.)</p>

<section id="toc282536" class="eb-topic-section" print-toc="toc-387266main-282536">
 <h2><span class="eb-topic-section-title">Modernism in other arts and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/32876/architecture">architecture</a></span></h2>
 <p>Composers, including <a href="/EBchecked/topic/527896/Arnold-Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/568550/Igor-Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a>, and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/638603/Anton-Webern">Anton Webern</a>, sought new solutions within new forms and used as-yet-untried approaches to tonality. In <a href="/EBchecked/topic/150714/dance">dance</a> a rebellion against both balletic and interpretive traditions had its roots in the work of <a name="ref1077278" id="ref1077278"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/301347/Emile-Jaques-Dalcroze">&Eacute;mile Jaques-Delcroze</a>, <a name="ref1077280" id="ref1077280"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/326639/Rudolf-Laban">Rudolf Laban</a>, and <a name="ref1077281" id="ref1077281"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/221888/Loie-Fuller">Loie Fuller</a>. Each of them examined a specific aspect of dance—such as the elements of the human <a href="/EBchecked/topic/213661/form">form</a> in motion or the impact of theatrical context—and helped bring about the era of modern dance. In the visual arts the roots of Modernism are often traced back to painter <a name="ref1077171" id="ref1077171"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/361791/Edouard-Manet">&Eacute;douard Manet</a>, who, beginning in the 1860s, broke away from inherited notions of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/453061/perspective">perspective</a>, modeling, and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/570733/subject-matter">subject matter</a>. The <a name="ref1077284" id="ref1077284"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/45399/avant-garde"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/45399/avant-garde">avant-garde</a> movements that followed—including <a href="/EBchecked/topic/284143/Impressionism">Impressionism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/472185/Post-Impressionism">Post-Impressionism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/145744/Cubism">Cubism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/222921/Futurism">Futurism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/198740/Expressionism">Expressionism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/134466/Constructivism">Constructivism</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/566242/De-Stijl">de Stijl</a>, and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/1963/Abstract-Expressionism">Abstract Expressionism</a>—are generally defined as Modernist.</p>
 <p>Over the span of these movements, artists increasingly focused on the intrinsic qualities of their media—e.g., line, form, and colour—and moved away from inherited notions of art. By the beginning of the 20th century, architects also had increasingly abandoned past styles and conventions in favour of a form of architecture based on essential functional concerns. They were helped by advances in building technologies such as the steel frame and the curtain wall. In the period after World War I these tendencies became codified as the <a name="ref1077286" id="ref1077286"></a><a href="/EBchecked/topic/291280/International-Style">International style</a>, which utilized simple geometric shapes and unadorned facades and which abandoned any use of historical reference; the steel-and-glass buildings of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/381736/Ludwig-Mies-van-der-Rohe">Ludwig Mies van der Rohe</a> and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/137221/Le-Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> embodied this <a href="/EBchecked/topic/570255/style">style</a>. In the mid-to-late 20th century this style manifested itself in clean-lined, unadorned glass skyscrapers and mass housing projects.</p>
</section>
<section id="toc282537" class="eb-topic-section" print-toc="toc-387266main-282537">
 <h2><span class="eb-topic-section-title">The birth of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/472215/postmodernism">postmodernism</a></span></h2>
 <p>In the late 20th century a reaction against Modernism set in. Architecture saw a return to traditional materials and forms and sometimes to the use of decoration for the sake of decoration itself, as in the work of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/242348/Michael-Graves">Michael Graves</a> and, after the 1970s, that of <a href="/EBchecked/topic/305408/Philip-C-Johnson">Philip Johnson</a>. In literature, irony and self-awareness became the postmodern fashion and the blurring of fiction and nonfiction a favoured method. Such writers as <a href="/EBchecked/topic/632808/Kurt-Vonnegut">Kurt Vonnegut</a>, <a href="/EBchecked/topic/484673/Thomas-Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon</a>, and <a href="/EBchecked/topic/97218/Angela-Carter">Angela Carter</a> employed a postmodern approach in their work.</p>
