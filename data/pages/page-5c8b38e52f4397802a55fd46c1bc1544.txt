<div class="entry-content">
<div id="asset-11762989"
  
        class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right">
    <span class="adv-photo-large">
       <img src="http://media.nj.com/entertainment_impact_arts/photo/11762989-large.jpg" class="adv-photo" alt="slonlineo28A.jpg" height="492" width="380">
       <span class="photo-data">
          <span class="caption">The cover of a fun look at MAD over the years</span>
              </span>
  
</div>

<p><br />
'Totally Mad: 60 Years of Humor, Satire, Stupidity and Stupidity' <br />
With an Introduction by Stephen Colbert and Eric Drysdale<br />
(Time Home Entertainment, 256 pp., $34.95)</p>

<p>Several generations of us wisenheimers have Bill Gaines to thank. The late publisher of MAD inspired us to question authority and joke about everything.</p>

<p>I was no older than 6 by the time I was hooked, and MAD became my first subscription. It was one I held onto for decades - so loving the irreverent magazine and its publisher that I invited him to my tiny wedding. (He declined - with one of his famous stamps and a handwritten note.)</p>

<p>MAD's influence cannot be overstated. Politics, history and pop culture are presented amusingly through its loopy drawings. Kids read it and learn. I have MAD to thank (or blame) for pushing me toward journalism. Not a lot of other jobs mandate that you read - and question - everything.</p>

<p>Montclair resident and Comedy Central mainstay Stephen Colbert feels similarly indebted to the humor magazine. He and Eric Drysdale, a writer, introduce the coffee table book in a dialog.</p>

<p>Colbert writes: <em>"Well, my first MADs were the ones my older brothers left behind when they went to college. So the first stuff I saw was the 1950s Harvey Kurtzman and Bill Elder stuff, and I just didn't get the cultural references. So that kind of fits in with what you were saying about the different humanistic endeavor continuums in history and the like. Right?"</em></p>

<p>Drysdale goes on to play the straight man to Colbert, explaining how the magazine held this intrigue; that it was a slightly dangerous periodical to read. And Colbert recalls learning about President Nixon as a boy.</p>

<p><em>"I remember I saw Nixon on TV a bunch when I was a kid, and I knew how people talked about him that he was the president and was in some kind of trouble. But because I read MAD, I was able to piece together all of the information and eventually get to this moment of, 'Oh! I get it now! He's the President of the United States ... but look at his funny nose!"<br />
</em></p>

<p>In MAD, no one was exempt from ridicule. </p>

<p>In a 1989 poem, called "The Reagan" (with apologies to Edgar Allan Poe), Frank Jacobs writes:</p>

<p><em>"Once upon a cold November, back in '80, you'll remember<br />
Came to pass a great election, with a wondrous change in store;<br />
By a landslide, one was winning, promising a new beginning;<br />
Tall and proud, he stood there, grinning, like so many times before;<br />
Who was he, this cool one, grinning, like so many times before?<br />
'Twas the Reagan, nothing more."<br />
</em></p>

<p>What the magazine did - besides skewer the pompous - was hold us up to the mirror. This book has fun reprints from famous MAD's pieces, including the 1953 classic, "Superduperman." There are essays from longtime staffers and photos of "the usual gang of idiots," which is how Gaines described the staff. He had to be doing something right; he held onto the staff forever.</p>

<p>Gaines used to take his staff on an annual trip.<em> "Many of the writers and artists had never met,"</em> Jacobs writes. <em>"What better way for everyone to get to know their brethren than to fly them, all expenses paid, for a week or two in a foreign clime? These vacations, with their anticipations and memories, would knot the family ties even tighter. Especially if the trips were stag."</em></p>

<p>Yes, that made my jaw clench, but let's remember when MAD was published, or at least launched. It was the real "Mad Men" age; in fact, their offices were, as the address on the masthead proudly pointed out, on MADison Avenue.</p>

<p>The first of these famous trips was to Haiti, which Jacobs writes, was "<em>a Gaines' favorite watering hole. The tone was set the first day. Discovering that the magazine has one subscriber in Port-au-Prince, Gaines piled his charges into five Jeeps, drove to the lad's home, and presented him with a renewal card.</em>"</p>

<p>MAD's spoofs of movies, usually drawn by Mort Drucker and written by Stan Hart, remain timeless. "Rocky" became "Rockhead" and included lines such as Rocky talking to his goldfish: <em>"Y'know, you goldfish are my only friends ... but I'm afraid you're put of shape, too... 'cause every time I take you out for a walk, you pass out before we're halfway down the block!"</em></p>

<p>Silly? Absolutely. Subversive? They hoped so. As Colbert recalls, presidents were not exempt; they were targets for questioning. Along the way, you learned.</p>

<div id="asset-11762996"
  
        class="entry_widget_large entry_widget_right">
    <span class="adv-photo-large">
       <img src="http://media.nj.com/entertainment_impact_arts/photo/11762996-large.jpg" class="adv-photo" alt="slonline028B.jpg" height="495" width="380">
       <span class="photo-data">
          <span class="caption">The usual gang of idiots, as MAD writers and artists were affectionately called, contribute to this book.</span>
       </span>
    </span>
  
</div>
The book is not as exhaustive as "Completely MAD: A History of the Comic Book and Magazine" by Maria Reidelbach, which came out 11 years ago. But this is somehow cozier, more behind the scenes.

<p>Any book that features Paterson's Don Martin art, which offered a truly nutty take on the human form, deserves to be read.<br />
 <br />
Even if you don't know his name, his art - featuring people with protruding eyes, skinny limbs and geometric bodies - will likely strike a bell..</p>

<p>As Colbert writes, <em>"Let's just say it was really funny, we learned a lot, and we wouldn't be who we are if we hadn't read it. O.k.?"</em></p>

<p>Definitely, Stephen Colbert. We would not.</p>

<p>Jacqueline Cutler: jacquelinecutler@verizon.net</p>
