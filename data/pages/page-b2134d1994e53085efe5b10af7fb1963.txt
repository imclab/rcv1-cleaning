			<h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://blogs.cars.com/kickingtires/2013/08/drive-in-diners-motor-into-21st-century.html">Drive-In Diners Motor into 21st Century</a></h3>
		
		<div class="entry-content">
			<div class="entry-body">
				<img alt="IMG_6391" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e20192aca44ecb970d" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e20192aca44ecb970d-800wi" title="IMG_6391" /><br />
<p>As baby boomers, my husband and I have fond childhood memories of family road trips that included stops at drive-in diners that dotted the nation&#39;s byways in the &#39;50s and &#39;60s. So we were delighted to discover Red&#39;s Root Beer during a recent vacation near Paw Paw, Mich.</p>
<p>A by-product of the nation&#39;s love affair with cars, drive-in diners surfaced in the 1920s, survived the Great Depression and thrived afterward because of their &quot;good cheap food and fun,&quot; writes artist Don Sawyer, who captures this slice of Americana on canvas, on dinerdon.com.</p>
<p>The popularity of drive-in diners peaked in the 1930s and &#39;40s, falling victim to the rise of fast-food giants in the 1960s and &#39;70s. Like drive-in movie theaters, drive-in diners seemed to be headed down the road to extinction.</p>
<p>However, the once-ubiquitous drive-in diner may be poised to pull a U-turn thanks to TV shows like the Food Network&#39;s &quot;Drive-ins, Diners and Dives&quot; and the Travel Channel&#39;s &quot;Man v. Food,&quot; and chains like Sonic Drive-In, Ruby&#39;s Diner and A&amp;W.</p>

			<h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://blogs.cars.com/kickingtires/2013/08/honda-helps-drive-in-theaters-project-success.html">Honda Helps Drive-In Theaters Project Success</a></h3>
		
		<div class="entry-content">
			<div class="entry-body">
				<img class="whiteimage" title="HondaProjectDrive" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e2019104b6ed37970c-800wi" border="0" alt="HondaProjectDrive" /></p><p>
<p>Cars and drive-in movie theaters, naturally, go hand in hand. Now one automaker is lending a helping hand to an industry whose history is so intertwined with its own. Honda announced today that it has launched a national campaign to help revitalize drive-in movie theaters as they face the latest threat to their existence after decades of decline: the digital revolution.</p>
<p>According to an April story from The Hollywood Reporter out of CinemaCon, the annual theater-owners convention in Las Vegas, Hollywood studios will cease distribution of 35 mm prints of films by the end of this year. That means theaters that have not yet made the pricey conversion to digital by that time — estimated to cost between $75,000 and $100,000 — will be without a product to exhibit. That’s particularly bad news for owners of drive-in theaters, who say their profit margins are far too slim for them to absorb such an expense, and that the end of two-reel celluloid prints will effectively put them out of business.
</p>

			<h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://blogs.cars.com/kickingtires/2013/07/car-cd-players-heading-toward-extinction.html">Car CD Players Head Toward Extinction</a></h3>
		
		<div class="entry-content">
			<div class="entry-body">
				<p>
 <img alt="CD1" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e201901e689bc7970b" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e201901e689bc7970b-800wi" title="CD1" /></p>
<p>In-dash CD players are finally going the way of Razor scooters, shell necklaces and Ja Rule. <a href="http://blogs.cars.com/kickingtires/2007/06/automakers_coul.html">Automakers have predicted this for the better part of the past decade</a> as car stereos augmented the standard CD player with auxiliary MP3 jacks, USB/iPod connectors and, eventually, streaming Bluetooth audio. As far back as late 2011, market researcher NPD Group said <a href="http://blogs.cars.com/kickingtires/2011/11/no-more-cutouts-where-do-aftermarket-stereos-go.html">nearly a third of people listened to music in their cars via smartphones or MP3 players</a>. And this year, J.D. Power&#39;s Automotive Performance, Execution and Layout Study found that as many people listen to CDs as external devices, like an iPod or a smartphone. In past APEAL studies, <em>more</em> drivers still listened to CDs.</p>
<p>
It&#39;s already happening, and not just in tech-heavy cars like the Tesla Model S. Opt for the 8.4-inch multimedia touch-screen in a 2013 Dodge Dart, and the CD player goes into the center armrest — a spot that underlines just how many drivers Dodge expects to use it. Up-level versions of the all-new 2014 Jeep Cherokee and redesigned Grand Cherokee, meanwhile, forego CD players entirely. You can still get one, but it goes in the center armrest or glove compartment. Chevrolet&#39;s 7-inch MyLink touch-screen in the 2014 Sonic eliminates the CD player altogether. And in the 2014 Kia Soul and 2013 Chevrolet Spark, you can&#39;t buy a CD player in <em>any</em> trim.</p>
<p>
Are we just a few years away from CD players becoming extinct? And what are car designers doing with the free space?</p>
