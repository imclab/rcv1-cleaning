					<div class="body">
						<p>It&rsquo;s been 32 years since Louis Malle&rsquo;s &ldquo;My Dinner with Andr&eacute;&rdquo; became a cause c&eacute;l&egrave;bre among critics and art-house regulars. It&rsquo;s still a unique experience, eavesdropping on experimental-theater director and actor Andr&eacute; Gregory and his close friend, actor and playwright Wallace Shawn, as they engage in a marathon dinner conversation ranging from nagging pet peeves to the life-changing impact of existential adventures.</p>
<p>Now we have a kind-of sequel that the late Malle would&rsquo;ve relished. &ldquo;Andr&eacute; Gregory: Before and After Dinner,&rdquo; is an insightful documentary/love letter directed by Gregory&rsquo;s second wife, Cindy Kleine, who married Gregory when she was 39 and he was 63. 
</p>
<p>Their mutual devotion informs the film without compromising Kleine&rsquo;s portrait of Gregory (now 79 and a lymphoma survivor) as a captivating raconteur and still-daring director, finally ready to stage his production of Ibsen&rsquo;s &ldquo;The Master Builder&rdquo; after 14 years &mdash; yes, years &mdash; of informal rehearsal. (The production was filmed by Jonathan Demme for an upcoming release.)</p>
<p>Balancing personal and objective observations with humorously engaging narration, Kleine deftly combines familial histories (they share Jewish lineage) and other biographical details while focusing on the present. </p>
<p>That Gregory&rsquo;s distant, emotionless father might have been a spy for Hitler weighs heavily on Gregory&rsquo;s psyche, and he describes Stanley Kubrick&rsquo;s &ldquo;The Shining&rdquo; as &ldquo;a documentary about my childhood.&rdquo;</p>
<p>Gregory may embellish his memories, but it&rsquo;s only because he enjoys a good yarn.
</p>
<p>
Kleine sees no appreciable difference between objective truth and her husband&rsquo;s occasional embellishments, and she has a point: By any measure, Gregory&rsquo;s passionately artistic life has been extraordinarily well-lived, undeniably eventful and entirely worthy of this affectionately upbeat documentary.


</p>
