        <h2 class="rtside-hdricn rtside-hdricn-check">Whenever You Take Your Car in for Repairs:</h2>
    
    
	    <!--String to be replaced is = src=" -->

<ul class="one_column_bullet"><li>Choose a reliable repair shop. Family, friends or an independent consumer rating organization should be able to help you. Look for shops that display various certifications that are current. You should also check out the shop's record with your <a href="http://www.usa.gov/directory/stateconsumer/index.shtml" title="state and local consumer protection offices">local consumer protection office</a> or <a href="http://www.bbb.org" title="Better Business Bureaus">Better Business Bureau.</a></li><li>Describe the symptoms. Don't try to diagnose the problem.</li><li>Make it clear that work cannot begin until you have a written estimate and you give your okay. . Never sign a blank repair order. If the problem can't be diagnosed on the spot, insist that the shop contact you for authorization once the trouble has been found.</li><li>Ask the shop to keep the old parts for you.</li><li>If a repair is covered under warranty, follow the warranty instructions..</li><li>Get all repair warranties in writing.</li><li>Keep copies of all paperwork.</li></ul><p>Some states, cities and counties have special laws that deal with auto repairs. For information on the laws in your state, contact your <a href="http://www.usa.gov/directory/stateconsumer/index.shtml" title="state and local consumer protection offices">state or local consumer protection office</a>. In addition, the FTC provides a complete <a href="http://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0211-auto-repair-basics" title="FTC consumer guide to auto repair">consumer guide to auto repair.</a></p>

	<h3>Know Your Rights</h3>
	<div class="inside">										
        	         	         	 
            <p class="lftbox-alrt-img lftbox-icon-question">My dealership went out of business. What happens to my vehicle warranty?</p>
            				
                    
        	        
        	        	<p>Even if your car dealership has gone out of business, you may still have coverage under your warranty. Manufacturer warranties are still valid and are not affected by dealer closures. However, you may have to travel farther to get a certified dealer that can honor the warranty. In the event that you bought a car from an affected dealership, the manufacturer should notify you about where to take your car for service. However, if you have a dealership-backed or third-party warranty and the dealership goes out of business, your warranty may be worthless.</p>
