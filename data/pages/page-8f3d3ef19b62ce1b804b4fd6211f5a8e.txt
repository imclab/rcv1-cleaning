	<div class="wl-digest-photo"><a href="/entertainment/arts/2013-08-26/story/arts-notes-cummer-adds-another-augusta-savage-sculpture-its"><img src="http://jacksonville.com/sites/default/files/imagecache/digest_160_x_119/12553600.jpg" alt="" title=""  class="imagecache imagecache-digest_160_x_119" width="160" height="202" /></a></div>
	<div class="wl-digest-title"><h2><a href="/entertainment/arts/2013-08-26/story/arts-notes-cummer-adds-another-augusta-savage-sculpture-its">Arts Notes: Cummer adds another Augusta Savage sculpture to its permanent collection</a></h2></div>
	
	<p><span class="wl-date">By: <a href="/users/dispatchdaemon">DispatchDaemon</a> on August 26, 2013 - 9:31am - <a href="/entertainment/arts/2013-08-26/story/arts-notes-cummer-adds-another-augusta-savage-sculpture-its#comment-form">Add new comment</a></span>The Cummer Museum of Art &amp; Gardens has acquired a painted plaster sculpture by Augusta Savage (1892-1962), whose 1939 bronze &quot;The Diving Boy&quot; was one of the 60 pieces Ninah Cummer bequeathed to the museum to begin its collection.
A native of Green Cove Springs, Savage moved to Jacksonville at the age of 16 and began her career as a sculptor, doing portrait busts of prominent African-Americans. In 1921 she moved on to New York where she was important figure during the Harlem Renaissance.
The Cummer&#039;s new acquisition, a bust called &quot;Gamin,&quot; was made about 1930 and won her a scholarship to study in Europe. &quot;Gamin&quot; may have been modelled on Savage&#039;s nephew, Ellis Ford.
Like many of her painted plaster originals, &quot;Gamin&quot; was never cast in bronze because of Savage&#039;s chronic shortage of funds.
The sculpture, which was formerly in the collection of Theron Fowler, a photographer and friend of Savage, was acquired with funds from the Morton R. Hirschberg Bequest. It will be on permanent display in the Helen Murchison and Edward W. Lane Jr. Gallery.</p>
