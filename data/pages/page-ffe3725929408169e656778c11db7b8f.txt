<p>A <b>coupé</b> or <b>coupe</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> past participle <i>coupé</i>, of the infinitive <i>couper</i>, to cut) is a closed two-door <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">car body style</a> with a permanently attached fixed roof. The precise definition of the term varies between manufacturers and over time.<sup id="cite_ref-Adolphus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adolphus-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The term was first applied to 19th century carriages, where the rear-facing seats had been eliminated, or cut out.<sup id="cite_ref-Adolphus_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adolphus-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pronunciation" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Europe (including the United Kingdom), the past participle <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> spelling, <i>coupé</i>, and (mostly in English speaking countries) a modified French pronunciation (<span class="nowrap"><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'k' in 'kind'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">k</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/uː/ long 'oo' in 'food'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">uː</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'p' in 'pie'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">p</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">eɪ</span></a></span><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling" class="Unicode"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>koo-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;"><span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">PAY</span></span></span></b></i></a></span>), are used. The stress may be on either the first or second syllable; stressing the first syllable is the more <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">anglicized</a> variant. Most, but not all, speakers of <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a> currently use the word without the <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accent</a> and pronounce it as one syllable (<span title="English pronunciation respelling" class="Unicode"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;"><span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">KOOP</span></span></span></b></i></a></span>). This was a gradual change occurring prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> A North American example of usage is the <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rodders</a>' term <a href="/wiki/Deuce_Coupe_(car)" title="Deuce Coupe (car)" class="mw-redirect">Deuce Coupe</a> (<span title="English pronunciation respelling" class="Unicode"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Wikipedia:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span class="SMALLCAPS" style="FONT-VARIANT:SMALL-CAPS;"><span class="NOCAPS" style="TEXT-TRANSFORM:LOWERCASE;">DOOS KOOP</span></span></span></b></i></a></span>) used to refer to a 1932 Ford; this pronunciation is used in the Beach Boys' 1963 hit song, <a href="/wiki/Little_Deuce_Coupe" title="Little Deuce Coupe">Little Deuce Coupe</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 19th century a coupé was a closed four-wheel horse-drawn <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriage</a>, cut (<i>coupé</i>) to eliminate the forward-mounted, rear-facing passenger seats,<sup id="cite_ref-Adolphus_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adolphus-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> with a single seat inside for two persons behind the driver, who sat on a box outside. If the driver had no roof over his head then it was a coupé de-ville. Commonly, a coupé had a fixed glass window in the front of the passenger compartment. The driver was protected from road dirt by a high curving dashboard. A <a href="/wiki/Landau_(automobile)" title="Landau (automobile)">landau</a> is a coupé de-ville with a folding top. Where only the passenger compartment has a folding top but the driver remains covered, the style is known as a <a href="/wiki/Landaulet" title="Landaulet">landaulet</a>.</p>
<p>During the 20th century, the term coupé was applied to various close-coupled (rear seat that is located further forward than usual and the front seat further back than usual)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> automobiles.</p>
<p>During the 1920s, the <a href="/wiki/SAE_International" title="SAE International">Society of Automobile Engineers</a> suggested nomenclature for closed cars that included the following:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt>Coupelet</dt>
<dd>A small car seating two or three with a folding top and full height doors with fully retractable windows.</dd>
<dt>Coupé</dt>
<dd>An enclosed car operated from the inside with seats for two or three and sometimes a backward-facing fourth seat.</dd>
<dt>Convertible coupé</dt>
<dd>A <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadster</a> with a removable coupé roof.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Through the 1950s opening-roof <a href="/wiki/Convertible_(car)" title="Convertible (car)" class="mw-redirect">convertible</a> automobiles were sometimes called drop-head coupés, but since the 1960s the term <i>coupé</i> has generally been applied exclusively to fixed-head models. Coupés generally have two doors, although automobile makers have offered four-door coupés and three and five-door <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a> coupés. Modern coupés often have the styling feature of frameless doors, with the window glass sealing directly against a weather-strip on the main body.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_descriptions">Definitions and descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions and descriptions">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions and descriptions" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Standard</a> ISO&#160;3833-1977 defines a coupé as having a closed body, usually with limited rear volume, a fixed roof of which a portion may be openable, at least two seats in at least one row, two side doors and possibly a rear opening, and at least two side windows.<sup id="cite_ref-ISO3833_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO3833-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Alternatively, a coupé is often distinguished from a two-door sedan by the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect"><i>B</i> pillar</a> to support the roof. Sedans have an <i>A</i> pillar forward at the windscreen, a <i>B</i> pillar aft of the door, and a <i>C</i> pillar defining the aftermost roof support at the rear window. Thus with all side-windows down, a coupé would appear windowless from the <i>A</i> to the <i>C</i> pillars. These fixed-roof models are described as a <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> or pillarless coupé. Though, to confuse things even further, there are many hardtop/pillarless two- and four-door sedans. <a href="/wiki/Targa_top" title="Targa top">Targa top</a>, or just 'T'-top models are a variation on the <a href="/wiki/Convertible_(car)" title="Convertible (car)" class="mw-redirect">convertible</a> design, where the roof centre section can be removed, in one or two sections, leaving the rest of the roof in place. Yet another variation on the convertible or drop-head coupé is the fully retractable hardtop. In this form the car has all the advantages of fixed-head vehicle but, at the touch of a button, the entire roof lifts off, folds and stows away in the trunk (boot). Though retractables were tried many years ago by Peugeot, in Europe and Ford, in the US, with the Fairlaine Skyliner, it is only in the 21st century that there has been an explosion in the popularity of this bodystyle.</p>
<p>Manufacturers have used the term coupé in several varieties, including:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Club coupé</dt>
<dd>A coupé with a larger rear seat, which would today be called a two-door sedan.</dd>
<dt>Business coupé</dt>
<dd>A coupé with no rear seat or a removable rear seat intended for traveling <a href="/wiki/Sales" title="Sales">salespeople</a> and other vendors who would be carrying their wares with them.</dd>
<dt>Opéra coupé</dt>
<dd>A coupé de-ville with a high roofed passenger compartment such that the owners could be driven to the opera without the need to remove their large hats. These often had 'occasional' seats that folded for use by children or extra passengers, and allowed easy passage to the rear seats.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> These cars most closely approximated a motorised version of the original horse-drawn coupé. Often, they would have solid rear-quarter panels, with small, circular windows, to enable the occupants to see out without being seen. These <a href="/wiki/Opera_window" title="Opera window">opera windows</a> were revived on many U.S. automobiles during the 1970s and early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
<dt>Sports coupé <b>or</b> berlinetta</dt>
<dd>A body with a sloping roof, sometimes sloping downward gradually in the rear in the manner known as <i><a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a></i>.</dd>
<dt>Four-door coupé</dt>
<dd>A luxury <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a> with classic coupé-like proportions. The low roof design reduces back seat passenger access and headroom.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The designation was first applied to a low-roof model of the <a href="/wiki/Rover_P5" title="Rover P5">Rover P5</a> from 1962 until 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> but was revived by the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Carina_ED" title="Toyota Carina ED">Toyota Carina ED</a>, the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Infiniti_J30" title="Infiniti J30">Infiniti J30</a> and finally by the first model 2005 <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_CLS" title="Mercedes-Benz CLS" class="mw-redirect">Mercedes-Benz CLS</a>, which stands in Mercedes hierarchy between the E and S class, and has appearance of a classic coupe and sedan. The term was also used partly from marketing reasons. German press accepted concept of four-door coupe and adopted it to similar models from other manufacturers such as 2009 <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XJ_(X351)" title="Jaguar XJ (X351)">Jaguar XJ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Also, other manufacturers accepted it, producing recent competing models like <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Passat_CC" title="Volkswagen Passat CC" class="mw-redirect">Volkswagen Passat CC</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMW_F12" title="BMW F12" class="mw-redirect">BMW F12</a> and even five-door coupe, <a href="/wiki/Audi_A7" title="Audi A7">Audi A7</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The organization <a href="/wiki/ADAC" title="ADAC">ADAC</a> on its website also adopted this concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In Germany the definition of the coupe was finally divided into the classic coupe and 4-door coupe. This definition and concept of four-door coupe (instead of sedan) are evident in Germany, but they are not yet widely known in the rest of the world.</dd>
<dt>Quad coupé</dt>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Quad_coup%C3%A9" title="Quad coupé">Quad coupé</a></i> is a marketing name for cars with one or two small rear doors with no <i>B</i> pillar.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
<dt>Combi coupé</dt>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Combi_coup%C3%A9" title="Combi coupé">Combi coupé</a></i> is a marketing term used by <a href="/wiki/Saab_Automobile" title="Saab Automobile">Saab</a> for a car body similar to the <a href="/wiki/Liftback" title="Liftback" class="mw-redirect">liftback</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>With the growing popularity of the pillarless <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> during the 1950s some automakers used the term <i>coupé</i> to refer to hardtop (rigid, rather than canvas, automobile roof) models and reserved the term sedan for their models with a <i>B</i> pillar. This definition was by no means universal, and has largely fallen out of use with near-demise of the hardtop. Similarly, a <a href="/wiki/Rover_P5" title="Rover P5">Rover P5</a> saloon model came in a body style with a lower roof that was called a coupé. Technically, it was cut, as the original definition required, but it was not a shorter car body.<sup id="cite_ref-Autotropolis_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Autotropolis-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_usage">Current usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current usage">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coup%C3%A9&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current usage" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today <i>coupé</i> has become more of a <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> term for automotive manufacturers, than a fact of the vehicle's design and technical makeup.<sup id="cite_ref-Autotropolis_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Autotropolis-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> They ascribe the term to any vehicle with two, three, and now even four-doors, for the term's perceived luxury or sporting appeal. This is because coupés in general are seen as sportier than sedans; hence a coupé would be marketed as a sportier vehicle than a two-door sedan.</p>
<p>While previous coupés were "simply line-extenders two-door variants of family sedans", some coupés have different sheet metal and styling than their four-door counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/AMC_Matador" title="AMC Matador">AMC Matador</a> coupe of the 1970s had a unique design sharing almost nothing with the 4-door versions.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Even 2-door cars with a backseat are now being referred to as "sedans" in which the terms "coupe" and "sedan" are used interchangeably. Two-door sedans with front bench seating have phased out with the 1995-1999 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Monte_Carlo" title="Chevrolet Monte Carlo">Chevrolet Monte Carlo</a> being the last model to offer it.</p>
<p>However, 2-door cars in general have fallen in popularity, with the popular exception of <a href="/wiki/Convertible_(car)" title="Convertible (car)" class="mw-redirect">convertibles</a> and 2-seat <a href="/wiki/Roadster_(automobile)" title="Roadster (automobile)">roadsters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">Sedans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a> and <a href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV" class="mw-redirect">SUVs</a>/<a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagons</a> have had fewer 2-door models (especially ones with <a href="/wiki/Car_seat" title="Car seat">backseats</a>) in recent years since the cost of 4-door cars has gone down along with engineering to ease access to the back seat area.</p>
